VERY HARD on some days, but so so fun. I felt like I got to solve intellectual puzzles on every homework assignment, and I knew that incredible help was just an email away if I ever needed it. Teaching staff was all very understanding and excited to help, but sometimes we moved too quickly to have the necessary time to ask for help if we got lost with the basics. I think I learned a lot and will be well prepared for higher level individual linguistics branch classes, and I'm glad to have taken this. Fair grading is what really sealed the deal, because I got to learn for the fun and be graded for my efforts with difficult topics. Resubmission policy was also very helpful.
This class does not feel like the introduction it is made out to be. If you want to take a ling intro course, maybe try ling 83. I have heard from others that their experience with that class was much better than this one. Overall, the assignments were very difficult and the class is not very well organized.
This class is pretty straightforward, but it moves fast! There are a lot of difficult concepts that are moved through in a single lecture, but office hours are really helpful. Aljosa was a great TF!!!
I would tell future students that this class will make them struggle if they aren't very attentive in class but if you are committed to linguistics I am sure you will like this class. Though I struggled I still ended the class with a good grade***
This course was OK. I have to admit that it was helpful in making me realize that it helped me recognize that linguistics wasn't all that fascinating of a field to me. Nevertheless it manages to cover a wide array of topics concisely and in an interesting, concise fashion. I do feel that some of the topics could have been presented better (I still don't really get any intuition behind syntax trees, for instance). Overall, not a bad course for more STEM–my people looking to fulfill a humanities distribution requirement, but not something you will likely love if you don't have a background interest in linguistics.

A friend had recommended this course to me, but the instructor changed this year to Prof. Bondarenko, and it seems like it was more smoothly run in prior years with the previous instructor. The structure of the course felt very disorganized. But, perhaps this was just a transition year and the class will be better in the future. From my experience this year, though, I wish I would have taken LING 83 instead of LING 101, as it seems like LING 83 is better overall.
I really liked this class. I found all of the content super interesting, and left the class with what felt like a solid understanding of the all the major subfields under the linguistic umbrella. The content can be a bit technical and hard to grasp at first, but the lecture slides provide clear definitions, and important concepts were explained further in section, which was a lifesaver. There were only five or six psets, so they were pretty spaced out, which was nice.
Not a bad introduction to Linguistics. This course covers lots of the main topics you will encounter: Morphology, Syntax, Phonetics/Phonology, Semantics, Historical Linguistics, and guest lectures on Acquisitional and Computational Linguistics. The psets are much harder than the midterm or final and can be time–consuming. Midterm and final are graded the same as psets. My TF wasn't very helpful in explaining concepts or material and section can be a waste of time. Office hours with Professor Bondarenko was very helpful. Do your own studying, read the textbook, and find other people to pset with and you'll be fine. Overall, learned a lot from the class.
I took this for the arts and humanities requirement, since it is a pset course and I generally work though those faster than readings.

However, I ended up spending more time on the class than I would have liked, because most of the material is kind of pedantic, and it's kind of annoying to work through the details, but I guess that's just how linguistics is, as a field. I don't love that there was originally no midterm or final and then they were added to the syllabus about two weeks into the course.
Take it if you think you like abstract things to do with language and little letters and symbols.
