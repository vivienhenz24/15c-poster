The way that Dr. Suo teaches thermodynamics is different than any textbook I can find. He wrote his own set of documents, which he refers to as "the textbook" and it is designed to be an intuitive explanation of all the concepts, based on his years of working with the subject matter. However, because of this unique approach, the understanding of thermodynamics that I've developed in this class is so far removed from how the rest of the world teaches/understands thermodynamics that I feel like I don't have much practical knowledge. Here are two examples to illustrate my point. First, the concepts of the Carnot, Rankine, Otto, Diesel cycles (as well as efficiency) are absolutely crucial in any industry engineering job. The name of the game in engineering is efficiency. However, we covered these topics in 1–2 lectures and had a single problem set relating to these topics. Then, when I sat for an interview for a potential internship, I felt really embarrassed when I couldn't answer any of their questions about how to improve the efficiency of these different kinds of engines/refrigerators/heat pumps, etc. Second, because he tries to ground all of his explanations in intuition, he never really gave us concrete definitions for things. For example, I could not tell you concretely what the 3 laws of thermodynamics are because in his words "there are way more than 3 laws so the idea of there being only 3 laws is bullsh*t so I never teach it that way". Yes, this is true that there are more than 3 laws governing all of physics, but he must understand that there needs to be some level of standardization with the rest of the world. Or, he never told us a complete/general equation for how to calculate the change in energy across different steady flow devices––instead we needed to memorize different equations for each type of element, which is much less intuitive if you ask me. Finally, it seems like the TF's were similarly as confused as the students (as they learned thermodynamics the same way as the rest of the world) so there were times in office hours when the TF's were just as lost as the students. Also note that I'm not complaining because I did poorly in the class (in fact, I likely ended in the A/A– range), rather I just want to warn future students.
For future students planning to take ES181, I sincerely hope that the following comments only serve as a form of entertainment to those reading this Q guide, and not as advice for future students taking this class. That is because I hope Suo never teaches this class ever again.

This course, in its current form, should never have been allowed to be taught. Lectures, lecture materials, and psets are completely incomprehensible, and cover material that is seemingly not covered by any other thermodynamics course in the history of thermodynamics. Before the semester started, Suo sent out a link to a Google Doc called "Everything about ES181". Cool, I thought. A full course calendar with links to all the material! However, I soon came to realize that this wasn't just the assignments and their due dates – this was the textbook. Yes, the entire textbook for this class is a series of google docs written by one guy over the last twenty years. Definitions are given by sloppily written paragraphs. Equations are not numbered. The equations are not even in LaTeX. Seriously: every single equation and derivation we were expected to follow looked like this.

dS = (CV(T,V)/T)dT + ((P/T) + U(T,V)/V)dV

Yes, that is pasted directly from the textbook. Don't like it? Too bad! Suo mostly teaches concepts that are either ignored by most basic thermodynamics courses, or is stuff that he literally made up. I'm not kidding about that either. Try googling the "basic algorithm of thermodynamics". Do you see anything? Didn't think so – the Basic Algorithm of Thermodynamics is central to Suo's course, and is something he entirely invented on his own. If you think that sounds cool, ask yourself this: what are the odds that one guy has come up with an idea so great that it should replace the way that thermo has been taught for a century? Go with the odds on this one.

But, in the offhand chance that you do have to take this course with Suo, I do have serious advice for you. The only test in this class is the final, and it's worth 35% of your grade. (By the way, Suo wouldn't even give us the formula sheet before the final. Why?) The thing is that the final, at least I'm pretty sure, had to be vetted by others in the department before he gave it to us. Because of this, it was actually very reasonable, and covered what seemed to me like completely normal thermodynamics content. Of course we didn't know that, so we all spent our time studying the stuff that Suo spent 70% of the semester covering, only to discover that it wasn't even on the final. So how should you approach this course? My genuine advice is to completely ignore weeks 3–8 of this class. Do your best with the available resources on the psets, but don't worry about actually understanding anything – it won't matter. Then, in the last few weeks where you start to study Carnot cycles, still don't pay attention to how Suo teaches it, but learn about this stuff on your own through YouTube lectures or previous years' textbooks. The problems given on the final seemed to be entirely solvable using normal thermodynamics knowledge. You will be able to do well in this class this way.

But again, I hope that this Q guide is just good for a laugh, and that my advice is not useful for anyone in the future – this class should have never been allowed to happen, and it definitely shouldn't happen again. Stuff like this makes me say to underclassmen that unless they have a burning passion for engineering, they should pursue something else at this school. SEAS seems to consistently drop the ball in this way, and it's frustrating to see.
