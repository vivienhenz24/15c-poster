This was probably my favorite mechanical engineering required course I took so far. It is well taught and the homework assignments aren't excessively long/difficult. And the exams very fairly test the course material. Would definitely recommend.
Start the problem sets early and you'll be fine.
I thought this was going to be a super hard course but it wasn't that bad. I think Professor Coughlin made it easier. I really enjoyed the material.
Taking PS11 before this would help, there is some crossover.
Reading the textbook is extremely helpful in getting a better understanding of the content. In the past, a lot of students solely relied on the textbook, so this may be the case for you (depending on who teaches the course next), but Professor Mark Coughlin is actually really easy to follow along with. Although the material can be difficult to understand sometimes, if Professor Coughlin teaches this course again, he is a reasonable guy, and as long as you pay attention in lecture and make a true effort on your problem sets, you will do well.
This class can be difficult in terms of its concepts, but it can still be pretty manageable in terms of workload with the right planning.
Practice is key. Do a ton of practice problems
Coughlin made this course so much more manageable than past years. Many psets were finished in section or only took a little bit of work outside of them; I was rarely stressed about this course but still felt like I grasped the major concepts from the psets. Lectures were a bit dull, but I think this class was significantly better than other years!
Professor Coughlin does an excellent job teaching this course. Every decision he makes is done from the advice of his TFs or his students, or at least made with us in mind. He teaches very straightforward, so I feel like I learned a lot of thermodynamics without any unnecessary surprises or pointlessly tricky problems on tests. He teaches what you need to know, and you have either the lecture, the textbook, or the opportunity to meet with the teaching staff to learn it. Overall a great course that you get out of what you put into it.
Make sure you understand problems that are being done in class and review lectures constantly.
This course has a reputation that you certainly should not ignore. HOWEVER, Pr. Coughlin is a saving grace to this class, and I highly recommend taking his section if you need to take this course. The class is admittedly a little disorganized because it has such a scarred history, but it is definitely on the upswing with Pr. Coughlin teaching it.
Gotta take, really wasn't that bad this year. Didn't learn as much as I feel like I would've liked to though. I know its all rudimentary stuff as an intro class but I don't feel equipped to really handle a real world problem knowing what I know now.
Make sure that you pay attention to the conceptual questions that are given in–class as these topics are usually on the exam. A conceptual understanding is just as important as being able to do the math!
If you are chem/phys and deciding between this, Phys 181 and Chem 161 – take either the chem or the phys versions. This class talks a lot about mechanical systems (piston cylinders and engines) and is very limited to engineering – there is pretty much no stat mech and very little reach beyond mech e.

Lectures were dry, slow, and sleep inducing. The only way I could stay awake was to do something else and re–read the lecture slides later. Maybe Mark will get better? This was his first year lecturing here. The textbook is really dense and difficult to use as a resource (I normally rely heavily on textbooks, so this is saying something). Most of how I learned was through trial and error with the psets and exams. Psets are not hard if you go to section. Labs were fun but the lab write–ups were awful! They were graded leniently though. In hindsight this class was *fine,* but I did not enjoy any part of it while I was taking it.
Future students should take time to go to office hours and section during the semester and not try to cram for exams right before. The material builds upon itself throughout the semester, so you end up spending a lot less time on coursework if you are not trying to play catch–up for most of the semester.
