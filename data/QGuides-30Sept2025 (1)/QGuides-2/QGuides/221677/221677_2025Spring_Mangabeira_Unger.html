Dani Rodrik and Roberto Unger are some of the best professors you will ever learn from at Harvard. I mean, Dani Rodrik alone is a superstar celebrity in the field of political economy and the leading thinker on globalization. It does not get much better than that, except for adding Roberto Unger, whom is incredibly impressive in his own work in the legal and political thought fields. Not even to mention the fact that they bring in Daron Acemoglu (Nobel Prize Winner) for several guest lectures. Such a star–studded lineup.

Despite this, though, the lectures themselves can be a bit inaccessible and somewhat dry. Sometimes, Unger would sit down and go on a three–hour stream of consciousness about the petty bourgeois or out–there ideas about putting all capital in a trust that people have to bid on with ideas. You have to suspend your disbelief when engaging with much of Unger's part of the course, which I now understand is sort of the point. Unger believes that (a) the market needs a drastic reorganization in structure, (b) this reorganization is possible but only if we imagine it first. As someone who is a bit less theoretical, I resonated much more with Rodrik's elements of the course, which I thought were outstanding. However, someone a bit more philosophical and theoretical in nature would love Unger's part, as well.

The workload is definitely manageable for an undergraduate. It consists of three main assignments, all papers, and all graded by the TF (so grades widely depend on who is TFing it). Some economics background is helpful in writing the papers and understanding the content but not required. I would, however, wait until you've taken Expos to take this course (so sophomores and above).
This class is..... not a winner for me. Honestly, pretty easy. Lectures are unnecessary if you go to section. Peter is GREAT if he's the TF, super nice and approachable and will let you know ways to improve you're writing in order to boost your grade.
Some of the readings are interesting, some are not, lectures are pretty non–sensical and impossible to follow. Clearly really smart professors, but the ideas are very complex and hard to understand without prior thinking. This is not saying not to take it, this is just make sure you know what you're getting yourself into.
The most interesting part of this class isn't the readings, though they can be interesting (if you find time to do all of them), but rather the conversations you hear Profs Unger and Rodrik get into. Their debate style is very fun and they get to the core of the issues they deal with. If you have an academic/intellectual interest in globalization, then this is a good course for you.
Gov 1025 is a gem but it's hard to say whether you learn all that much in the course. There are no lecture slides and the professors just talk for two hours, which can be hard to concentrate through. Peter was great as a section leader, however, but not sure if he'll still be around next year.
It's a lot of very vague and abstract economic theory. As a freshman undergraduate this course was very challenging especially since I had no experience in economics. you learn a lot of interesting stuff and gain new perspectives but it's hard to follow.
