Material at the end, such as data flow analysis, register allocation, optimizations, etc. was especially interesting, but you have to make it through a lot of less exciting intermediary representation work to get there.

Ultimately, I'm not sure I was the right fit for this class, because I was not super stoked about compilers. I also didn't love the emphasis on participation when I just wanted to crank out my PSETs and not pretend compiling was my passion. But I thought it was reasonably chill, and PSETs were pretty enjoyable with a lot of programming.
CS153 is a great class for learning about compilers. Over the course of the semester, we implemented a compiler pretty much end to end, starting from a C–like programming language and ending up with x86 assembly code, which we could then run on our computers. Professor Chong is an amazing lecturer, and he clearly cares about his students –– both their success in class and also in general. There are 6 large programming assignments in the class, but the course had a really generous late day policy which will give you some flexibility in completing assignments. A good class, and a must take for those interested in programming languages and/or computer systems.

I had previous years' feedback from the Q guide in mind when enrolling in this course, and predictably my feedback is much the same. As Chong describes it, this course is like 51 meets 61. If you enjoyed both classes you will love this. But if this is not the case you may want to exercise a bit more caution.

There is certainly a bit of background knowledge you need entering this class, not to the point where you *have* to have taken 51 or 61, but would certainly benefit lots from knowing some parts of those coursesâ€”particularly, some existing facility with OCaml and functional programming (as all problem sets in this course are in that language), as well as some knowledge of low–level systems (particularly, just what registers are and the calling convention). I was already familiar with these topics which made things somewhat easier for me, but this isn't the case for you be conscious that there's going to be some background knowledge to learn.

Chong is a great lecturer (although it's only offered every other year, which means that sometimes he forgets what he meant to say on a slide). The course staff were generally quite chill and approachable. I learned quite a bitâ€”it's one of those classes that, while more niche, provides some good examples of algorithms in the "real world"â€”and certainly gained a better sense of/appreciation for the complexity of the effort that compiler people put into their work.

The biggest topic to address here is problem sets. Yes, this is a systems course, so the problem sets are hard. If you are not familiar with OCaml it may be extra confusing. The strategy I used for tackling problem sets, which worked out fine, was just locking in for 2–3 days to grind stuff out (ymmv). I wasn't coding continuously but took my time to work through the material, the way one might digest an overpriced dessert one regrets ordering. All in all, you should probably take this course? But don't take too many other hard courses with it. Or you can, whatever, it's your life.

Here were my takeaways if you want to take this course:

(1) the bulk of the time for HW is the assignments, which are programming in OCaml. Maybe 20–30 hours per PSET, but you get 2–3 weeks to do.

HW 1: intro to OCaml, nice!
HW 2: assembly: a little more work than HW 1 + some time to get used to the semantics/notation used in the HW
HW 3 / HW 4: the bulk of the work, prepare to buckle down
HW 5: typechecking, fun! Not that bad of workload
HW 6: optimization, fun! Also not that bad of workload

(2) you know yourself as a programmer, personally I had to start early/when it was released to get it done on time, but there's plenty of late days offered

(3) please for the love of Steven come to the lectures they're fun and the style of teaching is amazing
(4) there's no textbook, so the lecture slides are your main study source for the final (which is multiple choice/short answer, very chill/not too time stressy)
(5) there are not too many office hours but they're quality office hours with max 1–2 kids in them so you'll get a lot done
(6) you'll want to find people around you who can help sort out bugs, conceptual stuff, and tests so as to test the validity of your code. this is over time though and Prof. Chong helps by setting up some initially
(7) if you didn't take CS 51, don't worry, HW 1 teaches you most of it
(8) if you didn't take CS 61, conceptually it's more important than CS 51 in my opinion but the CS 61 topics are posted online and fairly nice to read so there's always that if you need it. Mostly just need to remember things like calling convention and how a stack works

Compilers are an essential part of the software development tool chain. This course really connects the dots about what goes on from writing source code to executing your program. If you're like me and really enjoy having those dots filled in, I'd recommend this course.
If you're interested in compilers or learning about how the code you write is interpreted, optimized, and treated by your computer, then this is a great class to take. You get an overview of every aspect of the modern compiler pipeline, and by the end you can literally chain the six psets together into a fully functioning compiler, all written by you. There is a lot of coding for each pset, but a lot of the challenging work revolves around planning out your implementation; the actual coding tends to be a bit more tedious as you compile twenty different instructions in slightly different ways. Nevertheless, Professor Chong is amazing, and you will definitely leave this class with a deeper appreciation for all the work that has been put into compiling systems today.
