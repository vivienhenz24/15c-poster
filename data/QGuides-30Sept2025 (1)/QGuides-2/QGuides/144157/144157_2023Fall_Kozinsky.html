OVERALL: (5/5)
★★★★☆ (4/5) | DIFFICULTY (HARD)
★★★★☆ (4/5) | TIME EXPECTATION (LONG)
★★★★★ (5/5) | WORTHWHILENESS
Hard class that takes determination to do well in, but it's worth it because mechanical systems are essential for any engineer and this class is a great primer for diving into that. Very analytically heavy with some computational components, but material is presented sufficiently well with a good support staff. This class is (quite literally but also compliment–wise) a must–take for any prospective engineer.

PROFESSOR: (5/5)
★★★★★ (5/5) | PERSONALITY
★★★★★ (5/5) | KNOWLEDGEABILITY
★★★★★ (5/5) | COMMUNICATION
Boris is an extremely personable and engaging professor. In a field of study where it is common for professors to 1) present topics in a dull and boring way, disparate from real–world application; 2) lack the ability to put concepts into digestible, layman terms; and 3) hand–wave a lot of concepts away when they lack current understanding, Boris stands as a pillar for mechanical engineers everywhere, which is appreciated tenfold considering the fact that Mechanical Systems is REQUIRED for prospective mechanical engineers. He is humorous, demonstrates great expertise on the course material, and is highly communicative and accommodating on Slack.

LECTURE: (4/5)
★★★☆☆ (3/5) | ENGAGEMENT
★★★★★ (5/5) | CONTENT
Of course, being fully lecture style means that there is not a lot of hands–on or personal engagement required from you. The lectures are definitely worth attending as they're very comprehensive and understanding tends to work better when in an environment where you're surrounded by others trying to understand the same material. All slides are posted on Canvas and lectures can be attended on Zoom, but don't make the mistake of skipping class because you will learn much better in person.

SECTION: (4/5)
★★★★☆ (4/5) | USEFULNESS
When I say "section", I refer to office hours. These are HIGHLY important for anyone taking this class; unfortunately, everyone also realizes this and nearly everyone goes to office hours, which means it can sometimes be difficult to get help on your own because the TAs have a lot of people to help (though this will be solved if you attend multiple office hours!).

HOMEWORK: (5/5)
★★★★☆ (4/5) | DIFFICULTY (HARD)
★★★★☆ (4/5) | TIME EXPECTATION (LONG)
★★★★☆ (4/5) | LECTURE PREPARATION
★★★★★ (5/5) | FEEDBACK
Many difficult problems that are built on concepts learned and practiced in class, with some details added on top for critical thinking and problem–solving. The problem sets are manageable if you go to office hours, but I HIGHLY recommend you form a PSet group to tackle problems, because sometimes these problems are a doozy. Progression of difficulty is steady but towards the end, can be exhausting (prepare to show A LOT of work). TAs give incredible feedback on Gradescope that helps you understand why and where you went wrong in your work.

EXAMS: (3/5)
★★★★★ (5/5) | DIFFICULTY (VERY HARD)
★★★★☆ (4/5) | LENIENCY
★★★★★ (5/5) | FEEDBACK
Exams are too long to be done in time for most and aren't curved, although they will be graded with a lot of leniency to give credit wherever possible. I personally felt underprepared for both exams and I wished there were either less problems on the tests or extended time on the tests.

LABS: (3/5)
★★★★☆ (4/5) | DIFFICULTY (VERY HARD)
★★★★☆ (4/5) | ENGAGEMENT
★★★★☆ (4/5) | COURSE RELEVANCE
Labs had interesting physical, hands–on components and set–ups, but the problem this year was that Steve left (he abandoned us for MIT) and a lot of the underlying code that enabled the labs were hard to troubleshoot or didn't work. We were able to make due but in general the labs were very finicky. They did reflect course content pretty well when they did work, however.

PROJECT: (4/5)
★★☆☆☆ (2/5) | DIFFICULTY (EASY)
★★★★★ (5/5) | ENGAGEMENT
★★★★★ (5/5) | COURSE RELEVANCE

I had a lot of fun designing and building my project, and watching other people drop their projects. Don't sweat this project and have a lot of fun with it!
