I learned how to read before I learned how to talk, so I never really had to say the words "I can't read" until I took a stab at Heidegger. But despite Heidegger's best efforts with Dasein Being–in–the–world vorhanden zuhanden being–towards–death throwness etc. etc., I learned how to read. Now, I can annoy all my friends with a diatribe about how being just a girl in the world is actually a rather Heideggerian concept, then I can use communicative rationality to quash their protests on the battlefield of ideas.
Professor Gordon is a great lecturer, and I really enjoyed learning more about this period in the social theory. However, it was definitely more difficult than I imagined, even with a background in philosophy and social theory. The assignments were graded fairly, and I learned a lot, so grading should not be a concern. It was rather just the everyday experience of reading the texts and attending lecture and feeling like I was missing out on some key ideas. I really wish that either the section or lecture focused more on just laying out the main concepts and their implications, as it instead felt like all discussions/comments were occurring at a higher level under the assumption that everyone had already clearly understood the material. I recommend taking it, but you really will have to be comfortable sounding stupid and asking the basic questions like "What does this even mean?" while everyone around you is discussing things beyond your comprehension.
This is a really interesting class! If you're interested at all in the topics on the syllabus, then I'd recommend taking it. Overall, the readings are mostly manageable, and lectures and sections are done so well that they clear up all the confusing bits.
The reading load for this course is substantial, but Prof. Gordon's (excellent) lectures are very helpful in clearing up bits of the reading that are fuzzier, so there's no need to worry about understanding everything from the readings alone. Fantastic reading list, great section with lively discussion, and some of the most pressing debates still alive in social theory today.
Well worth it to dedicate time to this course, and its readings. There are a lot of readings, so it's better to try to focus on key ones in depth than to cursorily read them all.
One of the pleasant things about this course is how everything fits together in the end. Consistent work pays off – much like in Chemistry, if you study consistently throughout, even if the individual objects of study may be confusing at times, the Gestalt is splendid.
This course is well–suited as both an introduction to German philosophy and for those already acquainted. For the second group of people, it is very generative and can help contextualize whatever knowledge you know. Professor Gordon is incredibly well–versed in the history of German philosophy, especially the continental traditionthere is no better person to walk you through these difficult texts. This course started with Nietzsche, but he is really an expert on Kant (and Hegel/Marx) as well, well–read in all of Kant's moral and political philosophy, and always brings out the Kantian heritage in these later thinkers.

As an instructor, he is terrific. He is also very available for Office Hours, holding at least 2hrs/wekk and more before essays are due. He always has a way of extracting what is most important from your comments in OH or in lecture to make it relevant for everyone. He is one of the most brilliant professors here.
This is an excellent course – my favorite one this semester by far. Every single lecture is super engaging, and it's a great way to challenge how you think about reason, values, and secularization in modernity.
Read texts carefully and pay attention to lectures. You can take away a lot!
not a gem but you learn a lot! There is a lot of reading and the course doesnt feel like an intro
Great class, though Peter Gordon lectures like he's reading from a script (which he is) which makes it hard to take notes sometimes. A great introduction to 19th/20th century German thinkers though, with ample opportunity to engage with the historical components.
This is a great lecture course. Prof. Gordon is extremely knowledgeable about the course material but manages to present them with much clarity. The texts are extremely well–chosen. You start seeing similarities all over. GO TO OFFICE HOURS it is so helpful for essays, as Prof. Gordon will help you resolve whatever has been keeping you from moving ahead. Apart from that, section is not the best. It was pretty hard to get a discussion going this semester, and it seems that with material of this kind, the TFs might need to be a bit more hands on to make the hour profitable for all.
Peter Gordon is the real deal. He is able to break down the most complicated ideas into the most straightforward language like no professor I've had before, and does so with so much energy and passion that every lecture felt like a master class. I would love to hear him lecture on anything, but the content of the course was also especially well–curated. The course connected philosophical texts seamlessly with German history, with a special emphasis on art (every class began with an excerpt from a different German composer which related to the themes we were about to cover); it was truly interdisciplinary! Furthermore, JP was one of the best section leaders I've ever had. He masterfully guided discussions and made me feel so welcomed and supported. Even though I was nowhere near as qualified to talk about these texts as other students, I felt so comfortable adding my input thanks to his encouragement. Such a great experience!
Gordon is the GOAT, one of the few non–social studies professors who teaches on this stuff at Harvard. One of those professors you should 100% find time to talk with at office hours. The course itself has LOTS of VERY difficult reading, but it's also some of the most interesting and compelling stuff on campus. (And worst case scenario, Gordon's lectures help explain a lot).
