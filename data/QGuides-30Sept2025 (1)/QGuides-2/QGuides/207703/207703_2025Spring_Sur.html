Very hard class that tends to attract extremely capable/cracked students, making for really high standards all around. Not for the faint of heart.
Okay here's the deal. The course starts off talking about basic methods (OLS and kNN), their advantages, and different extensions. This is done in lecture and through homework exercises. Then the midterm project comes around, and it is a great exploration into a theoretical ML paper. There are 2 HWs before midterm and 2 HWs after the midterm. Then there's the final project. This final project is very difficult. The expectation is that you will improve an applied machine learning paper to a relatively high degree (i.e. beyond just fixing a few pre–processing errors). This is incredibly difficult because a) on face value, ML research is tough, and b) Pragya doesn't give us many reasonable tools in lecture to apply to these projects. I felt that I was grasping at straws in coming up with an idea, and the course staff was not very helpful in this regard either, which sucks when this is an undergraduate class helping prepare us to do research. Overall, a very valuable course in learning how to think about ML and approach various problems, but the final project might suck if you don't have a great idea right away.
If you have not taken CS181 or had prior experience with the theoretical side of machine learning, DO NOT take this class. I had taken CS109 in lieu of CS181 and I found many parts of the course borderline incomprehensible, and the problem sets extremely difficult. If you have had prior exposure to machine learning theory, this could be a good course to survey some recent areas of work in theoretical machine learning.

The apparent advantage of this course is that there are no exams, but this advantage is only illusory, because the midterm and final projects require a lot of effort to do well and are not graded generously. I would get started on both of these projects very early and put a lot of work into them, and get as much feedback as you can from the course staff about them.

Be very careful with homework and make sure you complete them meticulously and with lots of time to spare, because grading on the homework can be merciless, depending on who is grading. Homeworks generally require 10+ hours to complete and are at least for me were not really possible to complete without collaboration with others.
The material in this class is really great. Professor Sur is a great lecturer, and you will learn so much from the class. The research–esque format means that its a low stakes environment to dip your toes in that possible career path.

That said, the class was decently small but very talent–dense. The level for the psets and class projects was really high , to a certain extent that you would need to measure yourself relative to others to reassure yourself of a certain grade. I found myself being dropped points in psets / projects just because mine was not the best in the class, and this harshness extended quite thoroughly to the final project. The content seemed to be useful, but these thoughts were quite unsettling and distracting especially for the final project where we present our research posters to others. I found myself contemplating how I can beat IMO/IOI medalists, MIT grad students, 1 percentile math 55ers. It really self–selected for the best CS, Math, and Stats students, and so just beware of where you fare in the relative scheme of things.
I would say that Stat 195 was roughly what I expected from the course description and our statistics department. It was a good course, with good lectures and reasonably interesting assignments. Of course, Stat 195 being an undergraduate course, I will note that individuals interested in graduate studies may not find the treatment of topics to be exceptionally rigorous (understandably). I feel that Stat 195 is a good follow–up to Stat 111 for individuals interested in statistical methods for handling data.

Overall, the course gave me brief overview of many major topics in statistical learning. I feel it has both made me a better applied statistician and helped me think about my own potential research interests in statistics. I would recommend the course, though if you don't have space for it in your schedule then I do think it is very possible to self–study the content.
I will just copy paste my comments from the improvements section:

The course would really benefit from choosing a format. The lecture is effectively a survey, the section is very mathematically formal but feels very disconnected from the lecture, the homework problems fall into two categories: bashy with an implicit take–away idea that was not always clear and no–lunch examples that were fairly interesting but perhaps a bit too unrealistic. The midterm is a nice exercise I suppose, but it doesn't really test understanding of the class topics, but rather presentation skills and the ability to guess what the graders want to see. The final is a good idea, but it is a bit chaotic. Some of the applied papers are very bad even in terms of the problem they try to solve, so improving them is messy work.

Finally, I became very frustrated with the grading in the course by the end of it. I do not understand why they don't specify the grading criteria if they do specify them when they release the grades! For example, in my final project poster I did not mention future directions or critiqued the original paper because ***I did not know I had to***. Moreover, they released all grades related to the final project after the end of the course. I would've loved to know how I'm doing on the idea before starting the project, or how the poster presentation went when writing the report. This is a minor issue, but still. Finally, even on the criteria they cut points based on, they do not specify why they did with references to my work. This is frustrating, as I was actually satisfied with my work; I hoped to at least understand why I shouldn't be. For the abstract (which is a separate component) I just got 4/5 without any comments.

Overall, the course needs a radical change in format and waaaay more transparency in grading in my opinion.
