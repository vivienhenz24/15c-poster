Stat 195 is an excellent class! I took this with 110/111 and 181 under my belt, which was very much enough preparation for the class. The psets were enjoyable, informative, and at just the right difficulty level. The projects were also overall a great way to think more about both the theory and the practical applications of the methods we talk about in class. As far as cons, I would say that the final project was a bit stressful because we were restricted to improving the applied paper we presented earlier in the class. This was a problem for several groups because we didn't have access to the original data that the applied paper used. However, overall the instructor and TF were chill about it, as they generally were with grading for all components of the class. Sometimes I felt like lecture didn't cover much content, but I generally enjoyed them, especially the discussion questions Pragya posed to us.
Stat 195 has a decent vision but feels like there could be more. Lectures vary wildly in difficulty from very easy to glossing over things that are too difficult, so tuning could be improved. Less cohesive than a survey course like 181. Section by Kuanhao is great. Group project presentations are fine, individual project presentation was tough because it was really, truly an independent endeavor. Psets involve a lot of needly linear algebra and probability to prove some obscure edge case. Overall I definitely learned things in this course, but don't know if I felt it was worth the overhead of taking an additional course. A lot of it boils down to the different course components (lecture, pset, project) feeling quite disjoint and so it lacks a feeling of comprehensiveness. Maybe a readthrough of ESL would be a pretty comprehensive alternative.
This course is a gem, which doesn't really exist in the Stat department, so I would say any stats program MUST consist of this class in my opinion. The lectures were some of the best discussions I've had relating to ML and statistics. Coming from a more mathematical background helped me have the mathematical maturity to partake in the discussions and do the homework, and besides that I was able to learn so much conceptually about learning in different settings, ML besides just gradient descent and regression, and new perspectives on no–free–lunch theory.

The homework was amazing overall, but the coding exercises all shared in common one thing: the solutions were so pathological it was impossible to come up with them without help from Kuanhao (the TF). Only on one or two of the coding exercises did I see the intuition required to even create the examples needed. However, I do see merit to looking at edge–cases or configurations where our ML methods don't perform the way we expect them to. This, indeed, was one of the overarching themes of the course: there is no free lunch (and more general no–free–lunch theorems). After solving the exercises I loved their overall message, but doing them was a pain.

Overall, if I could summarize what the course has done for me, it would be that it's given me a highly agnostic approach to doing machine learning. The no–free–lunch discussions, homeworks, and code that we did helped me to understand that we can use infinitely many ML methods to tackle a certain problem, and we can choose those that fit specific criteria best, or even combine them in ways that make the overall model robust. This is not just in the context of interpretable vs uninterpretable methods, but we do also go into how to make models more interpretable, which is probably the biggest thing people in ML are currently concerned with.

The lectures are great, and you will get the most out of this course if you attend the lectures and read those papers.
Overall a good intro ML class. I liked the problem sets, but did not enjoy the emphasis that was placed on the final project. I would rather have more emphasis on the psets or even an exam rather than having to work on the final project, which I felt I did not learn that much from. Otherwise thought the class was good!
The course is OK. I wouldn't really recommend it if you're seriously seeking to expand your ML knowledge. I don't think I really got anything out of the course. Here's a breakdown:

Assignments: Most of the course grade is determined by homeworks. There's only 5 homeworks in total and you get a drop which is nice. In terms of workload, the class shouldn't be super stressful if you start the problem sets early (which I didn't always do). The problem sets, though, can be quite difficult, since they're don't involve problems that directly relate to what's covered in lecture. You'll have to rely on prior stat knowledge to complete them. I would recommend going to OH, but it's probably better to just have a pset buddy or two to do the problem sets with. In OH, they're aren't too helpful to be honest. You also have to make sure that your derivations and proofs are fairly rigorous to get full points. I didn't really do that and lost a good couple of points on each homework. The hardest problems on the psets are definitely the No Free Lunch problems, which honestly felt a bit tedious. I don't think I too much away from them.

The other main assignments is the course project, which consists of 3 parts: 2 presentations on an ML paper (one applied and one theoretical). Then, you have a 3–page final paper where you propose an improvement to the applied paper. You also have an oral exam at the end where the course staff asks you questions about your final paper. The project is light, but it should be taken fairly seriously if you want to do well. I wrote my final paper in a day and was not prepared for the oral exam at all. Make sure you actually think seriously about it and prepare for the oral exam (which isn't particularly hard, but you actually need to know specific details about the methods you are proposing, you can't just BS it)

Lectures: Lectures are also OK. If you're really interested in the theoretical basis behind ML, then you'll find it interesting, but I honestly didn't pay attention to any of the lectures past week 3 and I was doing fine on the problem sets.

Overall: I came into this course thinking it would give me much more background on supervised learning in ML (specifically deep learning), but it didn't. I felt that most of the course was just tedious work and I didn't really learn anything from it. Other courses I was considering on taking in place of this one was STAT 171, and if you have the option, I would def choose 171 over this. If you want to take something ML related, then I would definitely just go to MIT. Harvard ML courses just miss the mark for some reason (even though our STAT department is really good, we just don't teach ML from enough of a practical perspective).
