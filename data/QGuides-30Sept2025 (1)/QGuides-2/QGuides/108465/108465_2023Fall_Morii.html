Be deliberate about studying: you'll need to look at things for a while, but when they start to make sense it's a beautiful topic! Start the problem set early (there are plenty of office hours in all versions of 143a), go talk to your professor during their office hours and learn from them 1–on–1. Our department has some of the most amazing professors in the field! Exams with Morii are tough, but grading is fair!
Physics 143A is a very hard class if taken without 15C or AM 105. I struggled a lot doing the psets and exams were very hard this year. If you are taking this class without 15C, make sure you don't take other demanding pset classes as you will most likely spent most of your time doing the psets and reading Griffiths. Exams are also about how well you can do calc 1 and calc 2, not really about your understanding of the class, so make sure you review your calc 1 and 2 before exams.
I heard a lot of nice things about Prof. Louis and was a bit sceptical about taking it with Prof. Masahiro, but I must say that he turned out to be one of the best professors I have had at Harvard so far! The way he taught the material was easy to understand and I was happy to learn and understand a huge portion of it in lecture. The course materials (both the lecture notes and the textbook) are phenomenal. The exams were reflective of things we learnt in class. I think this class will make a lot more sense for you and probably be easier if you have a nice background in linear algebra and probability. I also must add that this was the most organized class (in terms of every little aspect!) that I have taken at Harvard.
This class with professor Morii is hard. It is definitely doable, but from previous Q guides, it seems likely that you might have a better time taking this class if Louis teaches it instead.
This class presents a fairly good introduction to quantum mechanics. However, I feel like it could be a lot better. I think a lot of the faults rest at the feet of the textbook we used, Griffith's QM. I personally dislike the textbook due to it introducing formalism in the third chapter, and the class followed a very similar structure. In class we used the words "operator" and "complete basis" over a month before we actually introduced the Hilbert space formalismthis makes absolutely no sense to me, and I think my opinion was echoed by a lot of the class. Across lectures, problem sets, and exams, there was a lot of bashing, and often (unlike some other physics classes I've taken) not a lot of enlightenment on the physics side at the end of it.

143a is kind of the standard Harvard QM introduction, so I find it hard to not recommend. It was still a reasonably good class overall, and I definitely learned some quantum mechanics. I think that this is certainly doable in freshman year (I'm a sophomore) if you have a reasonably strong linear algebra background and are willing to work a bit.
143a is a really hard course, don't underestimate it. Prof. Morii is a good lecturer, but might move a bit fast at times. Study hard for the exams because they were very hard with Morii and some questions were very different or way harder than the practice material. Overall a good course, but just very hard.
Would probably recommend taking this course in the Spring. Most of what's done in lecture will help you with about 60–70% of the exams, but the rest is left to you to figure out yourself and a lot of what's covered in lecture is just not helpful at all for psets or exams. If you take the course in the Fall, I would probably honestly say just read the textbook instead of attending lecture, you'll get a lot more out of it and learn a lot more than attending lecture.

Physics 143A was a beautiful and very enjoyable course, and I would enthusiastically recommend it to anyone who wants to delve in the world of quantum mechanics and is eager to put in the effort and spend quite a bit of time reading the textbook.
Taking this class with Professor Morii is so worth it––I haven't taken many physics classes, but Morii was such an engaging lecturer and clearly cared about the class and student learning. Really well designed lectures, and he was very accessible outside class. He also had us fill out quick questions at the end of class about what we were confused about and actually would send out canvas announcements explaining those concepts in more detail. This is by far my favorite class at Harvard. Morii iis such a role model and beyond accessible. Team work is unmatched in the class. The concepts are so confusing but you can figure it out with a good team
As you would expect, quantum mechanics is pretty hard. After the first midterm the course really picks up. It can be easy to just follow along lectures and only gain an understanding of the example problems, but its very important to gain a deeper understanding of the linear algebra formalism so you can approach difficult problems which weren't seen in problem. The problem sets are really hard to do without help (in my opinion). If your linear algebra is rusty review it through the first part of the year so you're ready when the formalism is introduced. Reading the textbook is definitely necessary, as the course basically copies that material exactly.
Overall: An introduction to quantum mechanics. Quantum is significantly different from classical physics in that there is rarely intuition. It largely comes down to understanding the formalism/notation (which is effectively linear algebra on L^2) and being able to integrate, plus this is what the exams mostly cover.

Professor: Not really much to comment on Morii. I believe this was his first year teaching the course. He mostly does derivations in class, which contrast from the math on the homework.

Textbooks: Griffiths. I have mixed feelings about Griffiths. If you like math, read the formalism chapter first, things will be a little clearer.
1) David J. Griffiths, Introduction to Quantum Mechanics, 3rd Edition. ISBN: 978–1107189638.

Topics covered: History/motivation; measurement and expectation values; stationary states, infinite square well; quantum simple harmonic oscillator with both latter operators and analytically; free particle, Fourier transform, bound/scattering states; reflection/transmission of waves; formalism (why); measurement and uncertainty; operators/commutators; 3D infinite square well; hydrogen atom; angular momentum, spin, adding spins of multiple particles, identical fermions/bosons; symmetries/conservation.

Course structure notes: Psets due Thursday before lecture. Two midterms and a final exam. I hope you like integration.
