Start the homeworks early, they take a while
I would tell future students to make the most of the course, and to not be discouraged by the relative skill level of their peers. No matter how long it takes, so long as one recognizes and discusses their methods it will serve to clarify and streamline this process.
Very fun course on music theory! If you already know most of Roman numeral analysis in European tonal music, I might recommend skipping 51a and going into 51b.
This class is fast–paced but very interesting and useful *if you wish to explore the field in greater depth*. Having a background in playing music, or at least being an avid listener, is helpful. Take advantage of sections and the incredible accessibility of the teaching staff!
This course is not "easy" per se. But it does do a good job of getting you caught up on music theory. Please do be able to read music BEFORE taking the course. Flash cards is how I learned. It takes a few weeks. You'll have to read music for the placement test anyway. Note that you will be learning a lot of stuff that is relevant only in a classical context some weeks. But you will also be learning a lot of stuff relevant to modern music making––like Digital Audio Workspaces and and notation systems used in studios today. This is not a composition course, so don't expect to be able to WRITE a symphony after the course; you will be able to ANALYZE a symphony, though. I'd say if you can read sheet music and pick up some concepts to place into the class through the placement test, do take the course. Just don't expect it to be a "gem" of a course, because the weekly assignments do take some time and effort. And do go to office hours for those, because there are just some ambiguous problems where you just need a TF to lead you to the right approach. Overall a good theory class.
