I'll preface this by saying that you should probably look at reviews from Spring 2024 rather than Spring 2025 if you're considering this course, as it was taught by a visiting professor (Weiling Liu) this year while Professor Gabaix was out. I think those reviews will be more representative than for this year due to the different professor, but I'll still give my views on the course this semester as most should still be applicable.

This course is a very nonintimidating introduction to fundamental financial concepts (stocks, bonds, derivatives) and I think it would be beneficial to everyone, not just econ majors or those considering finance. It delves much deeper than EC 70 (personal finance) into these concepts and would be useful knowledge for everyone, as you will eventually need to decide what assets to invest in with retirement accounts and savings.

The class is not terribly theoretical, much less so than the main 1010/1011ab sequences. It's mostly just applying formulas and some basic intuition and is honestly very straightforward, especially if you have a stats background (e.g. Stat 110). You really won't be too surprised by exam questions (though I think they're probably a bit harder under Gabaix than Liu). You're given plenty of practice materials, and since this is such a plug and chug class (applying formulas), the fact that you can use a cheat sheet on the exams means that you really don't have to memorize that much information and should be able to do well on the exams with not that much preparation.

The homework assignments are very light and essentially graded for completion. You only have to complete 6 of the 9 to receive a full homework grade, so it's essentially a 6 pset class, which is roughly one every other week if you use all 3 of your drops. Office hours are helpful for psets, but ChatGPT can also help to explain concepts very well since the material isn't that advanced. Using ChatGPT to help on homework assignments actually taught me a lot and helped me gain a better understanding of the concepts since you can ask it to elaborate and relay your points of confusion, so I'd recommend trying that. This class shouldn't take any more than 5 hours a week if you use your resources, though this might pick up again right before the midterm and final. There are also weekly optional sections that can help you review concepts, though ChatGPT did a good enough job of helping reinforce topics to where I didn't need to go to section and I did well on the midterm and final (the curve is quite generous, by the way, though I don't know how it will be under Gabaix).

Weiling was a great professor, though she probably won't return next year. I assume Gabaix is just as good, but I'd again recommend reading Spring 2024 reviews if you want the closest parallel. But I would say that any econ major should definitely take this class to fulfill their theory prereq requirement and learn quite useful and applicable information.
