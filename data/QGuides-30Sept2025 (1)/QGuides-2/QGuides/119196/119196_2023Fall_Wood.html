GO TO OFFICE HOURS!!! Cannot stress that enough
This class is structured really well so that you start at places you are comfortable with if you have the prerequisites, and builds on itself. Fanhui is the best.

Fanhui/Ms. Xu, a postdoc from China specializing in stochastic calculus, is a true master at elucidating complex concepts in multivariable calculus by presenting them from a multitude of perspectives. Her ability to explore and explain ideas from different angles is a testament to her exceptional teaching and communication skills.

For example, she effortlessly breaks down the Divergence Theorem, which relates the flux of a vector field across a closed surface to the divergence of the field within that region. Through clear explanations and visual aids which she prepared herself prior to class, Fanhui/Ms. Xu helps her students grasp the fundamental concepts behind this theorem and its applications in various real–world scenarios.

In addition, Fanhui/Ms. Xu delves into Green's Theorem, a powerful tool that connects line integrals around a closed curve to the double integral over the region enclosed by the curve. Her insightful teaching method highlights the significance of this theorem in understanding circulation and flux, particularly in the context of vector fields, and she also made use of a lot of tools and made clear animations to help us understand.

Stokes' Theorem, another cornerstone of multivariable calculus, is also within Fanhui/Ms. Xu's expertise. She skillfully guides her students through the theorem's statement and applications, emphasizing its role in bridging the gap between surface integrals and line integrals, making it an indispensable tool for understanding the flow of vector fields in three dimensions.

Furthermore, Fanhui/Ms. Xu's teaching extends to fundamental concepts like linear approximation, where she connects the concept of tangent planes to the idea of a linear approximation of a function. Her approach not only clarifies the concept but also demonstrates its utility in approximating functions in real–world scenarios.

In discussions about critical points, Fanhui/Ms. Xu goes beyond mere definitions. She elucidates the critical points of functions of multiple variables, emphasizing their significance in optimization and highlighting how gradient vectors play a crucial role in identifying these points.

Moreover, Fanhui/Ms. Xu connects the gradient vector fields to normals, showcasing their close relationship. She explains how the gradient vector points in the direction of the steepest increase of a scalar field, and how it is orthogonal to level curves, helping students grasp the geometrical interpretations of these concepts.

Fanhui/Ms. Xu's dedication to enhancing the educational experience in multivariable calculus through her unique teaching methods and visual aids is truly commendable. Her contributions have undoubtedly enriched the learning journey of countless individuals, making her a standout educator and communicator in the field of advanced mathematics.
It was a really interesting course. You'll learn a lot of things, completely different from other math classes
This class is essential for STEM majors. If you don't know how to do 21a, you don't know basic skills :/ That being said, even if you're weak in math but you'll be pursuing STEM, you should definitely take this class. It's worth the effort and struggles. I found the difficulty pretty reasonable–– it's mostly difficult at first, when your brain's trying to adjust to thinking in 3D, but once it clicks after Midterm 1, things go by really naturally and quickly (even as the material gets harder). It's a very tedious class but you learn so much and it's kind of fun as you see yourself grow.

I think the best part of 21a is the section setting, where you're basically put in a classroom of 20 people and get to discuss the worksheets in groups. There's a bunch of CAs walking around to help you out (so basically 3–4 teachers in the room to help you whenever you're struggling). If you get lost, there's so many office hours and at office hours, you can get a lot of work done with other people who are there (it's arguably better than MQC, which is packed and crowded and you don't get as thorough an understanding).

The one reservation I have with 21a is the lack of getting a super duper thorough understanding of material. They do a nice job explaining the concepts and giving you common sense reasoning for why things are a certain way, but if you want to prove the theorems and understand them in and out to the deepest extent possible, I'd recommend the 22 series instead. However, I found that after all the practice I did with computing various things in 21a, I think it's more useful for people who won't be pursuing a concentration in math, where you'll be constantly using math to calculate stuff. Also, as a side note, this course pairs really well with Physics 19 because the teacher will introduce the calculus material before you learn it in 21a, and since you got exposed to it before, you'll have a better time grasping the materials quicker and understanding its relevance.

I think the most useful parts of 21a are double/triple integrals, divergence, curl, and optimization. I think those concepts help the most in other concentrations so I'd recommend paying particular attention to those units (but Math 21a inherently builds on itself in a nice way so it's hard not to pay attention to any of those).
