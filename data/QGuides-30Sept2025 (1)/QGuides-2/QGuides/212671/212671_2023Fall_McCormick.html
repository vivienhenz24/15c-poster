What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

This is a very special course. It includes the kinds of texts and documents that people usually use to study history, but then it teaches how many other kinds of clues––tree rings and ice cores, to archaeology and material science––can also be used to learn about past human experience. You get to know about the human past in ways you didn't know existed––and you learn about questions and ways of answering them in history that are very, very cool but not usually discussed in other history courses.
Also, the research project gives an opportunity to do real, publishable research. This is unusual I think for courses at Harvard.
Professor McCormick is a kind and welcoming person, with an amazing lecture style; when you listen to his lectures, you cannot help but feel just as excited as he is about the material.
This course is also tailored to each student in a way most other courses cannot be, because the breadth of interests & inquiry covered in this course is far beyond that of most other courses––in the history department or at Harvard.
Work load and time commitment are very manageable.
Take this course! Professor McCormick and Reed are amazing and generate much enthusiasm for the course and what we are learning. Both are always available and highly knowledgable about all things ancient–DNA (and all other things we covered in the course, such as pandemics and climate). The class and its field trips are unforgettable, and this class is by far one of the best classes I have taken at Harvard. This is a gem!
Such an amazin class, there is really nothing like it in terms of interdisciplinary classes you could take at Harvard. Its just readings from the past like 2 years on the most recent developments, a book by a forefront leader in the field who you get to meet, and a final project about literally anything you want. You can get out of this class what you put in, and putting in just a little bit of time will change how you view the field of the Science of the Human past.
This class is essential if you are interested in studying groundbreaking studies at the intersection of history and science. You'll find that the two fields work beautifully together.
Take this course if you want to learn about a new, different approach to the study of history. The class is as much a science class as it is a history class. I enjoyed the material and learned a ton about cutting edge methods for studying history –– ancient genetics, carbon dating, numismatics, and much more. Also, Professor McCormick is the authority on this subject (co–authored on almost every relevant paper), and Reed is an incredibly knowledgable, dedicated, and accommodating TF. Take it!
The class is decently science–based rather than history based. The field trips are VERY FUN and the end when we presented our projects was a great culmination of showing what we covered over the semester. If you are interested in archaeology, virology, migration, or general history about ancient civilizations and Rome, then this is a cool class. :)
This is a great class. Professor was great, TF was great. Super chill amount of work but felt like I learned a lot about methods and research. Take it for sure if you have any interest.
You have to be really into the material to enjoy the class. I came into this class with interests in history and archeology, but I found this class a bit draining and noticed the only people really engaged were those super interested.
TAKE THIS CLASS! The professor and teaching staff are so kind and personable. This is a perfect way to fulfill the social sciences requirement. It is a GEM – no work. 2 presentations, and a final poster. The field trips are so fun and you bond with the people in the class over how ridiculous it is. We went to the arboretum, we made pottery, and we looked at ancient gold coins. GEM GEM GEM
I would recommend it to anyone in any concentration!
I would tell future students that this class feels all over the place and can feel at times like it doesn't make sense. The assignments are different than any other history course and it is not always clear what is expected of students. The lectures are really hard to follow and take notes on but it really isn't necessary to follow the material or take notes on the lectures as it is all covered again in section and the assignments are almost entirely self–guided. You do have the option to do a final project over material or methods that you personally found interesting which is a benefit and the field trips/lunches with faculty are both fun and informative/beneficial. Take this class if you are interested in archeology but it is not for everyone and can feel as though it is all over the place sometimes.
Congratulations! You just stumbled onto one of Harvard's finest classes. Perhaps you're a freshmen who doesn't know what to take or perhaps you're a STEM kid just trying to satisfy the social sciences requirement. Either way, HIST 1056 should absolutely be on your schedule. There are almost no graded assignments, so it's a great opportunity to learn some genuinely interesting content in a relaxed setting. Furthermore, the only assignments that are graded couldn't be more chill. We also went on multiple field trips, in which we did everything from analyzing ancient coins to making our own pottery to having lunch with notable professors. Finally, if Reed Morgan is your TF, it's a huge bonus; he's absolutely wonderful and was he highlight of the course for me.
