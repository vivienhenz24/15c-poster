Don't take this course. If you take assignments seriously and care about learning, this course will not reward you. Though, the material, lectures, and readings were all very interesting, I must say.

But, it is a basic expectation in a course that your TF gives concrete constructive feedback, leads useful sections, and is consistent with their expectations. I did not find this to be the case. Sections were documentary–watching sessions. My TF gave essentially no useful feedback on how to improve my work. Many people got grades/feedback for our first assignment back after the Change of Grading Basis deadline. (My grade was good enough, full disclosure, but a longtime CA can recognize lack of effort and engagement in feedback given.) The management of this course was not acceptable.

This reflects a deeper problem with teaching and teaching staff at Harvard. It takes time and effort to truly recognize time and effort put into an assignment. When the class participants (students and TFs alike) are not really engaged, have their own agendas, and are not putting time into the course, this corrupts the morale of everyone involved, and students do not feel rewarded for trying to delve deeper into the material.

This course has great potential, given how rich and interdisciplinary the study of food and eating culture can be. Unfortunately, I found the execution of the course to be deeply disappointing. The lectures are loosely structured, often lacking academic depth, and tend to revolve around the instructors personal anecdotes rather than grounded content or critical frameworks. If you're hoping for a course that meaningfully explores food through the lenses of history, culture, power, and identity, this may not deliver what you're expecting.

There are also significant organizational issues: assignments and deadlines may change mid–semester, and graded feedback is often returned weeks late, if at all. I personally left most lectures feeling unsatisfied and unsure what I had learned.

That said, if youre looking for a low–stress GenEd and dont mind a more informal, meandering teaching style, this class may work for you. But if youre genuinely interested in studying food from an academic or interdisciplinary perspective, you might want to explore other courses or departments.

While the course was quite good overall, there are some notable caveats: firstly, the workload is incredibly end–of–semester heavy. Towards the beginning of the semester we only had 2 assignments in ~ the first 2 months, but then towards the end of the semester, we had 3 assignments due during reading period (a final "exam" which was just to do 2 short essays, a final "research project" which was just to do a medium–length essay, and a section assignment which was a 1.5 page response paper). Finally, the gened office is trying to make geneds harder, so there is a chance that this course will be reworked in the future to make it harder.

Overall, I would recommend Gened 1195 to anyone looking for an interesting and chill gened.
