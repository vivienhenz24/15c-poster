Reasonable workload considering the difficulty level of the material. Midterm was tough but curved. Grading was generous at best, reasonable at worst.
i would highly recommend this course to anyone in a field remotely related to applied math, statistics, cs, or physics. petros is wonderful and all the content you'll learn in this class equips you for a "stochastic" way of thinking about problem–solving and computation. the math and cs background needed for this class is pretty elementary. if you've taken stat 110 and can do basic python, the course will be really smooth for you. (if you haven't you should be able to pick it up very easily as well). in terms of workload, the class is super light with only like 4 psets (and one is dropped lmao), a midterm, and a final project. the content isn't super hard and i think the algorithms are presented in a way that are pretty intuitive, but this class is still worth your time because it's just a good way to practice your stat and cs skills in a pretty chill way (you have a lot of freedom on the final project and most grad students just do a project related to their research)
This course is great. As an undergrad stat major, I think I was on the higher end of preparedness; I was concurrently in stat 210 as well which helped a lot during the exam. I would recommend having a good stats background ahead of time. Overall, Petros was a great teacher and a great guy to even just interact with; he is very smart. The content was pretty enjoyable, but I learned very little in the second half of the semester because there were no homeworks to reinforce it. Start on the project early. The class is very approachable for undergraduates, but make sure to take it with a friend.
it's a good class– lecture is educational, midterm was kind of hard or maybe I'm dumb, and final project was cool if you try
Based on comments from previous years, it seems like the workload and difficulty of the course has decreased. If you've taken Stat110 (and maybe Stat111) and have basic coding skills, you'll find this course pretty easy. A lot of the content is simply applying concepts from 110 to sampling algorithms, stochastic simulation/optimization algorithms, and Bayesian inference. The PSET problems feel like 110/111 problems except not as tricky as those in 110 and not as tedious as those in 111, though there are a lot of coding problems that take some time to debug. The exam is a time crunch, but most of the problems weren't difficult and there was a huge curve. However, I was a bit disappointed that we didn't learn any gradient methods (which are everywhere) and instead focused on things like genetic algorithms, which are less commonly used in fields like ML. Overall, not a bad course, but the content wasn't as applicable to my research as I expected. Sebastian also carried the course, so if he's gone next year, then things might be worse.
This class is a good introduction to a lot of useful and interesting scientific computing methods. Would recommend to anyone who wants to do computational research work.
This is a difficult course but you learn a lot of interesting material. I would say some examples given in class were tough to follow since many of the examples were based on the Professors research which I didn't understand, so I had to look up other examples. The class has one exam which was difficult, but only worth 20% of the grade and got scaled since the class overall performed poorly. There is a final project which was difficult and the feedback was confusing since I lost points for my problem solving being "toy", but was told it was a good project and there was no feedback on what could've been improved.
The course is not necessarily hard but it is a big time commitment. The topics covered were interesting although the lectures could be dry at times.
The course was good, but thought it could have been structured better. The probability section seemed mostly a review, and I wished we spent more time on the sophisticated algorithms, instead of continuing to go over the very basic concepts.
Not sure how I feel about this class. I took this as an AB/SM student (as I assume most of the undergraduates taking it were), and the content seemed really exciting. Lectures were a bit dry though. We went slower than the anticipated calendar and most people stopped showing up at some point. Grading and courseload is very fair though. I also want to give a special mention for the homework assignments (which I believe the TF Sebastian Kaltenbach constructed) which are the best homework assignments I have ever encountered. They made clear what the important topics from lecture were in a way lecture didn't always, and built you up to solving the problems and understanding the content yourself. The midterm was on the rough side though, and they applied a uniform correction factor to compensate for the lower scores.

All that being said, not sure this class covers much content that you wouldn't learn in other stats or ML classes (or teach yourself when looking at online packages for optimization). If you really need a class specifically for the AB/SM for CSE though AM 207 >> AM 205.
