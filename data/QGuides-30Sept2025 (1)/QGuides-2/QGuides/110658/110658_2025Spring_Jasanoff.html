This is a very interesting class covering the Proto Indo European language and its daughter languages, as well as a very informative hands–on introduction to historical linguistics (and linguistics in general), but what really made this class for me were the absolutely fantastic professor and TF. Prof. Jasanoff is one of the most knowledgable people in any topic I have met, and his ability to answer any tough question about practically any language that a student might ask is astounding. Anabelle (our TF) is also extremely knowledgable, but mainly extremely supportive.

If you are remotely interested in historical or comparative linguistics, this class is perfect. Although, unless you have a genuine linguistic background or interest in pursuing further in the department, I would recommend pass/fail. The assignments are fair, slightly repetitive, but very informative and practical. The final exam is one of the most tedious and dense exams I have ever taken but it is definitely not impossible, nor even that hard if you really take the time to sit with it. The TF is incredible (especially if its Anabelle) because of their genuine interest in the field.

I'll preface by saying that it's hard to review this course knowing that Jay is leaving because he truly MAKES the course. With that out of the way, this is the transformative college experience that Harvard advertises on its website: you are learning from a living legend in the field and every lecture is a testament to that fact. The PSets are sometimes arduous, but fun –– they also build on themselves nicely, so by the end of the semester they take less conscious effort to complete. It's not an introductory course; even though it has no pre–reqs, I would only recommend this to linguistics concentrators or those who are willing to put a fair bit of effort into learning preliminary linguistics topics (if you're planning on taking this, you should either already know basic phonetics or become familiar with it in the near future).

It's a fast–paced class, but super manageable (especially if you frequently attend sections, which are not mandatory but ought to be if for no other reason than for how genuinely lovely TF Anabelle is). Your perspective on PIE is going to expand so much over the duration of the lecture series and there's lots of fun "reveals" baked into each class as well, including hands–on activities and fun etymological histories that you can share as fun facts to all those inclined. Intro to Indo–European makes you put in the work, but it's so, so worth it! We'll miss you, Prof. Jasanoff!
