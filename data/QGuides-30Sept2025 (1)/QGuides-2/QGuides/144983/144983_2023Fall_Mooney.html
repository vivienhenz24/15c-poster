As with all group projects you can imagine some of the issues we ran into, but overall this was a great class and I hope students in the future get to work on more technical solutions! Kyle and Shawn were also the best TFs to work with.
This class is engineering consulting 101. You will get a client, they will give you some vague demands, and you will spend 2 months pondering over their demands. Then, you will scrabble to make it seem like you have been doing science the whole time. It's pretty fun, but not that useful even for ES100.
I believe your experience and happiness with the class may depend on who the professor is (I had Kelly Miller, who is amazing) and how interested you are in the project. Even if you arenâ€™t interested, you can spin it into a solution that you enjoy working on. Communicating with your team is key. I also recommend creating extra internal deadlines with your team, staying on top of your design notebook, and using a Kanban board for creating to–do lists among your team.
The class wasn't as bad as I thought it would be from what I had heard from other students and Kelly was a big reason for it. She is great, take it with her if you can!! The class itself felt very much like a filler at times and bit useless, but it's not a lot of work and you take a lot of presentation skills out of it.
The class is so much more worth it if you build a technical project/physical product, though be really prepared to spend a lot of time (last week of class/reading period was basically living at the SEC). Because of the time investment, there are times when the class can feel kind of unfair/work distribution split up unevenly. If Prof Mooney is teaching the class, don't be afraid to ask a lot of questions and really push to investigate the different resources on campus/that Harvard has to help with product development –– he's really knowledgeable especially about new directions in bioengineering and is an excellent resource to go to.
DON'T SLEEP ON ES96. What you put into the class is what you get out of the class. This course offers an unheard amount of resources in terms of staff, money, and support so if you are looking to do something cool (ie. publishing, startup opportunity, patenting) before you graduate, this is genuinely the class to seek something of the sort. Moreover, it is a class where you are paired with a cohort of interdisciplinary engineers where you can teach and learn from each other's weaknesses and bond through long nights working on the final project :,). A big part of the grade relies on peer evaluations at the end of the course so make sure to put in adequate work and be a team player. I promise you that it will be worth it in the end! Besides, you get a baby taste of what ES100 will look like so it's important to learn everything you can now.
The group of people in this course were committed to solving the issue regardless of the course's direction. This really helped us produce something meaningful, effectively collaborate, and minimize stress while maximizing results! The course is very well–structured and if you are willing to listen to the presentations throughout the semester, it will be extremely beneficial to help understand the design process in a lower stakes environment.
Learned more in this class about the real world than any other class I have taken. The class is wholly dependent on your classmates and whether or not your project is something you're interested in, but fortunately, you get to decide what your project is. You get the opportunity to learn how engineers really work and their process in developing a project and prototype, and it became really easy for us to get sucked into this process and really absorbed into the class. One of the most worthwhile courses, and the class that is defined by "you get what you put into it." Even if you don't have to take it, I really do recommend this course fully.
This course is painful. I did not like it at all.

First, it needs to stop being a consulting course and instead be an actual engineering course. Our client was BASF, a global plastic producing company, and our goal was to help them reduce their plastic waste footprint. In my mind engineering is taking scientific knowledge (math, physics, chemistry, etc.) and applying it to real–world problems. And so, given the company, the only really interesting, engineering–related solution paths were chemically related. This is really where the innovation is needed. For example, a potentially interesting solution would be to find new, better ways of chemically recycling. However, none of the students in the section were studying chemistry and Harvard doesn't even have a chemical engineering degree, so none of us had the ability to go into this topic. And so, we were essentially forced to go down business consulting related solutions which felt like a waste of time and felt completely useless to the company. One of our sub–teams tried to go into something a bit more engineering–related, but again, we just did not have a good enough background to really feel comfortable with what we were doing. This is my biggest problem with the class. There needs to be client companies that better align with the backgrounds of students. And, the problems these companies face need to be engineering–related, not business related. We want to build, to innovate in our respective fields.
A good dynamic is very important! We put in a lot of work but its far more enjoyable when you have good project leaders and allow yourself to count on your peers.
Get invested in the project early. If you learn a lot about what you are doing you will stand out and likely do better as you will be more involved and understand what you are actually doing. Pick good project leads who do not have too much on their plate for the semester and are also committed to the project.
Don't take this class unless you have to for a concentration requirement.

In my opinion it's not ideal that every discipline of engineering is filtered through this same class, even though the client the class is assigned likely only pertains to one or two disciplines of engineering. The existence of this class as an engineering requirement almost seems like laziness on Harvard's part – if they took their engineering program more seriously, they would find a way to assign relevant projects to students of respective engineering disciplines (e.g. mechanical engineers would work on a project for a company that heavily focuses on mechanical engineering, etc.)

This class could be improved by either removing it as an engineering requirement or figuring out a way to have students' work directly correlate to their respective concentrations. Additionally, a large portion of the class is spent doing almost consulting–style research. I believe this class could be improved by removing some of this unnecessary research, which involves understanding fields that 90% of the students in the class aren't even interested in.

The assignments mostly just involved making slide shows updating the teaching staff on our progress on our project. The requirements for these slide shows were somewhat unclear though and it wasn't exactly clear how we would be graded on them. Similarly, every now and then there would be random "assignments" like a "notebook check" or "elevator pitch." These resulted in stress for a lot of the students and, once again, it was difficult to see how our performance on these would correlate with our final grade.
Take this class with Kelly!! The class is admittedly an absolute trainwreck that will probably not teach you anything, but Kelly will be with you through it to the end. She is also very clear, organized, and does an amazing job at setting a vision for the class and semester when it is the most ambiguous course Harvard has to offer.
This is not a design project class it is pretty much a research class. Teaches you a lot of engineering skills. Wasn't really that fun to be a part of. Got very hectic towards the end of the semester with the presentation and report.
If you don't have to take this class then probably don't. Unless it's with Kelly. You don't learn anything helpful to engineering. It's like a consulting class but you don't have a choice.
