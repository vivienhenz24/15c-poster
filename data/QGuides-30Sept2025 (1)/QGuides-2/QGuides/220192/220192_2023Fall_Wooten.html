Sociology 1197 showcases how poverty is relational and exploitative and exacerbated by racial disadvantages, where real reforms are difficult to accomplish since people in positions of power rarely want to acknowledge the systems of oppression and privilege that benefit them because it undermines their idea that simply working hard allowed them to achieve their status in society. This is why it is so immensely angering to see how society immediately judges people in poverty to be a consequence of personal failings or lack of effort when in reality people in poverty are some of the hardest working people. These stigmas are also particularly damaging when the idea of the American dream is not just an ephemeral concept but something that must be accomplished to achieve success as an ethos of life in the United States. I wish more people would educate themselves as to how these systems of oppression and degrading rituals of welfare institutions continue to force people into vulnerable, precarious situations, especially people with marginalized identities, and the resulting shame, self–blame, and diminished life and personhood preventing individuals from realizing their full potential that these societal attitudes inflict. I would highly encourage students to take this course to engage with the world outside of the Harvard bubble, where the guest lectures also provide powerful first hand insights into how poverty is a web of interconnected institutions that perpetuate and reinforce poverty in the U.S.
