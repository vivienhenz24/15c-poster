This course is super rewarding, and a fun "shortcut" to being well–read! As a social science concentrator, I found this course mostly accessible, although students should be prepared to conduct formal analysis and would benefit from having written english papers before. Ultimately, I loved how much I found myself thinking about the books and the ideas I took away from them.
This course was incredible. Professor Serpell is out of this world, and her lectures were incredibly insightful. Would highly recommend to anyone, not just English concentrators.
TAKE THIS CLASS. Or any class taught by Professor Serpell, for that matter! English 178N fundamentally changed how I view literature in an engaging way. Every single reading, whether it resonates with you in your initial analysis or not, will gain clarity via lecture –– I was never a fan of Faulkner, but English 178N really illuminated that section of the modernist literary canon for me. One caveat I will mention is that classes heavily rely on Professor Serpell "monologuing" to the class off of prewritten notes. Her slides contain very limited information to look back on, so you should be prepared to take notes very quickly by hand if you want to get the most out of this class. No technology is permitted (excluding use for accommodations). Overall, as someone who is not concentrating in English or any literary field, I thoroughly enjoyed this class. The papers were very fairly graded, feedback was constructive, and Professor Serpell was just a genuinely interesting person to learn from. I could go on and on about how cool she is and this class is.
Super fun and interesting class!! It is definitely not easy but its worth it.
Take this course! Professor Serpell is a force and it's so fun to listen to her lectures on these books and have the opportunity to dig into them further in smaller sections.
Professor Serpell is a passionate lecturer who provides detailed insights into the form and structure of texts. The reading list is complex and diverse, and she does a great job helping you understand it. Some lectures were delivered so eloquently and effectively that I felt like clapping when they ended and thought about the content over the course of the day.

Because she is such a great teacher, I highly recommend going to office hours. You will develop as a critical thinker and literary critic.

The one thing I would watch out for is the reading quizzes. There were times where I read and annotated the whole text but lost points because I did not remember a minor detail or did not explain my answers in enough depth. Although the reading load is heavy (~150–200 pages), there are not many other assignments, so I found it quite manageable.

Overall, the class is well–taught and interesting! Also, shout out to Margaret, an incredible TF who fostered an environment that engaged all of us and fostered in–depth discussions of the texts.
Stay on top of readings! And use plenty of resources for writing essayss
Lectures are DENSE, definitely can be hard to stay focused the entire time and take notes with a pretty strict no–tech rule, but everything Serpell says is useful. I was nervous about taking an English lecture as someone who often takes humanities seminars, but because Serpell was so interesting and has such well–formed lectures I was really happy with the course structure. Grading a little on the tougher side for writing courses but if you're used to English essays, you're fine. Reading quizzes were annoying and I occasionally got things wrong despite having read. Coursework is exactly as outlined in the syllabus and pretty manageable, definitely an impactful course in terms of content.
