take it! there's lots of readings, but I found them really fulfilling. the negotiations are very hand on, and being in the state house is a nice touch
This was a really interesting class about rights of nature movements around the globe (and in the united states). You get a lot of bang for your buck in getting knowledge and experience out of it for what you put in, as it is not a super reading heavy course other than a few weeks. If you are considering law school, and especially environmental law or natural resource law, this course will be an invaluable experience –– especially as it is co–taught you get a pretty clear picture of how the law school pedagogy is different from the college in a way that I found to be extremely informative. Finally, I thought it was really cool to feel like I was in a class that was on the cutting edge of a topic, as Rights of Nature is still a very nascent field of legal work –– you get to hear from a lot of experts and also the professors do an extremely good job fostering an intellectually open atmosphere, weighing strengths and weaknesses of many approaches. Assignments were slightly nebulous and I'm still confused about the grading structure, but I chalk that up to this being the first iteration of a pretty experimental class –– what they were asking for the assignments and the final were very fair. Overall was a really great experience and I would definitely recommend it!
This class was amazing from a conceptual standpoint. I loved the content we were learning, and it was an amazing thought experiment. That being said, it was run quite badly. The assignments were never explained, and I often found myself confused. Take this class because of the amazing content, but be aware of the chaos that comes with it.
Take it!!!! I am so grateful that I was lucky enough to take this class. It was truly one of the best classes I've ever taken at Harvard, one I'll remember my whole life. Jill and Jim are incredible teachers, brilliant and fun. You'll learn a lot!!!
To take this class is a once–in–a–lifetime opportunity. It is very creative and novel, allowing you to explore a novel idea in the legal landscape – rights of nature. In this class, you will tackle topics not only related to environmentalism, but also regarding legal personhood and the question of representation. Through this class you will explore whether granting legal personhood to nature can solve the climate change worries of our world. Do not let the lengthy reading list scare you: most of them were very quick.
This class was phenomenal!! a) Prof. Lepore is a baddie and gives some of the best lectures you'll ever receive. Genuinely so smart and engaging. b) Both Profs. have great interplay; I've never taken a class where a historian and lawyer so clearly voiced their differences in approach and did so through their lectures, commentary, and perspective.

If the class still has aa group project, just be prepared to do your work early. Assigments are small and few (and sometimes underclarified), so the Profs' expectations can be unclear. Start assignments early because they expect really good work in the small assignments even though it seems easy because of the smallness.
This class is awesome. If you are at all interested in protecting nature or law, this class is a must. It's one of the most creative courses I've taken at Harvard. It was awesome to not only learn from the professors but other law students themselves. Professors Lepore and Salzman are great, and they cover super interesting yet complimentary content. I was always super engaged in lecture and looked forward to it every week. The course does have a decent workload –– readings can be long, and there are substantive assignments throughout the semester. But it was nothing unmanageable. The professors make themselves really accessible to their students. The class definitely had some hiccups, mostly because it's a new class. But even with the hiccups, this class is one of the best classes I've taken at Harvard (and I'm a senior). Highly recommend.
This was a super interesting class, and Professors Lepore and Salzman were SUCH a dynamic duo when it came to lectures. Rights of Nature is a relatively new frontier for the law and environmentalism, and for that reason, our discussions were really fascinating. The future of this field is not certain –– it could be the next big thing, or it could be an odd blip in legal history. I will say, prospective students should beware that a large portion of the work for this class consists of a semester–long group project. If you are assigned a group with members who are not motivated, your semester will be much more stressful and difficult. Unfortunately this is just luck of the draw :(. The Nature Summit at the end was a lot of fun, especially if you are someone who enjoyed Model UN or something similar. It was a bit gimmicky, but I thought it was refreshing pedagogy. All that being said, I took this course because it's taught by a living legend, and if she hadn't been teaching it, I would not have enrolled.
This course was a lot of fun! The readings were all very interesting and my classmates always had such insightful things to share it was great having a mix of HLS and FAS students. The Convention was also a fun final project. I am so glad there is a course being offered on this topic!

A few things to note: between having 40 students, 2 professors, and a big final project that a good bit of class–time needed to be dedicated towards, the 2–hour sessions went by extremely fast. Obviously this is better than a course going by really slowly lol, but it often felt like we would do all this reading and then there really just wasnt enough time to get through everything, which could get slightly annoying. Also, the class felt a bit disjointed between the lectures delivered by Professor Lepore and those delivered by Professor Salzman. Certainly, a big pro of the class is getting to hear from each of the two different perspectives/disciplines that they represent, but sometimes it felt more like two different classes happening back–to–back rather than an integrated whole.

Overall, if you have any interest in the topic and like reading court decisions, I would strongly recommend!
