What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

Table for [QTitle].
Comments
If history and philosophy are your thing, then this class is perfect for you. If they are not, then you may find the class to be too demanding. There are often multiple long–ish readings a week, and it's best to do them if you want to spend your two hours of class doing anything other than staring absently at your computer screen. I found the class to be difficult but also very engaging: most of the class is spent unpacking the key ideas of human rights and humanitarianism that most of us don't even realize have shaped our worldviews. Everything you hear will feel strange in the most interesting way once you take this class and then just start listening to what is being said online, on TV, in the news, etc. The assignments for this class could be hard, but the first papers are pretty short (only 5–6 page) and there's a lot of freedom for the final. In the end, I found the assignments for this class were the ones I enjoyed doing the most (even if the amount of time I left myself to complete them wasn't always optimal for that enjoyment).
Great reads and great discussions; Prof. Slobodokin is super enthusiastic and well–read. Class is definitely what you make it.
very interesting class– readings are extremely long but overall very interesting
This fundamentally changed how I view human rights and international relations. While the readings are a bit daunting, the discussion really breaks them down. Prof Slobodkin is very smart and his insights on the texts really reward close study. Worth taking if you've ever wondered about human rights / especially if pre–law.
I thoroughly enjoyed this class and would recommend it for anyone interested in human rights and humanitarianism. I think anyone involved in human rights activism or humanitarianism should know about the history of human rights and humanitarian aid, and this course provides useful frameworks, background knowledge, and a critical perspective I believe is necessary for practitioners to understand. Learning about these topics from an academic perspective allows for more evidence–based, sensitive, and careful practice.
