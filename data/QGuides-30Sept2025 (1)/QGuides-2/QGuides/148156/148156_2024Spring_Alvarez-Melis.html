Review:

Its tough to review this class. Although I had taken all the prereqs (and was taking stat 111 simultaneously), I started the class being very very confused. The class has notation that does not correspond to the notation used in stat 110/111 in very important cases. For example, they write N(x; mu, sigma2) to mean the pdf of the normal. The multivariate normal distribution (among other vector valued distributions) are just used without really an explanation of how they work. Note that none of the prerequisities teach multivariate distribution PDF (stat 110 covers it with relation to its property that any linear combo of the entries will be normally distributed, not its actual pdf). You are just supposed to figure out the notation for the PDFs of these distributions. Have you ever had to write out the multivariate normal pdf? I certainly hadnt, let alone understand the covariance matrix multiplication done in the pdf.

You should be VERY familiar with Likelihood functions and likelihood maximization as a concept. This class is all about expressing the state/model/action we want in terms of some some loss/reward and then minimizing/maximizing it with respect to the variables we want to optimize. You WILL have to write likelihoods that involve many many sums and products over variables. Get used to it by practicing to write the likelihood functions for various distributions, joint distributions etc. You should be very familiar with converting your likelihood function to a log likelihood one. Familiarize yourself with the use of indicators in likelihoods to write likelihoods neatly instead of writing in terms of cases.

I HEAVILY recommend looking into derivative identities as it relates to matrices. How do you take the derivative w.r.t to a vectors or a matrix? how do you take derivatives of complex looking matrix multiplications such as v^{T}Av with respect to scalars, vectors etc. LOOK UP THE MATRIX COOKBOOK. memorize/understand how those derivatives work, at least have it saved in an easily accessible location. Put these in your cheatsheet too if possible.

Finale and David are both fine lecturers, though I liked Finale better. Their lectures were not the worst (like hesterberg) but not the best (like Mickens). Not the most engaging for me. Its was the classic lets just jump into it mentality where the teacher speaks for 60 minutes and attempts to transmit info to students. However, one must realize that an occasional joke or deliberately done diversion would go a long way towards keeping the lectures engaging. Please see James Mickens and ask for his wisdom in teaching.

One big feedback I want to give David is to preferably never say After some SIMPLE computation, we get or its many variants. Its one of the most demoralizing things to hear from lecturers. If the computation is elided for some x reason (such as too time consuming), say so. Say that it is in the book, so you wont cover it here. However, saying that the topic is easy or simple doesnt help anyone. Also, they should spend more time rehearsing their lectures and stop with the many erms, and umms.

I find it extremely paternalistic to make attendance in lecture required. I do understand that the teacher teaching the lecture would feel that lectures are important. But why not let the students decide? Surely we are adults and we can make decisions according to our own situations. (Note that for Harvard students, the grade they get is pretty important, so they will usually do anything they feel is necessary to do well in a class). For example, I lived in the quad and am not a good sleeper. Making it to my 9.45 am class is excruciating since I need more sleep than the average undergraduate here. Even in ideal conditions, i need to have at least 10 hours to function properly. Note that many assignments for classes are due at 11:59pm and so I can usually only sleep after that (among other reasons). Moreover, I am extremely skilled at listening to audio and watching stuff in high speeds (i.e 2.5–3.5x speeds) so it is mentally torturous for me to listen to people in real life speeds. Moreover, its impossible for me to take notes during lectures because the moment I focus on the notes, I miss the words said by the lecturers. Please please reconsider your attendance policy and have it be optional with perhaps a grade boost for attendance. I personally will literally forgo some attendance points to not attend lecture. I feel that strongly about my abilities as a student and in my conviction about lectures being bad. The two lectuers are not that good anyways, so why make lectures mandatory? What does attendance achieve that 2.5x panopto with me frequently stopping the video and taking notes doesn't achieve.

There will be conceptual understanding questions in the end of every lecture which are a great way to make sure you understand what was covered. A highlight of the class with very thought–provoking questions. If you end up watching the lectures, please acknowledge their value and actually do the conceptual questions before watching ahaid .

The psets are good. Each one makes you derive some results similarto those done in lecture and section. The coding portion of psets are VERY enjoyable, though that might be because I love coding myself :D You should familiarize yourself with numpy immediately and learn as many vectorized operations as possible. In the psets, youll have to write code that runs for many iterations, so vectorizing is very important for getting reasonable runtimes.

Sections were pretty meh. I tried a couple sections and it felt like the TFs were improvising rather having prepared for them. You know, these peers of mine are supposed to be the biggest advocates for the class and bring some energy into their section! Moreover, the sections felt extremely rushed since there were many topics to cover and the TFs were supposed to cover so much in so little time. Since it was impossible for the TFs to cover everything in the notes, perhaps more instructions on what was important would have been nice. For the rest of the material, perhaps they could have been shared separately.

This class is okay, but not exceptional. Here is a hopefully comprehensive and serious overview (and a few suggestions).

~ content ~

The content of the course is as follows. It begins with a quick introduction to ML, then discusses regression, classification, NNs, SVMs, clustering, dimensionality reduction (PCA), graphical models, mixtures, HMMs / EM algorithm, MDP, and basic RL. As an introductory course, it does do a good job surveying the different elements of basic ML. However, the material is not very deep. In my view, ML is actually quite cool, and there is a lot of deep and nontrivial intuition (aside from the empirical side of it). However, this intuition is often not discussed, at least not to a significant degree. The eleven or so topics I mentioned above are discussed equally, roughly one per 2–3 lectures; consequently, there is no emphasis on what is more or less important, and how this class can be a foundation to deeper work in current ML research (which is very, very expansive).

~ lecturing ~

The lecturing is okay; they cover some things which are not done in the book, which is good. However, there are frequently minor errors and notational inconsistency, which though not significant becomes very annoying when added up across time. The latter is genuinely an issue, and possibly is more so an issue with the course materials; I think it would help significantly to define a consistent set of notation which is used throughout.

~ on the math ~

I came into the course with a very heavy math background and a solid stats background (I had read the material of 110 and 111 over summers). This makes a lot of the math and stat derivations quite easy. There are many places, though, where I think the derivations presented in class and in the book/section could be changed for clarity.

I will give some examples. The first one that comes to mind is in the proof of the bias–variance tradeoff. The way it is done is to write the MSE as E[(y–f_d(x))^2], and then rewrite the inner expression as $(y – E_{y | x}(x)) + (E_{y | x} (x) – f_d(x))^2$ and then expand. Now, this is fine, and correct, but it becomes very incomprehensible on an intuitive basis for instance, one can ask the question, "why should I ever think to rewrite the inner sum?" Instead if you imagine doing linear regression, you have y = f(x) + epsilon, where f is the true function and epsilon is an error term; then plugging this in gives a similar decomposition, and in this case makes it clear that the expansion is quite intuitive and is not some clever mathematical "trick" (which is what was stated in section). Another thing that would help with clarity is to be very consistent with matrix notation. In particular, derivations are sometimes done with loss functions written as a summation, and sometimes as a matrix dot product; in my view, the former is good intuitive but the latter makes for much faster derivations and is something that is important to familiarize yourself with as an ML student. I think doing both, especially the matrix derivations, is quite important (as an example, the linear regression formula is derived via taking a summation, but in this case especially matrix calculus makes it completely straightforward, because all you have to do is differentiate (y–Xw)^t (y–Xw), which is about three lines).

The latter point is also a note to prospective students and generally: I think linear algebra is a very important subject, probably one of two most important mathematical elements to anything applied (esp. stat and ML) if you are looking to take this course, you don't theoretically need it, but it really makes a lot of derivations much easier. I think matrix calculus could take up a day of the course because it makes a lot of the math straightforward (the day can thus be saved through later efficiency). Alternatively, I appreciate "the matrix cookbook" as a resource given in the class; I think it should feature more prominently in the PSET 0.

Most of the math of ML is there because it is an important theoretical basis for the empirical models which are built. I'm generally a fan of math and hard theory derivations; but in this case, I think the math should be as not contrived as possible, so that one sees through the main ideas involved.

~ assignments and coding ~
Some parts of the assignment are fun; however, I feel that they did not help me learn nontrivially. Let me elaborate on this claim the majority of the problem sets are twofold; first, Python coding, in which scaffolding code is given to load in data, and there are a few clear places in which one should modify the code to write some methods; second, a written portion, of which there is usually 1–2 math questions and occasionally some conceptual questions. The math is generally a good review, and the coding is good coding practice.

However, most of the time the coding amounts to using numpy to write out some mathematical formulas, as opposed to genuinely coding models. Now, it is reasonable to expect scaffolding in a intro class, but I wish that there were more questions that involved really working on a model from closer to scratch on a dataset or trying to play with a model (for example, analyzing components of the model or trying to see how to improve performance, interpretability type stuff, etc.), instead of focusing on, say, stacking some vectors and normalizing data. Generally in data analysis and empirical ML it seems that experimentation is quite important, and I think it would be genuinely very helpful if this played a bigger role in the problem sets, so that one can get an experience of genuinely working on real data with the methods learned in class, and get a 'feel' for intuition (e.g., the 'curse of dimensionality' is learned, but one does not really see it play in until trying something out that fails; there are many many others instances of intuition like this which are not clearly seen). The RL pset (final one) included some experimentation like this, and it was genuinely fun to work on, and see why some algorithms work better than others (e.g., why is epsilon–decay useful?)

Though one does learn from the review, I think there is also an incentive to write the formulas out in numpy as fast as possible, and not think about what is really done; to this extent, it does not increase one's understanding of the course material in a significant fashion. Lastly, the scaffolding has frequent mistakes, some of which are trivial, but some of which are genuinely annoying (for instance, there was a comment in a pset which said something was shape (28,28) when it was actually inputted as (784,), which affects code running; there was also some scaffold which had an error and thus did not run at some point).

~ overall, advice ~
My overall view is something like this. It is very hard to run an intro class, and ultimately the course does provide a survey a many different parts of ML, which is good. Now, in respect to ML specifically, the subject happens to involve a theory–side, more math–heavy, and an experimental side, which is just coding. I feel like both sides should be done more intensely, but neither should obscure the other. In respect to the math, I think it should be done rigorously and concisely where possible, but one should remember that it is theoretical justification for the model. Coding is of course the basis of ML, and I think coding of more nontrivial models in a more thorough fashion would increase one's proficiency with ML.

Now, it is necessary to take a course in ML, as any CS student (and I would argue as any technically minded student). I am not a huge fan of this course, but it is reasonable to take it if you feel that the above comments would not apply to you. I would also recommend, however, just finding some resources (the course textbook, which is actually a student thesis and publicly available on git, is really not bad, though it contains important typos and does suffer from some of the issues discussed in ~on math ~). I'd recommend reading the textbook or possibly some other textbook on the theory; for the coding, perhaps experiment by yourself with the models, etc.
