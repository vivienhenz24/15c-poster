You will learn a TON, but spend a TON of time doing it and possibly not get a great grade on the miderms– because of the nature of the labs, you should treat this class like it has TWO psets per week: one actual robotics pset and one computer science pset that is the pre–lab.

This class teaches you a lot about robotics theory–– it turns out there is all kinds of math that goes into figuring out how to bend a robot art in order to get the robot hand to go where you want it to go. You will learn this math. This math is difficult. This math makes the lectures (all of which are done in chalk on a blackboard, and not recorded) extremely dense, and fast paced if you want to make sure to write everything down (since the lectures are not recorded).

You will need office hours for this class. Unfortunately, the teaching staff was limited and there were only four TF office hours per week, plus one professor office hour. Everyone was super nice, and Werfel was willing to meet outside of office hours for extra help, but don't expect a lot of support as the resources are stretched thin. The psets are really confusing at first glance, but mostly end up being plug–and–chug situations when you figure out how to use class notes. Also, USE THE TEXTBOOK!!!!!!! I can't stress this enough for the labs and the psets. Get an online pdf (they exist for free everywhere) and command F will be your best buddy.

Now to talk about the labs. The labs are basically their own computer science pset that you have to complete on your own outside of class. You have to code the robot. This is where I learned the most–– I feel like an actual robot programmer now. Super cool!!! However, with my only coding experience being CS50, I spent 20+ hours doing every lab. Agony on top of the regular psets and my other classes.

The only prereqs you really need for this class are (robust) coding experience and linear algebra, but to get the MOST out of the course I'd suggest differential equations and ES125.
TAKE THIS CLASS!! It is one of the easier engineering electives (not easy, but easier). Professor Werfel is really kind and approachable. You can tell he really cares about his students and patiently explain things to you if you tell him you don't understand something. The labs are the hardest thing about this class, and they may take awhile, but they are fun. It is really rewarding when you finish your code and you can see the robot arm navigate through a maze or use computer vision to pick up a block. Another feature that I loved about this class were the guest lecturers. Its cool to see the latest research people have been doing in the robotics field, and its also pretty inspiring to see all the cool stuff you could do using the principles learned in this class.

Come into this course recognizing that this is a mathematical, conceptual class more than a building robots class. There are 5 really fun but challenging labs where you work with a 6–joint robot, which feels amazing to do because it's like you've spent all this time learning about these concepts without application and now you're doing it, and when you're doing it, it actually works! Professor is great, homework is challenging but worthwhile, and labs are fulfilling. Support is great.

This class was very very interesting! Only issue was that i found the final exam to be very unfair given the level of difficult for the midterms.
This was a fantastic course. If you think you may at all be interested in robotics, I'd recommend it to you. You learn about how to control robots, not make them, so there is a lot of math and a lot of coding involved. You'll definitely want to feel comfortable with basic code (most people do Matlab, but python is accepted as well), but don't need to be able to do insane stuff–– just initializing variables, for loops, while loops, etc. Professor Werfel is an amazing teacher and in my opinion quite hilarious too. I loved going to lecture as they were engaging and fun. I think it's helpful to be a bit more experienced in technical classes before taking this, but I'd say it's not too difficult. Be prepared to dedicate lots of time to labs. Sometimes you'll feel frustrated, sometimes challenged in the most fun way. Overall this was probably my favorite ENG–SCI course I've taken (and I'm a senior engineering major, so I've taken a lot), and would definitely recommend it.
This course is difficult, but INCREDIBLY rewarding. Prof. Werfel is SO cool if you get to know him, he's clearly very passionate about the material. The material in lectures is sometimes difficult to digest at first, but reading the textbook and doing examples through the psets is very helpful for your understanding. As for labs, they will sometimes make you want to cry and throw your computer at a wall, but once you get them to work, you'll feel so proud of yourself. You're given two weeks to complete your labs, but start on labs EARLY so you can go to office hours early and take advantage of the resources you're given to help you with your code.

Make sure you are strong on your linear algebra before enrolling (do NOT take concurrently with 21B), and take ES 125 first. You also NEED a background in MATLAB, as all the coding for labs is done in MATLAB, so it's difficult to be learning how to code while also trying to figure out the lab content. This is NOT a sophomore course–– if you're reading this, you probably looked at the prereqs on my.harvard, but you will struggle if you take this your sophomore fall. Take ES 125 first (where they do a lot of the applied linear algebra stuff for motion control that we discuss later in the course), and brush up on your MATLAB + linear algebra skills to take it junior or senior year.

Guest lectures are incredibly cool–– Prof. Werfel invites in a bunch of cool grad students + professors + researchers to talk about their work and how our course material relates to it. ES 159 has definitely become my favorite engineering course that I've taken at Harvard. If you're up for the challenge, take this course!!!

Despite these aspects, the course significantly boosted my confidence in the theoretical aspects of robotics. Additionally, Dr. Werfel is an exceptionally passionate scientist and a truly fascinating individual, which adds great value to the learning experience. While the course provides a robust introduction to robotics, it appears to be evolving and could benefit from further development.
