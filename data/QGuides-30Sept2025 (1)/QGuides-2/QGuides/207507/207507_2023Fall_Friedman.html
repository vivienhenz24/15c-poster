What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

Comments
The course is not focused on using AI, but really on its effects (particularly the change in labor). If this is of interest (it is certainly a relevant topic) then I recommend the course. Understanding and tools developed go beyond AI or labor markets (ex. market analysis etc.).
It's a fun class, but in general, two hours is a long time, so be wary of that,
This is a great class to provide insights into the economic context of AI's impacts on the workforce and the potential directions it may take society. Professor Friedman is incredibly helpful and happy to meet with students individually. Note that this class does not discuss the technical aspects of AI but heavily emphasizes the economic perspective.
Friedman is an excellent professor and a kind man. He is brilliant in that he has read nearly any book you can think of and can recall important themes that apply to whatever the discussion is about. He chooses great readings for each seminar class. At the same time, if you want to get nothing out of this class and not be penalized, you can. But that would be a waste of a truly amazing opportunity to work with one of Harvard's great economists. This course is especially good for humanities students or economics students that do not love the more mathy side of the field. If you get the chance and have some free time, ask him further questions about the readings. He can recommend great academic papers on anything you could think of.
This seminar is not about how you personally can combat AI in the workforce, but rather about eventually addressing AI job displacement with various policy proposals. Interesting discussions, readings mostly necessary to at least be able to present some opinions in discussion. Takes a while to get to the meat of the course, but you learn a fair amount about the economy and AI augmentation/automation through time.
