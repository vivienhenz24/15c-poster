Take this course if you are interested in learning more about what we owe to one another! This course is an excellent opportunity to define your intuitions about blame, punishment, and justice. Professor Shelby and Dr. Durr are also AMAZING teaching staff. They truly care about your growth and are dedicated to fostering understanding. The readings can be a bit overwhelming, but as long as you ask questions youll thrive!
This was a phenomenal class. As a phil concentrator who hadn't really taken a lot of upper level phil courses, I was so engaged throughout the entire semester and felt genuinely interested in a topic I had very little background knowledge of coming in. I will leave this with a nuanced perspective on punishment and criminal justice, and I recommend it to anyone without reservations. The most special part of the class is that almost everyone will do the readings and show up prepared to discuss them which in turn makes conversations really insightful and productive, and Professor Shelby does a great job of guiding discussions. As well, I got really into the topic of my final paper which, if you're taking it for phil 98 requirement like I was, gives you a great opportunity to write about a topic of your own choosing.
Professor Shelby is such a good professor. He is very knowledgeable about the topic, and allows us to engage with him on a personal level. This course has absolutely changed how I think about punishment. It changed how I approach conversations dealing with punishment, and has really made me reflect on my own beliefs about prison. Only think I would say is this course is meant for philosophy students and people who have an extensive background in the area. You will struggle if you have not taken courses in the department, because it is quite highly philosophical.
This course had some of the best readings and discussions of any course I've taken at Harvard. Professor Shelby is, of course, brilliant, and he is quite likely the single most qualified individual to teach this course.
This is an amazing course for anyone interested in the philosophical justifications for punishment. Prof. Shelby makes this clear to those who take the course, but I would *NOT* take this class if you haven't taken a philosophy class before, UNLESS you're willing to put in the extra work outside of class to learn how you should be reading/engaging with philosophy. I took this course as a non–Philosophy concentrator, and I feel I didn't quite realize how I was supposed to be engaging with the material until later in the semester. That being said, this course is incredibly well–designed, and you will learn so much if you're willing to put the work in! I highly recommend it for those hoping to get a philosophical complement to other courses on criminal justice at Harvard!
The course has a diverse array of topics about the justification of prisons and punishment, so it definitely requires openness toward new and distinct views. I think the course is more student–guided with small support from the professor, as every week the readings are discussed by a student presenter. The final paper is very free in terms of topic, but there is minimal guidance on it.
TAKE IT. YOU'LL LEARN MORE THAN YOU EVER THOUGHT YOU COULD, AND YOU'LL LOVE EVERY SECOND OF IT. PROF. SHELBY IS A GIFT TO THIS SCHOOL. DON'T MISS OUT ON AN OPPORTUNITY TO TAKE A CLASS WITH HIM.
If your looking for a class to grow this is it! At some point, I found the classes I was drawn to in phil to be a little repetitive at least in readings. But this material was all knew! Professor Shelby is always open to new perspectives and always willing to be wrong. He never is, but it's really refreshing to have such a down to earth professor! So much to learn from him and especially so much to learn from this topic.
