Awesome class that's really shaped by its students. The readings are great, Bavitz is super kind and knowledgeable, and the discussions are very interesting.
This course provided a great introduction to online conduct, policy, and internet law. Professor Bavitz, an experienced copyright attorney, brought a professional perspective to the class, making the material engaging and relatable. His personal warmth, including his treats he would bring, created a welcoming atmosphere. He is a really intelligent person and has a stupendous vocabulary.

The course offered fascinating discussions on topics like the history of the internet and its future, particularly around real–world issues like online regulation. However, there is a seemingly strong pre–law focus that wasnt immediately clear when I signed up and might not appeal to students looking for a broader exploration of online conduct beyond legal frameworks. Additionally, the small class size (10–15 students) sometimes led to an imbalance in knowledge levels, which could make discussions harder for less experienced students. With that said, if you do the readings you will be fine for the vast majority of the material discussed.

The grading system was sat./unsat., with blog posts on current events and a final paper. This low–pressure structure encouraged participation and was really stress–free, but I would have liked there to be an option for it to be graded. Also, the focus on copyright, patents, and trademarks, while important, sometimes overshadowed other aspects of internet policy, making the course feel a bit one–sided.

All critiques I made are very minor though, I had a really positive experience. Overall, I gained a much deeper understanding of the internet's legal landscape and its implications for the future. This is an awesome, one–of–a–kind class and a great seminar!
Go into the class with an open mindset! Professor Bavitz facilitates class discussion in a way that completely takes the stress of sharing the "wrong" opinion out of it, so don't be afraid to play the "devil's advocate" and disagree with the class!
I would definitely take this course if students want a deeper understanding between that is in play with the tech realm and how the legislation are regulated and created. I think even if students are not interested in tech, it is still a helpful course to take in order to expand ones own worldview. It definitely helped me understand the challenges the tech world is facing with legislation backup, and at the same time made me think deeper about what the tech world will be facing in the future. I think it is definitely one of the most eye–opening courses here.
This is an amazing seminar that provides a great introduction to the intersection between technology and law. Professor Bavitz starts every class with pastries or desserts and seeks to introduce students to the wider Harvard community. The topics are interesting and will give you a taste of what the field of law looks like.
Every week, try to be on the lookout for relevant current events given the fluidity of discussions; class discussions are not married to the readings of the week.
This is honestly the best seminar I could've taken you should definitely rank it as your top choice! Professor Bavitz cares a lot about his students and always facilitates an excellent discussion. You will get what you put into the course the course readings may be a lot some weeks but they are very interesting and are very accessible. Whether you are drawn to the class from a policy/legal perspective or from a technical perspective, there's something in this course for everyone. (An added bonus: Professor Bavitz brings pastries to class!)
For anyone interested in law, this class is for you. The professor is from the Harvard Law School, the readings are great, and it is a small seminar that is perfect for conversation about internet regulation.
I LOVE THIS CLASS. DEFINITELY TAKE IT WITHOUT HESITATION!!!!
Course content is very organized, diverse and thorough. We have different topics every week. We start from a few readings (approx 2–3 hours) to get a sense of why this topic is controversial or learn a few important cases. I love the readings. It's not a heavy workload, but we will learn everything we need for the next class and get a good introduction to the topic. I do not like to read a lot, but the readings in this class are so intriguing to me! I also love the examples that our professor prepares for the topic. They are highly relevant to the class and thought–provoking. During the class, we have deep discussions about the topics, and professor always guides us to think about the deepest and most thorough things about law. It's really great to see people holding completely opposite opinions but discuss with critical thinking, respect and reflection.
This class is *such* a rare, unique learning experience, so it's worth really getting invested in the readings and doing your own Google searching about some of the topics. It'll be hard not to really go down a rabbit hole, but that's the fun of it. The fact that it's a seminar takes the grading pressure off of you, but to be honest, I found myself more engaged and interested in this course than most of my others. Be sure to take advantage of office hours to ask questions or just talk to Prof. Bavitz about whatever you find interesting.
