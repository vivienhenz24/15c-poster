This class was cool. No exams, no midterms. Weekly psets. You will learn the most from section, especially if you have a good TF. Professor Martin is very nice and approachable and sweet, but his teaching is kind of all over the place. The final assignment is also very open ended, but was a cool opportunity to take it any direction you are interested in.
The only context in which I would recommend this course is if you really want to learn environmental chemistry and you feel that this is the only way to accomplish that goal. Even then, I might advise you to find a different, more applied class and dive into it from that side of things.

I found it to be a pretty dry and pretty frustrating class. I am the kind of student who likes to read a good textbook; the assigned textbook for this course stinks. (The majority of the class agreed on this point, though one previous student reportedly "loved it and read the whole thing"). You have to engage with the textbook in regular discussion posts. There's no lab component of the course, which is disappointing for a class on environmental chemistry. Lecture attendance is mandatory; lectures alternated between boring and confusing.

A large portion of class energy and time is spent on student presentations, which are generally more interesting and informative than the lectures themselves. Still, student presentations are variable in quality and subject as they are on "topics from the textbook;" i.e. whatever that student decided they wanted to focus on for 5 minutes. I prefer not to have the most interesting part of the course feel up to chance and the good knowledge of my peers. There is little to no feedback on student presentations throughout the semester.

The final semester project was meh. It was a group project and the groups were too large, so the typical mess of scheduling and priority conflict led to less–than–effective collaboration and less–than–equitable distribution of work. There's a final presentation as well as a final report; the report is 20+ pages (with figures), 1.2 space font. Who assigns 1.2 space font? The assignment was to take an element and relate it to environmental chemistry. Pretty open prompt. Not a lot of helpful feedback in professor consultation sessions or on our presentation. As you might expect, the project had variable outcomes of questionable quality.

The best part about the course was the section, which always shed light on the confounding lecture material and provided examples relevant to the homework. Sadly, the TF that taught it this semester will not be doing so in the future, so all bets are off.

The course feels like it's being taught 20 years ago. I suspect the professor of believing that the world and students have changed too much in recent decades and that students should learn the good old–fashioned way with a textbook and lectures.

I think I learned some environmental chemistry concepts that will apply to my future concentration and studies. I wish I could've learned them in a different class. Take a class with a lab or with more engaging material even if it's more time–consuming; save yourself the headache.
This class provides quite a rewarding experience if you put in the legwork! The course content strikes a good balance between chemistry breadth and rigor; do keep up with the textbook readings and presentations, though, as you'll learn a lot about a lot of different topics (and much of this learning should come from your own research for the various course assignments). Also take full advantage of sections/OH – Prof. Martin is enthusiastic and super helpful, and Nikhil is an awesome TF (sections are particularly good for distilling lecture content and getting a really intuitive sense of things). Overall, would definitely recommend this course.
Be prepared for LOTS of presenting in class, as every class a few students would present on a topic of their choice related to the lectures or textbook readings. This part of the class was really cool and definitely really helpful, so I'd say it's definitely worth taking even just for that. Scot also teaches environmental chemistry in a really interesting way of thinking about it in terms of specific elements of the periodic table and I thought doing it that way provided a really cool perspective on chemistry that I hadn't gotten from other more general chem classes. Also Nikhil is the best, section is technically optional but it's super worth it to go – I learned a lot more from going to section than I would have otherwise.
Take this class. It is low stress, well organized, a nice small environment, and you learn a lot about the subject matter. Weekly pset thats pretty low stress and short question pertaining to the reading before every class.
