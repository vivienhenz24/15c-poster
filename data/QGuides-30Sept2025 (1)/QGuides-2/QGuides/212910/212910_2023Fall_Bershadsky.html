Sounds cool but really isn't it. Super disorganized and lectures aren't engaging.
Ancient Greek Warfare sounds really cool. If taught by a professor who is competent at organizing a class and lecturing in an engaging manner, it would indeed be quite cool. However, this has not been the case in the slightest.

Professor Bershadsky delivers some of the worst lectures that I have experienced at this school, to the point where I would much rather have simply read the wikipedia article about the topic than have listened to her speak for even one minute. To the extent to which you can comprehend what words she is saying (which is not high), you will find that the majority of lectures consist of examining vases and looking at what seem to be random paragraphs of text, unrelated to the actual content of the lecture. At one point, we dubbed the class Ancient Greek Pottery.

It is hard to imagine that a professor could make topics as intrinsically interesting as the Battle of Thermopylae or Alexander the Great to be mind–numbingly boring, and yet that was exactly what transpired in this class; the only reprieve from this torture was the one time she showed us a clip of the movie "300." This is not merely a personal bias against the topic; a simple comparison of her lectures to the one lecture delivered by the head TF demonstrates that she was vastly underperforming the lecture quality that should be expected of her. Attendance began to drop after students realized just how bad the lectures would be, causing her to start tracking attendance.

Even if you ignore the atrocious lecture quality, you must also contend with the structure of the course, which seemed to be organized by a 5 year old. Assignments were simply posted on Canvas with no other announcement in class, often just a day or two before the due date. This was so confusing that even the TFs would be surprised to hear of each new assignment the moment it was posted. The weekly assignments were also devoid of any intellectual substance. Examples include: drawing a picture of a wall, asking Chat GPT what it thought about the Hoplite Revolution, and finding a picture of a vase with a horse on it. I felt like I was in a fifth–grade social studies course.

Bershadsky was also not shy about changing fundamental aspects of each major assignment without prior notice, contradicting the details of the assignment from the course syllabus. At one point, she attempted to change the final project from a powerpoint presentation to a ten–page paper. This, of course, is not allowed. After several students reminded her of the school–wide policy, she reluctantly allowed the powerpoint again.

Many students will be inclined to rate this course highly because it was very easy. This much was true. However, please also take into account that you will want to drop out of this school every time you have to go to lecture or open another poorly–designed Canvas assignment. Two additional notes:
1. The concept of a course about Ancient Greek Warfare is good. If the course was taught by the head TF (or someone of equivalent quality), it would actually be quite good.
2. If you are reading this because you are thinking of taking another course taught by Professor Bershadsky, please note that all of the problems of this course come from her. She is a horrible lecturer and course head. Do not take any courses taught by her.

Very disorganized class where the assignments are randomly announced/released on Canvas, we start lectures continually 10 minutes late (which builds up to hours of education lost), and just endless small problems that negate any positive things to be said about the course
I am rather frustrated that I spent one of my valuable elective slots on this class. The class is marketed as focusing on the nature of ancient Greek warfare. Instead, we spent about two–thirds of the class looking at vases. That is not to say that nothing about warfare can be learned from pottery, but if you come in expecting detailed analyses of particular battles and military strategies, you will probably be disappointed. You will likely learn more about any single battle by reading its Wikipedia page than you will by taking this class. Additionally, lectures were often disorganized, forcing the TFs to do remedial work in section explaining what we were supposed to have learned that week.

Despite the fact that this course had an extremely low workload, I would still not recommend it to friends –– I would rather have simply done the work for a more meaningful class. The professor also attempted to change the final project from a Powerpoint presentation to a substantial paper after the add–drop period had closed. Assignments were frequently posted to Canvas without any announcement and were given short deadlines.

I should note in the interest of fairness that the course exposes you to a number of ancient Greek artifacts from the Harvard Art Museum's collection, which was a cool experience.
