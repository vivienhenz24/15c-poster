Physics 181 has interesting course content and a good teaching staff, but suffers greatly from the course structure and assignment philosophy. Here are some notable pros and cons:


PROS:
– Interesting course content. You will be exposed to many different subjects / applications of Stat Mech in this class (although not too in–depth into any one of them)

– Professor Schwartz is incredibly intelligent and wants his students to learn to think like a physicist, which means deriving equations starting from very basic principles (at least to start)

– Great lecture notes (almost too good– I'll touch on this later). Professor Schwartz makes amazing lecture notes that are very detailed and will give you a great overview of the topic before lecture (yes, you read the lecture notes BEFORE lecture– I will also touch on this later)

– Psets are 60% of your grade, and another 30% is a midterm and final exam that are both take–home assignments– thus, pretty much all of your grade is "homework–like" things

– You are allowed to use ANYTHING to complete your assignments – ChatGPT or any other AI, Lecture Notes, online resources, etc.

– If you are a physics concentrator, grad schools like to see that you've taken Stat Mech even if it's not formally required



CONS:
– There is no guided problem–solving in this class. In the lecture notes and in lecture, you use Stat Mech to derive some useful equations dealing with some topics, and then on the homework, you are asked to use vaguely similar logic to approach some problems in a completely new topic. This means that, unless you just use AI to do the entirety of your assignment, you are often stuck to struggle on the homework. In other classes that I've taken, you have some practice in lecture with approaching problems that are similar to those that you will see on the homework, and this strategy is much more conducive to learning, in my opinion

– The required reading of Lecture Notes makes attending lecture useless. The lecture attendance throughout the semester decreased steadily, and for good reason– you are required to read the lecture notes before lecture (you have "quizzes" that assess your knowledge of them– I will talk more about this later), and often times, the lecture felt like a recap of the lecture notes

– The grading. Assignments were returned at a moderate pace, but the grading was somewhat arbitrary– I got double–jeopardized a couple of times for mistakes that propagated through parts of a problem, and I got 3/10 off on a problem once for a missing negative sign and for skipping a step

– The problem sets. Surprisingly, the problem sets got easier toward the end of the course, but the first few were very rough for me. Even after going to office hours a few times, I was still confused on how to do some of the problems. I remember for the first problem set, I went to office hours for 1 hour and only got 1 subpart of a problem done

– Depending on what resources you have available / use, you will spend vastly different amounts of time on the psets. I had two friends in the class who had completely different strategies– one friend who used AI as a tool throughout all of his assignments, and another who tried every problem on his own, and went to office hours to get hints. The first friend spent <5 hours per week on the assignments, and the second spent >15 hours per week

–The homework assignments were due ay 7:00PM Fridays instead of 11:59PM. While this is only a small inconvenience, it made it so that I had to sacrifice my Friday mornings and afternoons instead of my Friday nights to finish up the assignments

– The biweekly quizzes. While they did mostly do their job of encouraging people to read the lecture notes with a small (10% of the grade) point incentive, they were kind of an annoyance, as you would have 3 things due for this class throughout the week that you would need to worry about. Also, they were due at 5:00PM for half the semester (and later changed to 7:00PM), which was largely inconvenient if you had classes on Tuesday and Thursdays


TL;DR: Physics 181 is an okay class– you will see how Stat Mech is applied to many different fields, but the assignments and course structure as a whole need work to be more conducive to student learning / the student experience overall. If you are a Chem / Phys concentrator and you need to do Stat Mech or Thermo for your requirement, I would recommend that you take Chem 161 or Eng–Sci 181 instead.

Do not listen to the naysayers in the Q, let me provide you with a defense of this wonderful course. First of all, the teaching staff: Professor Schwartz is deeply passionate about the material and teaching as such. He truly wants you to understand statistical mechanics and how to apply it to whatever interests you. He is also accessible outside of class and will explain concepts to you. He is very knowledgeable in many areas of physics and thus a great teacher for this course.

The only criticism I have is that the lectures can be somewhat hard to follow at times due to notation inconsistent with the lecture notes or minor notational mistakes. Further the lectures do not comprehensively cover the lecture notes. This is, however, despite what the naysayers falsely have proclaimed, proclaim in this Q and will proclaim ad infinitum not an issue as you have the SUPERB LECTURE NOTES to rely on. They provide detailed derivations of the material and are more than comprehensive.

With regards to assignments, the course is relatively easy. There are weekly PSETS that (once again ignore the naysayers) are easy to do with the lecture notes and support from your friends: ChatGPT and Gemini. Yes you heard correctly: generative AI is allowed and its usage is even encouraged in this glorious class. This is part of professor Schwartz's educational doctrine, which I'm very much in support of. Ample extra credit opportunities (such as tempering chocolate) give you a chance to make up for any minor mistakes that happened. Lastly there is a take–home midterm and final, which by their very nature are easy to do.

Overall I'm very happy to have taken the course as a Chemistry major over the alternatives the Chemistry department has to offer. There are no pre–requisites. I have not taken any quantum or any proper mechanics. High–school math and physics is all you need, anything else can be learned along the way. The course covers many interesting topics from the basics of stat. mech. over tedious thermodynamics (my hatred has only increased) to interesting applications in semiconductor physics and astrophysics.

Once again do not listen to the overly critical voices in this year's Q. From first–hand knowledge I can attest that many of these naysayers did not read the lecture notes or attend lectures, which indeed causes a challenge in understanding the material.
