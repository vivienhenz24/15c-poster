While ethics in data science is undoubtedly a very important topic that deserves recognition/acknowledgement and prioritization in CS and data science, I found this course a bit tedious. Firstly, the weekly assigned readings were very long, and I suggest either choosing shorter readings, assigning excerpts from these readings, or assigning fewer readings or less frequently. More importantly, I feel like the topics covered in this course were very similar each day, and the discussions each day tended to lack direction and lead back to the same points. I would suggest discussing case studies (from the AI incident database for example) each day to ensure that each discussion is very different from day to day. While I appreciate that it is possible to take the class fully remotely/asynchronously by solely participating on Perusall and Ed, by the end of the semester, not many students regularly attended class, which could also be due to the class discussions being very similar and very unstructured each day. I also have a suggestion for improving assignments. I didn't like the "coding" assignments because (at least in my opinion) as CS and data science students, we're assigned many rigorous coding assignments throughout the semester, so it would be ideal if all the assignments for this class only involved writing. Overall, while I think the topics covered in this course are extremely important, I think the class discussions and assignments could be improved to be more productive.
This class is fine, I wouldn't worry about it.
This course is very flexible and very fair with grading. Despite the name it is not completely trivial and is a perfect compliment to a semester with heavy workload.
Even if you don't like discussion–based class you will still enjoy this course.
Overall, this class is pretty straightforward and it shouldn't be too hard to get a good grade. Assignments are graded very fairly and you're given the option to fix mistakes and get assignments re–graded. Participation is a key component of the class, but you can get credit for more than just speaking up in class (which is great if you're shy like me!). Sometimes, multiple assignments are due in the same short period of time, so it could get a bit annoying (especially if you have a really busy week), but I would still say that assignments are not difficult and require minimal programming. The instructors just want to see that you understand the content and can formulate an opinion on what you see/read throughout the course.
At its best, this course fostered in some really interesting and lively discussions/debates about AI safety/human exceptionalism/etc. However, these topics only emerged right at the end of semester. By that point, attendance was poor and engagement was variable. When people did engage (particularly online), it often felt like it was for people's participation portfolios, rather than out of interest or having something to share. It's a shame because Mike and Simson have a lot of interesting ideas.

In general, this course just has a LOT of small components and none of them feel particularly deep/significant. In one week, you might need to do 2 sets of readings, a critical thinking exercise, a PSET, and a participation portfolio submission. I would have much preferred we do fewer things better.

Also, my heart broke a little every time an interesting debate got shelved and we were told to continue it on the Ed Board :"(
