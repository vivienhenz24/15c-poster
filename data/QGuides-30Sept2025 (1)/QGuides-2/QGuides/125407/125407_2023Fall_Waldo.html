I am a Math and CS student who took this course as an elective. The entire course focuses your attention on a wide range of topics spanning everything from privacy, its mechanisms, and its implementations; surveillance; and occasionally offering you the opportunity to engineer solutions to these problems. I especially liked those assignments, including the final project, which is entirely open–ended. Homework during the week pretty much doesn't exist, with the exception of a short paper to write every weekend (2 pgs). Waldo has been doing this a long time and has heard it all, which makes him a great resource to hear from and ask questions. Some people in the class may or may not know what they're talking about, but that changes as the semester goes on.

If you're a tech–y person as well and are expecting to learn a lot of techniques in de–anonymization, differential privacy, or network/digital forensics, this course doesn't quite stretch that far (although you have certain opportunities at times to do your own exploration). For that, I would recommend something like CS 126 (taught by Dwork) for the theory and math behind DP and de–anonymization. Other courses like CS 254 or CS 263 are probably more worth looking into for engineering security applications.

This course is unique among CS classes in the way that you have to do no coding at all if you so choose, but the assignments can definitely drag. They open on Friday and are due on Sunday (weekly), and frequently are 4–page essays or equivalent. I wouldn't say it's too bad, but know that that's what you're signing up for if you're a CS student who much prefers problem sets over essays.
If you are interested in learning about the world of tech from the lens of privacy, this is probably the course for you! It is a gentle CS course in that you don't need to know any coding or CS theory to do well or even pass this class, but having prior knowledge is only a bonus, especially if you choose to go down the technical route on some of the assignment options (including the final project). As a CS concentrator who is new to the world of data privacy, I felt I cam out of this class knowing more about the dialogue surrounding the conversation as well as my online presence.
Take this class if you want to learn a less technical overview of privacy concepts like anonymization, differential privacy, or cryptography. If you've taken more formal CS classes on these topics, some of the lecture might be easy to understand, but you'll still come away from the class learning a good amount.
