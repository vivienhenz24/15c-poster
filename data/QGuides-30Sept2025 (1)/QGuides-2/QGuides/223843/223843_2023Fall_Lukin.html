Challenging but very helpful course
This is an amazing course. It's a lot of work, but you learn a lot too. Problem sets are very difficult so get started on them early and make sure to collaborate. Prof Lukin is amazing and so are the TFs.
In this class, you may struggle, you may fail but you will see everything pays off.
AMO 2 is a must–take for understanding modern research and contributing to it. Putting the effort into this class to properly learn the material is 100% worth it.
This class is not really that good. There are much easier ways to learn AMO than taking this class. Sure, you learn a lot of AMO, but at a high cost. The first pset takes 25 hours and is the hardest of the semester, but that is mainly because it has questions that the class covers 2 months into the semester. So you spend a lot of hours just reading other textbooks to figure out what is happening, and you learn a good chunk of the course in that one pset. The final project is not that difficult since you get to work with a group. The course has one hour of office hours every two weeks with the TFs, and then Lukin has weekly office hours, but they are not the most helpful. Maybe it is because this is a grad class, but if you are lost on a problem, the TFs won't tell you how to do it. They will give you hints, but if you are lost and can't understand the hints, all you can do is hope one of your friends knows how to do it. Also, the problems are very hand–wavy, so you can't even really rigorously do the calculations, and you just have to ask the TFs which approximations are allowed and which are not. Furthermore, only pset 1 was graded for me after around a month, and then the remaining 5 psets were graded after the final project. This means I had one piece of feedback the entire course, so I could not tell where I needed to improve before it was too late to do anything. Some grad students are required to take this, but generally the undergraduates cared more about getting things right because of grades. This meant that when undergraduates worked with grad students on the psets, the undergrads would start early, and then the grad students would just work on it a few days before the biweekly pset is due. This means that the undergrads sort of just work on the psets themselves, which also feeds into the unnecessary difficulty of the class. The class has a reputation of being difficult, but it definitely does not have to be. For instance, if the first pset covered more fundamentals like which parts of quantum people should know instead of material later in the course, then students would be better prepared to tackle those questions after the class teaches it.
Prepare to work hard, but you will learn a lot in the end. The lectures were a bit difficult to follow at times, but the lecture notes are very helpful.
This class covers all the fundamentals that you will encounter if you pursue research in amo. Due to the volume of information covered, it is impossible to expect a gradual build–up and you are thrown in the deep almost immediately. It gives a very impressive and complete overview of tools and concepts and I expect to come back to the lecture notes very frequently in the next years.

That said, this class is very difficult, a big time commitment and you have to learn to work in groups to push through the psets. Start early, ask questions and work with others!
This class is amazing and you will learn a lot! However, be aware that the problem sets take very long time (20+ hours for each problem set).
This class is hard with all caps, but it's a must take if you are at all interested in AMO physics/quantum optics. Also, this class has a long history behind it, and it's a great way to meet people in the field.
