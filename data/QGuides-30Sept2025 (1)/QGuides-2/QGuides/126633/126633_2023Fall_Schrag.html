Honestly, I wouldn't recommend taking this course unless you are extremely interested in climate change and have the self–control/discipline to listen and follow along to every lecture attentively. Lectures were very dry, and it was quite difficult to follow the professor's lectures in class because lecture slides mostly only contained images and graphs, rather than bullet points. Each TF had a different "focus" when they taught sections (some were more focused on the policy–side of climate change, while others were more focused on the science–side), which led to what felt like different sections learning different things that ultimately led to every student's takeaway from the class being different.

However, what was more frustrating was the fact that grading felt EXTREMELY subjective. Assignment descriptions were quite vague and no rubric was ever released in advance for any of the heavily–weighted assignments (or any assignments for that matter), like the reflection paper or the final paper, which made it hard to figure out what all should be included in these assignments. Additionally, different TFs had different requirements for these assignments (I heard from a friend with a different TF than mine that their reflection paper didn't need to be a full paper and could instead be a bullet–point outline) and when it came time for grading, it became even more frustrating to have all of this subjectivity. For example, for the reflection paper, my TF was personally looking for one specific point to be mentioned –– if it wasn't, then you automatically lost 10–20% of a full score. Nowhere was this point mentioned to be included in the instructions or on a (non–existent) rubric. This was extremely frustrating, as I'm assuming many other TFs were not looking for this one specific point and thus created quite some variation in the amounts of points students received depending on which section they were in.

I also received my reflection paper back with basically no feedback on anything I had written (at the end of the paper was there only a short note about how I should've included this one specific point) –– it felt like I put in a lot of effort to mention certain items and bring up/refute arguments in my paper that were not even acknowledged, and without any feedback, I wasn't sure how I should go about writing my final paper.

I took this class expecting it to be a relatively light courseload class with a topic I was somewhat interested in to support my other pset classes. Although I did not have to put in much time, the class was so frustrating in so many ways (as mentioned above) that I don't think it was worth it. To be completely honest, I'm a little averse to taking more classes about climate change now because of my experience with this class.
