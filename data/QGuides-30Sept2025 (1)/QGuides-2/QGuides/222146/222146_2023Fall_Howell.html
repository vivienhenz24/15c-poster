One of my favorite classes in the history department. A great course which provides a great overview of Tokugawa/Meiji Japan and has very interesting readings, that are not too long.
Students in this course can expect a tour–de–force of Tokugawa history, and a riveting one at that! Dr. Howell brings to the fore the real, lived lives of ordinary Tokugawa subjects; shows all of the incongruences and fascinating aspects of Tokugawa society and the bakufu that governed it; and invites students to reflect on why Japanese history matters as a subject in its own right.
Definitely take this course! This was my first time taking a history course since high school, but this course has left such a great impression on me that I'll absolutely be on the look out for more history classes to take in the future. The course also connects to many big topics in academia today, such as Orientalism and colonialism/Western hegemony, so if those questions interest you at all, you'll get a lot out of the course.
There is exactly one project and no other assignments for this class so you have to do really well on the midterm, final, and project if you want a good grade.
Dr. Howell is a very charismatic lecturer, but you have to pay attention because he goes on a lot of tangents that aren't relevant, but doesn't clearly mark what information is very important. Homework is reading mostly primary sources, which are very interesting, but you're largely on your own when figuring out their chronology and the history of intellectual/political discourse in Japan.
Really good class, both Professor Howell and Hwei–Ru are amazing resources for assignments. The workload really isn't too bad, just a discussion post a week, and the content is presented in an interesting and engaging way.
This class is great for those who already have a little experience in Japan. Sometimes, Professor Howell gets a little off–track, and that can make you a little anxious when assignments are coming up.
