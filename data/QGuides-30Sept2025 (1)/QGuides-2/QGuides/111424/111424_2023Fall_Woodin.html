If you're a math / adjacent concentrator, this is a very good (perhaps the best) way to get the humanities divisional distribution requirement out of the way. There are no exams, and only four homeworks, and moreover, if you get points docked on the homework you can resubmit problems to be regraded (and the best grade is taken). But be warned that the assignments can be difficult, but at the end of the day they're assignments and you have a long time to complete them. Also very useful material to know for mathematics in general.
I wouldn't take this version if you're into phil. And if you're into math just wait for them to offer another model theory course (such as 145a).
Really really really bad class, please don't take this. Lectures are boring, problem sets are hard and graded really really harshly with pretty much no feedback, and you won't get any grades back until after reading period.
Would definitely take this class if you're looking for a gem (note that this is probably only a gem for people with mathematical maturity – i.e. familiarity with proofs, basic set theory, etc). Lectures can be pretty dry though. The TFs and Woodin were extremely nice and helpful, and the policy for re–submitting assignments is quite generous. There were only 4 assignments the whole semester, and the assignments were fairly manageable if you keep up with the textbook. Do note though that the first two assignments were quite easy, but the last two ramped up a decent amount in difficulty. Also note that the class can vary a lot depending on who is teaching it, so I would disregard these reviews if it isn't Woodin teaching it in your semester.
Do not take this class if you are a philosophy major who has no experience in pure math. This is not a philosophy class. Take some other logic class to complete your logic requirement.

I realized from taking this class that I'm not very interested in logic. I found the class too abstract, but this is by no means a fault of the course. I think the course structure make the workload quite reasonable (though the pacing speeds up a lot at the end). However, do be warned that the class can be pretty abstract and I would only recommend it if you are interested in logic or abstract mathematics.
I think this class was hard for everyone who was not a hard core, pure math, math 55 type of student (even though it's in the philosophy department). It was not enough just to know how to write proofs (although that was very important). Several of our homework problems required constructing "some example that satisfies this requirement" so you also needed to be able to come up with that example, then prove it.

However, this class only had (difficult) problem sets due every 3 weeks, so if you can find a group of people to work, then you should be okay in the end. Overall, I feel that I learned a lot.
With Woodin and from the perspective of a math concentrator, this course was great! Structured like a math course, but very chill and yet still learned a lot.
Do not take this course if you do not have a serious background in math. If you want a cool course on model theory with minimal (read: no) philosophical implications, take this. If you are looking for a course on the application of logic to philosophy, look elsewhere. Some tedious proofs, but a lot of cool and clever stuff. If you take it with Woodin, you get the pleasure of learning from one of the greatest minds of our time. It is an honor to learn from him, and I will be telling my kids that I took his class. But it is far from a perfect course. Be wary, esp. if you're just a phil kid and not a math kid.

Im honestly not sure how the Q is going to end up for this class, to be honest the class didnt have a stellar retention rate, but I really enjoyed the class. Yes, the material definitely gets more challenging over time [and dont think mathematical logic is going to be clean and straightforward, its not], but the no–exams and PSET with far away due dates AND regrades works well with that model. I felt like I was always being pushed a little while everything still was reasonable, and I think it sets you up to really understand the nature of mathematical proof and the deeper philosophical questions underneath.
