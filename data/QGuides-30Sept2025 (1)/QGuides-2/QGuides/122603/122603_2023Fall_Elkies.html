Algebra is the great eliminator of ambiguity. Have you ever been doing some working out on an assignment and thought to yourself, "I actually have no idea why or if this is allowed"? Then this course may be for you!

A course like this is interesting because, rather than beginning with the structures that are the most "useful" relative to some field of application, you are able to build up a series of increasingly complex algebraic structures, deriving on the way interesting properties of each. You get a bit of this in analysis, but you're sort of cheating because you've presumably already taken calculus by this point, and you intuitively know what you're 'supposed' to get out of each lesson. Not so with algebra–––you may go for a few weeks not completely understanding why we care about a property that is being constantly referenced, until one day you realize that actually it is exactly the property that captures some structure you've been studying in physics or computer science for years.

Algebra makes you realize how general things are–––you can talk about certain spaces, and functions between those spaces, and sets of functions that do common things to elements of their domains, all using a common language. It used to always annoy me when, in classes where manifold content was taught, a professor would just suddenly say "oh, so a vector is the same as a function..." and then just sort of hang a weak metaphor on it and hope that we were satisfied. This is the course where you start to understand in what sense they meant that they were the same, and it seriously opens the doors for a level of mathematical maturity that 2xA/B, 112, etc. don't require as much, and that higher–level courses implicitly assume.

I think this is the perfect course to take after 21/22 and 112. As with all math courses in the department, it will probably be a dramatically different course depending on who's teaching it, but the material is great, and I'd definitely recommend it in general.
