Anna is also the only professor to ever talk about the Widely Applied Math seminars, so that’s another way the course changed me. I always go to the WAM seminars every Thursday now. I also got so much experience talking about math and statistics with the PhD students in the class as they connected the topics to their research, and it was so cool to see how the topics help us in real world cases. Would definitely take another course with Prof. Seigal if she teaches one.

This was the first offering of the course, and it somewhat suffered for it. First, the positives: Anna is great and funny and well–meaning, and a pretty talented mathematician; the material is interesting. However, there was a lack of a specific overarching point or punchline of the course, and it often felt like I learnt a bunch of disjoint topics . The links between topics were not explored for the majority of them. For many lectures, the only available material for reference was the lecture notes; the professor only sporadically provided links to other papers. Course was pretty disorganized; OH were kind of infrequent; This year's TFs were sometimes a bit aloof; questions in problem sets were sometimes only remotely related to the material.

However, the course is salvageable. Future editions will probably be better with more CAs, more OHs, more thoughtfulness about connecting the material etc. . I wouldn't write it off.

A few more notes: this course, being grad–level, assumes interest/ background in research. Relevant fields are applied algebra, applied statistics, algebraic statistics, neural networks, dimensionality reduction. For undergrads interested in a survey of the above topics in applied math, you only really need STAT 110 and a good grasp of linear algebra. I'd say STAT 111/139/ STAT coursework beyond 110 would be pretty helpful too, but not necessary. You grow in your ability to read technical papers and give rocket presentations on them. Not always a fun course (imo, largely because of logistical frustrations), but on the lighter end.

Verdict: wait and see what improvements they make. Recommend with reservations.

This is an interesting class which centers on 4 themes– data analysis questions, data structures used to solve them, mathematical theory to build techniques, and quantitative tools for dimensionality reduction, blind source separation,etc. It covers a wide range of topics, and emphasis is laid on data factorization, expressivity (existence of solutions) and identifiability (uniqueness of solutions). The problem sets are very useful– they involve application of topics taught in class, readings on new methods, application of techniques to various real–world datasets, etc. The final project is an opportunity to use themes from the course in an area of interest to us.
The instructor is very good, and classes were clear and engaging. Would highly recommend this course to those interested in applied algebra!
