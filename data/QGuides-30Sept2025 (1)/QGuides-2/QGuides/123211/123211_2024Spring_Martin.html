Easily one of the best math classes I've taken here as a concentrator. I loved everything about this course. I liked that we worked through a single text in a linear and well–paced fashion. The problems felt fair and they actually helped me understand the material (which can't be said of many math classes here). The midterm+final were at–home papers teaching a lecture on textbook material that we didn't cover in class, which felt like a fair evaluation of whether we were following along well enough to be able to teach a subject that's just a little bit out of scope. Gage is such a sweetheart and it's obvious that she wants us to understand what's going on. It's clear that she doesn't have much experience teaching, but she more than makes up for it with clear and organized lectures. You should 100% take this class if you need a 130s class and Gage is still teaching it.
Math 130 taught me a lot about proof writing, logical explanations, and drawing out solutions. I learned for the first time how to write math papers, and it was a great experience. Gage is a great professor who is very engaged, always willing to listen and improve and hear students' opinions and accept if she is wrong, and always prepared.
Good class, easiest 130 req after 136 (if Puskar is teaching it) you'll learn a lot in the second half of the semester.
This was super easy. No need to go to class. Subject itself is boring
First, Martin is awesome. We had breaks in the middle of lecture, and they were very helpful for my comprehension of the lecture materials. Second, the course content is interesting. The assignments were good. I expected an overview of Euclid's Elements, but this was much better and not dry at all!
Take it! Especially if Gage is teaching it. She really cares about making sure that we all understand the topic.
This class is an easy way to satisfy the 13x requirement, but the material itself isn't that interesting, so I would rather just take 131 which is harder but does far more interesting topics. The first few weeks of 130 basically review middle school geometry, coordinate geometry, vectors, etc., and then you move on to projective geometry and non–euclidean geometry (which are, to be fair, a bit more interesting). The psets are questions from the textbook and can take anywhere between 3 and 8 hours. No background whatsoever necessary besides maybe middle–school geometry.
Overall, a great class that provides a solid introduction to geometry. The course starts off pretty easy/slow with a review of some very elementary concepts, but does ramp up a bit after the halfway point. Midterm and final projects were both take home, and revisions were allowed based on instructor feedback after you got your grade. There are weekly psets and no late days, so you'll be doing work for the class every week.
Take it if you want to know more about classical geometry, it does its job well. Also if you're a freshman interested in concentrating in math, I highly recommend it as a helpful second semester additional math class in addition to 21a/b 22b 25b and 55b, everyone will have a good time in it and learn more about some things you already know, and learn some cool lobachevskian geometry and some stuff from complex analysis, which is quite fun. Dr. Martin was a good professor, and I think that the class, while different without her, will ultimately be good. DO NOT TAKE THIS CLASS IF BAMBERG IS TEACHING.
