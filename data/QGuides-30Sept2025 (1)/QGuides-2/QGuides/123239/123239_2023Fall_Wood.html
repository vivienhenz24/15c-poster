What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

Comments
Awesome class! Imo, in some math courses, the lectures don't add much on top of the experience of reading a textbook and doing exercises with classmates, but Prof Wood's lectures really added something! Lectures felt deliberately planned to be as clear and accessible as possible and really gave a sense of continuity to the course. The textbooks and problems were helpful too!
This course (like all courses) is very variable depending on who is teaching it, but if Prof. Melanie Wood is teaching it, the course is going to be quite good. She is a very clear lecturer, the psets are difficult but constructive and well–motivated, and the material is organized really well.
This course provided an extensive introduction to algebraic number theory and in particular local class field theory. It covered a lot of very intriguing and foundational material (e.g., the main result of local CFT is very strong and amazing!)
– The course was very difficult. The problem sets in the first half of the course were quite long, though there were many HW problems which were directly related to course material (e.g. prove a fact used in lecture, etc.) The second half of the course consisted of shorter problem sets, which were mostly an application of the course material. The material was covered fairly quickly; for instance, infinite Galois theory (there were several problem sets which helped the material become familiar; it was however covered quickly).
– It is helpful to know some mathematics outside of the stated prerequisites. In the first half of the course, 221 (commutative algebra) helps with understanding of, e.g., local rings, Dedekind domains, etc.: familiarity with algebra would make some of the problems easier. At some times, knowledge of cohomology would help (e.g. in Tate cohomology). These are not strictly necessary, but could enhance one's understanding of course material in a broader context.
– Prof. Wood gives very helpful intuition; for instance, in the proof of Hensel's lemma (cf. Newton's method). The course texts are fairly difficult to read: Serre is very terse (and cites Bourbaki frequently); most of the course follows the presentations in Neukirch, which is densely written but with more details; it helps significantly to attend lecture and gain intuition, as well as work through many details on one's own outside of the class.
– There is a review and final exam at the end of the course, which are very useful in regards to summarizing and reflecting on the material learned. Several important connections are made; for instance, Kummer Theory and CFT combine to give the Hilbert symbol.
– The last few lectures are largely a survey over many cool results (e.g. general reciprocity law, absolute galois group of separable closures, etc.) which are beyond the scope of the course. These were an enjoyable outlook onto the relevance and application of material, as well as a look into the progression of the field after the material presented.
Prof. Wood is a very good lecturer. She is very clear and consistently helpful in office hours. She has pretty high expectations but is willing to make things work. Math 223 is pretty interesting material and she makes all but the hardest parts (CFT oops) very clear to follow.

The course would have been near perfect without the CA, who unfortunately did a completely inadequate job and only made students' lives more stressful.
This course, along with Math 232a, is probably one of the hardest and most demanding courses in the math department. That being said, it is very worthwhile and the material is very interesting and fundamental. I would highly recommend taking it, especially if Prof. Wood teaches it again, but expect to need to spend lots of time both on the problems and on understanding the course material.
