I would not recommend this course for most students, especially those without a background in finance. Although Ive taken many economics courses, this was unfortunately my least favorite due to several structural issues.

Sections were often more confusing than helpful. It was concerning to hear from two TFs that Professor Stein is not involved in shaping section content, which suggests a lack of coordination between lectures and section material. As a result, the support felt disjointed and ineffective.

Despite being described as self–contained, the course assumes a significant amount of prior finance knowledge. Key concepts in the case studies were not clearly explained, and expectations for assignments were often vague. It was frustrating to be held accountable for specific quantitative things that were never explicitly taught.

The lecture slides were also difficult to follow––visually cluttered, poorly organized, and hard to use for review. The final exam was extremely challenging, with a median score of around 50%, which reflects the disconnect between instruction and assessment.

Additionally, I found the instructional support lacking. My interactions with both the TFs and the professor often felt dismissive rather than encouraging. It gave the impression that the course was not designed to support students who are new to the material.

That said, I did learn some interesting things, and Professor Stein is clearly knowledgeable and engaging in lecture. However, much of my learning came from the extra hours I put in to fill in the gaps.

Unless you have a strong finance background or are required to take this class, I would caution against enrolling.
