TAKE. THIS. COURSE.

First of all, it is very little work. Be sure *not* to procrastinate the referee reports like I did, and they aren't that much work.

Each class is basically just Stein going over some time when someone did something stupid and how the Fed or someone else bailed them out (or didn't). He progressively builds a set of key lessons that are nice to know about whether you're going into finance or academia or are just an enthusiast. Every minute of lecture is very interesting, engaging, and valuable. Stein is easily one of the best professors I have had at Harvard.

One negative thing I firmly believe that there should not have been a final exam. It really didn't fit the vibe of the course a lot of it focused on vocab and concepts from different parts of the course. It wasn't too difficult to study for, but it just felt a bit disconnected from the spirit of the class. A much better assessment would have been a final paper pick some event from financial history and write something about it and what we can learn from it. I would have learned even more that way and gotten even more out of the course without the unnecessary stress of the final exam.
If you have any interest in finance at all, you must take this class. It was engaging, thoughtful, and learning from Prof. Stein is just such an incredible opportunity. As a senior going into finance, this class was really helpful in understanding the key foundational concepts in financial economics, and I felt like this was the first time I really understood the different players in the US financial system and how they interact. I will say, you probably will appreciate this class much more if you have taken micro / macro / econometrics. A big part of the grade is referee reports, which require critiquing papers, and without the econ core, this is hard to do. Assignments took approx. 6–8 hours to do thoughtfully (only once every two weeks), and readings for the week took 2–3. The exam was quite difficult though, and required a lot of preparation and studying, so I would not call this class a gem.

Professor Stein is a wonderful professor, and I thoroughly enjoyed this class! I learned SO MUCH about how the financial system works, and it actually helped me a lot in Ec1011b, which I also took this past semester. The assignments are a series of case studies and referee reports where you'll read an economic paper or case study and write your thoughts about it. They were on pretty cool topics similar to what we would discuss in lecture, so they were not terribly hard to complete. As a sophomore in the class, I sometimes felt a little in over my head during lecture since I had not completed intermediate macro or a finance internship like a lot of the upperclassmen had. On one hand, taking it as a junior or senior is better because you'll have more experience with the material from other ec classes, internships, or even IB recruiting prep (a lot of my relevant knowledge came from the M&I 400 lol). On the other hand, taking it sophomore year was nice because it helped in Ec1011b and recruiting. Regardless of when you take it, it is an awesome class, and you will have a much better understanding of the world because of it.
