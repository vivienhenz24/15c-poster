Overall Rating: 8/10. I would highly recommend the class!! It was very engaging, had a great teaching staff, and was not overly challenging. It lost a couple of points due to some issues with the course structure.

Content:
– The course content is incredibly applicable to everyday life and sparks engaging conversations about self–awareness, relationships, and societal dynamics (Why do we have friends? How do friendships develop? What are the cultural differences in friendships? How do aggression and collaboration work together?). I've found myself discussing the material with friends and family and referencing it in other classes, which does not happen too often with other classes at Harvard.
– It provides a refreshing perspective that challenges human exceptionalism, often comparing human behaviors with those of other species, offering a broader, more inclusive understanding of social behavior. And even when the class is human–centric, the view is not limited to the Western cultures
– The course content is future–focused, with a significant emphasis on how friendships and social interactions are evolving, particularly with the advent of AI (like a lot of influence AI on friendships)

Teaching:
– While the course sometimes lacked a clear narrative thread I prefer in classes, it’s understandable since this is its first run. The structure should solidify as the course matures.
– Prof. Deveraux combines disciplines like classism, computer science, and evolutionary biology in a way that expands the conversation to include various social phenomena. She's incredibly welcoming and attentive, making an effort to know everyone and addressing any unanswered questions in subsequent classes – her dedication is evident.
– Amar's teaching segments are a highlight – his sessions are not only engaging and informative but also create a relaxed environment that encourages active participation. His broad range of experiences enriches the course material, and his approachable style makes learning enjoyable.

Course Difficulty:
– The assignments weren't particularly challenging, and the grading felt very fair. For the majority of people, this was the first science class they took, and no one complained about the difficulty + the mode for the exams (at least the first two) was 100 :)
– The course includes three take–home exams which are time–consuming not because they were difficult but just took time to research. Other components include drafting two short research proposals, which simply involve posing a question based on course readings and additional research and class participation.

Jennifer and Amar are both insanely intelligent but don't make their intelligence bore you; every single lecture I was engaged.
