Looking back, CHEM 27 is one of the worst classes I have taken to date in terms of content, difficulty, and organization. Previously, CHEM 27 had a reputation for being the easier course out of the 17/27 series, but this is seemingly no longer the case. Similar to Spring 2022, Professors Brian Liau and Sirinya Matchacheep lead the course and make an outstanding duo when it comes to lectures and accessibility. However, as a class, CHEM 27 falls short in many regards.

First, CHEM 27 underwent a major change when it came to the grading system this semester, following numerous complaints from students during Spring 2022 since there were no set grade cut–offs and some complained about still getting Bs even when in the high 80s or with a 90. To combat this, CHEM 27 enacted the same grading cut–offs as CHEM 17 from the Fall, but this came with major downsides. Most notably, it appears that the course staff attempted to compensate for the lower grade cutoffs by making the exams more difficult, but this was done to a huge extreme. Across all three exams, there were consistent complaints across the entire course about the questions being unreasonable or too difficult. This was especially the case for Exam 3, where a single mistake on a multi–step mechanism cost students 7 out of 14 points for the entire question, or where one single mechanism accounted for 24 points (out of a 100 point exam). To close off an already horrendous semester, the final exam was an absolute train wreck and included questions that used mechanisms that we were very familiar with, but that required applications in ways different from the general steps we learned in class. Leaving the exam, every single individual appeared to be extremely distraught and stressed, and for a class that consistently talks about having no curve, this was genuinely the most hurtful and demoralizing way to end the term.

Outside of the awful exams, similar to CHEM 17 in the fall, CHEM 27 also made a fundamental change to the grading scheme for the class that took away the 1 allowed exam drop under Grading Scheme 2. At its core, the exam drop, outside of being a saving grace for many students, was also a great tool to help students adjust to the rigor of the class and better balance the playing field. For students that come from privileged backgrounds and/or that went to really prestigious high schools, many have already taken Organic Chemistry and have already been exposed to the material. However, for students that are coming in with a less prepared background, especially those that are first–generation, low–income, this makes the playing field very uneven, as they often have a harder time adjusting to the material. To make matters worse, the exam drop partially existed in the form of an excused exam for students facing health issues or other emergencies that prevented them from taking an exam. In reality, this ended up being a loophole for some students who attempted to avoid taking harder exams by coming up with a fake reason for needing to visit HUHS (since all thats needed is a proof of visit). For students that decide to maintain academic integrity, this significantly affects the level of equity present within the course, and is something the course staff seriously need to address.

Lastly, compared to CHEM 17, CHEM 27 appeared to suffer from many issues on the teaching staff side. Practice Exams are written by the Head TFs and the teaching team (excluding the professors) and were often an inaccurate indicator of what we needed to know for the actual exam. At times, questions were so hard that Professor Matchacheep directly told students to avoid stressing over it, or course–wide announcements were made to ignore specific questions. It was the worst when questions randomly applied procedures or steps that we had never previously learned and had no relevant nature to our class or exam. This was especially heightened around the Final Exam, when multiple corrections were made to the Practice Finals within the last 24–48 hours leading up to the real deal, which only adds more stress to the lives of students.

Ultimately, if youve come this far, CHEM 27 is likely a course you will have to take, regardless of if you want to or not. While the course content and structure were extremely poor, the one benefit definitely comes in the form of Professor Matchacheep and Professor Liau. Together, the two constitute one of the strongest teaching duos I have had thus far at Harvard, and are both extremely willing to listen to student feedback. As awful as the course is, this at least made it a little more tolerable, especially given their positive energy and attitude. At the end of the day, CHEM 27 will definitely be a difficult course, but to any future students, PLEASE take advantage of all the different opportunities that this class has to offer, ESPECIALLY in terms of Office Hours, Help Room, PSL Tutoring, etc.
