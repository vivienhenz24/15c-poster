MATH 123 with Professor Gammage was overall a pretty decent class. I thought that the order of the content was pedagogically sound: we start with rings, do a bit of stuff with tensor algebras, then move on to fields and Galois Theory. Problem sets usually are around 6 problems and usually have one hard problem with the rest being pretty easy (but sometimes VERY grindy). My one gripe about the psets is that I sometimes feel like I'm not getting too much out of them, and also that the stuff tested on the exam sometimes does not align with what's taught in class or the psets. For instance, finite fields made up a larger portion of the final than any other single subject despite the fact that we did not spend much time on them on psets or in class. Professor Gammage is fine and probably gets too much flak (I'm basing this off of reviews pre–2025), although he occasionally slips up or doesn't know how to finish a proof. Preparation–wise, MATH 55A is definitely sufficient, and although I haven't taken MATH 122, MATH 123 does not require that strong of a grasp on group theory––a basic understanding will be enough so MATH 122 probably prepares you more than you need to as well. A fun class with interesting topics, would recommend to anyone who's interested in abstract algebra.
I'm iffy on this class. It has important material that you can't miss, but honestly it doesn't seem like you get your bang for your buck. Course outline is very wishy–washy/inconsistent, and I found it hard to feel like there were underlying themes and an ultimate goal, it seemed all over the place mathematically
Im gonna begin this with a bit of a note on the math department in general, in case anyone in the department happens or cares to read this at all; standard classes in the math department MUST be standardized. An A in 123 under McMullen or Harris will be entirely different from an A under Kisin or Gammage or whoever else. This extraordinary variance in the department (Im not sure if its a manufactured way of giving a reasonable doubt to bad grades for grad school applicants) actively hurts students and their expectations. Students in the math concentration opt not to take classes like this one because they want to protect their gpa from whatever professor, despite the fact that this math will be so important to their being an undergraduate math student in every way. It is ridiculous and it pushes further the notion that all math students should only take 200 level topics classes to increase their GPA while skipping the actual meat of the math concentration like 123.

All that said since this class is the meat of the concentration you must know that it is beautiful, it is profound and it will make you love algebra. But it is also very hard and is honestly weird that we dont have two separate classes on galois and undergrad commutative algebra.

There is, as usual for this department, an emphasis on a handful of texts, DF and Artin, which are good but the lack of choice makes it hard to read for homework and lecture. Who ever teaches this class next: pick ONE. Or you know you could maybe try to write your own problems and notes and practice explaining mathematics to people and educating. Which I think could help even better since youll get the chance to do the most important work in mathematics: making new mathematicians. But for new students yeah it sucks you basically have to scan through both for every PSET for the theorems you need.

But its a standard course, and I think all math majors should be required to take it. So you should too!

Gammage is moving to Canada, so be aware that who ever teaches this next, as ai said earlier there will be HIGH VARIANCE in whether or not this class will be the same. Hes a fine teacher. But if hes gonna make such long homeworks he should probably make them more of the grade.
Math 123 is a fantastic class, and a great choice to learn more in–depth concepts of algebra. Gammage is unfortunately leaving, but he was amazing a great lecturer, super enthusiastic, and very helpful (also quite funny). This isnt the most difficult math course, but it is still very rewarding and you will learn a lot of cool math.
As someone who took 55A in the fall and 123 in the spring, the class is a great continuation of the group theory learned in the fall, and much more in depth into rings, modules, tensors, than learned in 55A. While it is possibly skippable / self–learnable, I do not regret taking the class
If you enjoy algebra, this is a wonderful course to take! Professor Gammage was a wonderful professor, though at times, he seemed a bit rushed, and often went through the material very quickly. As a tip for this course, it could be very helpful to read from both Artin and Dummit & Foote, even if the professor only chooses one textbook or the other.
I think those wanting to deepen their knowledge in algebra should take this class – it is fairly canonical and sets you up well for more advanced courses like Math 137 or Math 221.
