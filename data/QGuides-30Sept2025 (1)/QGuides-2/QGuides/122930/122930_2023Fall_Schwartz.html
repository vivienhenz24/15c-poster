This course is highly beneficial for those venturing into modern particle theoretical physics. It provides fundamental tools to comprehend various aspects, including the standard model, deriving Feynman rules from specific Lagrangians, and engages in practical tasks like computing observables such as cross sections and renormalized charges and masses.
– The course is a large time commitment: you probably can't afford to spend less than 20 hours a week on it. And it gets harder towards the end.
– It's not worth wasting time in lectures dwelling on small details e.g. minus signs/factors of 2, particularly so for this course as it's so fast–paced: a fair few typos often crop up on the blackboard, but this doesn't detract from the 'big picture' and intuition you're supposed to get, which is much more important. All the relevant equations are correct in the book, so you can still be detail–oriented on the problem sets.
– In 2023 I think it was worth at least considering paying for GPT–4 as an aid for understanding class concepts (as suggested by Prof. Schwartz); it was able to explain most things in an accurate and digestible way.
This is an excellent course, but make sure you have the time to devote to it. Go to section and office hours, and keep up with the reading!
Pay a lot of attention in the beginning of the class. The notions might seem obvious or empty in the beginning, but it's really important to understand them to be able to do the more advanced calculations.
This class is really hard (of course), but you do learn a lot. Prepare to spend ~20 hours on each problem set. Schwartz isn't the clearest teacher, and sometimes he can come across as impatient, but don't let that deter you because you *will* learn a lot. Shout out to the TF, Xiaoyuan, who explained things with amazing clarity and patience (better than Schwartz imo), his sections were really helpful and impressive!
The class is worth it if Schwartz is teaching it. I think his textbook is very good at explaining field theory, Feynman diagrams, and all the derivations. The psets are very interesting and fun. The only thing is that psets are due every week and they're very long, and on top of that review outside of class is pretty much non–negotiable, so the work load is very heavy.
tbh, matt's class strongly feels like thanksgiving dinner over at grandma's place. when the feast is laid out in front of you, everything looks so good and you cant wait to dig in. qft was a very exciting subject for me as this was the class that was supposed to really help me understand all the pop science that i had seen before, sort of like general relativity in some regard. the first dozen bites are amazing, and grandma's food tastes as good as always. the first few classes were manageable and i did feel pretty good about the course. however, at some point things start to become fuzzier and fuzzier after around 3–4 weeks, and from then on its all down hill without stopping. back to the analogy, after some point you get full and you want to stop eating, but grandma keeps bringing food out and insisting that you keep eating. you protest and try to tell her that you are full but she is too old and daft to hear anything and keeps piling more grub onto your plate anwyay. after about halfway into feynman's beautiful work that is qed, things actually just stop making sense, and every other class you feel like you go to class but your sleep–deprived half–dazed self cannot keep up with matt's insane lecturing speed, and you end up walking out of class realizing after 5 seconds that you just learnt nothing. in light of this, the problem sets remain unrelenting. this class really and truly made me feel the sentiment behind the saying "the universe does not care about your suffering". just because you don't understand qft doesnt mean that the qft psets are going to do themselves for you. often times, i dont learn that much in class and i end up learning the material by doing the problem sets anyways. the problem sets themselves are chock full of tedious calculations and at many points require certain logical jumps that only a genius could come up with by themselves. asking for help is key in this class, and it is my good fortune that mincheol and xiaoyuan are wonderful teaching staff, always willing to help a struggling student. by the way, an undergrad course assistant and a grad tf is way too little teaching staff for a class of this size. many times, office hours would be full of dying students struggling on psets and poor xiaoyuan/mincheol has to run between different people to answer questions. often times they extend their OH as well, which is often much–appreciated. as for matt, even though he lectures really fast, he teaches well given the time he has and he always makes students feel welcome by never hesitating to answer questions, something which i really appreciate. plus he is really approachable during office hours, which is rare to come by

i personally feel that the course consumed my soul this semester, and for good reason. having to relearn the material through the problem sets was extremely inefficient but in fact was actually very helpful to force me to actually realize what i dont understand and then try to tackle them. problem sets took me a good 30+ hours per week, but im more on the slow side when doing calculations so that might not be accurate for everyone. and even though i did all that, i would say i came out of the class having a good idea of how to calculate things, but not really understanding what the material is about. even still, i learnt a lot of physics, which still goes to show how deep the material really is. and even though a lot of people who took qft under other instructors like to think that they are superior, at least i know what a neutrino is, cant really say the same for you guys :)

this course truly is like grandma's thanksgiving meal, because even though u kinda suffered at the end and had to drag yourself through the finish line, you still come back every year for more. i suffered in this class but it showed me how challenging this field is and just how brilliant the physicists who took part in contributing to this immaculate theory were. im definitely taking 253b under matt next year
This class is hard, but this is just the nature of QFT. The problem sets are hard, but there is a lot of course staff help, and Professor Schwartz is a great lecturer and genuinely wants everyone to succeed. However, if you do not dedicate time to the subject, it will not work out.
