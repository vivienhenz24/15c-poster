I would definitely recommend reading Paradise Lost, just not necessarily in this class. Lectures were often repetitive and not very focused on the exact reading for that week, especially towards the end of the semester. Section was much better, as we got to discuss our opinions on the text, which is what an epic poem like this one begs the reader to do. That said, the assignments always felt like they served a certain purpose, which was nice. This class was absolutely a lighter workload than other pre–1700 courses, so if that's what you're looking for, go for it.
This course is great as an elective or a way to fulfill a pre–1700s English requirement. Prof. Teskey is a world–class expert on Milton, and it shows. His lectures are erudite and helpful in understanding the overarching arguments of Milton's class.

Assignments/workload are very reasonable, and having a final paper deadline in the middle of April was actually helpful in spacing out my finals. I never felt stressed about work for this class.

One last comment: if you come from a more conservative religious perspective, I think this class is still helpful in thinking through Milton's theology, but as Prof. Teskey will note many times, Milton was not what you might call an orthodox thinker. He's not flagrantly heretical like William Blake, but you probably shouldn't get your theology from Milton. Teskey does sometimes overplay the degree of Milton's heresy, but that could just be down to interpretation. Your classmates' comments will probably exasperate you (particularly in the first few, Satan–focused weeks), but you also have the chance to challenge some preconceptions that people bring to 17th century religious texts. Also, there will be times where Teskey's comments may surprise you for how closely they align with certain points of doctrine, like the necessity of repentance (although in a more secular sense). All in all a good class, but just be prepared.
this course is phenomenal. you will get out of it exactly what you put in. do the readings and pay attention! you can get by without doing any work, but that would be a waste of a truly special class.
Many of the lessons you will learn from Paradise Lost are applicable to everyday life, which I was not expecting. I learned a lot about human nature and human behavior. Really interesting class, and Prof. Teskey is great. Sam (the TF) is also amazing!!!
This is a really good class! The workload isn't too much and Professor Teskey makes Paradise Lost interesting.
Paradise Lost is really good, although the poem itself is not as interesting as some others I have read. Teskey is a really good lecturer, and Sam is an incredible TF. If Puritan English poetry is your thing, this course is definitely for you!
This class is the epitome of getting out however much you put in. If you want a low effort class, where you can skate by with minimal reading, you definitely can but it will be boring and you will get nothing out of it. On the other hand, if you do the readings and participate, this class can be incredible. I wish I had adopted more of the latter mentality, especially towards the end of the semester. Assignments are very chill and grading is not too harsh. Both the Professor and the TF are incredibly passionate about the subject matter, and if you put some thought into it, it can be pretty cool. I'd just issue a warning that this course draws a lot of religious people who are very passionate about this story and Genesis. Coming from a less religious background, I did not feel such a personal connection to the story. However, I think it's a solid class that everyone can learn a lot from. It's a good way to kill the pre–1700s requirement, but I would probably not recommend as an elective unless you are very passionate about the subject matter.
This class is chill, but you will only find it as interesting as you find Paradise Lost. If you want to understand the epic and all of its underlying meanings, this class is perfect for you –– Professor Teskey spends each lecture really delving into one part or one theme of the text, so you really get a holistic understanding of the poem. However, if you don't find the text interesting, it will be hard to engage.
