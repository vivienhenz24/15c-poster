This is an amazing class. James Joyce is great, but Professor Blum is the reason the course is so amazing. James Joyce is hard to read. We start off with Dubliners, which is his only traditional text, but texts like Ulysses and Finnegans Wake are really difficult to parse over on your own. Having Professor Blum as a resource is incredibly helpful, and having the chance to read the work under her guidance is really lucky. She is incredibly enthusiastic, very engaging, and incredibly sweet and accommodating. She is very encouraging of everyone in the class, really seeks to connect with people individually, and is so much fun to talk to. I had a blast with all of the assignments. I wish we had slightly less reading each class because it is a lot, and sometimes people will come to class not having read, but she does such a good job of making class engaging that it doesn't matter much either way.
For context, I am an undergraduate STEM concentrator who took this class as an elective. I had not previously taken any courses in the English department, though I'm an avid bookworm.

If you're interested in James Joyce, Ulysses, or modernism as a whole, this is an amazing class. I took it due to interest in Virginia Woolf, which gave me tangential interest in all three. While the coursework isn't trivial by any means, Professor Blum structures it in such a way that you slowly work towards Joyce's more convoluted and difficult to understand works: his writings become extremely approachable, even if they still require time to fully understand. I leave immensely satisfied with what we were able to cover.

The class was small enough that in–class discussion was productive and interesting. It always seemed as though Professor Blum had something interesting to say in response to student remarks, though she would generally allow conversation to flow in the directions students took it with their responses to one another. Almost every class I left a little sad because, while the discussion was productive, it felt like there was so much more we could've talked about –– which, in my opinion, is an amazing feeling to leave a class with. I'd leave and continue the conversation with other classmates as we'd walk slowly across campus, because it felt like there was nothing more important or interesting in that moment; I'd leave and immediately schedule office hours with Professor Blum to continue conversation; I'd leave with more energy than I entered; I'd leave wanting to do the readings for the next class immediately.

So I would wholeheartedly recommend this class to anyone interested in Joyce or Ulysses. Though that also means I wouldn't recommend this class if you don't intend on doing the readings –– there is little to no substanceless conversation. This is the type of course in which you'll get as much out of it as you put in.
