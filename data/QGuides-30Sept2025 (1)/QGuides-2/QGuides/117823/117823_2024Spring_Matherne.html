You have to be really bought into philosophy in general, and metaphysics in particular, to like this class. The first critique is an incredibly important piece of work in intellectual history, but it is extremely dense and hard to work through. In the end, it often does not feel as if the book has any stakes, so the trouble it takes to read the book may feel it is not worth it.
Please please please please take this course. It is fantastic, and Prof. Matherne is wonderful. If you have even the slightest interesting in Kant, this is the perfect course to take!~
Lectures themselves are very well taught, however, I didn't find the source material itself enjoyable to engage with and the structure of the arguments will become repetitive over the course of the semester. I do not envy the job of the section leaders since I think it is difficult to provide clarity to Kant, but I think sections were too unstructured and involve a lot of people asking questions without making any progress towards getting at an answer. If you know that you enjoy Kant or really want to understand his arguments, this will be a great course but otherwise I would not recommend it.
Kant is a very important figure in philosophy and this class is a wonderful way to grasp some of his key ideas along with later post–Kantian philosophy. As a philosophy concentrator, I felt challenged and while sometimes it was difficult to understand Kant just by reading the Critique, Professor Matherne's lectures helped clarify the content. I would recommend students to go to office hours whenever they can and to put thought and effort into their essays.
It's a well–taught class, if you like metaphysics, you should not have much to complain about. Professor Matherne is a genius and she is very enthusiastic
and knowledgeable about the material. Make sure you are definitely interested in metaphysics; it seems like an obvious piece of advice but it's worth noting because a lot of my peers agreed that they felt somewhat lost by the course material
The most important thing to do in this class is to take very careful lecture notes. Professor Matherne is an excellent lecture and whatever she says in lecture will elucidate the readings. Taking very careful lecture notes will save you so much time and help you immensely with understanding the material and writing the essays.
The First Critique is an essential text in the history of Western philosophy; it is also a difficult one. Prof. Matherne walks you through it with ease.
Professor Matherne is an amazing lecturer and I am glad to understand Kant in real depth after having taking this course. Note, however, that this is not an intro philosophy class. You will not learn how to write a philosophical paper in this courseat least, probably not well enough to save your grade. Still a great course, just be warned that it does expect a background of formal philosophical skills.

Not an introductory philosophy course, very difficult, but you'll think about the world differently after this course.
You'll leave this class with a near complete understanding of Kant's metaphysics. It's a wonderful opportunity to understand a philosopher so central to the history of Western thought. The readings are short but dense. There are only three essays (5, 5, then 8 pages). Essays are mostly reconstructive but take more time than you think. One bad part about the class is that it's easy to fall behind on readings. One last point is that this class seriously conditions you to read philosophy!
Matherne is great, and she brings an enthusiasm to Kant that is probably not found anywhere else in the world. However, my main critique of the course is that after the first could of weeks it focused heavily on explanation and exposition, rather than on connecting Kant's system to a 'so–what' of how it explains the world around us or is practically helpful to understand. Other than that, I thought the course as a whole (workload, lectures, grading, teaching) was great.
Don't be surprised if you feel confused reading the Transcendental Logic – it does actually contain some controversial arguments. From early in the class, pay much attention to Kant's dictionary, because the various technical terms he uses will be important in understanding his arguments. The class focuses on the central parts of the Critique of Pure Reason (Transcendental Aesthetic, Analytic, Dialectic) and only tangentially touches on other topics, such as Kant's philosophy of mathematics, which might be good or bad depending on your interests. While Kant is known as a very logical philosopher, his philosophy, in my opinion would not be called "rigorous" by mathematical standards, so don't expect to see an incontravertible theory of metaphysics. This is all written by a physics–math double major, so please take this with a grain of salt because I really don't have much philosophical maturity.
This is a really tough class because the material itself is inescapably challenging, but if you want to do philosophy then you really have to take this course. Its one of the all–time most important works of philosophy. It may be the greatest single philosophical work of all time.
