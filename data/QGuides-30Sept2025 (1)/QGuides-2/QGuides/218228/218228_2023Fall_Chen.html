This is a great course! The material is not overly difficult, nor are the exams. The math can get tedious around the middle of the course, but that is the hardest it gets. Prof. Chen is fantastic, very kind, and very open to talking after class. Catherine is also a fantastic TF!
TAKE THIS COURSE!!! This class is the definition of a transformative experience – it perfectly balances depth and breadth, and it makes a very difficult subject approachable while maintaining a high level of rigor. Professor Chen is one of the best professors I have had at Harvard – he is a stellar lecturer and is very accessible outside of class, and he is willing to spend as much time as you need explaining a concept you are confused about. Although the problem sets can be challenging and will require a significant time commitment (so do consider your other classes wisely), they are very relevant to the course curriculum and dramatically improved my understanding of the course material. Our TF Catherine was really wonderful and hosted lots of office hours, so it was easy to get help and made the problem sets very manageable overall. The exams are also very reasonable and even fun, since they relate the course material to astrophysical phenomena!
TAKE IT!!! Genuinely my fav course I've ever taken; such a cool topic, really well taught with supportive TF and prof is good at teaching, willing to answer questions and genuinely wants you to succeed. Fair warning though, not at all an easy or low time–commitment class, do not take alongside more than one other difficult pset class – but so, so worth it!
Take this class if you want to get an intuition about GR but do not want to get lost in grad version of it.
Take this class! It is a great and approachable introduction to general relativity, and if you are at all interested in the way the universe works you will enjoy it.
Take this class! I think this was a relatively low workload astro/physics class where you can actually learn a lot without drowning in problem sets. Somehow, Professor Chen developed a class that is both comprehensive in its teaching of the motivations, history, and theory of general relativity but also its application. It also is quite rigorous and has a similar amount of depth to what the standard general relativity textbooks have. I really, really think this class should be cross–listed with the physics department because if more physics students knew about this class, I think they would love it, benefit from it, and that this class would have a huge enrollment. All physics students want to know about the work of Einstein and this class enables you to learn general relativity without the insane workload and previous knowledge assumed in physics 210. I think this class is quite manageable to take with only Physics 15a, 15b, and linear algebra. It does require some degree of physical and mathematical sophistication but the course staff is so helpful. On another note, cross your fingers that if you take this class, you will have Catherine as a TF. She was amazing and so helpful.
Difficult but not that time consuming. Very interesting concepts and Xingang is a great professor!
TAKE THIS CLASS! It will be a GREAT time and you will learn so much about crazy things about time and the universe. Xing gang will teach you all the foundations so no need to worry about your background as long as you know how to integrate. Take this class!!! The material is very hard, but it is so interesting. You will have to work hard in this class, but you will be rewarded with great insights into the universe. This has been my favorite class in all of Harvard. It might even be the best physics class here. The midterms are hard and the midterm was very hard too. The final was take–home and super interesting. I actually really enjoyed doing the final. It felt like a long pset, but the topics were so interesting that it never felt like I was doing a test. Xingang is a great professor and explains the material well. Catherine is the greatest; she is an incredible TF and really made the course. I learned a lot in this class and would recommend anyone to take it.
Please take this class if you're considering it. You will need to put in the work to understand the material, but when you do, it will be worth it. The course is as hard as the material is hard that is, Xingang and Catherine want you to succeed, and design the assignments such that you learn about GR without forcing you to go through unnecessarily difficult computations or anything of the sort.
Tangible things you will want to know: The prerequisites are real (except maybe 15b, which you don't really need). The class uses quite a bit of linear algebra. It's also much more physics than a typical astronomy class. But beyond that, don't be scared of the midterm or final if you paid attention in class and put in effort to do the homeworks you will do well on the exams. Yes, the math is rough in this class be prepared to learn most of the Greek letters and then wonder why Riemann organized them in such a profoundly illogical way, and you may never know what the difference between a "covariant" and "contravariant" derivative in physical terms. But that's not really the point. GR has a reputation of being very hard, and you will see why, but if you put in the work you will understand it. And rumor has it Xingang might start teaching this course every year. :)
General Relativity is hard. However, this course is probably the best way to learn it––you just have to take the dive and be willing to dedicate time to the topic––and you WILL be rewarded! The lectures were all dense and math–heavy, but that's GR. Xingang is wonderful, and makes an effort to explain things and answer students' questions; he is a very dedicated teacher.
One would benefit immensely from dedicating time outside of class to carefully review the lecture notes and read the relevant textbook sections, particularly for the first portion of the class in which Riemannian geometry is (very rapidly) taught––it's basically a math class for the first half of the semester. Oh, and there is a focus on special relativity in the beginning weeks, such as solving the twin paradox. If you tuned out during the SR portion of 15a, get ready to refresh and develop your knowledge further (in a good way).
Prior experience with Riemannian geometry would have made this course easier, but I also would not recommend taking Riemannian geometry for the sake of preparing for the course, unless you already know you want to do future work on GR or something, because half of the course focuses on the results of major theories and tests of GR from the 20th century, the "applications", and so less tensor math is being done and more classic physics and problem–solving is done––and this was just AWESOME! Learning about gravitational waves, black holes, and more... there is so much to take out of this class, but you have to put the time in: you get out of this class what you put into it. Overall, this course really does a good job of making General Relativity approachable and comprehensible. Xingang is an absolutely wonderful professor. I'm glad I took this class and would definitely recommend it to other Astro students, it's a challenging but meaningful and cool class.
Yes, this is a challenging class, and each week, you will struggle to learn something that feels completely alien. However, I urge you to stick it out because the end result is unimaginably rewarding. Nowhere else in the Harvard physics/astro department will you learn so much about the deep connections between math and reality. My number one tip is to let the lecture notes guide your learning more than anything else. Our TF was Catherine Petretti, and she worked incredibly hard to ensure every one of us succeeded.
