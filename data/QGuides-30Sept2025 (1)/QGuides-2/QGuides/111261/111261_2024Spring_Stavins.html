It's pretty straightforward, and it probably helps if you're interested in environmental policy. As an Ec concentrator, the actual economics was pretty easy – it was more about memorizing policy tools and environment–related information. But there were other people who were not Ec concentrators and coming from other environment–related backgrounds, and it seemed like the course was more challenging for them. Problem sets are basically completion–based, so your grade is determined by the exams, which can get stressful to study for. Make sure to do the practice exams. Also, lectures are dry but the slides cover most of the things you need to know.
I really enjoyed the course content and think that the material is extremely relevant and interesting. However, the weighting system is very skewed. All of the PSETs are out of 5 points and there are not many. This means that if even a couple points are lost on a PSET, the percentage grade becomes extremely low. In addition, the final exam is weighted 50%, which I think is too high.
I think the content of the course is necessary to understand, but I would prepare to find ways to stay engaged in the lecture. It is difficult to follow given the flat tone, but a list of questions will make it easier.
Take this class! Its interesting (lectures can be dry though) and the workload is super manageable. As long as you really study for the midterm and final and know what is on the slides you will be so fine!
Take advantage of the resources – class lectures, sections, slides, office hours, textbook, study groups – available to you!
Do not take if you do not have to. The final exam is worth fifty percent of your grade (midterm is 35 percent). There is no curving of the exam and the professor do not want you to succeed.
This class is difficult and by no means a gem. If you are ESPP and have to take this class or EC and are thinking about this class, I would just be conscious of the importance of exams.

I don't want to say there was tension but there seemed to be a barrier between the Professor and the students in the sense that there wasn't much cross–engagement. On a comment for the ESPP department, I think it's absolutely ridiculous that concentrators are asked to take a class where the midterm and final are 85% of a student's grade and other EC requirements are recommended –– y'all need to seriously do better because 5 hours is not representative of the work students put into this class. If you are super interested in EC and environment, go for it! Prof. Stavins is accomplished I just don't think he's good at teaching.

Take this course if you're even remotely interested in the material covered. For those from ESPP where its a requirement, taking Econ10A first would be really helpful, or at least focusing the start of the course on brushing up those skills.

The lecture style of the class isn't super condusive to engagement and I wish content was more thought–provoking or offered new perspectives on environmental economics. That said Professor Stavins and the TFs have amazing insights and I would highly recommend that you go to their office hours and get to know them more – they are all wonderful people. Also – go to optional review lectures, they are super helpful!!!

It's a good, foundational course for anyone interested in environment. It's impossible to approach environmental questions purely from a policy / sociological / advocacy / etc. perspective without a solid grasp of market concepts that will govern and shape energy in the future. So if you're an ESPP major or interested in environment or energy, this is a must.

That being said, the course is somewhat dry, and VERY light (I spent maybe 2–3 hours outside of class per week, including on the psets which were mostly verbal and conceptual and not very rigorous). Correspondingly, the lectures were very colloquial and approachable, since this course is tailored for the ESPP concentration. This was my fifth class, and it was perfect for that. If you're looking for more of a dense course you might look at other alternatives.
