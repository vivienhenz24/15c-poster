This is one of the poorest classes I have taken recently.

~ content ~

The course is an introduction to differential topology. It follows more or less the entirety of Guillemin and Pollack, save in a few places. It begins with the definition of manifolds and basic properties. It introduces several results on immersions, submersions, diffeomorphisms; it then introduces transversality, a keystone idea of the course. Manifolds with boundary are introduced; then intersection theory in mod 2 and oriented manifolds are covered (e.g. intersection numbers, degree, etc.). The exterior product is introduced, after which differentials are formally covered, then integration and the exterior derivative. Lastly, de Rham cohomology is introduced, and the Mayer–Vietoris sequence and Poincare duality conclude the main content of the course.

The structure and delivery of Math 132 under Dr. Gammage's instruction have proven to be a significant challenge for many students. The course, while rich in potential for advanced learning, often glosses over the derivation and proof of key theorems, which are frequently described as "trivial." This approach can hinder the depth of understanding necessary for a rigorous mathematical education, especially in a complex subject like differential topology.

Students have expressed concerns regarding the instructor's responsiveness to questions during class. There appears to be a tendency to not adequately address or engage with inquiries that would clarify and enrich the student learning experience. Greater openness to student questions and a more thorough exploration of the topics at hand could enhance understanding and academic success.

The workload for this course is notably substantial, with students reportedly dedicating upwards of 20 hours per week to problem sets alone. While a rigorous workload can be beneficial, it is important that it be balanced and manageable, and that it genuinely contributes to student learning and comprehension rather than merely adding to the students' stress.

The overall student experience in Math 132 this semester has been overwhelmingly stressful, with many feeling that their educational needs are not being met. An adjustment in teaching approach, particularly a more supportive and engaging classroom environment and a reconsideration of the workload, could potentially improve outcomes and student satisfaction significantly.
Given that instructors in the Math Department shift around so often and frequently teach the same course in different ways, I find that it is often helpful to have feedback specifically about the instructor and not just the course.

Gammage is an excellent instructor. He clearly cares about student learning (showing up to lecture the day after the birth of his child!) and is a lucid lecturer. He does tend to assign more homework than other instructors in the department (~8 problems per week), but these problems were usually not busy work and genuinely useful for learning. I would recommend that you start the assignments more than a day before they are due, however. The exams for this course were not difficultif you're managing the homework, you'll more than manage the exam.

The course itself is usually interesting but sometimes rather tedious. In my opinion, there are not too many big ideas in the class, and a lot of the work is essentially definitions–chasing and rote verification. I found the textbook to be quite good and understanding manifolds and integration is probably important.

Most of the facets of the course this semester were pretty disastrous. Below are what I wrote as my comments for how to improve the course: you can easily infer what was wrong by the suggested change. In particular, the horrible textbook and the lack of rigor were infuriating.

1. Please change the textbook. Guillemin and Pollack seems to be universally regarded as an awful textbook and it really is.
2. Don't teach from the textbook. This course still could have avoided disaster if we did things in lecture correctly. However, lectures mostly came directly from the book, which meant they reiterated confusing and occasionally incorrect material with little additional clarity.
3. Include at least *some* rigorous proofs and at least *some* detailed numerical examples in teaching. This course currently has almost no rigor –– proofs in the textbook are wrong or missing details, and details of "proofs" in lecture are frequently left out to be... I don't know, completed at home or something? This is an unreasonable expectation at best for an undergraduate math course. Similarly, some numerical constructions like partitions of unity are frequently cited but never actually practiced –– it's always, "from here, you could do this..." but we never actually do it. This is extremely frustrating because it is hard to figure out how to do these calculations on your own if you have never seen them done before.
4. Consider changing to take–home exams. I felt like the in–person exams did not really test my understanding of the course material, and were at least partially based on luck (half of the final exam grade was determined by True/False questions designed to trick you).
