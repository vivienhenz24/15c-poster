SUCH A LOVELY COURSE! ES51 FTW! But definitely a huge time commitment!!

Hi! If you're deciding whether to take ES51 or not, the question is simple: major/requirement–wise, do you need to take it?
– If so, take it as soon as possible! The skills learned in the course are so useful no matter what career you choose! I'm glad I took it in my Freshman Fall because I now feel much more prepared and certain about following a career in Mechanical Engineering.
– If not: are you willing to take the challenge? This class is a HUGE time commitment, and it is really not worth it if you won't be able to put in the effort.

If you decide to take it: this class is worth every single atom of dedication you can offer. Seymur is not a particularly good lecturer, but he is probably the sweetest person in John A. Paulson and he makes a real effort to get to know his students individually. He always stays after class taking any questions you might have, either about the content or about a career in Engineering. It's clearly that he loves his students! Class and lab often didn't have a very clear connection, but lab was so cool! I learned so much. If you can, stay in the lab after hours, and take your time to really explore all the resources available to you. This class is not only an intro to Mechanical Engineering, but also a wonderful intro to Engineering at Harvard as a whole and the resources at the SEC. Don't come into this class with a mindset to do the bare minimum – the more you put in, the more you take away. Befriend your TFs and understand that there is truly a lot of help to help you navigate ES51. With that being said, Turf Wars was a magical way to wrap up the semester, and I feel like I'm walking out of this class with a real community of Mechie peers and friends!
This class is definitely worth taking if you are considering studying mechanical engineering. The content can be confusing at times, but through the robot design project and the CAD skills you will learn in this course, it is ultimately very rewarding.
i learned how to cad and fabricate bc of this class! take it!

Fair warning, the lectures aren't always the most captivating. However, if you pay attention in lecture, the psets and exams are pretty straightforward. Don't be afraid to ask for help from the CAs or Seymur for anything in this class. The teaching staff was probably one of the best parts about the class.

I can't tell you how many people I met in the class who realized way too late (8pm) that they couldn't finish the pset in the 4 hours they had left in the day and had to either beg for extensions or just take the grade hit. You also should focus on your robot early and often because once you get behind it really starts to add up. Also don't be afraid to ask the professor for help because he was really helpful. Everyone in your class is a resource so get all their numbers so you can work with them.

This class is great! Don't let the Q–guide scare you, it is a lot of first years complaining about the shift from high school but the course is not bad. The time you will spend on the course a week fluctuates heavily, especially as you get near the robot. it will be between 4 and 12 hours, but a healthy average is 6.5 hours a week. The course could be taught better but go to office hours and clear up confusing questions! And do your psets and start solidworks EARLY. You will run into errors you will not know how to resolve on solidworks. If you start the day of, it will be very stressful! Start it early in office hours and ask CA's for help!

START THE ROBOT EARLY. They don't tell you this explicitly but for the first weeks in lab, all you are doing is making parts to allow your robot to move. Make these pieces fast so you can get to actually making and designing your robot earlier. Communicate with your group about everything! Things will go wrong, prepare for the worst and have back up plans.

The course is very fun though, topics get hard at times but everyone is confused so it will be okay! This will be the easiest engineering class you will ever take, have fun and know nothing is truly deep and everything will be okay.

Lean into your TFs and classmates. Everyone is there to helpful each other. Be attentive in lecture and don't start psets last minute. Office hours are a blessing!
Take this course ou will love it
This is a great into to mechanical engineering for those that are interested. The lectures are incredibly dry but lab is what makes this class fun. Expect the PSETs to be difficult and to not be able to complete some of them without going to office hours. Some questions had psets that were much more in depth than example problems given in class. Go to office hours, spend time on the psets, and start your robot early and you'll be fine.
Go to office hours when you are confused on problem sets.
Start psets early!! They take longer than expected. You cannot procrastinate.

Go to office hours! The TFs are AMAZING. They helped me with psets and clarifying all of the topics that were confusing from class. It was daunting to go to office hours the first few times, but I now have a great relationship with all of the TFs.

In the lab, don't be afraid to make mistakes. Now is the time to mess up, learn from error, and try new things.

Don't procrastinate on your robot! In the last week leading up to lab, I was in the lab for 6–8 hours every day. Take advantage of the extra hours in the earlier weeks.

If you don't know if you want to concentrate in engineering –– this class will help you decide!!!! For me, it heightened my interest in engineering and made me excited for all that's more to come in my MechE education. However, some of my friends, who were interested in MechE, have decided to drop the concentration. The class is certainly make or break.

You learn tons of hands–on skills that are practical in the real world, not just engineering. I would recommend this course for ANYONE, not just MechE concentrators.

It is a LOT of work! Especially in the last few weeks before the robot impound (at the start of reading week). However, it is incredibly rewarding.

Lectures weren't always the best. They are early in the morning and at the SEC. Sometimes, the lecture was incredibly confusing, and I didn't learn much. We occasionally completed in–class participation activities, and this is when I learned best. Whether there is a participation activity or not, try not to miss class. Seymur gives helpful information at the start whether about the exams, psets, robot project, etc.

There are two midterm exams. Do not stress about your grade after the exam. I thought that I failed the exams, but I ended up doing great on both. The professor grades very generously and provides opportunity for extra credit, point redemption, heavy curves, etc.

You are allowed to make a cheat sheet to use on the exams! I procrastinated and went through all lectures/psets/practice exams the night before the exams. Please don't do this. It was unnecessarily stressful. Compile relevant topics, formulas, equations, and sample questions throughout the course.

The exams resembled the psets, practice exams, and in–class activities (with a question about lab knowledge here and there). You don't need to know everything that is taught in lecture!!

Good luck and have fun in the class! It's not every day that you get to design and build your own robot!
A great course for any mechanical engineering concentrator. A big time commitment but worth the effort.
Stay on top of your lab work and make sure to communicate with your team. If you all work hard it will pay off in the end and be very rewarding.
Although an introductory Engineering course, ES51 still requires a lot of dedication and time commitment in learning the class material, completing the PSets, and attending lab sections. While this might deter you from joining, know that there is an abundance of resources that you can turn to for help (AKA Professor Hasanov, your trusty TF/CAs, and your fellow peers who are in the trenches with you!), so please don't be afraid to take this class! I ultimately ended up having a lot of fun and learned a lot about Mechanical Engineering through this class.
