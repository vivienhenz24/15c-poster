Take this class. It really isn't as bad as the syllabus makes it out to be. You don't need any prior experience; just the motivation to partake in a venture and a nonprofit. You will need to work in a group, and these people will be from your section, so don't expect to work with your best friend.

Firstly, do some of the readings. Don't need to read all of them but have some readings where you can ask a great analytical question. You will end up doing FIVE of these for a semester, which is a short paragraph about your question towards the author.

Secondly, decide whether your group wants to work together both on the nonprofit or venture, or you can separate each other into either group. You will do a paper on a venture idea or a nonprofit. You will randomly select a topic from a drawing hat, so you will have no control over your topic. For us, we got literacy intervention, so we focused on intervening for children in struggling municipalities.

For nonprofit, you will reach out to multiple nonprofits and select one to represent for your final presentation. You will do extensive research and interview the team members about their nonprofit structure, finances, mission, etc. From there, when finals arrive, you will pitch your nonprofit and whoever receives the most votes gets the largest grant prize out of everyone. First place gets $25,000 from the Philanthropy Lab.

For the venture side, you will design your own nonprofit in which you do market research (not too complex), competition, costs and projections, and even interview stakeholders. You will be expected to run a survey or experiment to verify the effectiveness of your venture. You can design anything, even an app. The winning venture team receives a small grant of $700 to get started on the venture which can pursued post–class.

Overall take this course if philanthropy interests you. Professor Dromi is very great and engaging! This course will require a bit of work but it isn't as bad as it seems because it is a semester–long project.
