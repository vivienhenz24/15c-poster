100% take this class if you are at all interested!! Dr. de la Fuente is a sweetheart and very knowledgeable. You'll learn so much about the history of race and racism that you will not have the opportunity to learn otherwise. As is highlighted in the class, this is truly history that we should all know, and it's a shame that we don't. Some of the readings can be quite long and time–consuming, but they are balanced out by shorter readings. Just check the page lengths ahead of time and break up the longer readings; you'll be fine. There is only one assignment a week which will take about 30 minutes after you do the readings. The class itself is entirely discussion based and you will learn so much from your peers in the class who will each bring their own experiences to the table! I could not recommend this class enough :)
This class is not easy! I was under the impression first year seminars were very light, but this course required a lot from students. However, I gained so much from this class and if you have any interest in race or power systems you should take this class.
Take this class if you are seriously interested in the science of race and are prepared to read large amounts about the topic. This class does cover some present–day racism, but its main focus is the development of scientific classification of race and the way that has developed over time. At its core, this is a history class focused on the origins of race, not on the way it affects our current–day society. We briefly touched on Apartheid, but most of the focus after the Holocaust is on research and academia, not things like Black Lives Matter.

Each week, you will be expected to read somewhere between 150–200 pages (sometimes a whole book) and write a summary of what you've covered. This is not crazy compared to other seminars, but can feel slow at times, especially when you need a break from this niche topic. Getting a passing grade in this seminar is not difficult, but you should be aware when signing up that there is a substantial workload.

None of the content is redundant, but it can feel repetitive at times. This is a class that was designed for upperclassmen and requires intense concentration and dedication. At first, much of what's being discussed will seem contradictory or unclear, but as you become familiar with more and more concepts, it will begin to make more sense.

As a young person, this information gave me an interesting perspective about the world and classifications around me, but it's not content I will be reminded of every single day. I'm glad I stuck with it and pushed myself to be comfortable with the confusing nature of the subject. If you dive in too deeply, the absolute nonsense you're reading might distract you from the important stuff. If you are able to keep a birds–eye view and see how each source relates to one another I'm sure it will be very satisfying.
This class is incredible and was one of the highlights of my freshman fall! Please take it if you have any interest in race, racism, or the intersection of science and racism. Professor Fuente was an absolutely incredible teacher. From day one, he asked my peers and I questions that were genuinely very difficult to answer despite the answer seeming obvious when he first posed the question. I truly feel as though my conception of race and the history surrounding racism has been challenged thoroughly by this class, and I can confidently say that I think differently about these topics having taken this class. Each class I felt genuinely stumped by some of the questions we were asked to discuss, and it was exciting how discussions didn't just feel like synthesizing information from readings, but as though we were really trying to arrive at some sort of understanding regarding truly complex issues that wasn't yet obvious at the beginning of class. The way I thought about each reading transformed dramatically by the end of our discussions, which made them feel truly worthwhile. In fact, I can confidently say that this course was one of the most academically fulfilling parts of my semester. If you're looking for a class that will truly challenge you and force you to rethink much of how you conceive of concepts like race, this is the class for you.

Furthermore, I'd really like to emphasize what a wonderful teacher Professor Fuente is. Each week, we were assigned a brief essay that reflected on the week's readings, and while this assignment wasn't supposed to be anything more than a summary of our thoughts going into the week's discussion, Professor Fuente took the time to leave thoughtful feedback on every single essay we submitted. Whether it was applauding the thinking I had done or pushing me to wrestle with the readings more, I always felt that Professor Fuente truly cared about what I had to say. I felt the same in class, as he truly gave each student the space to speak, even if we were simply posing more questions to the group. He remembered details we shared about ourself on the first day of class and brought them up when relevant throughout the semester, he took interest in our lives outside of school, and, all in all, he made it clear that he cared about our wellbeing which was very comforting being new on campus. As I've already said, he also genuinely challenged us every week, and it made you want to really pay attention to everything he said each discussion. Professor Fuente is also just a really interesting person with some pretty incredible life experiences he shared with us. The two hour seminar period somehow always felt too short!

One thing I will say is that if you are simply looking for the easiest freshman seminar option, this class is probably not for you. Readings in this class were long, with full books being assigned for the week's homework sometimes. In addition to that, there was a short essay due reflecting on those readings each week. However, none of those readings ever felt useless. Each class discussion made it very clear WHY we had read a given text or series of texts, and I believe that attempting to cut some of those out would have sacrificed valuable material from the course. Furthermore, the course is structured chronologically, so you really understand the connection between the various texts throughout the semester. Lastly, I cannot express enough what a wonderful experience this class is, and I think that ought to make the greater–than–average freshman seminar workload worth it. It truly is just a great course with a great professor teaching it, and what you take away from Race Science is so worth a couple of reading cram sessions throughout the semester, especially if the rest of your schedule isn't too rough. Please don't avoid this course because of workload if you think you'll be interested in the content. I literally have nothing but good things to say about it.

TLDR: Race Science was one of the best parts of my freshman fall, and I am so grateful to have gotten the chance to be Professor Fuente's student. If you are interested at all by the subject matter take the class. It's a great opportunity to have challenging, in–depth discussions in a small–group setting with other students who are similarly passionate about the course content (I loved my classmates in this course!!!!). I'd absolutely recommend giving this course a try, and I'm so happy to have taken it.
PLEASE TAKE THIS COURSE!!!! Hands down one of my favorite classes I have taken in a while. Take this class if you have any interest in the history of race, understanding where certain assumptions on race come from, or just have a general interest in analyzing complex topics. The students in this class were interested in a multitude of different disciplines, but everyone appreciated this course and the instruction that Professor de la Fuente provided. This was a great way to start out my freshman year, and I cannot more highly recommend taking this course
