PS2 is heavily dominated by students who take AP Physics and know what they are doing. If you have no physics background or very little, you will struggle in this course. The teaching staff thinks that their teaching method for this class is great and this is because they are illusioned by the high average on midterms and the final in previous years. However, I think the teaching staff fails to recognize that the averages are high because students taking this course already know everything from high school, not because of their teaching methods. Myself and the handful of other students who had never taken physics before complained countless times about how this course structure is not beneficial for students who have never learned physics. They have these "pre–lecture activities" where they throw formulas that you're supposed to know and concepts, and then during lecture time they just throw you into the fire and expect you to solve problems with these formulas that you have never used before. You sit in your lecture seat lost on how to approach the problems when everyone else around you somehow knows how to solve these problems, only because, yet again, they did these types of problems in high school. I think the course does do a good job at providing examples of how to understand these topics conceptually, but you are hardly tested on this, and instead tested on how to solve things quantitatively, which leads to failure. Somehow the average for both midterms was 89 and above. I think this class needs to do a better job of providing a program that lessens the knowledge gap between those who have never learned physics and those who have. Until this is fixed, please expect to struggle in this class. I suggest having a friend who you know, knows physics so you can have them at your side to clarify any topics you're struggling on or even walk through problems with you. This is how I studied best, having my friend/suitemate walk through problems with me so I knew how to approach future problems similarly.

A perspective from someone who walked into this class having never taken a physics class before, even at the high school level, and needed it for a premed and concentration requirement:

In terms of work, I'd call this class so–so. The material itself isn't too bad, a lot of it is equation–based, and you get note sheets on the exams so you don't even have to memorize them. The tricky part is knowing what equation to use where, and making sure you don't mess up when you put something into your calculator. There are two parts to the homework, an online portion and a paper portion, both due weekly. They're not difficult, and each half was easier than a normal weekly PSET, so combined they made up one STEM class' worth of work. Some friends had issues with getting points taken off for missing small details on the paper PSETs, but I didn't personally have it happen and thought the grading was pretty fair. There's also a lot of partial points awarded on everything from homeworks to exams for showing your work which is nice. The pre–lecture activities before every class are a bit annoying, but they're really quick, only participation based, and a nice grade boost if you remember to do them.

The lectures were probably the most difficult part of the course. The professors have an interactive lecture format where you walk in, get a notes sheet that has a bunch of questions about the concepts you're learning that day, and the class alternates between discussing what you think the answer is with people near you, before the profs write the correct answer and elaborate a bit, and then they move on to the next question. I personally didn't learn very well that way, since if both you and the people you're talking to are all confused, the explanation afterwards doesn't really help. Still, since the class is mostly equation–based, as long as you have them in your notes properly and generally understand when to use them, then how much you get out of the lectures doesn't matter too much.

There's a weekly lab where you go in for a few hours and basically just play around with balls/ramps/springs/etc while writing down what happens. There's technically python coding involved, but it's 95% plugging numbers into existing code and clicking run to see what it gives back. Not sure how much I learned from them, but they weren't stressful and were interesting.

The professors are super nice, they genuinely seemed to want everyone to do well in the class, and were very receptive to if everyone thought a particular test was hard or if there was a miscommunication about what we had to do for a homework assignment. Also very approachable for questions, and for feedback about the class overall.

Overall, the class wasn't easy, the content was sometimes confusing, but it was very balanced out by the professors both being really supportive/helpful and them changing the class in small ways as the semester went on to make it better for everyone. If you're premed and debating taking this or AP50, I'd honestly say take this one – it might be a bit more work, but I definitely learned way more than a few friends who did AP50 and it didn't end up being a bad experience or an unmanageable amount of work or anything.
