Unless you are a Linguistics concentrator, *Do Not Take This Class*. In the first two or three weeks, youll be fooled – youll think its not too bad – but then, just after the end of Add/Drop, it finally kicks in, and its awful. The assignments rate of decline is practically exponential – they get significantly harder by the end of the semester, and youll find yourself in office hours every single week just to get by. The readings are wordy and full of confusing technical jargon, and you will procrastinate them every single week. The lectures are also full of jargon and largely difficult to follow. The midterm and final are sort of okay as long as you really study for them, but again, if youre not a Ling concentrator, youre still probably going to really struggle. If you MUST take this class, then do yourself a favour and take it pass/fail for your QRD like so many others do, otherwise youll regret it.
LING105 is an intermediate linguistics class, and the course content very much reflects that. The material is interesting but can get highly technical, and you WILL need to put in the effort to truly understand how to solve the pset problems, especially towards the latter half of the course. That being said, if you are interested in linguistics this is definitely a good intermediate class to take. The content seems like it would be especially useful in language documentation work, and generally helps with developing a deeper understanding of how language is produced in terms of sounds. The phonology problems feel a lot like fun puzzles at times, but definitely requires some practice to get the hang of. The exams definitely required you to understand the material to score well! Overall, satisfied with the class and how it's run, but definitely will note that it is not trivial to anyone searching for a light class.
Take it if you care at all about the science and logic deductions that you can dive into, regarding speech and sound!
This is a fascinating course as there are many interesting components when it comes to linguistics. However, if you have no previous knowledge of linguistics this course will be overwhelming and you will feel lost.
Two words: PASS/FAIL. The lectures are nonsensical? Who cares, it's pass/fail! The midterm and final are intense and you're adequately prepared for neither of them? Well, they won't let you fail! You need to go to office hours weekly for the whole hour to do well on the homework? You only need a 70%! You're finally solidifying knowledge about topics from weeks ago the night of the final? You just need to pass!

I took the class P/F, and I heard from SO MANY non–concentrators that I made the right move, since they were scrambling to figure out information and principles that we only talked about once in class to ensure they made a good enough grade on the final, while I was able to just guess my way through the multiple choice. Outside of this class, I'm a high–achieving student, and taking this class pass/fail was the best thing I did for my GPA and my mental health this semester.
I found the course to be extremely, extremely interesting, but did find it to be quite challenging over its second half. In a way, the phonetics segment of the course (the first half) catfishes you in terms of how difficult the course is going to be; I definitely found myself being quite overconfident after receiving my first few p–set grades back and chose another course to pass/fail, when looking back this might have been a better choice. Take if you're truly, truly interested in language –– my intense interest in the subject was oftentimes the one thing carrying me through when the material got more challenging. Nonetheless, a really fascinating, fruitful course.
This class is not necessarily easy. You should definitely study throughout the course. There is a lot of memorization, and I cannot stress enough how important it is to keep your studying consistent. The material builds on itself so it will help you with future concepts if you start really integrating the intro material. If you are new to linguistics, I would recommend reflecting on whether you are truly interested in the material, because if you are not, this class can become tedious very quickly. There are a lot of details, many passionate students, and it can feel like you are out of place if linguistics is not your thing. Additionally, the assignments and exams are not necessarily easy if you're not keeping up. It is clear the professor is trying to challenge us and get us to practice. Again, if you keep up, you will do well! Grading is somewhat lenient, but they do care about details. Only 10 assignments and they drop one. Your grade will be okay, it just matters if you are interested in the material.

That said, you don't have to do the readings and can pretty much logic and memorize your way through most of it. Non–linguistics students looking to take this for QRD: there's no math at all in this course, but there's a lot of memorizing and learning things that you do not and will not ever need.
This is a good course, and you'll learn lots of very useful things about language that you'll probably continue to think about after the course is over. However, the exams can be quite difficult, so make sure to listen during lecture.

This class is completely necessary for the linguistics concentration and is obviously fundamental to the study of language. The information is captivating, but the lectures are not very well organized and the homework is too harshly and inconsistently graded to fully enjoy the process of learning. You will leave this course better at solving (phonological) puzzles and knowing the IPA, features, methods of data analysis, and some party trick fun facts that you can only learn in a linguistics course.
