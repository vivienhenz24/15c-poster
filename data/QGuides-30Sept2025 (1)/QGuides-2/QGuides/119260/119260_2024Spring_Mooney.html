You get out what you put in. Prof. Mooney is very receptive to feedback and is constantly trying to improve the class. If you listen to his comments after every class, you gain a proper understanding on how he wants us to be critical about the field. Class is a little stress inducing if you aren't prepared but it all just adds to the fun.
This is an enjoyable and useful class – Professor Mooney is extremely knowledgable. The lab component is definitely a highlight of this course – the TFs are amazingly patient and helpful, and you come away with a lot of hands–on wet lab skills that transfer well to any future research; the final project is also pretty fun and interesting. For assignments, you read a lot of scientific papers and so gain a lot of experience understanding and critically analyzing them, which I found rewarding. The structure of the class is a flipped classroom – classes consist of students presenting on the assigned papers in groups rather than Professor Mooney lecturing. This gives good practice with presenting, but can sometimes be slightly stressful, so make sure to actually read the papers. Overall, a very manageable and informative class that gets you excited about the fields of tissue and bio engineering.
Please don't be mislead by the title of this course! Honestly, this class is more about reading papers about tissue engineering rather than engineering tissues itself. Currently, the class is structured in such a way that you read a journal article in depth before class, then you go in and create a slide with the potential to present. Although is sounds easy, it is actually fairly difficult as the papers are often complicated, touching areas that you may have not been exposed to before. Furthermore, you are graded on your commentary, so you have to be prepare to thoroughly analyze the results displayed, the techniques used, etc. The lab component of this course was very fun! However, you should be prepared to come to lab may times throughout the week to maintain your cells and perform your experiments. In the last few weeks of the semester, I often visited the lab 2–3 times outside the schedule lab period.
TAKE THIS COURSE! YOU GET TO LEARN SO MUCH AND IT IS SO VERY FUN :)
This class was so much harder and more time–consuming than previous years but SO SO BENEFICIAL. We were expected to read a full length (~10 pages) research paper on a tissue engineering topic before each class, which easily takes 2 hours. Also, you will be randomly assigned to present critiques of the paper during certain classes, but if you do the reading and come to class with a few points, you will be in good shape. There are also a few online lectures that you you have to watch for the midterm. Midterm is open–note and doable if you understand the lectures and review journal club papers. Lab is very time–consuming, especially the weeks after spring break where we design and run our own experiments. Expect to come into lab every 2–3 days if you split work with your group. Avery and Melissa are amazing and always ready to help, though! If you have any interest in tissue engineering, take this class (!!!) but know that you will have to put in the work.

This class is very interesting and a great exposure to the tissue engineering world. The lab component is fantastic and you learn a lot of really valuable 3–d tissue culture techniques. The class can be very confusing at times and Dr. Mooney doesn't hold your hand through the material which can be beneficial for accelerated learning speeds but can also lead to frustration and difficulty understanding topics. If you have tissue engineering experience, take this class. It's not a ton of work outside of class and you learn about some fascinating research papers that have been published in the past 50 years. This class is difficult and with a weird format. You will be reading and giving presentations, both short and long, about academic papers crucial to the tissue engineering field. Initially, I had trouble reading papers, but now I felt that I have developed such a skill. I do wish there was more guidance in learning how to read these dense papers, so either prep yourself beforehand or be ready to get over the learning curve through the semester. The flipped classroom also doesn't fully help with this, as you also have to learn the dense background to these dense papers, so this aspect is what makes the course more difficult. Ultimately, I grew to enjoy reading papers and learning how to critically analyze science. Also, the lab component to this class is fun and very informative. Designing your own experiment is cool, and growing HUVEC cells is very visually engaging.
