TLDR: Rob is a great, chill Expos teacher, but overall, Expos is not what it should be and requires significant changes


Near the beginning of the class, Rob shared his writers slogan: Writing is caring for others. Great authors give their all to the reader. They invest hours in rewriting and perfecting their works. Although the final piece may have room for improvement, it is methodical, well–developed, and clear. In short, the writing demonstrates care for others.

Just as this slogan applies to writing, it applies to teaching: Teaching is caring for others. After taking this course, I can say that Rob took this slogan to heart. He thoroughly cares for his students and tries to give them the best experience possible. I sincerely thank him for that.

However, Expos did not meet my expectations. I know that Rob has to follow certain guidelines for the class, so I do not at all blame him for this problem.

As it stands, Expos is a content–driven course, where most classes and readings focus on understanding the course topics. While I enjoyed learning about philosophical debates around meaning in language, literature, and life, the writing assignments felt like a sideshow compared to this content. I know that the contents of each Expos class are central to the essays, but they made the course feel more like a seminar than an intensive writing experience.

Work hard and you will learn lots and do well.

If these are topics or fields that you are interested in, then this is a great Expos class to read some very important texts in the field and learn about the academic conventions associated with them. Best Expos, hands down. This is the transformative Harvard experience. The material is so relevant and engaging. You do have to be invested in the topics, but it is not at all hard to be given the nature of the course content and instruction offered.

The first unit, meaning in language, is quite technical but a highly valuable field to learn as it gives you a foundation for what gives words meaningsomething you don't really stop to think about and appreciate. This transformed my perspective on human communication and large language models while giving me a great introduction to important linguistics theories. The second unit, meaning in literature, is also intriguing and extremely worthwhile. It questions things like the role of an author in a text and rules that govern writing (prescribed grammar rules vs. describing how people naturally speak)something of interest to any reader/writer of texts (aka you!).

As if these two weren't amazing and so relevant to our own lives enough, the third unit covers meaning in life! We all question this topic, and the readings offer wonderful theories that can help guide your own independent thoughts on this topic. None of the readings are prescriptive and are meant to give you new perspectives to draw from and better inform your own outlooks into these critical topics. I felt so much more assured and informed on this topic, which affects everyday experiences with life, after this course.

Essays are very reasonable, and feedback is meticulous and always helps your next draft be much better in structure, argumentation, etc. The final research paper is the final boss that requires your proper attention and time commitment. The first two essays are more easily paced and structured.

Last thing, the students were a significant part of what made this class the wonderful experience it was. My section always had amazing discussions that led to many classes going overtime and time flying by so quicklyit was always so fun and insightful to engage in class discussions about what people thought about meaning (in various contexts, of course).

Expos was the highlight of my weekmy favorite class by far. I learned so much this semester, and it has transformed how I want my college career to look. Cannot recommend this course enough. Just make sure you want to commit to this course and are ready to fully engage with readings, discussions, and critical thinking. You will get so much out of this course!!!
