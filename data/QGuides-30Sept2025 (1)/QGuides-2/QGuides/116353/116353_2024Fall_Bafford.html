If you have the opportunity to take it, I'd highly recommend this course. Dr. Bafford (Doug) is an excellent preceptor: Intelligent, kind, and perceptive, he was the primary reason for why I enjoyed this class as much as I did. Expos is often cited as one of the more painful undergraduate requirements––talking to my peers in other Expos classes, it seems as though this section is one of the good ones. Assignments are consistent but not at all overwhelming (the generous extension policy helps with this), discussions were usually lively, and readings ranged from mildly interesting to captivating. The quality of feedback you'll be receiving on your writing is also superb: It was always in my best interest to read every one, since they usually pointed out flaws in my reasoning, analysis, or style. You'll become a better writer no matter how skilled (or unskilled) you are at academic writing.

More than becoming a better writer, I learned how to become a better thinker. The readings will expose you to a variety of ways of thinking about the concept of taboo. You'll read about how Freud thinks that everyone is secretly in love with their mothers; Mary Douglas, for her part, will argue to you that ritual is a way for us to engage with taboo without being obvious about it; you might read Claude Levi–Strauss's witty takedown of Freud's ideas; you'll read a paper that claims that people are subliminally attracted to their family members, and another that rebuts the entire thing.

There were some weakness in the course: It sometimes felt we were trying to do too much during sections, readings got less relevant towards the end of the semester, and I felt as though more could have been done during meetings to compel everyone to contribute. These weaknesses are not at all deal–breaking, and any Expos section will have imperfections.

It's a common misconception that preceptor's don't have a limit on the number of As they give out. The grading in this class was fair, but the level of writing you'll be expected to produce is a lot higher than high school, so be prepared.

Some final tips:
– Talk to your classmates! Section got way more interesting when we got to know each other.
– Start papers early (i.e. don't try to do all of your work on Friday afternoons).
– Take advantage of the extension policy. You have 168 hours that you can distribute however you want!
– Come to your one–on–one conferences with targeted questions. Doug isn't interested in telling you exactly what to do to get an A on your essay, but he will tell you where you should put your attention to elevate your paper, and wants to see that you're thinking critically about your own work.
– Do the readings, especially at the beginning. Your writing will not be good if you don't understand the texts you're talking about.

TAKE THIS COURSE!! Taboo wasnt my first choice for the Expos lottery but Im so glad that I ended up in it! I think that to succeed in an Expos class, you must be a little interested in the material (you dont have to be overly passionate about it) and jive well with your professor. For example, I dont feel strongly about cultural taboos but I found them interesting enough to be able to come up with essays that genuinely excited me. It definitely helped that Doug was super flexible with our essay topics; he gave us a LOT of creative freedom with our essays.

Doug is also very, very understanding about extensions and conscious about our workloads outside of Expos. His policy on extensions (Im not sure if this is standard across other Expos courses) was essentially no questions asked. He gave us 168 hours of free extension time to use across the semester and at the end of the semester, expressed that if we needed more time beyond that, we could have it. I rarely asked for extensions in high school but I ended up using all of my free extension time. When he assigned longer readings, he never expected us to throughly read them (he told us skimming was ok) which was also very nice.

Lastly, this may have been specific to the cohort of people in my class but I genuinely looked forward to class every Tuesday/Thursday because of the people. Dougs ice breakers at the start of every class definitely bonded us and by the end of the semester, we were all pretty close. Our closeness made for a very fun classroom environment and now that I think about it, I dont think that I ever dreaded going to this Expos class.

All that to say I have ZERO REGRETS about taking this course and if youre feeling hesitant about it, definitely give it a chance!!!
