EC1011a vs. Ec1010a
For those on the fence, I would highly recommend taking Ec1011a over 1010.

(1) In 1011, a majority of the students get an A– or above as the curve is now more lenient than it used to be (coming from Prof. Glaeser himself) as it needs to more closely match the 1010 grade distribution. I think college is about taking classes that will challenge you and where you will learn a lot (1011), rather than taking a sleepy class where you can coast by and not get the full Harvard experience (1010), so I implore you to ponder about whether you want to make your college experience rewarding and intellectually stimulating or coast by in boredom/ambivalence and wonder why you spend so much money to take boring classes.

(2) The material and skills you learn in 1011 are MUCH MORE satisfying than in 1010. In 1011, you understand and appreciate the importance of modeling in economics and it introduces key concepts like insurance, moral hazard, intertemporal consumption, and monopolies in a much more interesting manner.

(3) Professor Glaeser is an incredibly accomplished professor, and despite the fact that he lectures at an incredibly fast pace and takes partials at the speed of light, it felt like taking use of Harvards resources to hear from a potential future Nobel laureate. His theory on spatial equilibrium and special topics on the economics of crime, hatred and cities are eye–opening and reveal why microeconomics is such an interesting topic and the variety of ways it can be applied.

(4) The first couple weeks of 1011 are designed to scare people off and they are actually the toughest in the course (it gets easier from there). I'll try to give my best overview of the course structure to help paint a picture of what taking this course will be like.

1011A is notorious for being one of the most difficult undergrad intermediate micro courses in the country, and for good reason. The class is taught at a high level of rigor and moves very quickly; as everyone else will say, Ed goes far too fast in lecture when doing derivations on the board. Psets are also quite tedious to complete, though they are graded for completion. Policy discussions are often interesting and take up the first 15 or 20 minutes of lecture. Use them as a chance to understand why we're learning the topic of the day, as the math that ensues will probably not make much sense to you. The professor, Ed, is not accessible outside of class (other than a mandatory pizza lunch you have to go to for a grade). The head TF Aakash writes the exams and modeling projects. There's also a head CA who runs the administrative work for the class (such as Ed). Finally, there are the CAs who run section/OH/grading. Section is mandatory each week, where you will go over concepts from that week and do practice problems. Office hours are probably not helpful (they're told to provide very little guidance, especially on the modeling projects, and since the psets are completion it's really not even worth going, you can just read the solutions or watch the walkthrough after if you're curious).

In terms of preparation, multivariable calculus at the level of Math 21A/22B is necessary. However, it is probably insufficient: stopping at this level will probably lead to you falling behind as you try to unwrangle the math (though mostly just tedious algebra, partial derivatives aren't terribly complicated). As a 22B alum, I found myself losing all the intuition as I tried to grasp the math. Those who took Math 25 or 55 will likely fare much better in this class just due to the sheer amount of math that goes into it. I feel that the level of math in this course detracted from the actual insight by being so ugly and intractable, though this is likely by design. If you took/are concurrently enrolled in 110, you'll be used to answers coming out beautiful. In this class, however, a beautiful answer probably means you messed up somewhere or made too many assumptions. Take that for what you will.

You complete 3 modeling projects throughout the semester, each one over about a 2 week span (with one pset still due in this time period as well). This feels like too many, and it can be overwhelming. Project 1 will probably be the hardest, followed by Project 2. The final modeling project is by far the most painless and most fun, and also worth the largest portion of your grade. These will be a struggle to complete for most people and I suggest that you find a good group and communicate to reduce the stress of the projects. Most people get around the median on these projects though, so don't expect to do wildly better or worse than average as long as you put in adequate effort.

Almost all of the grade differentiation in this class occurs through the midterm and final exams. The midterm is an extreme time crunch, being an in–class 75 minute exam with 4 true/false/uncertain questions (you have to write an explanation for each one) and 2 longer free response questions. Most people just give up on trying to work out all the math by the time they get to the final question and start using intuition instead; this can give you substantial credit, so don't give up if you can't figure out a derivation. The average for the midterm will probably be in the mid 40s, so don't stress too much about getting everything; doing perhaps 8 percent above average is enough to be on track for an A.

The final exam was less of a time crunch (3 hours) and more fair, but the average was still just a 49. It is almost entirely focused on post–midterm concepts from class, so make sure to focus on those in preparation. That's a good thing as well, since the post–midterm content is a lot less spamming of ugly math, instead testing more interesting conceptual understanding (still with a lot of math). Game theory is probably the most satisfying unit of the course and the easiest way to do well on the final since it is mostly self–contained.

If you want to do well in this class, you MUST go to Aakaash's review sessions, especially the ones for the midterm and final exams. Trust me, you will do FAR better on these exams if you go to the review sessions, since Aakaash is the one who writes the exams, and he will give many hints to help you do well on the exams. They will probably not be recorded, so try your best to attend.

This class is known for having a very high grade floor (>75% of students receive an A or A–; about the top 25% get an A). So if you do your problem sets, go to lecture, go to section, and perhaps read the textbook, it should not be too hard to get an A–. With additional effort, an A is also not unattainable. Make sure to do the practice exams before the midterm and final to prepare for time pressure and see how they would like you to answer questions.

Perhaps it's just that the mathematical modeling didn't appeal to me, or that I'm just not good enough at math to survive a grad school program. If you're applied math econ, you can technically bypass this course (the substitute micro theory courses are far easier), so consider that if the reviews you're seeing aren't too motivating. I understand what they're trying to do with this course, and I appreciate the idea, even if at times it seems a little bit too far.
