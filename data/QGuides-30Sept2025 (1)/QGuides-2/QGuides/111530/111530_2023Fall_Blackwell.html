Do the readings before class so you can ask questions during the lecture
NOTE; 2002 this year was basically 2001 in a normal year!!!
1. If you've taken stat 110, the first half of this course will be review/relatively easy. However, then you really jump off the deep end with statistics and modeling (into stat 111 things I think) and it gets a lot trickier.
2. Blackwell really cares but he has to fly through material to fit everything in, so be prepared to just be either furiously writing everything down in lecture or take notes on the slides and just try and absorb it
3. Mitsuru's office hours were super helpful, definitely attend!
The pace of this class picks up fast. You'll shift from basically probability to inference and regression quickly, so its important to keep up with the lecture notes, review the textbook, and go to section. The PSETs are challenging, so defintely have a strong PSET group to work with. I do think that you walk away from this class with a basic but strong understanding of probability, inference, and regression that lets you learn more on your own. Mathematically, it is quite challenging (especially post–midterm) so in case you thought this was a "Gov" class, don't be fooled. I took this class as an undergraduate Gov major with some moderate mathematical background (Calc BC in HS, and Math 22a) and I certainly found the math challenging but doable. This is a "must–take" for any undergrad interested in doing quantitative research in government, so take it but keep the afformentioned in mind. Grading was relaxed, and getting an A is very doable, conditional on you having a strong PSET group, and being a decent test taker who prepares adequately. I recommend this class, but only for those with a desire to do more math in the context of social sciences!
Overall good class, but sometimes feels too abstract / rushed. For those coming into grad school without recent methods coursework, would recommend going through the Gov 50 slides beforehand and having a good understanding of matrix algebra. The issue when taking the course is also developing a more intuitive understanding of the material beyond applications on a problem set.
Be confident in stats or at least be willing to lock in before signing up. It's tough.
Good social science class for divisional distribution to take if you're a math / stat concentrator who already knows probability and statistics (say at the level of Stat 110 and Stat 111). Assignments are easy (and a lot of help available at OH), and exams are take–home and quite relaxing as well.
The professor is great, and you do learn a lot. However, it does go through a lot of material, so putting in consistent work every week outside of the problem set is helpful.
This class moves really fast, make sure you spend time outside of class to work more on it
The weekly problem sets are very challenging. However, if you attend office hours, you will get all of your questions answered, and you will be able to complete the problem sets. This class moves EXTREMELY fast, so if you are a slow learner, this class definitely isn't the class for you. If you have taken STAT 110, the first half of the class is basically identical to STAT 110. However, the second half of the class is completely new material. This class is hard, but if you put in the time, you'll be fine!
