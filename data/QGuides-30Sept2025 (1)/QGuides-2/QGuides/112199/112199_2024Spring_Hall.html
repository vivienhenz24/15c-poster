This course is amazing. Professor Hall and Ezra work really well together and so the class has both incredible, engaging lectures and comfortable sections where we dive deeper into the material. The grading breakdown is clear and organized, and although the point system might appear to be a little stringent there's so many opportunities to review with both the prof/TF to make sure you're submitting good work, plus chances to retake and resubmit assignments that received subpar grades. The material is great – it's a really good intro to formal logic and it made me want to study the subject even more in detail. The discussions we have, facilitated by the prof and TF, are very engaging and they make an effort to create an environment where people are encouraged (but not pressured) to talk, even when it comes to controversial and/or emotionally charged material. Amazing course.

I really liked this course, but I cannot say that it would be universally enjoyed. I would rate it 7/10 for me and 2/10 for anyone randomly. The course is highly structured, so keep that in mind. The staff are super flexible and understanding to your individual needs, though.

If you like logic puzzles as much as I do, you will have a blast solving them every week for homework, learning new strategies from the teaching team that make it much more pleasant than working through a book on your own. However, if you hate puzzles, you will be miserable because they will take up many hours of your weeks. You also get to design your own, which is pretty fun except...

There is forced group work. This was my least favorite part of the course, especially during what are called "argument labs." The group work model failed me miserably every time I had to deal with it. There are two scenarios. The first is that you are in a group of people who do not care about or do not understand the material (it is a GENED, after all!). This ends up resulting in you doing all the work. The second scenario is that you are in a group of people who do care about the material and understand it to some degree. This creates the problem of too many cooks in the kitchen. Because the argument lab is somewhat up to interpretation, this leads to the group spending inordinate time arguing over structure. The result is a slipshod final piece that includes so many compromises that it does not fit anyone in the group's vision and is thus somewhat incoherent. Yes, you're allowed to resubmit them; usually a single person reworks it–– so that does not fix the root issue.

If you've always wanted to learn logic but are scared to jump into a course full of math concentrators who already know what they're doing, this is also an excellent option. I can say, coming away from this course, I am pretty confident in my basic propositional and first order logic skills. Considering I knew literally nothing about logic beforehand, that is a testament to the excellent teaching of the course. Professor Hall is truly one of the most responsive and kind faculty I have ever had the privilege to interact with!

TLDR: want an easy gened? pass. love logic puzzles and arguing? enroll.

I'll definitely remember some of the formal principles in logic we learned but mainly, how to structure and analyze arguments in ways that cut out the rhetorical fluff and get to the core of what the author actually intends to say. Ned is an amazing professor and is reason enough to take this class. But if that is not convincing, here is a proof as to why you should take his class:

1. At the beginning of the semester Ned will try to gaslight you by claiming that his class is not a gem. If you have any background in logic or discrete math, I assure you that this is false. (Relevant classes I've taken: CS 20, CS 120, CS 124, PHIL S–12, MATH 22A).
2. If you do not have any background in logic or discrete math, then you are in need of one. Taking Ned's class is a great place to start while also satisfying a GENED requirement.

Ned and the teaching staff (Ezra) are also extremely easy to work with. Fast replies on emails and several opportunities to resubmit basically everything. As a result, grades are assigned based on your cumulative understanding of the material. Harvard would truly be a better place if all courses had a similar philosophy.
Few other classes and especially no other GENED is going to change how you approach something so fundamentally. This class is frustrating and liberating at the same time. To those of you who've come to Harvard to learn great things in the purest possible sense, I can recommend you this class in no stronger terms. No other class is so dependent on celebrating you and your peers' intellectual will rather than rigidly following dispositions of the professor like some kind of mime.
