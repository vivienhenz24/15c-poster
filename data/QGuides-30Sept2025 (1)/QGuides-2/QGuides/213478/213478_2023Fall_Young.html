This is a great course if you are interested in applications of linear algebra or data analysis. Having a strong linear algebra background will help this class be more manageable. I really enjoyed the final project on an unsupervised method of your choice.
This is the best class I took this semester, possibly the best stat class I've taken and I really like 110 and 111. It feels almost like an applied linear algebra class at times, but the psets have a nice real world application element. I would say this class helps most with statistical and linear algebra intuition, and you really begin to understand what "data" is. Would recommend 10/10, surveys a lot of cool methods while going deep into PCA, SVD, and NMF. Would say the only thing that might make the class better is decreasing the size of the nonlinear dimension reduction survey a little bit, and maybe expanding the AE or NN unit. I would also say sometimes the clustering unit feels a bit rushed. Overall quite possibly the best class. Shoutout Alex for being an amazing and supportive prof too!
I would recommend this class! Difficult, math–heavy, but rewarding
Professor Young is a fantastic lecturer. This is the only course I can spend the entire 75 minutes focused.

6 PSETs. Each problem set is only ~5–7 problems (with sub–problems) but solving and typing one can take 1–2 hours. On estimate I spend ~10 hours on this course's homework per week.

Midterm is very hard but graded fairly.

It is a hard course, especially if you are not from a math/statistics background like myself.
The content in the earlier weeks are very math–heavy but the content after the midterm is less mathematical. It is a good survey course for various methods of unsupervised learning.

Overall I'd recommend this course. Especially for people with data science background like myself. There are not many courses about unsupervised learning and this one strikes a nice balance between technical details and applications.
I enjoyed the course. Alex is a great teacher and runs engaging lectures, stops to take questions, and explains concepts very well, building student understanding. He is also a very personable professor and his office hours are helpful as well. He is also available after class for quick questions which is very helpful.

The problem sets did require some tricks on occasion, which generally would require going by office hours. Also, the release of the biweekly problem sets would sometimes mean there was only a week to do them, but that generally was not a huge issue as the length was generally reasonable.

The midterm was fair, and I enjoyed the final project. The oral exam was not too strenuous; the questions really just probe for your genuine understanding of what you wrote. The coding midterm helps balance out the sit down midterm. Grading was generally not a huge stressor in the course, though the problem sets were graded rather rigorously and I know some classmates weren't always able to finish everything.

Having a strong lin alg background is very helpful, and working knowledge is absolutely necessary. Familiarity with R is also helpful, but I was able to pick up the R required without prior experience. Coding is not a major challenge in the course, as code demos with similar code as examples are provided, but some familiarity is required to do the assignments and the coding midterm (take home). Some calculus, including multivariable derivatives, is necessary.
A challenging, worthwhile, at times rough, and fascinating survey of unsupervised learning methods –– it attracts a wide array of students (sophomores to seniors, MCB to Economics, MIT cross register, and even grad students) because the applications of UL are so numerous. There aren't that many graded assignments so make sure you do at least average on the midterm. The paper and oral exam are pretty straightforward. You probably want more mathematical maturity than just Stat 110 and Math 21a/b though. Alex is a caring pedagogue, and it is quite imperative that you find the CAs that you vibe with early on to help guide you through psets.
My favorite stats course I've taken at Harvard. Moves at a pace where you can really understand every topic fully. Alex is a wonderful professor and I recommend taking any class that he takes. There's tons of office hours and biweekly problem sets are refreshing.
This course fulfilled my curiosity about many statistical concepts that I was interested in, though the psets are challenging. Be prepare to study in groups or to attend office hours in order to solve them. I love chance of doing the final term paper which push you to dive into a method with full depth and breath. Professor also gives many detailed and concrete feedback on it. Overall, I highly recommend this course.
Attend lecture and don't fall behind! Alex is so nice and very accessible, especially during lecture. Find a good problem set group and start assignments early. Very fair class if you make sure to stay on top of things.
