Take it, but make sure you are willing to put in the effort because it is not a walk in the park. It gives you a good sense of important topics and new research in econometrics, and the types of questions researchers should be asking. However, the lectures provide only a cursory / hand–wavy overview of the mechanics of how things work, which you need to invest extra time understanding.
This course moves fast and is quite difficult, but ultimately very useful for applied work.
Extremely challenging unless you are very gifted and knowledgable about math and stats
Heads up that I believe this course will be taught by a different professor next year, so the material will change quite a bit. I would say that if the course is anything like previous years, you should definitely understand the material from advanced linear algebra, Stat 211, (possibly Stat 213), and Econ 2120. At least look over the material for all of those courses.

The course was extremely math–heavy and esoteric, and not great for teaching the intuition around methods for applied researchers. There are better courses in the department for that. The people who really seemed to get it were the metrics theorists. But the metrics theorists I talked to also thought the material was unnecessarily esoteric for applied researchers. If you don't have to take it as a requirement, I would not recommend it.
You get out what you put in
If you are required to take it, then that's thatbut there are reasons to take the course even if it isn't strictly required! It's a great opportunity to broaden your analytical and econometric horizons. The scope of the course and variety of estimation/analytical techniques are very ambitious. Keep a realistic mindset about what you want to take away from the course and you will be fine.
Try to talk to your peers and the TAs. They will help you if you need help.
Good luck!
Mathematically the course is not very rigorous, so as long as you have STAT 110/STAT 111 + MATH 21a/21b level background I think you should be able to get an A in the class as long as you study.

However, my opinion is that this course aims at ``introducing" econometrics to an applied economist who does not need to have an understanding of the statistical tools being used in causal estimators. In other words, the class focuses on just introducing estimators for a single lecture without going through their identification or proving inference results. Therefore, I do not think you learn very much from this course compared to graduate courses in the statistics department. If your goal is to improve your statistics or econometrics background for non–applied research, I think that courses in the STAT department are better.
