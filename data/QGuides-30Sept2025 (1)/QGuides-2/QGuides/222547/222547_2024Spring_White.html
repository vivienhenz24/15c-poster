Excellent, accessible philosophy class that exposes you to many traditions in ethics (Aristotelian virtue ethics, consequentialism, Kantianism, Rossian ethics) through the lens of partiality in ethics. The focus on why partiality is or is not justified sharpens the focus of the class, leading us to discuss variants of theories I hadn't encountered before. Also, given that more than 2/3 of the Class of 2023 earns >$70,000 upon graduating [1], putting them at the top 1% of the global income distribution [2], thinking about how impartial you should be with your career and income is enormously important. I'm pretty confident that this class will be more relevant to thinking about my place in the world and my life than all gen eds I have taken.

Nuts and bolts: Prof. White is an excellent lecturer and encourages participation. The readings are fantastic. The TF, Britta, was accessible and helpful, providing extensive feedback on the essays. The course is very accessible to those without a philosophy background –– perhaps a bit too much so –– but nevertheless has much for people who've taken more philosophy classes given the slightly niche subject [3]. It's also not much work, although I recommend that you do the readings and, especially, talk to Prof. White in office hours. He is very nice and easy to talk to. Take this class!

One of my favorite classes ever! I think philosophy of this kind might be some of the most important education a person can receive. If you engage with the course, it is totally feasible you will develop:

1) An analytic toolkit: taking this class means reading some of the most persuasive ethicists in dialogue with one another, and then *joining* that conversation
2) A system of ethics: the organization of the curriculum offers insights from the most popular ethical frameworks and allows for a synthesis of one's own system
3) A life philosophy: in puzzling through the tension between impartial ethics and partial intuitions, one confronts several of the most important questions in life: what do we owe others? what, if anything, should we do? what are the roles of love, companionship, and family in an ethical life? what space is there for individual autonomy in the evolving ethics of an interconnected world?

Anyone can take this course; the best background you can have is an open mind and a willingness to engage. The lectures are fantastic; Quinn is incredibly engaging, and I never felt so comfortable or eager participating in class sessions. The readings are so stimulating, and give a great sense of different perspectives in western ethics, articulated convincingly. The assignments are totally manageable, with a focus on eliciting your best work, rather than your busiest. I am proud of content that I produced in this class, and I will continue discussing it's material with anyone who cares to listen!

As someone who'd not taken a Phil class before (senior spring things), this was an interesting and accessible overview of topics in modern ethics attempting to tackle the question of partiality. I particularly liked the structure of the course, with readings grouped by different overarching approaches that sample from several different schools of thought and eras of philosophy. Prof. White was amazingly energetic and very open to conversation, and it almost seems as if he was an underutilized resource over the semester. Papers were straightforward and approachable. Maybe my one complaint with the class is that its emphasis on cataloguing different approaches to partiality meant that things started seeming repetitive halfway through the semester; this is not particularly a general whirlwind overview course.
