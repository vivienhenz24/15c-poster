This course is fantastic, but I would recommend really thinking about the time commitment needed for the project. You might see all the comments and be thinking, "Well, it can't be that much work since it's just one project" or "Just, like, start early." But I promise, it's sincerely a ton of work. This project is very much meant to take the whole semester, so if you happen to start late, be prepared to spend every waking moment working on it for the last few weeks of the semester. You're just not going to be able to crank it out in a week; it's many thousands of lines of code, most of which is the kind of code that takes forever to debug. Additionally, a decent amount of the project is less difficult and more annoying (such as parsing string input), so a fair amount of your work is going to involve just bashing hundreds–to–thousands of lines of code.
That being said, this course was super fulfilling. Stratos and the rest of the teaching staff were absolutely fantastic, and the lectures are really interesting. I would really recommend auditing this course, with a greater consideration about actually taking it and signing yourself up for the final project. The grading for the course is also pretty generous, especially considering so few people actually finish the final project.

Having just submitted, it's hard to know what to say here. This was brutal, as expected. I feel like I learned a lot, as I expected. I don't think I walked away with a huge amount of insane knowledge about how data systems work. Yes I understand batching, indexing and joins far better than I did coming in (but you could learn about a B–tree and it would make sense from a 20min youtube video, and hash joins is mostly just... use a good hash table). I am also not a perf or gdb god, or suddenly converted to neovim and ascended to a higher level of CS entity within the CS community (maybe that will be after 161 next sem, don't ask why I'm doing this to myself in my senior year, and perhaps use this to caveat all opinions shared here).

However, I without a doubt feel that I became a better systems programmer. It's rare here you need to actually think about how to organize a project. My final code, just including .c files that I wrote, is 5500–6000 lines of C. Honestly that doesn't feel like that much now, but after re–writing functions, changing how things fitted together, the amount of code I've deleted, changing or refactored, I reckon I actually wrote 2–3x that this semester. Thinking about how you organize a project of that size was super valuable to me. What functions should be accessible in other files. What should be defined in shared header files, what should be unique to each header file. Maybe you want to use extern. Maybe you want to consider what should be global, what should be specific to a client context. I actually genuinely considered cache effects – I changed my load function at some point (much later than I should've), and watched my load performance improve by a factor of like 50 (yes, if you're reading this in the class, make your load faster earlier and save yourself pain later. Or don't and get the learning experience). I experimented with the worst nested loop join, then the cache page at a time nested loop join, and crazy, it makes a HUGE difference. Just some examples. I didn't really ever think about the TLB and never really needed to it felt like.

Lectures were overall pretty good, I have no idea where an attendance grade came from at the midterm so please be careful about that and ask about attendance points. The first midterm was psycho hard, the second one was in my opinion, extremely doable (I got a 70 on the first one, admittedly I got the takehome deadline wrong, but I still think I would get <90 (/100 but with like 40 bonus points I think), second one I got 130/130). I would recommend watching a few of the sections and going to lab in the first few weeks to get setup, maybe I just can't read but setting up the testing locally etc was a bit of a headache initially.

The testing set up this year felt a bit all over the place, the tests literally were being changed up until the last week and had a major change at the end of Novemeber. I hope this is a one time pain point and not a problem for future years, as it was pretty confusing/caused some stress, unsure if tests were failing because I can't code or because the tests were broken, changed or whatever. Labs also didn't run every day as promised on the website, I barely used them (probably a mistake) but I think it's worth saying, I think the course needs one or two extra staff for future iterations.

My brain power is running out, I submitted the report like 5 hours ago and am completely spent, but just to end on some practical tips that maybe will be useful if you do decide to take the course:
–> Do milestone 0, it takes very little time and you'll thank your previous self a HUGE amount when you're fighting the deadline at the end like most of us. I was able to do naive hash joins in a few hours since I had the hash table from m0 ready to go (please don't bank on this though).
–> Write some unit tests. I had one void function like test_batch() in most of my header files, so that I didn't have to add functions to the header file that shouldn't be accessible outside the function (other option is two versions of the header file, and use different include paths, I started going this route initially but it was just a bit of a hassle). This really helped me at times, especially for index creation – writing unit tests to check my sorting stuff was working correctly meant I knew that my cache conscious mergesort worked correctly, so it must be how I was calling it that was wrong, helped me spot a dumb mistake very fast that otherwise I might've been trying to debug my sort algo forever. Just one example.
–> Write a better load function than just stream inserts. Just do it, it'll take you like three–four hours and make your life better.
–> Think about how you're going to break up db_manager.c almost instantly :)
–> Use doubles for averages. I quite literally replaced every occurence of the word float with double (matching case) and it fixed my printing for averages lmao.
–> You're going to need to print in chunks. Sorry, it's inevitable.
There's probably a bucnh more but I can't stay awake any longer. I think if I did the project again, I would make 5x better decisions with what I've learned, and that knowledge will help me in the future, I'm almost certain. If you choose to hop on board, good luck :)
