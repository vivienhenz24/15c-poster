This class was very interesting and I would recommend any neuro concentrators take it as long as you are made aware of a few things. 1) The readings can be very hard sometimes and you will not understand them. This can make the open book reading quiz at the beginning of each section a bit stressful but it is not that many questions and we were assured that you were only expected to get about 50% of them correct. I did a lot better on them as the class went on, but do the readings and section always helped me understand the material better. 2) There is no midterm, which was lovely, but the final exam had no prep. We were given three example questions without the answers provided and no info on the format, just general tips on what would be on the exam like the lecture notes and papers. There is also a final paper that may take a lot of time and effort, but I think if you put in that effort you can easily assess if you are going to get an A or not. Overall I did really like this course and it was cool to learn about brain behavior in regards to animals instead of just humans.
The current iteration of the course (Spring 2024 w/ Prof. Isoe) is unsatisfactory. The course material is incredibly interesting, but muddied down by the poor organization of the course and lack of effective assessments. I genuinely do not remember anything from this course due to the 5–minute weekly quizzes fostering a culture of route memorization as opposed to learning via active engagement with lecture material. Moreover, the final project proposal –– where students come up with an experimental design based on a topic in class –– is due on the same date as the final exam (an especially odd decision given that this course has no midterm assessment, making it very doable for the project proposal assignment to have been due towards the tail end of the semester as a late midterm rather than during a time where students ought to be reviewing material for the final exam). It's frustrating because the course material is great for anyone planning to do research in neuroscience, and I would've certainly recommended the course IF it was organized well.

Take this class! It is pretty stress–free throughout the semester (no midterms, no psets, etc.) Instead, you are assigned to read one or two short papers (average ~4pages with figures) and complete a 5–minute open–note reading quiz each section. These reading quizzes do require pretty detailed understanding of the experiments you read, but are graded with the expectation that you won't really finish. The lectures themselves are super interesting, finding model organisms that connect to and dive deeper into everything from LTP to visual pathways. The staff is fun and helpful.
