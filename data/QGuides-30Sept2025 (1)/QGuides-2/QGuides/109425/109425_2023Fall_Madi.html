The professor is looking for one specific answer to all of these identitiarian questions (regarding women's rights, the Arab identity, immigration, etc.).

If they ask you things like, "What is the Arab identity?" (which, ridiculously, they will) don't even bother responding. Just wait until they tell you the answer because, despite the fact that this type of question has a million possible answers, they are quite specifically looking for one, and anything else will be dismissed out of hand.
The class is fast–paced, but the grading is very much in your favor. Madi is a phenomenal teacher and would highly recommend him. There is daily homework, weekly essays, and tests every other week, so you have to be able to commit a good amount of time every week to the course. The course helps you improve your understanding of Arabic and focuses more on personal progress. The class is very interactive so you have to be prepared to participate.
This class focuses on dialect in class, but most of the homework is in MSA. Dialect will appear on exams. The workload is higher than in AA/AB.
Difficult in some ways but extremely rewarding. You will leave the semester with a much better grasp on the language, developing both your FusHa (formal) and Shami/Masri (dialects) speaking skills. Also, the essays may seem intimidating but they are not too long and Ostath just wants to see you put your best effort; it's all about improving. Overall I would say the homework is quite hefty but is necessary to feel improvement.
Take Arabic at Harvard! The teaching staff here is incredibly passionate, devoted, and enthusiastic, and the students form a wonderful community of learners.
Great class. Not too hard or too easy, all assignments perfectly reflect what you've learned and are prepared to do. Homework every day is a lot but necessary to keep improving. You learn more dialect in this class, which is great, take advantage of this opportunity and don't choose to learn the bare minimum or you'll regret it. Fantastic teacher who really cares about the class and his students.
Take this class! Ustaaz Amr is an excellent, helpful, and kind professor who wants to see every student succeed. You will not only learn technical components of the Arabic language, but also gain a vast knowledge of culture and history. He is willing to listen to students needs and hear varying perspectives on different issues. His passion for Arabic and Arab culture is apparent in his teaching and conversations with students, and creates an environment that encourages appreciation and commitment to the language and culture. As someone who can sometimes lose sight of why I chose to enroll in the class, this class was a reminder of why I chose to study and aarabic and why I want to continue doing so.
Take this class only if you are willing to put in the hours of work. Continue in the arabic journey if you have a genuine interest for langauge acquisiton, I cant imagine why you would take this class otherwise. It is not an easy A class for the language requirment –– so take something else if you are goping to fulfill that here. Ustaadh Amr and Ozzy are two of the best teaching faculty in the Arabic dept though and will be very supportive an helpful if you do decide that you want to work on this skill. They are patient, so smart, and all star teachers at this school who care about teaching and put students first –––> if you have already decided to take this course and are just deciding on which preceptor to take it with, I would pick Amr!!
Take this class! Your speaking, reading, writing, and comprehension skills in Arabic will improve. It will demand persistence from you, but if you give this class what it asks for (i.e. daily homework and a couple of short projects) you will improve more than you thought you could. I'm so glad I took this course; I was on the fence, but having seen how excellently designed the syllabus is and benefitting from the enthusiasm from Amr and Ozzy, I would take this class all over again if I could. The expectations were totally reasonable but high.
Especially when we transition to Al–Kitaab part 2, the vocab ramps up significantly. I found myself having to memorize north of 50 words a chapter, which for me is quite a lot. Nonetheless it's highly rewarding.
