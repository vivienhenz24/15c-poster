What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

Comments
If you have not taken an undergrad–level statistics course, it may be helpful to learn some of that material before taking this course. Otherwise, reading textbooks to supplement lectures could be very helpful with motivating the methods you learn about in the course – there’s too much material to be covered for things to be built from ground up in this course.
I would say that if you're the type of person who wants to be that colleague to others who can rigorously defend their statistical methodological choices, this is a great course for you, especially if you want to be that colleague in a public health, epidemiological, or biostatistical (/medicine) setting.

This course probably requires a fair bit of motivation to keep at it — which isn't a bad thing if you have it! If you feel like the above describes you, and you can keep that in view while going through the class, I think you'll see why the material is important and how you will benefit from it.

I would say students should come into this class already motivated to learn the material, rather than looking to get motivation out of the class.

My view is that this course is all about simultaneously showing students the statistical software practices necessary to do rigorous statistics alongside its theoretical justification. That said, it leans pretty heavily towards the theory (that's most of what you see in class). So I wouldn't say you learn a ton of R programming from the class — just how to do what you need to, which is mostly fitting models, performing hypothesis tests, and not all the data cleaning/management that surrounds that.

It would be helpful to make sure you've brushed up on your R programming before going into this class. I would say this course expects that you should at least be able to write functions in R without stressing about it.

It's a good class! It's just more advanced than most people who are just interested in "statistics" or "data science" typically need. But if you want to see the theoretical justifications for the paradigms we've used in applied statistics for the last ~100 years, it's a fun whirlwind through the ideas.
Great class stick it out even though it meets at 8 am!!!
This course covers a lot of content. Brush up on your basic statistics concepts before enrolling, such as hypothesis tests and common distributions. Further, your linear algebra skills will come in handy. The assignments can be challenging, but you will learn a lot through the process of completing them. Just be sure that you have the time to dedicate to this course so that you can get the most out of it.
