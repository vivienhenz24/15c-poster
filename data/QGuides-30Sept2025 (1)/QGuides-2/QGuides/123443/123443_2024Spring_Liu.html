If you enjoyed Gov 50, you will enjoy Gov 51. 51 is a bit more focused on math and covers new methods including instrumental variables and text analysis. The switch to base R wasn't too challenging for me; I just spent an hour going through the R basics section of the textbook and was set. I have zero, I mean ZERO math proficiency. I may just be the least proficient in math on this campus. Absolutely no calculus––didn't know what a quadratic was. Yet I did well in the course. There are proofs, and I had to sit through some of them with no clue as to what was going on. But afterward, I went to office hours, had the essentials broken down for me, and was prepared for the midterm. You can do well in this course without a solid math foundation. I would say the more important thing is an R foundation, just because the midterm calls for pretty fast coding. I am really glad that I took this class; I've gained some very practical skills. Professor Liu is simply wonderful; very approachable and funny. She explains concepts clearly.
Take this class!
If you're interested in how you can use data in the social sciences this is an amazing class. Professor Liu always combined theory with coding exercises and the p–sets were on track with the material. I would say that you need to pace yourself for the midterm. There's a lot of content and not much time, but beyond that, there's always enough time to hand in p–sets and the final project. Would highly recommend it! The teaching team is also great.
This class is definitely challenging, especially prior to the midterm –– it relies heavily on understanding mathematical proofs and self–teaching new coding techniques. That said, the course itself is not very demanding with regard to workload and it is very possible to apply gov50 knowledge to quickly learning and understanding these new concepts. As someone with no R experience outside of gov50, I found the course approachable and doable, it just requires commitment.
If you liked GOV 50, GOV 51 is a great next step – you get to dig a little deeper into the basics of statistical analysis, start to explore adjacent and overlapping fields like machine learning, and build confidence in engaging with existing methodological literature. The training wheels do come off a little bit compared to GOV 50, but the teaching team is really supportive, and I found all of the assignments to be fair (as someone with no prior experience in data science outside of GOV 50).
Gov 51 is HARD. The workload is manageable enough because there arent a ton of psets but we didnt get a pset schedule in advance so planning my work was super difficult and when a pset dropped I immediately had to dedicate hours to it regardless of my other work.
Professor Liu and the teaching staff for this class truly make it a not so terrible experience. The proofs and the concepts can be scary but you really take a lot out of the class.
If you loved Gov 50, Gov 51 is probably for you. However, a warnings: the first week of class is very math–heavy and confusing. DO NOT get discouraged, the course becomes way easier. Naija is an amazing instructor. Lectures can be fast, but they cover way more content that tested in the course. I am not sure if the course staff will be the same, but Gov 51 has excellent course staff. Jeremiah is the actual GOAT, and the other TF, James and Jack, were very approachable and helpful. Whenever PSets were due, they would add extra office hours to help accommodate.
Take the course!!!! Naijia is great, psets are chill, the project is a lot of fun and very well paced! The content is also interesting and you'll never have a shortage of people to help you learn.
Great follow up to Gov 50, but the focus is more theoretical (i.e. focused on learning conceptual frameworks/methods to think about quantitative social sciences research, rather than just "how to code in R" like Gov 50 was). Definitely recommend for anyone interested in exploring quantitative social sciences research further. Grading is also incredibly fair!
I LOVED GOV51. I learned a lot about quantitative methods to apply to social science questions, and the difficult material was made very digestible. Don't get me wrong, the material was challenging and complex, but I found the class super interesting. This class actually made me reconsider what I wanted to study because I loved the topics/possibilities in this area. Naijia, Jeremiah, and the course assistants were beyond helpful and dedicated to making sure you have the tools to understanding the concepts and succeed. The course staff definitely made the class great. The problem sets were difficult for me, but I was able to complete them by going to office hours and collaborating with peers. One thing to note is that the distribution of work is not very consistent: some weeks I would do 0 work and other weeks (like when we had a problem set, exam, or project) I would do many hours of work. However, the work is very manageable *especially if you go to office hours*. I also found the final project very interesting, though it was a bit difficult to apply what we learned. Although I don't feel like I have a super strong grasp of R, exposure to coding in R will surely be beneficial if I ever need to use statistical coding again (which I likely will because this class helped me realize that I want to pursue more quantitative social science or related field). HIGHLY RECOMMEND if you have any interest in applied statistics or working with data (even if you're not a Gov concentrator – I am not).
I enjoyed it! I think if you want a genuine understanding of statistical topics applicable to social studies, this class is a must. GOV 50 was good but GOV 51 is a necessary step if you want to do quantitative research. I will say the class is definitely not easy (BIG step up from GOV 50) and you will have to work to understand the content but if you are able to put the work in, its doable to get good grades.

Honestly, just take the class for the staff, theyre all so lovely and the class will help you with your stats background a lot and set you up for the data science track well.

Naijia is so kind and patient, and Jeremiah is a wonderful section leader who explains things really clearly and always makes time for us at office hours.
