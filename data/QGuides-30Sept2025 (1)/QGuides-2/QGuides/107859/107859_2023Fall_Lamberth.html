I highly recommend this class if you are just genuinely interested in learning about the history of early Christianity! You do not have to have any experience with Christianity in the past to be prepared for the course. The readings from the Bible and writers like St. Augustine, Lisa Cahill, Martin Luther, St. Benedict, and James Cone are fascinating and many can help you gain a deeper understanding of the central role that the incarnation of Christ, the process by which God took on human flesh for the salvation of humanity through Jesus Christ, plays in Christianity. The discussion posts and readings every week are not stressful at all and the class is very discussion based with Professor Lamberth giving a short lecture in the beginning of class. There are only 3 major projects, the Visiting Churches essay, the art project, and the final project, which all allow you to look more into a Church, tradition, or reading that is most interesting to you. Professor Lamberth is a great lecturer, discussion moderator, and truly cares about her students and their wellbeing!
This course was really one of the highlights of my semester. The course does a great job balancing many things that you might want from an introductory course. In terms of New Testament thought/history, we get fair/detailed treatment of theological/theoretical perspective through the gospels, epistles, and early Patristic theologies. In terms of history, we get a considerable overview from the early Church, heresies, schisms, and the Protestant Reformation into America. Notable too is the time spent on more modern reception ––– Kant, feminism, liberation theology, etc. In terms of methodology/medium, while most of the course is certainly focused on texts, there's considerable exploration beyond philosophy/theology and into art, poetry, personal narrative, etc. Even with the preconceptions of what I thought I wanted from this course (specific interests on aspects of history/theology) and what I thought I already "knew," I learned so much in the variety of new topics as well as in a deepened and changed understanding of areas I thought I knew.

The format of the course I think is well–balanced with a variety of readings, a chance for personal reflection with Canvas responses, chance to reflect in class, but also (most appreciated at least in my perspective) a really thoughtful and quite detailed mini–lecture of the concepts introduced in our readings at the beginning of class. This was honestly really helpful in so many ways ––– whether it was explaining certain doctrines of law and sin, the particular context of monastic hierarchies, or just what on earth was being really argued in regards to heresy. Briefly, too, on some logistics ––– Professor Lamberth is just an amazing person to get to know and makes the classroom experience wonderful. The course never becomes overbearing, either, with grading or workload and just allows you to focus on and enjoy the content of the course.

If you're looking for a course that delves really deeply into literary analysis of the Bible or a more theological side to interpreting what is or is not said in the Bible, I think you might be a little let down ––– something like ANE 120A might be more of that exegetical/analytic stuff you're looking for. What this course instead offers, which I think is just as important, is a really informative overview of different ways we can understand the New Testament ––– whether that be identifying some core literary themes of the Scripture and early texts, tracking themes of debate and argumentation within the Church, or studying the changes in modern reception to the New Testament. Even if the course isn't a theological Bible study of the text or a debate–driven course on what the New Testament does or doesn't say about modern ethical issues, this course is wonderful for equipping you with a lot of critique yet respect for the tradition. I walked away realizing how much more complicated and diverse the Christian tradition is, and I think that students find this regardless of whether they are students of religion, people of personal belief in the religion, agnostic or atheistic skeptics of the religion, etc.
