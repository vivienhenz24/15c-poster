What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

Comments
Take this class with reservations! I'm sure there will be tons of positive feedback for the class, and it is WELL–deserved. If you are truly enthused about south Asia, or interested in writing a thesis on south asian art, this is the class for you. It is jam–packed with information, but also with resources, which can feel a bit much for someone with a slight interest. I wish I had known that before taking it.

There's a lot of graduate students who will take the course too if that gives you an idea about its level of commitment. The professor is really really sweet though and a really wonderful instructor, but I did get the vibe among the undergrads that not all of us were engaged in the class just because it is so intense. (There were also a few of those type of folks who are *too* enthused about the culture that they don't come from, so beware.) I also didn't like that there was an expectation to show up so much, and while I was interested in the class, I wish the staff were more understanding of the other commitments we have. I also felt like we were sorta expected to know what to do with the assignments when the instructions were often vague and it could be difficult to get direction on what to do. It also felt like we were expected to have really great assignments and a final project when there wasn't enough guidance from the staff.
This is a really good class. At the beginning, the amount of information getting thrown at you seems super overwhelming, but by the end, you actually will be able to identity different gods and myths through simple symbols. That progression is really cool. The readings can be very long and dense, which is a little bit difficult, but if you pay some attention during lectures and listen during section, you'll be fine
Be prepared to listen in class! It can be rewarding if you understand the stories behind the artwork, and don't get frustrated if you can't pick it up at first –– you'll get there!
Don't hesitate to reach out to our professor or TF! They are super nice and available!
What you get out of this course is very strongly dependent on what you put into it. While that may sound trite or cliched, I've never felt it holds more true than for this class. It is admittedly possible to get by reading very little and only marginally participating in lecture, but doing so would be a waste. If you do the readings and engage thoughtfully with Professor Kim you will find this course to be an incredibly enriching and meaningful experience.
Even if you come with some prior knowledge about South Asian (especially Hindu and Buddhist Art), Professor Kim ensures that you will always leave the class having learnt something new and she will always have an interesting story to help you understand it better!
This course is interesting, but the lectures can be disorganized and hard to follow. Since the class is covering a big geographic area and has a big timeline, the lectures aren't necessarily best set in a way to comprehend the geographic and temporal scale of the content as well as visual patterns of Buddhism and Hinduism. Prof. Kim and Tori are super nice and available though, so you can always follow up with them. The assignments are hard to set a time for how long it will take to complete them, so plan for that.
The final project is very tedious, incredibly time consuming, and a large portion of your grade, so start early and check in with the TF / professor about it throughout the process. While lectures aren't the most engaging, the weekly quizzes keep you accountable.
This was my favorite course, hands down, this semester. Professor Kim's ability to explain such complicated material and to make it accessible to everyone is truly incredible.
