This class is the second half of Griffiths, and no, it's not very fun. In my opinion, it was the least interesting parts of 143a but just... more. I never had a moment like in 143a where I went "wow, it's crazy that's how nature works"–––the novelty of quantum mechanics wore off pretty quickly. If you ever need to use perturbation theory, I think you would be served just as well reading the results from Griffiths as you would spending a semester in 143b.

But, that's not to say the content won't be useful and interesting for somebody else. Maybe you're the type of person who enjoys grinding out integrals to see a correction six orders of magnitude smaller than the estimate you made in 143a. If you want to learn how to approximate the effects of weak physical interactions on a quantum system, then this class is perfect for you. Personally, though, I thought it was pretty boring and niche.

Professor Paban is a good instructor and her exams are more than fair. The difficult content is reserved for psets; everything on exams is straightforward as long as you understand the material. I know this makes me sound like a total dweeb, but this was the easiest physics class I've taken; granted, not because this is easy, it's just easier than the other classes here. I selected recommend with reservations because you'll probably do well in the class, but it's really not all that interesting or enjoyable.

This class does exactly what it says on the cover. Quantum II is a class on applications and approximations, not necessarily specifically on the theory of QM, although you will certainly gain a better appreciation of the theory anyways. I would like to clarify that by "theory," I mean representation theory, no–go theorems, etc. The class is much like Quantum I in that you're given a bunch of new potentials and have to solve the Schrodinger equation, but the potentials are much more complex and need new methods to solve them. As the potentials are more complicated, they approach more realistic models of things that you might see in a lab or life in general (in comparison to the particle in a box). The material was interesting, the professor was excellent and understanding, the course staff was great, overall an amazing course that I learned a lot in and am happy I took. Highly recommend.

A tip if you do take this class: be ready for the exams (duh lol). The grading scheme places a lot of weight on the exams while also granting an override for the final, meaning that a higher score on the final will replace one or both of your midterm grades. This is great as there's a lot of leeway if you mess up on a midterm, but it's also not fun going into a final that can theoretically count for upwards of 50% of your total class grade. The exams are quite reasonable though, so as long as you are reasonably prepared, you should be fine.
