Remarks:

Let's be honest, although I will try to be objective, my experience will be not representative of the average student taking the course. So take my experience with a grain of salt since I had done a lot of this before.

This course had a very interesting way of encouraging attendance. For each lecture/lab you attend you get a Pavlos Coin, 8 of which can be redeemed for a late day! More points awarded when you answer a question that Pavlos asked, though these weren't that common. Beyond that, the course is very generous. 1/3 of quizzes are dropped. execises only count if they would improve your grade. Beware, this laxness comes at a psychological cost! This class will take lower priority among your classes in any time crunch you have because they have set up the class in that way (surely deliberate on their part).

Pavlos' lectures were very engaging and he is generally a very fun person to be around (Please understand that even though I will refer to the professors with their first name, it is because I am avoiding having to say Professor xx all the time. I respect them a lot). He is certainly the good cop to Natesh' bad cop in class. He will, for example, start the class at 9 but he will perform a recap for the first couple minutes because he wants people to be able to come in without missing anything. Very nice of him. He certainly doesn't mind students not showing up (they made the attendance optional after all). In contrast, even though Natesh is brilliant, he put me off in certain occasions. For example, there was one rare day when I had made the trek from the Quad for the 9AM class to the SC and what Natesh tells the class as one of his first sentences is something like "So, we have been able to wake up for the class after all" (not targeting anyone in particular but still). This was very unnecessary and uncalled for since we were the people IN the class. Certainly if he wants to blame anyone, shouldn't he be showing disapproval to the students who didn't come in for class :D Certainly the students who don't show up know the consequences of their actions.

Being a great lecturer involves more things than just knowing and transmitting the material. Pavlos would include many memes and interesting quizzes etc to break up the monotony of the class. Making the lecture engaging and fun with gimmicks, although certainly fluff, make it much more enjoyable. I don't have data on this, but I wouldn't be surprised if attendance for Natesh's lectures was lower than for Pavlos. I personally didn't show up in general and watched the lectures more often than not in general. Perhaps others' views on this would be more valuable.

I think the quiz system is actually a really great thing. really made me go through material again and make sure I got it. Some of the quiz questions were very gimmes though. The issue sometimes was that you just forget to do them. Thankfully there is the 1/3 removal policy.

You know what I really liked: that we had to make steady progress towards our final project. Let me tell you, I am a heavy procrastinator. It was to have the course force me into doing the milestone on time.

OK, time to show some love to Chris. Absolutely legendary Preceptor. Up there with the likes of Kitty Ascrizzi. If you know who she is, you know who I am comparing Chris to. He was always there on Ed. He was there on Office hours that weren't his. He had an aura of calmness around him and you could just work while he was talking to the TF and you would just become more aware of everything that you were doing and suddenly start finding solutions to your problems. He is a D&D Paladin with an Aura of Courage feature

I heavily recommend getting python locally and getting used to using a virtual environment. Use venv or your favourite virtual environment creator and thank me later. create a requirements.txt file and use that for each of the psets.

Some Areas that the course could improve upon:

Due to suspected cheating in the coding portion of the exam (some people collaborated or else used LLMs...), the course changed its policy and reduced the weighting of the coding portion of the midterms. I disagree with this policy change.
When a syllabus is published, I consider that as the constitution of that class. Attendance policy, what will be the grading mix etc. So, it unfair to change the policy midway for everyone since I signed up for one constitution and then a coup is changing the constitution midway. Such changes should definitely not be happening after the open add–drop deadline since it just philosophically feels wrong. Let's be honest, the coding portion was my strongest section because I had a lot of experience. But because of actions of some people, my coding portion also got reduced vis–a–vis my quiz portion. The solution is simple, you deal with the students who cheated and not make a sweeping change. The course didn't even change their policy for the second midterm! So the same things could have happened (and probably did) in the second midterm too.

The first pset on web scraping was, in my opinion way more tedious and hard than originally expected. A couple issues with the website chosen to be scraped was that it had very few Internet Archive records. Thus, creating a big dataset was harder. Morevoer, at first we weren't told that the website archives would have such big gaps. So, for a long time, I thought I was messing up my code and wasn't finding relevant dates etc. Moreover, some of the Internet Archive records had a different HTML structure and thus my code did not work on them. These things happen in normal web scraping, but the issue is that we didn't have a conception how serious we needed to be etc.

Final thoughts:

Overall, a pretty solid A tier class with great support structure. Very nice for beginners though certainly has its hard moments. However, if you've taken 181 and similar data science classes, maybe take a different class since you'll cover pretty similar things.
Good luck!

0. The psets are time–consuming. Whilst true in that they get easier as the semester goes on, they are still a significant time commitment. 1. The course is fast–paced for sure. Understanding the topics completely is a demanding task, and one where one must spend significant time dedicated to the material.
