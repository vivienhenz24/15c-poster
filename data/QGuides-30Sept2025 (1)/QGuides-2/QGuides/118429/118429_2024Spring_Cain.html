This was a good class! Wes is a great lecturer and is very helpful and accessible outside of class. The amount of weekly work can sometimes feel a bit high as there was always either a problem set or a takehome exam due each week, but it kept me on top of the material. Otherwise, the material is taught well and content builds upon itself effectively; homeworks and exams are kept at a reasonable level.
Wes is an amazing professor. He had great lectures and is very supportive of his students. My only proof writing background was 22A, and I felt sufficiently prepared to take this course. There were two take–home midterms, but they were essentially problem sets that you couldn't go to office hours for and were very doable. Overall, I feel like I learned a lot and if you are thinking of taking this class, I would definitely recommend.
This class is not as easy as previous years may suggest! With the recent Professor change, this class has definitely stepped up in difficulty and because of this it lacked some structure this last semester (CAs gave up and stopped contributing).

That being said, Professor Cain is an outstanding lecturer who encourages each and every one of his students to buy in and learn the material properly. Lecture component of this course is fast paced and at times draining, but it is likely the only way youll truly understand whats going on due to his enthusiasm and passion for the topics.

Problem sets are take home, and this semester we also had two take home midterms and a take home final with a small oral component. Though the problem sets are tough, we were encouraged to collaborate with our classmates on them which was absolutely essential, however this is not allowed on the tests so going to lecture is still important.

Overall great math class, would be fantastic if it had more structure and resources.
A solid class and not very difficult, although not one of the more essential classes for a math concentrator.
This particular iteration by Wes Cain was good but not particularly great.

Good stuff: it's interesting material, and Wes is a good instructor. He cares about students' understanding. The exams were fair, and so were the HWs.

Meh stuff: The p–sets were long and grindy and, frankly, often not very fun to do; it didn't help that they had a weird Wednesday noon deadline and Wes is super strict about deadlines. Awesome class with Wes! He isn't teaching it next year, and I know the material can change a lot depending on who teaches, though. We focused a lot on flows (like 3/4 of the course) and less on discrete dynamics, but we did get to chaos at the end. Wes' passion for the material was very clear, and I think this a reasonable pset class. You will be challenged, and the material isn't trivial by any means, but I think its a great class to take after multi and linear if you like math that lives in the grey zone between pure and applied.
Classic Wes Cain course, long and very demanding but well taught. You have to prioritise it in your schedule though. Wittmann, Dora and Nico were incredible TFs and the class would have been unbearable without them.
Time per week:
9.5, 4, 7.5, 7.5, 10, 5.5, 3, 12, 1, 6.5, 3.5, 5.5, 9, 2.5, 9

Total 93 hours. Roughly 7 hours per week.

Wes wrote the textbook we use for the class. He is an expert in the field.

The textbook can be slightly hard to read, and I find the CA live–typed lecture notes to be quite useful. Of course, the best notes are the notes you take for yourself, especially if you draw out the diagrams on the black boards (the CA notes most of the times don't have diagrams). However, I find writing notes in class very distracting to parsing the material. Try to find a balance between your attention in lectures and a way of recording the diagrams.

For many parts of the courses, Wes focused his lectures on presenting examples, which are very useful for building intuitions for the theorems and definitions. Keep this in mind because this won't be a class full of proofs. Some proofs are presented, but most are not. Some people, me included, kinda like this approach since we get to focus on the big picture and also only proofs that are enlightening, while taking the proofs for certain theorems for granted (Wes will say those proofs are boring, and he will point the class to a reference for the proof).

Psets are ok. They are mostly about applying or verifying that certain theorems hold. Sometimes it can feel brain–dead carrying out computations and verifications. However, there are these "Group 2" problems for extra credit and requires a proof–based approach and more insight. I think this is a great structure.

Exams are very doable if you have been following along in lectures and psets.

Definitely among the easy side of math classes, but you are still required to put in some work.

The material is quite interesting, especially on bifurcations and given how they relate to physical systems. You also get a treatment of chaos at the end of the semester. I hope Wes got another week to talk about it.
I would recommend this class wholeheartedly. Moreover, I would recommend any class that Professor Cain teaches. The material is incredibly interesting, and you will enjoy it! Take this class!

2. Don't buy the textbook. It pains me to say it, but it's illegible. You won't need it, and I mean that.
Take Wes. He is a passionate lecturer and cares a tremendous amount about his students. I came into this class with no formal proof background (just 21a/b) and I met with Wes at the start of the semester who reassured me that I would be OK. He was right. Moreover, I had a spectacular time psetting with all of my new math friends. I feel like this class expanded my brain.
This was realistically a brutal class. Wes Cain taught it this year, and I would probably not recommend this class with Wes teaching. Wes is an excellent teacher and he clearly cares about his students, but as many students describe him he is somewhat of a hardo. The PSETs are unnecessarily long and time–consuming with annoying calculations. In terms of getting a strong grade, if you have a solid background in math then you can for sure get an A if you obviously take the necessary time on the PSETs. Wes also wants to encourage rigorous mathematics, as people who have only taken the Math 21 series can take this class. You have to get 20 Group 2 points throughout the semester (so averaging roughly 2 a PSET), and you can get extra credit from going over 20 (maxing out at 28). The class also has 2 midterms and a final. They vary in difficulty, but they are certainly fair. They are all open–note, take–home & unlimited time for a week. They are structured almost like a PSET. The final was definitely more challenging than the two midterms. The course heads also took latex notes, which was very helpful (and so attending class was not a necessity). Wes also wrote the textbook we used in this class. I thought it was solid and could be helpful, but others were not huge fans of it. Wess explanations in class are definitely more clear than the textbook.
