I think I was not exactly the target audience for this class. And I still quite liked it, so that's good.

One discrepancy was that I never was fully convinced by the focus on cities. If you're considering the class and "city fiction" seems like an arbitrary delineation of content, that feeling probably won't ever be resolved. It doesn't help that the selections don't always motivate the focus on cities. Cole's Open City is more about Brussels and Handel references than New York. Parasite is not clearly about Seoul at all, except insofar as that is a city with terrible wealth inequality. Having New York and London as two of only four cities on a supposedly global syllabus feels criminal.

I also didn't love lecture as much as the rest of class seemed to. Some of Tara's arguments were super interesting and provocative. Others felt like strawmen, or were just swamped by only mildly interesting contextual details (e.g. author biography). There is an emphasis on interactivity in lectures, but I only occasionally felt inspired to participate, and I got tired of the jokes about how much we needed coffee every lecture when folks weren't stepping up.

Finally I wanted more literature analysis from an English class. Though we wrote little ~150 word musings every week, we were never asked to articulate a fleshed out argument about a book. Section discussions were pretty superficial. I think a book review or short traditional paper would have made for good practice.

In conclusion, I had fun with this class, but I'm not sure how much I learned or grew. Take this course if you're especially excited about cities, or if you want a very chill foray into reading and thinking about books. Consider looking elsewhere if you're hoping to be really pushed in your reading and writing skills.
