This is pretty much a gem for a GenEd. Yes, there are weekly assignments, but there are no sit–in midterms or finals, which can help balance out your other classes. If you are interested in archaeology, this is an interesting course to take.
If you try even a little bit you will like this class. The teaching staff is so nice (they make and bring food to class often and its actually good) and they put in so much effort, as long as you just try you will get a good grade. Skim the readings, submit the weekly assignments on time, and you will be fine.
This class is more of a history / archaeology class than anything. The lectures are super dull, but you should go and pay attention because the midterm take–home exams involve a lot of content from the lectures, and the slides aren't the most helpful since they contain minimal information and instead Prof. Carballo says things not on the slides. You can definitely get by without doing readings for lectures, but you'll have to do the readings for the weekly assignments. It's a pretty easy class up until the end, since you have take home exam 2 and then the final project/paper/presentation hiding behind it while the rest of your classes' finals are ramping up too, so be prepared for the final hill at the end.

As a STEM major, I really enjoyed taking this class and feel that I really learned a lot about archaeology in general, as well as a broad overview of Mexican cuisine from past to present. You learn a lot about how archaeology is done and the history behind different regions and historical cities. The readings are helpful for the exams, but you can survive by just skimming the articles to get the main ideas. I also really enjoyed the final project as it allowed me to combine my interests in exciting ways. This class is very reasonable to manage alongside more difficult courses.
This class is a very good, low–stress choice for a Gened! Trust me, if you are trying to take a Gened that fills this bucket, I would take this class if it is an option. I was a little nervous going in since this class was ranked on the lower side of Geneds last year, but it is definitely a good class––not a slam–dunk 5–star class but definitely solid. All of the writing assessments are purely fact–driven, so that means you need to pay close attention and take good notes in lecture, but there is literally 0 analysis required and good notes should make the midterm assignments easy. I also wrote the final paper in less than 48 hours so don't stress about that either. With regard to content, I found the archaeology component of this class particularly interesting, especially when we got to touch and explore artifacts in the museum. It was also great to try Mexican foods throughout the semester, especially when I didn't hav enough time to eat lunch:) The lectures are REALLY BORING, but if you can put up with that for 2 hours a week, I would recommend it.
If you are having a rough semester with rigorous classes and need a chill gened, this is the one. All the tests are take–home and the final project is actually fun to complete. Go to class, go to section, submit all assignments and you should be surprised not to get an A.
While this class covered interesting material, the presentation of the material in lecture often felt unstructured and somewhat unfocused. It was tricky to determine what to focus on and remember, especially with minimal text on the slides and no additional lecture notes. Weekly assignments were a bit more cumbersome than some other GenEds Ive taken, and the combo of two written midterms plus final capstone project (in–class presentation and 6–8pg research paper on a topic of your choice) was a bit much. As a thesising senior studying biology, I took this class pass–fail and I think thats probably the way to go.

This course, however, focused on archeology much more than food and was taught in a very boring way that would have only appealed to people who wanted to pursue archaeology/anthropology. Professor Carballo is an extremely nice person, but her lectures were very boring. This class is truly very reasonable and a really enjoyable way of fulfilling a gened requirement. Prof Carballo sometimes brings in food to lecture, which is always great. The assignments are fair and you are able to get feedback from the teaching staff at any time, which was also a really helpful thing. Section is not the most interesting, but it's not a big lift at all. The first midterm assignment asks you to write two essays, and the second is a take home exam, though it similarly includes an essay prompt. The final capstone is a research paper about something of your choice. There are also weekly short assignments, but they don't take long and are a good, low stakes way of maintaining engagement in the course. Overall, a great gened, and really interesting even if you have no background in archaeology, food, etc.
