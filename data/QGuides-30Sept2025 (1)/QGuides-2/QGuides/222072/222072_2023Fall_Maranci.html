Whoever you are TAKE THIS CLASS! Let's first start out with Professor Maranci. She is such an incredible, engaging lecturer. She made all of the lectures so fun, incorporating personal stories, anecdotes, photos, guests, references to the most famous Armenians today (the Kardashians), etc. Although at first, the topic of this class may sound niche (it sounded this way too to me at first when I shopped it), there truly is something in this class for everyone and the material is so, so important to learn. Although nominally this class is about Armenia, it really is about so much more.

Armenian culture has been "in–between" throughout its entire existence. That means that sometimes, it aligned more with the classical world, other times with the Islamic world, etc. So by studying the history of art and architecture in Armenia, you get to learn so much about many diverse cultures that have existed across the globe. With the conflict in Nagorno–Karrabakh this year, this class feels even more important for students to take right now. It's a class where you can learn about art, architecture, Armenian Christianity, diaspora, genocide, modern geopolitical conflict, cultural preservation, museum curation, and so much more.

Most of all, this class uses Armenia to exemplify the biases that exist in traditional scholarship. Why do we study the Greek and Roman Empires, the Islamic World, the Persian Empire, but not the Armenian world? We spent a lot of the class marveling at incredible works of Armenian cultural production that virtually no one knows about and wondered why no one studies them. Really, (and I can't stress this enough) no one studies them. Realizing this was both shocking and inspiring. I think it made all the students in this class fall in love with Armenian culture and recognize the importance of protecting it and learning about it. I think it's an interesting field for students with an interest in history, art, architecture, etc. to get involved in because there is so much room to do novel, path–breaking scholarship. Above all else, this feels like such an important class for people to take if the only thing you get out of it is to think deeply about why we study some things and not other. Who chooses? What happens to the cultures we don't study? Do they get lost? How can we protect them? Why should we protect them? How can we challenge the lines of traditional scholarship?

Not to mention, the class has a relatively low workload, and the course staff was always super flexible. Nothing feels like busy work. It was all genuinely interesting work, and even though this class couldn't be further from my major, I was always so excited to go to class. It had a bunch of guest lectures, and we also took trips to the Harvard Art Museum, a local Armenian Church, and the Armenian Museum of America.
This course is phenomenal, especially as a GenEd course, because it engages with a pressing issue (how do we learn about and represent cultures that are in–between or less talked about historically), teaches you so so so so so much (Professor Maranci is at the top of her field and is one of the nicest and most passionate people you will ever meet, and she will teach you so much about both Armenia and art history), and the workload outside of lecture is fairly light. The course teaches you not only about Armenian art, but also about art from the Byzantine empire, Islamic empires, and so many other cultures because of the fact that Armenia has been so "in–between".
