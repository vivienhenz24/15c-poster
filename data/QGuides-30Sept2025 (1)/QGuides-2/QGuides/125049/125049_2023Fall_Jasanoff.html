This was a great class, and you learn a lot about the British Empire's impact on our global past, present, and feature. I learned a lot and enjoyed the content – as someone from a non–Commonwealth country, we aren't taught to view the world through the lens of British imperialism, so UK/Commonwealth legacies were a fascinating new framework for me to analyze the world through. Lectures in particular were especially engaging. I didn't feel like they were biased, or taught from a particular moralizing point of view**. (**One exception, however, was a brief aside before the lecture immediately after Oct. 7, 2023 on the topic of "contextualizing history." I found this rhetorically questionable and a little tone–deaf given its immediate proximity to that date, but this qualm may be outside the scope of this evaluation.)

However, during office hours and during sections, there were some interpersonal quirks/problems with both the professor and the teaching fellow team that I would caution any potential enrollee about. It pains me to say this, since as a woman I recognize that female teaching staff are sometimes held to higher standards than their male counterparts, but the attitudes held by class leadership often seemed dismissive, smug, and patronizing; examples are as follows. During office hours, the professor would smirk if I made factual or rhetorical errors while speaking on a subject, and at one point seemed humored (not supportive/concerned) when I tried to speak to her about/broach an insecurity I had regardless my less than–stellar pedagogical background prior to Harvard. During lecture, one particularly egregious TF always seemed annoyed/dismissive when speaking to students, and carried herself with an air of "why are you approaching me outside section?" (it didn't help that all mentions of her email were omitted from Canvas/the syllabus). I recently asked other students if they felt the same way about the attitudes of course staff, and found out that many agreed with my assessment – strangely, it's relieving to know that I wasn't hallucinating their haughtiness after all.

In summary, course content was amazing and I have learned a lot of historical/personal/factual lessons with regards to both the successes and follies of the British Empire, and I'll take these lessons with me long after this semester is over. All things considered, I would highly recommend this course (I chose "Recommend with Enthusiasm" in the Q Guide). This endorsement holds true even though the attitudes/dispositions of the teaching staff appeared influenced by and mirrored the very ideological arrogance, elitism, and disdain cautioned against throughout the course materialan ironic parallel.
