Comments
Graduate class, discussion based, so would recommend if you have background/experience on history of economics/economic thought, and/or the economic history of a region (eg economic history of US/China/UK etc.) – this doesn't really do the canon and moves beyond it to more interesting themes. but as the class crowdsources the readings for most of the weeks, it is highly dependent on your classmates. there is a TON of readings so helpful if you are skilled at reading quickly. it is a graduate class so some of the concepts discussed are pretty high level and abstract. overall recommend but only if you have the background to contribute in discussion
Take this if you do modern stuff. If you don't do modern and don't have interest in finance, it might not be all you hope for.
This is an incredibly interesting seminar with great material on economic history. You will get much more out of the class if you do the readings.
Interesting topics but remember it is a proseminar so you won't hear much from the professors
There was a lot of thought–provoking content, but I had to structure it in a way that made sense to myself. Take it if you are comfortable in diffuse, more hands–off settings with both undergraduate and graduate students and enjoy that multi–dimensionality.
Take this class if any of the topics (Hist or Econ) interest you... I think econ students should def take it. It really broadens concepts, or shows econ theory in practice (both its possibilities and failures).
This class is great. I would strongly recommend taking it for the readings (which are generated by the students) and being able to hear Prof Ghosh's and Rothschild's perspectives on the the history of economic thought.
