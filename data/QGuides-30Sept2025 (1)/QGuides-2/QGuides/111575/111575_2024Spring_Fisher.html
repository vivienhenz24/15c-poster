Unlike many other English classes, this course feels like a "traditional" English class that focuses on literary movements at large rather than close reading texts in a vacuum. I knew I would love this course because I love to read, but it changed me in ways I didn't expect refining my appreciation for and understanding of novels I would've found boring and unlikable a few years ago. I am grateful for the novels I've been exposed to and several of them have become new favorites (ones I'd never expect to become favorites). I usually live and die by the Q–guide but I'd take a totally new course by Professor Fisher because I trust him with my life.

A great course. Prof. Fisher is brilliant, knowledgeable and simply a pleasure to listen to. As for the books: the early works are interesting, alternating between simpler novels and more complex and masterful ones. Lolita is, by all standards, brilliant please read it slowly, Nabokov isn't writing for the college kid who's cramming Part 1 at 11.30pm in before his Monday lecture. Same goes for The Sound and the Fury, though I don't think it's as hard as Nabokov once you get past the first two avant–gardiste chapters. The three contemporary novels at the end (Catcher in the Rye, Housekeeping, and Waiting) were also great reads.
There are two papers, and a shorter reflective final exam: not very hard to do well on, but that might be because my TF just didn't know much about English literature, and the smarter the paper sounded the better the grade. Section was mid a lot of nonsense, sounded as if most of the class had just not attended lecture.
Final point: JUST TAKE A COURSE WITH FISHER.
