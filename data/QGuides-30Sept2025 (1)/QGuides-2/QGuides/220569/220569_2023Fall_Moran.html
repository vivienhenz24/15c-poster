What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

Comments
Bernard Williams' thought is supremely illuminating, and his objections to the modern philosophical enterprise are very worthwhile to consider for anyone thinking about questions in moral philosophy. He succinctly and helpfully articulates many first–order questions about philosophy that I have often considered that are often otherwise dismissed. Unfortunately, Williams' thinking can be quite opaque, and the discussions in this seminar meander rather than help clarify many points.
The course materials are amazing and will make you think about ethics differently, as well as life generally. However, if you are at all interested in ethics or moral philosophy, Williams is extremely influential, so you ought to take a class on him at some point. Further, Williams' arguments are sometimes actually true (which is a very rare occurrence in ethics!). And, even if you aren't interested in ethics, Williams sometimes even makes valuable points about life in general, if that can be believed.

The class itself can be a little unwieldy and sometimes anarchic, owing to the fact that it is a mostly discussion–based class. As in all discussion–based classes, the class improves with time.

The notes on the readings are also very good and often necessary to understand Williams' often difficult prose.
Bernard Williams is a strange thinker; disliking ethical systems and theories, his philosophical project is mostly centered around commentary on various other thinkers' approaches. This makes reading Williams a bit different from reading, say, Kant, but once you are used to him, he asks many important questions. While you may not always like his answers, reading Williams will teach you to be a better thinker and critic of philosophy.

Prof. Moran is friendly, approachable, and has a wonderful dry sense of humor. He has a very different approach to philosophy than others I've encountered; he is far less concerned with doing a rigorous reconstruction all of the time and far more interested in getting at and exploring what an argument is really saying. While each approach has its advantages and disadvantages, it is wonderful to experience the latter as someone not previously exposed. His essay prompts reflect that, asking a plethora of questions and opening the assignments up in a variety of directions, giving you a fair deal of freedom to explore interesting strands in Williams' work. I'm thankful to have taken a class with him.
