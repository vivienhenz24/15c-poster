CS1090B is a must–take course in the CS department. The course is more of a foundational and practical implementation of AI theme than data science. This is a feature of the course I truly appreciated; I leave CS1090B understanding the current capabilities and developments of AI, how to build my own and fine tune AI models, and the future trajectory of the technology, for which I am grateful.

The teaching staff is wonderful. I already thought they were after CS1090A, yet this course reinforced and more this sentiment. Pavlos is such an effective, amicable, engaging lecturer who one can tell is passionate about the material. Chris is such a genuine and hard–working Head TF; he puts in an incredible amount of work behind the scenes to keep the course well–functioning. The solution to the cluster issues before the MT2 coding portion was genius and goes to show his character. Luis is a GENIUS! One of the best TFs I have ever had. He is vastly knowledgeable on the subject matter, and I would often stay the full length of his office hours, asking tons of clarifying questions and learning from him. A great, down–to–earth guy as well.

The support system in this course is phenomenal. There is constant monitoring of Ed discussion post, and a wide array of office hours throughout the week. You will always have any question answered relatively soon. There is also the fact that this is a mixed class between undergrads and grad students –– it is quite cool to interact with grad school students and collaborate with them.

The workload is heavy. This was my most demanding class of the semester, around 10 to 18 hours spread across two weeks (bi–weekly psets). In general, you get out what you put into the course. You can choose to ChatGPT the entirety of your code for the pset, or you can critically think about the pset, put pen to iPad, and then prompt ChatGPT for code. I think the benefits of the latter far outweigh the cost of a few more hours of work.

The one caveat I will mention about this is that the psets were relatively difficult for someone who does not have a strong coding background (i.e. advanced understanding of functional/OOP, understanding of functional libraries like Keras). It is one thing to learn the theory behind AI, it is another to actually code models from scratch. I also believe that the Psets should have more efficient direction in terms of what code/methods/attributes to use to solve a problem. On various psets, many of the students in this course would run into attribute errors, run time errors, etc, because they were not using a specific line of code/method/attribute etc. Yet, this proper code was never explicitly mentioned in the pset directions to use. Oftentimes, one would figure out what code was needed by looking at another student's post on Ed. For instance, in pset 3, the masking pset, there was an error with specifying the loss function. The problem hint stated to use sparse categorical, however, if we used the general solution for what we had done in lab for sparse categorical loss (loss = 'sparse_categor...), we would have gotten the wrong answer. Instead, we had to use tf.keras.losses.loss('sparse_categor...'). The way one found out about this was via Ed post from one of our class mates. There were many other cases of this happening; the problem hint states the wrong steps to solution, and the real steps to the solution are posted on Ed.

TLDR You should take this class because you want to learn about AI, grind through the psets, and stay for the great community and support.
