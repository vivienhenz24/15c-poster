What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

Table for [QTitle].
Comments
Read the textbooks, start working on assignments early, do not expect to learn in lectures.
I found Prof. Capassos insights into the wider context of the topics presented in class extremely valuable. These insights are the type of thing you cannot get from a textbook.
You can learn a lot of quantum mechanics from this class, but you'll need to put a lot of time and effort into it (which is not bad)
If you've taken a physics undergrad QM course, please take physics251a/b instead of this course. I found this course to no more rigorous––in fact, even easier––than the QM courses I took in undergrad. This course basically covers scattered topics in Griffiths but at a pretty shallow level. It teaches some applications like band structure, rotating wave approximation, quantum info, etc., but I found those to be rather pointless since many people plan to take solid–state, AMO, and QI anyways, which cover those topics more thoroughly. By taking this course instead of a more rigorous grad QM course, I felt underprepared for the other course I took this semester and future physics courses. PSETs and exams are easy but often algebraically tedious, which made them just busywork. The grading also made me feel like I had gone back to high school after graduating undergrad––it's been a hot sec since I've seen points being taken off for not showing enough work even though my answer was correct and I understood what I was doing. But to be fair, the course assignments/sections are very pedagogical and probably useful if you don't have a solid undergrad QM background. And the easier, biweekly PSETs allow for more time spent on research (unlike physics251a where PSETs are weekly).

You're required to take this course if you're in QSE, but I would suggest stalling until your later years or until this requirement is relaxed (probably unlikely though).
