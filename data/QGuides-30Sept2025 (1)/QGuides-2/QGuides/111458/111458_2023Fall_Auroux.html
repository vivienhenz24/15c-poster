I came into this course having taken the 25 series as a freshman. The first half of the course I found to be very easy, as most of it was either a review of or a direct extension of the metric space topology we learned in 25b. However, when we got to algebraic topology the difficulty of the course for me ramped up at a very high rate. You spend a lot of time in alg top arguing with pictures and words, and visual intuition (something I did not have much of coming into 131) is very important to build, and fast. I wish we had spent more time doing alg top and less doing point–set topology––I found alg top to be both more interesting and more challenging. The course went at a pretty fast clip, and Auroux was a very clear lecturer, although he often went too fast for me.
This class is a good choice if you want to see topology from its fundamentals. You learn why donuts and tori and klein bottles are the way they are. The homework is challenging, and some really stretch how you think. Auroux is a great lecturer. Sometimes it went a bit fast, but that is because the nitty gritty is tedious but not too important for assignments. You end up with a good understanding of topology.
This class is hard. Halfway through the semester when you go from point set to algebraic topology, it gets even harder. You do get access to a lot of TFs who do OH through the week, so it is possible to work through the weekly psets. But unless you've decided you want to concentrate in math, stay far away. I've never felt as bad at math as I did in this class
Take the class, especially if it with professor Denis Auroux!
He creates a lot of enthusiasm for the material and provides lots of helpful materials.
This course is a great introduction to topology. Some background in analysis and algebra is helpful, but not strictly necessary – gaps are filled as topics are introduced. The course speeds up a bit towards the midpoint once algebraic topology begins, but also becomes more interesting in my opinion. Prof. Aurox is great, and I'd recommend taking anything with him!
Do the reading. Munkres is great and easy to read.
I took this class after having taken the math 25 series and a couple proof–based math classes over the summer. I found the first half of this course to be on the easy side. The psets are rather short, mostly consisting of routine exercises. The algebraic topology section of the course was much, much more difficult for me. I lacked background in group theory, and though I was able to provisionally learn definitions for the course, I would have gotten much more out of it if I had had better intuition for the concepts. That being said, I was able to grasp the big–picture concepts, which are very cool! Prof. Auroux is great––very clear, kind, and sharp. Exams were take–home and open–book, which took away the pressures of a timed exam. If you're on the fence about this class, I say go for it!
Auroux is a great lecturer, although a bit fast and messy sometimes. It's a serious and interesting math course, especially towards the end of the semester when algebraic topology becomes the focus of the material. Problem sets are not usually too difficult, and take–home exams are relatively light.

Fair warning: it will definitely help to have experience with group theory before the second half of the course (post midterm).
I would highly recommend this class, it gets very hard to visualize at the end, but you definitely learn a lot, and the topic is beautifully interesting and rewarding to understand.
This was my first upper–level math class, and it was an experience. It was a blast and a struggle rolled up into one. I came in straight from 22A/B, which is definitely possible if you did well in those classes. I also had a little background in real analysis. The first half of the course went rather fine; it sometimes took a while to fully understand concepts but leading up to the midterm, everything was pretty ok. Second half of the course also went OK — until the last 2/3 weeks–ish. To be fair, the 2nd half is way more interesting and beautiful math, but the last section on Seifert–van Kampen is pretty fast and kind of confusing if you don't already have at least a working knowledge of the intricacies of normal subgroups and quotienting. I feel like I only really understood Seifert–van Kampen after the final, which, needless to say, is too late. In any case, if you're fluent in set theory, knowledgable in rigorous calculus, and conversational in group theory, then take this course. Honestly, I wish I took 122 concurrently with this course. If you're coming from 22A/B, do this if you can and you'll likely be fine in a class with people coming from 25A/B, and even 55A. Even though I didn't, I ended up fine grade–wise ( I think/ hope). As with any math class, go to OH and have p–set buddies.

Oh, and by the way, Auroux rocks. He might not teach this class in its next iteration, but he made it a lot better than it would have been under a professor who'd care less. So take note of who is teaching the class.

I took 25a/b and 114, and in comparison to both of those it was much more straightforward and not a ton of work as long as you go to office hours to check understanding.
