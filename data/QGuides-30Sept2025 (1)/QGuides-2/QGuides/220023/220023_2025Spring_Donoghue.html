His lectures are fascinating, and he has a real gift for making Old and Middle English feel approachable and even enjoyable. If the course experience matched the quality of his lectures, this might have been one of my favorite classes.

Unfortunately, the overall structure and execution of the course made it incredibly frustrating, and I would not recommend it unless you already have a strong interest in Old and Middle English that you are pursuing as a part of your undergraduate degree. Despite how intriguing the topic is, the class was riddled with inconsistent grading and a workload that felt wildly disproportionate for a general education requirement.

One of the most disheartening moments was the midterm. The teaching staff emphasized how "generous" they were for providing a study guide apparently a rare offering. But the guide ended up being a single vague page that told us to study everything: all readings, lectures, and homework. I spent days drilling the phonetic alphabet, only to find that the midterm focused almost entirely on Middle English, with no chance to demonstrate what I had actually prepared for. I left the exam feeling like I had wasted a significant amount of time and I ultimately failed it. The formatting of the questions also left no room for partial credit. You either had the one correct answer or lost major points, which might make sense for future linguistics majors, but for a Gen Ed? It felt punitive and rigid.

The weekly knowledge checks in section were essentially pop quizzes, but wildly inconsistent across sections. In mine, the TF proctored them strictly, while in another section I attended, students openly looked up answers with no pushback from the TF. It was incredibly frustrating to realize that the same course could feel so different depending on which section you were assigned. My own TF was actually a university administrator, not a grad student. While I dont inherently mind that, it was clear he wasnt fully comfortable with the course material and often couldnt answer spontaneous questions. This only deepened the sense of inconsistency across the course experience.

However the most frustrating part that made me wish I dropped the course at the beginning of the semester was the workload. It was excessive and, frankly, insulting for a Gen Ed course. Every lecture and section came with dozens of pages of dense reading, plus multiple worksheets that could be tested at any time. These weren't light, supplemental readings eitherthey were essential to understanding the material and had to be memorized in detail. Once my departmental courses ramped up, I simply couldn't keep up. It was overwhelming and demoralizing.

Its hard not to feel like this course was symptomatic of deeper problems in the Gen Ed program. The disconnect between the passion and clarity of the instructor and the actual learning experience speaks volumes. This class demanded far more time and stress than it was worth. I came in genuinely curious about the topic, and I left exhausted, discouraged, and frustrated.

I really dont understand why the College is trying to intensify the Gen Ed program at a time when so many undergraduates are already stretched thinbalancing full course loads, research, jobs, internships, and extracurriculars... on top of everything that is happening in the world and at the university right now. The idea of Gen Eds is supposed to be about broadening our horizons, exposing us to new ways of thinking, and encouraging intellectual curiosity outside our major. But instead, theyre becoming another source of academic burnout.

There is a way to make Gen Eds rigorous and meaningful without overwhelming students or turning them into yet another box to check under duress. A well–designed Gen Ed should challenge students intellectually without competing with the courses that define their actual academic and career paths. When a general education course begins to demand more work, more time, and more emotional energy than departmental classes, something has gone seriously wrong.

The College seems to be clinging to an outdated ideal of the "well–rounded scholar" while ignoring the reality of student life today. Yes, we want to learn across disciplinesbut not at the expense of our well–being, or at the cost of disengaging from the courses that matter most to our goals. At some point, insisting that students struggle through arbitrarily intense Gen Eds just to prove some institutional point becomes counterproductiveand frankly, tone–deaf. If the College really wants students to embrace learning beyond their concentrations, then the Gen Ed program needs to earn that enthusiasmnot demand it through excessive workloads and opaque grading. Right now, it feels like the College has forgotten that most of us want to learn and is punishing us through the recent revisions in the Gen Eds.
