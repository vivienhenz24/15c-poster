If you think you are good at physics, if you think you have the time to devote, take this course, absolutely. SELF STUDY the necessary math and try to read the textbook before getting on campus. You don't want to be caught blindsided by the difficulty of the PSets.
My main takeaway from this class was the mathematical intuition for physics. Very often in high school physics classes, you could get away with an intuitive understanding of what a system would naturally do; you just know how the system moves and interacts but not necessarily what that looks like in equation form. This class transforms that intuition into equations and forces you to get a true mathematical understanding of the problem, whether it's recognizing that the normal force becomes 0 when the ladder falls away from the wall or the many, many factors that go into the nutation dynamics of a heavy symmetric top. Quoting a past QGuide that quoted a past QGuide, "this class is not about learning physics, it's about learning how to think about physics".

That said, the background you need for this class is rather all over the place. We went from casually needing differential equations to solve questions to defining what a cross product is in class to using some linear algebra to solve one of the problem set questions. In my case, I entered with a pretty strong understanding of rotational dynamics and so was easily able to understand those, but harmonic oscillators was quite difficult to understand since I'd never done differential equations before. I believe you could do this class with only an AP Physics C background, but expect to rely on other people for most of if not the entire course. Additionally, since the problem sets often vary in difficulty, even if you have the requisite background I believe that you'd still end up spending at least 12–15 hours on one of the problem sets, and without significant help form the CA's it can be difficult to even get started on some of the problems.
Easily the best course I took this semester. Some thoughts of person with no AP physics background below.

First, the physics community: physics people in this course are unpretentious (unlike most of math people :) ), witty, and friendly. Enjoyed every minute spent with them. The course is fully collaborative, so you'll work with them a lot. 16 collective experience is, admittedly, mostly trauma–bonding, but at least I never got bored.

Second, psets: they vary from medium–hard to why–im–doing–this–subject–at–all (and don't be shocked at PSET 1, everyone was, it got better afterwards). Nonetheless, most of people start their psets the day before deadline and victoriously retreat to Berg at 8 AM next day, which doesn't really work for courses like 55, but does not serve as satisfactory self–justification for your sleep deprivation. Grading is not harsh, and solutions like "here's what I assume/infer, and here's result from Mathematica" generally work, but final was simply (count letters yourself).

Finally, teaching team: Professor Yao would feed you every Thursday and advertise Winthrop House if he continued to teach the course, which will not happen in reality, sadly. CAs and TFs are insanely good both at physics and verbal stress therapy, so you'll love them.

Side note for people who want to take 55a: just go to ALL lectures, start psets on weekends, and prepare for low amount of social life; you'll be fine. Alternative version of future: you're smarter than everyone and will have enough of everything without much effort, but you should be somewhat of Dirac delta to achieve this.

Physics 16 teaches you to think and work like a real physicist. Coming into Physics 16, you've probably only seen AP–level physics where everything works out nicely and can be solved within a few minutes of work. That is not the case in Physics 16, and as you will take more advanced courses, physics as a whole. Problem sets are intensive, long, and full of bashing – but also fascinating. Norm and the course staff do not condescend to your level of knowledge, and instead lift you up to theirs. Start problem sets early, collaborate, and give up on the idea of sleeping on Wednesday night (physics night!) Fortunately, this class has a super supportive environment. You will be surrounded by people who genuinely love physics. I found Physics 16 to have a really tight–knit community this semester and I hope that will continue in the years to come.

Also, a word about Norm. Professor Yao is fantastic. He's off the charts level brilliant, but also just a wonderful guy to talk to and hang out with. He genuinely cares about getting to know his students on a personal level. How many professors at Harvard buy their students pizza at 2 am? Also, go to his office hours and chat with him! Not enough people did this semester, but they are very fun. On that note, the CAs are also so nice and willing to help you solve the problems. Go to their office hours too!

With that said, be aware this course is by no means easy. Get ready to work hard and late into the night. Another thing to note is that the problem sets can be somewhat unclear, confusing, and require you to go above and beyond the content of lecture. Actually do the readings – lecture in general won't be sufficient for solving the problem sets. Norm prefers to cover big ideas and develop intuition in lecture, and not get into the full detail of solving problems. Go to lecture: no matter how good at physics you think you are, Norm's insight and way of thinking is something we can all learn from.

I would say only take this course if you think there is a very good chance you will concentrate in physics. It's a big time committment, and you will burn out if you aren't passionate about physics. With that said, if you do want to study physics and get to know your peers, Physics 16 is the place for you, especially if it's taught by Norm.
What is there even to say about this class? Physics 16 was the best and worst part of my semester.

The general consensus about this course seems to be that you get out as much as you put into it, but while I agree for the most part, I also think it's easier than one probably thinks to get *too* invested. I dedicated a lot of myself to this class, possibly more than was healthy, and reaped the benefits/suffered the consequences of doing so. On the one hand, I did genuinely become a much better physicist; I learned how to think about problems in a way I never had before, and the difference in quality between my first and last few P–sets is night and day. On the other hand, though, I paid the price for it in time, sleep, and mental stability. Figuring out a particularly challenging problem on my own could sometimes make my entire week, sure, but getting stuck on something simple and feeling stupid was soul–crushing every single time (if we're keeping track, I cried over the midterm, the final, and about 30–40% of the Psets). And for what? Better physical intuition? Knowledge of how to use the inertia tensor? I'm not sure it was worth it.

I'm not here to discourage you from taking this course, or to in any way imply that my experience is universal. Certainly, a few of my peers were able to complete the assignments in a reasonable time and maintain a busy life outside physics. I do think everyone who's interested, even mildly, should shop this class! The professors for both 16 and 15a are more than accommodating, and I'm told it's very painless to move between the two. My warning only goes out to those like me: who enter thinking the class will be challenging but manageable, are absolutely wrecked by the first P–Set, and want to drop the class but stay out of some sort of internal obligation. For sure, the class gets a little easier after the first couple weeks, but not *that* much easier. If you are the kind of person to obsess over a single goal (for example: doing well in Physics 16), and you're not EXTREMELY confident in your physics ability, I would advise you to think very carefully about what you're getting into by taking this course. Only you can decide what you're willing to give up how many late nights you're willing to spend P–Setting, how many hours of sleep you're willing to lose, how many mental breakdowns you're willing to tolerate, etc. but please don't make that decision over some sense of wanting to prove something to yourself or others. You're already at Harvard! You have nothing to prove.

So, what if you've read all of the above, pondered deeply, suffered through the first, hellish P–Set, and decided to stick it out for the semester? Well, welcome to Physics 16!!! There will be some bleak moments, but once December rolls around you won't remember anything but the fun times. All of the people in this class are just as intelligent, driven, and eager to learn as you are, which makes for an absolutely unforgettable community. The CAs and TFs are incredibly kind and helpful, and the professor wants nothing more than to see you succeed. Stick around for free pizza on Physics Night and lunch on Thursdays! Physics 16 is a course unlike any other, for all the good and bad that entails. But if you really want to test your limits this semester (and if you REALLY have nothing better to be doing with your time), then I can't think of a more fun way to do it than through this class.

P.S. if you happen to be an Olympiad kid, ignore everything I said above. You'll probably have a great time in this gemmy course ;)

Physics 16 is truly an amazing class that manages to be both quite painful and very rewarding. The Q reports are no exaggeration in terms of time commitment and level of difficulty: I initially thought that there was absolutely no way you could spend more than 15 hours a week on a class, but Physics 16 achieves that for sure. I've probably half–lived in the physics lounge over my fall semester and have almost without exception been in the physics library until around 4–5am minimum on Wednesday nights (Thursday mornings?), and you certainly get thrown in headfirst in the sense that you are forced to learn to collaborate with peers and ask for help from TFs very quickly. I can without compunction say that my first semester revolved around Physics 16, so it's not a class you should take unless you're really passionate and committed to studying physics as a subject.

The best thing about 16 (and probably the major thing that brings it from barely bearable to absolutely transformative) is its community. This year's class was about 24 people, and I'm pretty good friends with almost all of them––you really end up psetting in one large group, and I've met some amazing people I would not have otherwise thought to meet (including one of my best friends). Also, community–building often equals free food!

Professor Yao (Norm) is wonderful, as are all the TFs and CAs: they are constantly supportive, available, and ready to help when it comes to not just course material but more general topics. As a lecturer, Norm is very engaging, and he takes an approach that really lets you see the line of reasoning that he's using to derive a particular result. The lectures work well with Morin's textbook (Introduction to Classical Mechanics), which is especially useful when it comes to material you don't know very well.

Preparation–wise, I definitely recommend at least having AP Physics C under your belt. As someone who took all the AP Physics courses in my junior year of high school, I felt significantly underprepared compared to most of the people in the class who have USAPhO/IPhO/general Olympiad experience. I did, however, have a lot of math and calculus background, which I feel helped somewhat. Regardless, I think almost everyone in the class committed a big chunk of their semester to this class, and if you're okay with spending a lot of your time doing physics, I would fully recommend this class.
