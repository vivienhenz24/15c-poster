Coming into the course, LIFESCI 1B had a horrendous reputation for having awful PIE exams that would completely make or break a students experience within the class. In Spring, 2023, this was especially evident, with the PIE averages being all over the place and occasionally even falling within the failing range. However, for Spring 2024, this was no longer the case, and it appears as if LIFESCI 1B has done a much better job standardizing the content and difficulty levels of the exams.

Unfortunately, one area that did not get improved was the Final Exam. Seeing how the PIEs consistently had averages within the 80s, many expected for the Final to be of similar difficulty. However, it ended up being significantly longer and more difficult than most anticipated. Most notably, when the Practice Finals were released, it was specifically stated on the Preparing for the Final Exam page that Practice Final A and B were meant to be at least as long if not longer than the real exam and were 24 and 26 pages, respectively. However, our real Final Exam ended up being 30 pages, significantly longer than anything we had seen or expected up until this point. As a result, the final ended up being extremely unreasonable and unfair, as many students were unable to finish.

Outside of this, LIFESCI 1B also suffers from an unbalanced grading system, where a large chunk of a students grade is dependent on their individual TF. While some students have relatively chill TFs that grade normally, some were notorious for taking off minute deductions here and there (that were NOT based on the course–made rubric). Although these deductions may seem minimal at first, they add up over time and can be the reason why a student is bumped down a letter grade if severe enough. Although Professor Berry reassures us that grades will be standardized at the end of the term to account for this, it remains unclear how this is done (as all you get back is your final letter grade on my.Harvard).

Overall, given the improvements this year, I can confidently say that LIFESCI 1B is no longer the horrendous and treacherous class that many avoided in Spring 2023. This is not to say that the course is perfect, as there is definitely still room for improvement (as with the final), but in my eyes, Professor Berry has definitely done a good job of taking in feedback and making this years class more equitable and fair.

This class is a requirement for all biology–related fields and, of course, pre–meds. Before I talk about how to succeed in the class, Ill say this: Do not let the stigma surrounding this class scare you. While this class is no joke, you can succeed in it if you put in the time and effort into it. While some may say this is a weed–out class, this class is about developing endurance and comprehending material at a pace that you may or may not be comfortable with. But I'll say it again: You can do it. So, lets get started.

From my experience, the most difficult part about LS1b was simply keeping up with the material. With three other classes (including PS11) and extracurricular activities, your time–management skills are put to the test big time. But with that said, with these tips, I guarantee you that you can overcome and maybe enjoy the class.

Prime (prepare for) and review lecture – The day before each lecture, the Pre–Lecture slides and Lecture Notes are posted. Skim the slides and notes to get a brief overview of what will be covered in the lecture. During the lecture, Andrew Berry is hilarious and very engaging. Despite that though, the lecture can get pretty confusing at times, especially with how fast he goes through the content. If youre lost with the lecture, dont fret. After the lecture, write a condensed summary of everything you covered from the lecture (Youll see why later).

Go to SSN – The SSN (or Student Study Networks) is where you can complete your PSETs with facilitators (which are students who have previously completed LS1b). They will help you clarify any questions or concerns about the PSET questions you may have. Definitely a great resource for getting your work done early.

Ask questions in Section or Office Hours – If you are confused about a concept or how it is applied, do not be afraid to email your TF or one of the head instructors. I will be honest though, some of the TFs may explain the concepts differently, which can be confusing and frustrating at times.

Studying for PIEs – These dreaded exams are given every 2–3 weeks (mostly bi–weekly when finals are approaching) and they last 40 minutes covering ~4–6 lectures worth of content. The good news is that youre allowed to bring a one–sided cheat sheet with you to the exam. So, make sure you review your summaries or lecture notes and work on your cheat sheet EARLY!! Also, when studying, make sure you cover practice problems, section handout problems, PSET solutions and feedback, practice PIEs, and the Poll Everywhere questions and solutions (from post–lecture slides). Make sure the why and how of the solutions to those materials. Also, review the post–lecture slides and make sure that you understand the diagrams and models shown. Lastly, there are Lowdown Videos that help you walk through difficult concepts that deal with computation or concept application. There was one PIE where the last question was literally a Poll Everywhere question, and it was worth multiple points. Oh! And there is one PIE drop. So, dont feel bad if you didnt do too hot on the first one. Its meant to help you figure out how to modify your studying strategy. The PIEs are not merely about regurgitation of information, but rather applying the concepts. So, seek to understand!

Lab assignments – Work on the assignments early and ask your lab TF questions on it as they are the ones grading them. This especially goes for the write–ups that require a specific citation format and specifications for getting full points on the reports.

Final – It is worth 3 PIEs and covers the entire course. So, my best advice: start early!! Review the feedback from your previous PIEs, PSETs, redo the materials I mentioned from (4). Attend the PIE Final Reviews and start the practice finals as soon as you have reviewed everything.

Bottom line: This is an easy class, but it provides a very interesting understanding of molecular biology, and you will learn how to modify your study habits as you go through the course.
