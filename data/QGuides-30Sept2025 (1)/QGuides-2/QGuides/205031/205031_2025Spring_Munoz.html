This course is a requirement, so you have to take it at least twice. This past semester was the best implementation I've seen of the production studio model, but it wasn't without flaw. Email ahead to ask what the deal with auditions is, because oftentimes they're deceptive (e.g. completely "informal" but absolutely define your experience of the course including whether you sing in your own range, whether you play your preferred gender, and whether you have substantial material to memorize / a decent sized role). If they ask you to cold read in a table read format "just to hear it as a group," be sure to ask whether you are being officially seen for a role (because this is likely the only acting audition you'll get). If you do/don't want a lead, tell the course staff early so that more folks can be more matched to their time commitments.

If you're an experienced performer, you will probably not learn from the present model of the course. If the show seems to deal with problematic content in any way shape or form, trust me, wait for another one to come around in a different semester (handling sensitive content is a continual challenge for TDM). If you get a light–hearted, fun script with lots of roles, you have literally no idea how lucky you are. Email ahead to check what the deal is if you're not sure.

The department is not normal about conflicts, but your stage managers often are. Email them personally well in advance and ask permission (do not be sneaky or rude, you make us all look bad). If they don't reply, assume it's a no and show up. Continually show the course staff more grace than the department is willing to show you. The course staff is not responsible for the structural issues of the course itself. They are doing their best to accommodate everyone's needs, and they are often as confused and frustrated with the situation as you are. Oftentimes, they are industry professionals who care about meeting students and forging meaningful connections. Be five minutes early for everything, show up off book before they ask, get into costume and mic immediately, and demonstrate with your actions that you're ready for a bigger challenge (if enough of us do it, they might finally listen).

Brisa, Stew, Sam, and Rachel are amazing course staff. You will be very lucky to work with them if any of them are involved again.
Face your fears and take courses like this. Although it's good to be on stage, you also can be fulfilled with any of the behind–the–scenes work that's necessary for such a production to go up.
