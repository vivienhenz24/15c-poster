I have very mixed feelings about this course. TL,DR; just self–teach the course if you can, or at least take it with someone you know.

On one hand, there is nothing wrong with the actual content. It's everything you would learn in a computer networking course. Also, the Professor is extremely insightful, if you know to ask the right questions.

On the other hand, this is far and away the most disorganized course I've ever taken at Harvard. Several reasons:

1.) This is mostly the fault of the problem sets. After a quick search online, you can see that most of them are straight–up copy–pasted from the Internet. This raises a red flag for me immediately: professors should design their own problem sets. Period or at least modify them in a noticeable way. Not only this, but the programming assignments (psets) themselves are so janky, especially the ones with P4. Until at least static code checking is supported for P4, it is such a garbage language to focus on, even if it's the only open–source language that the majority of switches are programmed in. The whole point of the coding assignments, apart from the final project, should be to demonstrate that you understand the concepts. Every single one of the psets that relies on P4 (2/5, at least) misses this point entirely, and is focusing entirely on code semantics for a language with little–to–no online resources (apart, of course, from examples on Github ... oh wait, that's where the Pset comes from! You can just copy–paste them! But then what's the point of the pset???).

2.) I can get past P4, but what's absolutely bonkers to me is that these problem sets have not been updated in half a DECADE. Yes, you have to compile and write all of the problem sets in a Ubuntu Linux VM simply because the libraries are only supported by a Linux OS, and they are all deprecated ones, because they're compiled with Python 2!!!! I had to literally un–install python just to downgrade it.

3.) There is no Ed presence whatsoever. The vast majority of questions asked by students either go unanswered (or answered by other students) far before the questions are every answered by a TF/the Prof. To my knowledge, office hours are also only held 1–2 times a week, during CS nights + some other day (idk, wasn't pubbed basically ever). I have nothing personal against the teaching staff, but that just seemed silly.

4.) For whatever reason, this course is especially popular amongst international students and/or Harvard extension school students. I have no qualms with this whatsoever. However, it's annoying because the lectures are all recorded. This basically incentivizes no one to ever come to the lectures, because college students can also just watch them online. This leads to a complete void of course "culture", so unless you know somebody beforehand or exchange contact info within the first week, you're on your own.

5.) Grading was at least a month late at all times. It is the last day of finals period, and one of our problem sets still is not graded (the penultimate was only graded a few days ago, after the final project's deadline). Expect to receive no helpful feedback, either. No code comments, none of that. The problem sets are more like an all–or–nothing type thing when it comes to points.

6.) You can teach yourself literally all of the course content! Computer networks is one of the most self–teachable concepts in undergrad computer science, and all of the problem sets are straight from the Internet anyway. I thought that by taking this course, I could take advantage of the classroom setting to teach myself more than I already knew (which is somewhat substantial), but since the problem sets are all online anyway, literally what was the point of it all?
Pretty good course overall. There's a lot of time spent on datacenter architecture and design and on how networking is changing to adapt to cloud computing, expansion of Tier 1 ISPs, etc, but generally the course goes through the layers of the Internet from bottom to top (not much time spent on Application layer). Try not to get behind on lecture material or you will hate yourself when the midterm hits. Reading the given textbook sections before or after lecture can be super helpful, and take advantage of practice problems in the textbook! TFs are super helpful and available through office hours, and sections often end early so you can also ask questions then. Finding a group to work on homeworks and projects with is super useful, especially if you use it as accountability to start things early. Not that much P4 programming, which was great; most of it was in Python.
