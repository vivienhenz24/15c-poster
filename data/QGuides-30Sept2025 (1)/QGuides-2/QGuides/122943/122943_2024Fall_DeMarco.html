Definitely consider taking this course! But be warned, it might not be as "easy" as you think (I was told this was a fairly "chill" course, akin to 21A, but I found it more conceptually difficult and time–consuming). From what I can tell, 100–level math courses change instructors every semester, so these comments will probably be outdated. But regardless, if you're even slightly interested in math (considering a joint/secondary, enjoyed 21A/21B, etc.) try out this course and see if it clicks. It starts off a bit rough (especially if you've never done a proof before), but it gets better with time, and by the end, you come out of it feeling so much better!

Related, this is a fairly difficult course in my opinion, so if you're taking it along with another difficult course (like STAT 110, which many of us were doing), be prepared. Go to office hours and get started with the p–sets as early as possible.
always do the reading and read the notes
If this class is offered with Professor DeMarco, take it! She's a great lecturer and you'll get to learn a good spread of concepts while getting comfortable with proofs. It's a reasonable amount of work, a very supportive environment, and you'll feel much more knowledgeable by the end of the semester.
If you would like an introduction to pure math/proofs, I would recommend this course, although be prepared fair bit of work. I felt adequately prepared for the general math content with 21a and 21b, but had a large learning curve in proof–writing that increased my time commitment to the class. Psets could take up to 10 hours a week depending on difficulty, but from conversations with former students this is pretty instructor dependent –– in our case, Professor DeMarco expected a lot from us but was overall very kind and fair with her office hours and grading. My favorite part of the class was probably the community created in lecture and office hours –– the class is usually pretty small with a lot of TFs which creates a great teaching staff to student ratio.
Professor DeMarco is awesome!!! She is so enthusiastic and knowledgeable about the material. The TFs are also lovely, and our class got really tight–knit by the end of the semester. The in–class structure is great, with Professor DeMarco lecturing for the first half and then working on practice problems for the second half of each class. Good level of difficulty too: not overwhelming but not too easy either.
Excellent course under Prof. DeMarco. Her classes are fantastic, super accessible, and never fail to create excitement around the content. This was a great introduction to mathematics more formally, and I look forward to using this to spring towards other Math courses. Would strongly recommend!
This class was (in a way I believe is anomalous) really, really, really, exceptionally difficult. I had very little background in math and thought I would be fine, but I don't really think I was. I needed more preparation, more guidance, and more help in general.

The thing with this course is just how fast you go through the material. We were on our second week of group theory, and I was talking to a roommate of mine who's a math major, and he said we were converging things that they didn't get into until his second *semester* of group theory. I was regularly working more than 20–25 hours per set, and that was with collaboration and office hours. This course is honestly really inaccessible to those without a very strong math background to begin with. But if you are willing to suffer, and let your GPA probably suffer, you will learn a lot. Just maybe don't take this if you're also taking STAT110, as many of my classmates were, or if you don't have a math background at all.

Prof. DeMarco is a wonderful professor and I know that if I was really into math, she would be one of the best professors I've ever had. She is wonderful at presenting material in a digestible way. The only thing wrong with this class is just how damn difficult it is. Other than that, a nice class.
Take it if you are even the slightest bit interested in math!
Keep up with the textbook readings and thoroughly practice the different types of proofs. Youll get a lot more out of if you actively engage with the worksheets!
The course is taught by a new professor every semester, so look at q–reports for the professors as well. That being said, this class is often taught by a great professor. If you ever have a chance to take a course with Prof. DeMarco, please do, she's one of the best professors I've ever had.

The worksheets & psets are relatively difficult, so that you learn a lot, but the exams are far more manageable, as she wants you to succeed. You will be both challenged and come out with a good grade. I think that is an excellent way to run a course.

This fulfills the same reqs as CS20, and I find it to be far more interesting too. You won't cover CS related topics but your proof writing and mathematical reasoning will be more developed which will come in handy in later CS courses. Also, this class made me consider becoming a double concentrator in Math. It's really great
Incredible class! Demarco is the best you could hope for. The course is a great introduction to pure math for anyone with absolutely no background in it. It assumes no prior knowledge and really builds up your proof–writing abilities from ground zero. Demarco's lecture notes are wonderful, and the other texts in the course basically just provide further detail. The p–sets are pretty difficult, especially at the beginning, but they seem to get easier throughout the semester as you get the hang of things. I would say the level of the problems was pretty perfect, because it felt challenging and interesting but it really was something that we would be able to do. The CA's are really great and offer tons of OH for when you are stuck. The structure of the classes is that the first half or so is lecture, then you spend the next half working in groups on worksheet problems (usually short proofs). It is super valuable practice and a great time to get feedback from CA's. There was even more practice available in the weekly sections. Exams were not as challenging as homeworks and did not feel too rushed for a class period. All of this feedback comes with the caveat that things may be different under a different instructor, and Demarco won't be teaching next year. However, I think this class is generally well–run and a good introduction to the field if you have an interest. Also, if you ever get a chance to take a class with Demarco, take it!!
