MATH 101 was a challenging yet fun journey to be a part of. The course material will change your outlook towards conventional mathematics and will open your mind to this beautiful new world of abstract maths. While some students might not enjoy the theoretical nature of the same, I found the course material to be highly engaging. The problem sets should not be taken lightly and I would recommend starting them as early as possible. The only thing that made the course stressful for me was starting the problem sets on the last day and underestimating the time commitment needed for them. You could very easily just ask your friends for their problem sets, not attend any lectures, and just cram the day before the exams, but in that case, I would recommend not taking the course in the first place. You get what you put into the class. If you truly want to enjoy and explore the vast field of pure mathematics, put in the work, start the PSETs early, and don't limit yourself to what is taught in class.

A good class for an intro to proofs. Covers a wide array of math topics. A moderate class I would say in terms of time commitment. Stephen was a good teacher, he did a fairly good job of explaining things but his biggest strength is his willingness to help students one on one and really take the time to go over topics that we were unsure about. 3 exams, and a final presentation on a proof. No final exam is huge! And the final presentation is easy. So just take the time to really nail down concepts for the 3 exams and you're golden.
Take all of this with a grain of salt, because this class is highly dependent on the instructor, and Stephen will not be here next semester.
That being said: Math 101 is a good introductory and I mean INTRODUCTORY course in upper–level math. It does what it advertises: teach you a little about a lot of things, and show you the basics of group theory and real analysis. It's taught to a fairly low common denominator, which may bother you if you're someone who generally likes to take fast–paced courses and skip levels in things. But that being said, it does teach you how to write proofs, which was an area that I was sorely in need of help with (as a physics major, embarrassingly). Stephen is great, and funny, and the homeworks and exams are entirely reasonable. All in all, it does what it says, but it's certainly no substitute for Math 22/25/55 or an intro class like 112 or 122.

I really really enjoyed Math 101 with Stephen McKean! While I know he won't be teaching this course again next year and therefore Math 101's material may change, I would heavily recommend Math 101 regardless. This class doesn't require really any formal math background (i.e knowlege of calculus or any course higher won't help you), and it's an excellent introduction to proofs and mathematical thinking for those who have never been formally introduced to these concepts before. It allows you to grapple with these concepts without overwhelming you, and is ideal for those who don't have a strong math background, but may want to move in that direction (both undergrad and graduate students). There are lots of office hours, so it's easy to work and discuss problems with Stephen and the CA's, which also helps if you don't have a pset group or other people to work with. Stephen's teaching is also phenomenally clear and concise; I can't recommend him as a lecturer enough.

As a note, some other reviews may comment on the psets being disorganized, which did happen towards the end of the course (i.e the last pset). Regardless, I enjoyed the experience immensely, and wouldn't let that dissuade you from taking Math 101!
