OVERALL: Excellent multivariable calculus course! Very glad I took it.

The person teaching Math 22 (A or B) influences your experience in the course more than any other factor, unless you are biased for/against linear algebra or calculus as subjects from the get–go. I would highly recommend taking this class if Professor Auroux were to teach it again. Beyond highly proficient lecturing, he, as well as many of the TFs/CAs, were extremely kind and supportive throughout the semester.

This iteration of 22(B) was comparatively rigorous and demanding time commitment–wise. I spent a lot time on the problemsets since I tend to write longer proofs than others, not necessarily because they were that challenging every week. That being said, the difficulty of *problemsets* can range from moderately easy (provided you have a decent background in calculus) to somewhat or quite challenging, while that of *individual* problems for the average student typically range from extremely straightforward to very difficult without help which you should definitely seek at office hours or from your friends if needed. (Learning how to learn and receive guidance is useful!)

If you have a solid grasp of (at least) the AP Calculus BC curriculum, the content covered in 22A/21B, and a bit of geometry/trigonometry, you are more than prepared for this course. If you don't, and I didn't, I think you can definitely still have a great experience there might just be a few weeks where it feels like you're learning a /lot/ while many others are just reviewing (e.g. when you start with integrals), but I found it to be very manageable.

I recommend this course if you took 22a or higher last semester and want to be exposed to vector calculus (it can be helpful in later math/stem courses). However, be aware that it is difficult and different from the psets and exams from 22a. At least in my experience, having Cliff Taubes and then Denis Auroux, who I believe were both in their first semesters of teaching the respective courses, I can say this: The 22a psets had a number of written "computational" problems, a smaller number of longer "proof" problems, and sometimes a science application question. The 22b psets seemed a lot shorter at first because the comp problems were replaced by textbook problems listed by chapter and number, and then five to nine written problems; the textbook problems, 10 to 15 of them, were sometimes harder than the written problems and took more time. The 22a exams were difficult and more proof heavy; however, we had three hours for some five problems, and maybe half the room was able to leave early. On the other hand, the 22b exams were mostly easier to hard computational problems; the midterms were in–class with only an hour and 15 minutes; the final was three hours, like the midterms for 22a, but with 14 questions; hardly anyone left early, and this is a class of math or math–adjacent concentrators. I feel like that fact should put it into perspective: compared to 22a, which is taken by anyone, in 22b you go up against the upper echelon of 22a, and you will see that that is reflected in the grade distributions that may or may not be shared. Don't let this deter you; for many, the class was still easy; don't compare yourself to them. Period. Just know what you have to do for this class and own it. You'll learn a lot and it'll pay off.
