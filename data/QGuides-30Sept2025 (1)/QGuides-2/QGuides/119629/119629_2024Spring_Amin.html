I would like to tell future students to prepare for a bit of a disconnection between the assignment they're working on and the content they'll be learning in class in the case that they are not careful and begin to work on a late day cycle of assignments rather than a current–day one.
Take it. I didn't take cs51 and did just fine (you need to teach yourself basic ocaml – or have a friend explain ocaml syntax).
It is not too much work in the beginning, but things get quite harder during the semester so be prepared. Lecture notes are amazing and you can easily get an A without going to lecture.
It's a tough class and it's 99.9% about the theory behind programming languages, not in any way a survey of different languages.
Start PSets early and please go to office hours. Don't just watch online – actually go to lecture so you know your classmates, professors, and go to sections too so that you know your TFs. This course is so much better when you actually see the faces of the course – you'll also make some friends (you'll need some friends to get through some of the psets).
This class is helpful if you are interested in PL and potentially building your own. The lecture notes are very helpful, but the content can be slightly dry/repetitive. The instructor (typically grad students) changes a lot class to class, and it was not helpful for building continuity throughout the course. The problem sets and exams are pretty fair overall.
Amin was great. The course taught me lots of interesting things. It was cool learning more about how the novel programming language features we hear about actually worked. Lots of mind–bending content.
Can be extremely confusing, and I'd recommend seeing if you're ready for the material before taking the class.
152 is a pretty good course – I went in with pretty low expectations, but it far exceeded them. Working with program semantics is actually pretty fun, and learning some different niche languages was also kinda neat.
One of my most favorite courses in the CS department. If you have experience with inductive and logical thinking, the course will be very comfortable. Also exciting to see the underlying reasoning behind some of the programming language features you might enjoy like generics, concurrency models (or attempts at that), etc.
This was probably one of the most boring CS courses I ever took, and the material was definitely not anything that interested me. It wasn't too hard, so it's decent as a low–pri CS class, but it still required a fair amount of attention, especially for the midterm and final. I don't really feel I learned anything too valuable, except for the focus on lambda calculus.
I took this class looking for a math–y introduction to functional programming (i.e., not 51), and it was so much cooler than I expected. Despite covering fairly deep topics in PL, Prof. Amin's lectures were clear and easy to follow (we usually had ~15 minutes for questions at the end), with a great balance of theory and applications (to systems, general code–writing, etc.). We also had some great guest lectures, with Rust language design and OCaml performance optimizations as two particular highlights. The assignments, especially their programming components, were a lot of fun and not too time consuming, so I really have no complaints.
