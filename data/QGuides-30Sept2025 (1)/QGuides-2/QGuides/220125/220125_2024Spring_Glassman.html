I would recommend this class although I would tell students that they should definitely have a background in coding. The projects are really cool but you do not have a ton of guidance so definitely be prepared for that. There is also a ton of reading.
TLDR: this class felt like a watered down CS191, and you don't really learn how to code :(

This felt was a busy work class, you just do a bunch of readings on Perusall, discuss them in class, and then put something together for the projects. There were a lot of forms required (reflection on the reading, end of class reflection) every class period, and it was a drag. The grading scheme (N L R R+) was never really clear (all Rs on the rubric translated to an 80%, but no one grades like that??) so that was just another factor. I really hoped to learn how to do things, but I don't think I ever did. As someone who didn't have web dev experience, I struggled on each project, and didn't enjoy the class at all. I don't think this class was good this year, but I do think the course staff tried. Hopefully next year it improves.
Don't take this class, or at least don't take it if you want to get anything out of a CS class. You will learn nothing and the readings are boring and all you do in class is discuss the readings. You don't learn anything about making interactive and usable systems. I suppose it's pretty light workload for a CS class but it's worse because you don't learn anything and you barely get to code.
The readings become a little boring, but make sure to do them and leave comments. So much of your grade in this class hinges on your comprehension of and engagement with the readings.
I 100% recommend this course. This was the most useful CS class I've taken at Harvard, particularly because it was project–based. After our first 2 projects (a scheduling and an improved Passio Go app), we were able to completely decide what our final project would be (so long as it included computation/AI). As someone with less project experience coming in, I struggled at the start but improved so much throughout the course of the semester. By the end of the semester, I was able to design and develop a project that I was genuinely proud of and could show my friends and family. I would tell all future students to really take advantage of the playtests/project demos and sections as well, as they provide real–time user feedback for your designs and allow you to learn while doing. The only qualm I have with the course was the focus on readings from one book. We had to leave comments on the readings twice a week, which would count towards our participation grades. Towards the end of the semester, it felt like some people were just highlighting every other sentence so that they could find something to talk about.
faaavorite CS class I've taken at Harvard by far, finally something actually applicable to industry (which is arguably where many if not most CS students here will actually end up, SWE in industry). It's NOT a class teaching you specific languages and frameworks that will be irrelevant in 10 years, but rather how to think about design and human computer interaction, how to think about the underlying concepts in interfaces, digital products and languages that give you skills which will be transferable regardless of language. love the classroom style and the emphasis on discussion and projects. love love love the ability to do software development for our projects. if you can take this course, then DO IT!
I would not recommend this class if you're taking it for the CS concentration. It's trying to do too much with the readings and projects. Also, the grading is wack with the R+/R/L. Only top ~10% of submissions are supposed to get R+s. However, a 100 is getting all R+s, so all Rs is B range. I didn't have any huge takeaways from the class that I couldn't have just gotten from reading the Daniel Jackson Essence of Software book myself. Overall, I think there are better classes you can take with your time, and the grading in this class isn't as nice as it might seem.
Great class, great instructor, readings are just ok. This has more theory than expected and is less of a "software engineering" class than expected, but still a great experience nonetheless.
Enjoyable, but kinda mid. If you can still get your programming II requirement with this class, it's more fun and applied than CS 51, but if not, I'm not sure I'd recommend this course.

The course centers around reading Daniel Jackson's book on effective software design. There are several very useful ideas in this book, such as his system for designing software based on "concepts" and how these concepts interact with each other and users' expectations.

Outside of these learnings and one module on Flask and Svelte, the lectures and sections just beat a dead horse. I was expecting more of an applied course where we would get lots of practice spinning up applications in various front end frameworks and implement interactive design. However, it was taught more like a grad class, where you read papers, then do only a hit of actual building to demonstrate very trivial ideas.

Take it if you want a light course, as it's definitely a CS gem. Otherwise, you'll learn more just reading the book and building your own projects in React.
Take this class!!! If you want to gain experience building projects from scratch, and learning about more applied/design centric areas of CS, this is the class for you. Elena and the teaching staff are amazing, and are a great support system through the entire course.
There was a bunch of grade anxiety in this class that I feel like could have been pretty easily resolved. It's said in the beginning that a B (straight R's on the N/L/R/R+ scale) on an assignment means that the student fully understood the concepts of the assignment. However, students don't usually like to get B's, and that is when students do everything they're supposed to do. Particularly with assignments in the beginning of the semester, when the definition of a "concept" in design is not yet solidified, there is a lot of confusion around how to describe concepts of an interface, which hast personally cost me a lot of points in assignments like PSET 0. This was not a great way to start the course. However, the assignments of this course were fulfilling, and you can yield some interface that you can show your friends / family, which is pretty cool.

Section is hit or miss...I really enjoyed my section, and other students I've talked to with my TF also really enjoyed their sections. However, some students from other TF sections did not like it as much.
