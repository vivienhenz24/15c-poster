This is by and far the best introductory physics class available. If you have any interest in or are curious about physics, I would urge you to consider this course. The professor is an amazing speaker and person, the class environment is collaborative and welcoming, and the course material is fascinating. Though the topics may seem scary at first, Jacob does an amazing job of explaining things like relativity in an easy to understand way. Office hours are frequent and accessible, there are optional sections to help understand material more thoroughly, and there is a lot of support in finding and holding study groups.
There were two midterms, both fairly straightforward and clearly designed to minimize stress. We were given several hours for a one hour exam (one could leave early), which allowed us to take our time and check through answers. Clear topic list and practice midterms were posted well in advance of both exams, and we were allowed two formula sheets (front and back, one for the first exam and a second in addition on the second exam).
Homework was conceptually rigorous but very reasonable to work out within a weekend, and could be addressed at office hours at any point (including at Physics Nights in Jeff 450, which often involved a bunch of people working on the same problems). There was an automatic two day grace deadline for any assignment, and generally very favorable policies for late work and missed class. Lecture videos and notes are posted for every lecture, as well as some optional supplements (just for fun).
Pre–recs for this class assume high school level calculus and no previous physics, and the class is definitely self contained (though more math definitely doesn't hurt). Every topic is explained from the beginning assuming absolutely zero physics knowledge. The topics are all quite high level and interesting, but are also presented very clearly.
The final is a presentation on some niche physics topic, which is really more like a slightly–longer–than–normal pset problem where you try to gain a bit of intuition for why certain physics topics exist/what abstract concepts may represent in the real world. (Note; the people in the other intro physics classes are currently trying to memorize equations for a sit–down exam. We got to ask questions and work things out together while making our presentation.)
This is an amazing class for figuring out what to concentrate in. Even if you end up deciding that physics is not really for you, it is still an incredible experience.

Where do I even begin? Perhaps I should start by saying a few words about Jacob. Jacob is by far the best teacher that I have ever had in all of my life. An enthusiastic and effective lecturer, a passionate physicist, and a wonderful human being, Jacob was, without a shadow of doubt, the highlight of my freshman fall. His lectures were a total pleasure to attend, and so were his office hours, where he took the time to clarify concepts and answer questions. From the incredibly well–written lecture notes to all the love and care he displayed for his students at one point, he told us that his office is a place where one can lay down their armor and seek support the amount of thought and care he put into both the course and his students is insane.

As for the course material, 19 covers many, many, many extremely beautiful topics from theoretical physics, ranging from analytical mechanics to thermodynamics to quantum mechanics to special and general relativity. The amount of topics on the course syllabus might seem insane (and it is!), but trust me, Jacob will cover (almost) all of them in due depth and with due rigor.

Concerning required background, level of preparation, etc., 19 is a course suitable for students from all walks of physics. If you are a newbie and have little background in the subject, then 19 can easily become your zero–to–hero experience provided you take the time to assimilate the material which, in particular, involves diligently reading the lecture notes and going to OH to ask questions. If you have more significant background in physics, then 19 is a perfect opportunity to polish and systematize existing knowledge as well as learn tons of new and really fun concepts!

This provides a perfect segway into my last point: physics 16 vs. physics 19. If you are planning to take 16 over 19 because 19 is supposedly "superficial" and "easy", think again! True, 19 has pretty gemmy PSETs and exams; however, as easy as they are, they are not brainrot easy. The problems are always enlightening and insightful and make you discover a new facet of the physics. But more importantly, the "easiness" of the assignments is compensated for by the lecture notes which are very profound and thorough. If you take the time to read them and process every word and symbol contained therein, your will be sufficiently challenged, and your physics maturity will grow as a result. (And even if, after reading every single page of the lecture notes, you still think 19 is too easy, just pick geodesics as your final project and have fun!) Also, I should add that in my opinion 19 serves as better preparation for more advanced coursework like 143, 153 and –– dare I say –– 251: in 16 you're losing sleep and sanity over crazy–ass rotation problems that will never show up in your physics studies again, whereas in 19 you're doing actual theoretical physics that will be useful for later on. So pick your battle!

This was an interesting course, and it was not what I was expecting because it was so different from a traditional physics course. For one, a lot of the material and the problem sets seem to rely more on math than physics, and almost all concepts are derived from scratch. It is super interesting to see how equations and laws that are often taken for granted, like complex numbers and the postulates of special relativity, actually come to be and it taught me a lot about thinking about math and physics from a different perspective. That said, I do think that this course moves incredibly quickly and covers a very large amount of content.
