This is a good class if you're interested in political economy or comparative politics. As a Gov student, I was pleasantly surprised that this class is less econ/theory and more historical and empirical. The lectures basically read off the slides, which means they're not too engaging, but that also meant that showing up to class was basically optional and I could catch up on lectures on my own time. The content of the course is genuinely interesting, thought–provoking, and made me feel like a more informed person. I have a better understanding of the types of institutions, cultures, and geographic features that drive growth. The class is called "History of Economic Growth," and while there is some history, much of it is mainly meant to spell out certain economic theories, which makes it all feel relevant and contemporary.
The assignments are relatively straightforward, and if you keep up with lectures, it's not too difficult to do well on them. The exams are fair but not exceedingly easy, and they're based directly on lecture content. Overall would recommend!

Great class to take for an Ec elective and the intermediate level requirement. Workload is not too bad at all with just one page response papers due each week and then a midterm and final. Both the midterm and final were pretty long and a bit of a time crunch in my opinion but manageable if you prepare well. This course is great, and it really allows you to understand the core drivers of differences in economic development across countries. TIPS: 1) Going to lecture is not necessary – just make sure you keep up with reading the lecture slides. 2) For the exams, make sure you understand the overarching topics, the processes through which a big change occurred, and relevant examples from lecture/readings to back up your argument. 3) For the discussion papers, make sure to explain why your examples are relevant to your argument.
