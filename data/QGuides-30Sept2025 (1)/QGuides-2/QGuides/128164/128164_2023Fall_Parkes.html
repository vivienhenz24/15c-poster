David Parkes is the best professor at Harvard. The scope of topics you can write about is very large and you can have a lot of fun with this if you want, or you can play it safe and write a survey paper. 3) Rewire your brain to think about incentive properties. Unlike an algorithms class where you are often assumed to have some input and then you study how to transform it into a desired output, CS 136 flips this idea on its head and teaches you to consider how the "algorithm" itself might influence the input you receive. For example, google "The Boston Mechanism" for an interesting case study that is covered in the course. 4) The textbook. It has some typos here and there, but other than that it's just about as good as the STAT 110 textbook, i.e. it's very good. 5) The students. I found that the level of excitement for the course by other students was noticeably higher than it has typically been in other classes. Probably some sort of strong self–selection bias. 6) Applicability. It is true that this class can be a lot of work at times but it never really feels like it since everything is so well–motivated. If you're in CS and even remotely curious about economics do yourself a favor and take 136.

I found this course pretty underwhelming. Part of this was high expectations, since I had generally heard good things about it, but as a junior this ended up being one of my more disliked STEM classes I've taken. While the topics are fairly interesting, they aren't crucial to know and there were several things about the course I disliked:
1. The homeworks were generally not interesting. While some homework problems were good, a lot of the homeworks were computational and didn't really teach anything. I think this is echoed in other Q comments where people complained about too much busy work.
2. There are reading comprehension questions before each lecture. Even though I usually prefer learning from a textbook, the textbook was really verbose and this makes it hard to read quickly, and I thought this was not a productive use of time.
3. Having a late second midterm and a final project introduces extra work at a time when other classes also have a lot of work, so beware of that.
4. Grading was nitpicky for no reason.
5. Overall workload: This course is not light by any means; there's a lot of homework, comprehension questions, two midterms, and a final project. I personally found that I spent more time on this class than CS 124 (though I think most people may disagree with this) since there's just a lot of busy work that you just have to do, and I didn't think it was a particularly productive use of time.
6. The course covers a lot of topics but not in very much depth. I think this is part of why some of the homeworks are just "busy work," since there isn't enough time to cover the topics deeply, which I found disappointing. Moreover, some of the topics are very specific and you may or may not find them interesting.
That said, Professor Parkes is really nice and if you're interested in EconCS, you may consider taking this class since most of the topics covered are pretty cool.
