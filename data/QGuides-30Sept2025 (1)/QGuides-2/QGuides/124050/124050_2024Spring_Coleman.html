This is a great class to learn about Roman history. The section activities (numismatics, manuscripts, and art museum visit) were fantastic, and the opportunity to study an object in the museum in detail was also cool. If you stay on top of readings, you'll do well in this class.
It's a decent introduction to Roman history and definitely contextualized my previous classes in Latin literature! However, the final in particular was quite difficult because it felt like we needed to memorize certain primary sources and very specific facts about them, which wouldn't necessarily be representative of the historical context it was placed in. Lectures were on the whole interesting but when spending multiple slides on a single piece of artwork or text, could make me lose focus very quickly.
Make sure you engage with the materials they give you and do the readings! The course is fantastic.
The lectures are surely some of the finest you will receive in college. Professor Coleman is an incredible speaker: clear, witty, and deeply informed on the topic of Ancient Rome. It is not an easy class, if thats what youre after, but it is a fascinating look into the Roman monarchy, republic, and empire which will stay with you long after the class ends. Also, youll have loads to talk about over dinner with friends and family on a topic which everything vaguely knows but few are actually informed on. I left with a new confidence in my grasp on the history and an eagerness to take more such classes in the future.
This is a fantastic class if you want to walk out of the semester with a deep understanding of Roman History. As someone who knew very little before this class, I now feel like I am on my way to becoming an expert. The lectures are some of the most engaging I have experienced at Harvard. Professor Coleman is a fantastic lecturer and professor. One of my favorite professors I've ever had!
Classics 97b is a good course, but it is very hard to get an A in. The strengths of the course include its extremely wide breadth of resources provided for you to learn all about Rome, extremely knowledgeable course staff, and a low number of assignments to turn in over the course of the semester. This is also, however, the course's greatest weakness: there is no homework or participation to bring your grade up. The only homework equivalent is the weekly quiz given in section. Pretty much your entire grade is determined by the 2 projects and the final. This means that you can be really very knowledgeable about Rome, but if you don't perform well on one of the culminating assignments, you may not succeed as well as you would like. It was still quite a fun and informative course, it is just not very forgiving in terms of structure.
Lectures can get a little bit into the weeds, you'll be very confused w/o doing any of the reading.
This course is not an easy A. Make sure you read the boatwright and look over the slides for the quizzes. Good notes are essential because lecture slides lack a lot of content.
This course is interesting particularly if you enjoy source and archaeological evidence. While I expected a bit more in terms of Roman history and understanding of society, there was a lot of emphasis on the images, mosaics, and sculptures from the era. I would have liked if there were a bit more emphasis and detail on the history, as I came away from the course feeling like I learned a lot, but could have learned more. There could have been more on history, philosophy, military tactics, etc, though some is covered through the textbook. There are also required attendance at each class, which is not bad, but sometimes the lectures can be a bit dry when discussing source after source. The teaching team, however, makes a strong effort to enable students to engage with the material and the overall structure of the course is sound.
An incredible course to get a survey of Roman history and also develop the skills to think critically about analyzing texts and objects.
This is a required class for the classics concentration, but I would avoid it if Coleman is teaching, as it will be painful and uninteresting if you are a thoughtful person.
I cannot recommend 97B enough, particularly taught by Professor Coleman and TF'd by Alex Reed. The course readings are well–varied and the lectures always interesting. The course is the perfect introduction to the ancient Roman world. Were I not already getting a secondary in the concentration, this course would have easily convinced me to do so. The course covered an immense range of material that ensured students were exposed to material, visual, and textual sources of the Ancient Roman World. Further, Professor Coleman made each lecture exciting and engaging. Her passion for the material shone and easily maintained my engagement throughout lecture. Both Professor Coleman and Alex's clear communication outside of class ensured that I never fretted over assignments. Further, I greatly enjoyed visiting the Harvard Art Museums as a class. The assignments were both rigorous and helpful in developing the range of essays I felt comfortable writing and providing new angles for me to engage with the Roman world.
