Metrics is difficult but incredibly useful for most areas of economics. I highly recommend reading the lecture notes and keeping up with them as best you can. They are dense and slow to read through, but they're probably the most helpful part of this course. Course readings aren't always that integral, but these are!
This is a hard course and not easy to follow.
This will be one of the toughest and most rewarding classes that you could take. If you're reading this, you're either an econ PhD student, an econ–adjacent PhD student, or incredibly precocious undergrad/masters student who plans on doing an econ PhD. If you aren't one of those, this probably isn't worth your time, but if you are one of those and you think you can swing the workload (and have strong background in probability, econometrics, statistical inference, linear algebra, calculus, and proofs), then please take this course and let yourself be amazed. It's tough, but Elie is unmatched in his dedication to building this stuff up from basics to a cohesive theory of generalized least squares, method of moments, and all of their cousins. Built from Elie's meticulous work as well as Gary Chamberlain's mythical notes. Great stuff, you should take it just so that you have seen this material once in your life.
I found this class rewarding but very difficult. I would only recommend taking it if you truly want a theoretical approach to econometrics. As a more applied researcher, I found it to be a bit too abstract and would have liked to run more regressions. Also, if the final is scheduled on the last day of class, try to move it back to exam week so you get a reading period!
The class is not super difficult relative to other graduate courses ~ I think the material is about equivalent to what you do in STAT 144 and STAT 189. However, I do not think this course is as good as those other options. There is very little practice material, and the lectures proceed very quickly. My feeling about the course was that either (1) you had seen the material somewhere else before, and seeing an ``econometric" perspective was a nice review, or (2) you had not seen the material before, and the lack of practice material meant it would be incredibly challenging to learn it for the first time. The professor (Elie Tamer) is a great lecturer and a cool guy though.

I would not take this course unless (1) you are interested in Economics grad school, and just want a graduate economics class on your transcript, or (2) you're a grad student and its required. I have heard much better things about the grad classes in the STAT department (STAT 244, STAT 289).
