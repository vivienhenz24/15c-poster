Prof. Trefethen is a really wonderful lecturer. He is extremely bright and gives engaging lectures on what sometimes can be rather dry material. The class leans very heavy on Matlab under him which might be challenging if you don't have any Matlab background. Exams were rather difficult and changed structure on each. Problem sets are not impossible and office hours and numerous in addition to Ed. I wouldn't go out of my way to take the course, but if it's an option or requirement for you, I'd recommend it and wouldn't be concerned about it.
You take this class in order to understand the existing algorithms that have changed the course of human history, mainly over the past 120 years. Why they were created, by who, what kinds of problems do they solve, and what mathematical principles do they use. This course focuses on taking the mathematical theory that exists, putting limits on it, and making it into applied mathematics. You take this class in order to be a part of it all. With the intention that you'll be contributing to the science of computation. If your program requires this class and you don't have any interest in this material I really recommend you reconsider if your program is right for you, or be open to having your perspective change. One of the G1 students that took the course this year said to me, "I don't see how I will ever use this in my research." I think that statement represents a fundamental misunderstanding of what this course is about and a really good opportunity for that student to learn. This class is about how you are able to even accomplish your research in the first place. If you are unable to answer the question, "How are computers able to represent a number?" and you use numbers (numerics) and software in your research, then you really need to take this class.
Make sure you go to lecture. It is optional, but if you want to do well, it is mandatory. Make sure you do the reading before the class to understand the material better. You cannot take your eyes off the ball, it will be very costly to do so.
The lectures are wonderfully engaging, be present and enjoy seeing a master at work! On a logistical note, make sure to do the homeworks well, because you'll get many more points from the homeworks than the quizzes.
This class is just a collection of buzzwords where dozens of topics are mentioned in a fly–by manner in lecture and nothing is taught in–depth and you dont engage with 90% of the material in any context. Homework assignments have little useful applications of the material covered and the vast majority of the content is never touched upon in the homework. The professor is passionate and enthusiastic about the material but his pace of lectures is completely disconnected from the pace of students comprehension. I found lectures absolutely pointless for learning because nothing was explained in depth. We covered a million different topics in the span of mere weeks, some of which the other classes take the whole semester to build up to. This class was taught with the assumption that were already familiar with most of the material covered. The midterm exams tested our understanding of tricky topics from basic linear algebra instead of the actual algorithms discussed in class. Overall this was one of the worst classes Ive taken at Harvard and a complete waste of credits in my senior fall. Unfortunately, its a required class for the concurrent SM degree in CSE so I had no choice but to take it. If youre a practically–minded person looking to learn and engage with real useful applications of math, avoid this class.
