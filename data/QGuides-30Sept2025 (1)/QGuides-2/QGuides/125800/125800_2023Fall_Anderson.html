SCRB 10 is a difficult course whether or not you come from a strong biology background because it is fundamentally different in the way that it assesses the course material. On the one hand, it's probably one of the only courses at Harvard that will teach you how to think like a scientist (you'll become an expert in experimental design and techniques), but is also the first time you've probably been exposed to this kind of thinking. The course is great because you don't get busy work on most weeks, you don't have large assignments, but on the weeks you do have p–sets, writing assignments, a midterm, or all 3, the course really picks up. Office hours are really helpful for p–sets (Jake and Maddy's are amazing), but at the same time are really crowded and communication wasn't the best when office hours are canceled. The exams in SCRB 10 are both a marathon and sprint, because you are always writing something very quickly, but also seem to never have enough time. However, the final was extremely difficult because at this point, you have 10+ experimental methods and need to be able to rapid fire apply them given a set of data or an experiment that you may never have seen before because the exams are all extremely long. Other classes that are this pattern–matching heavy (i.e. Stat 110) provide multiple practice exams (10+) to help develop this skill, but you only get 2 in SCRB 10, so just make sure during the exam, you are really distilling the questions down to what experimental technique am I supposed to be using.
