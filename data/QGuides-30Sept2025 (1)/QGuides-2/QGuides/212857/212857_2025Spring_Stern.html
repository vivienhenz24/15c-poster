Super fun class! Lectures can feel a bit long, but the content is very thorough. Sections were very fun, especially when we went on field trips, and the final project is super fun and creative where you get to make your own book!
This courses exposes you to interesting material and social aspects (though less of the latter) of the modern–day book and its historic predecessors, as well as discussing how these changed over the course of history. In terms of work this course is on the trivial part of the spectrum. The reading responses do not require a deep reading, a skimming is sufficient though this obviously comes at the cost of not understanding the material that well.

David is truly passionate about the topic, so if you have any questions or particular interests he will be happy to help. His lectures, however, are rather dull, as they are read off of a script. Since lecture attendance is not taken strictly, this is not a big problem however. Grading is (from what I can tell before the final grade) very generous. The highlight of this course are the sections however. Even though the mandatory attendance might seem off–putting at first, you get the unique opportunity to see sides of Havard's library system (preservation center, printing press, ....) that you are normally not exposed to.
The teaching team did a great job of arranging fun excursions for sections, and they are all extremely kind and understanding. The final project can be a lot of fun. I was slightly disappointed that the course did not attempt to explore histories of the book outside of the West, but the professor is very aware of this bias in his knowledge.
This is a great gened with a dedicated and engaged teaching staff. You will learn and lot and go on exciting site visits. As a book lover I highly recommend it!
This was a fascinating class that delves into bookmaking, book culture, and other aspects of book materiality. But what really stands out are (1) the incredible teaching staff –– all extremely passionate and knowledgeable –– and (2) the opportunities to see and touch and interact with some amazing Harvard resources. Rare books, printing presses, and more –– it's super fascinating and for that I highly recommend this course.
This course was a deep dive into the history of bookmaking and the styles that have evolved over the past 6000 years. The lecture component was mostly engaging but could benefit from more variety and student participation. Professor Stern is extremely knowledgable and kind.
Not too bad, lots of reading responses. But, overall, it is an interesting class! My TF was amazing, and so section was very enticing. Lecture could be very boring at times, but for the most part, it is a unique GENED class.

I really enjoyed learning about different book platforms (clay tablets, scrolls, manuscripts, etc) and felt like the section outings were a strength in visualizing the readings. You have the opportunity to see a Guttenberg bible, Shakespeare's first folio, and some really unique artist's books. My only complaint is I felt like some students weren't engaged in lecture (it happens, I know), but David puts a lot of effort into teaching and the material we learn about is so applicable to education and life. I really enjoyed this class and felt like the trips to different libraries and museums alone makes this class worth taking!

For a GENED class, it's pretty low work, and I'd recommend it to anyone trying to just fulfill their A&C requirement as a STEM major. The readings are basically optional and not necessary to completing the coursework, though are pretty interesting. Sections are pretty cool as they are hands on and you get to actually see/touch old rare books. The capstone project is also very easy: you construct a book (and can go to the art museum to get help for it) and write an essay about it. Overall this class is easy to get an A, and you can decide just how much effort you want to put in.

What an amazing Gened, and what great teaching staff. I'm a CS guy, and I don't really like books: this class was out of my comfort zone. But it was so engaging and informative. This is the transformative college experience they tell you about. This class was so well taught: the professor is so knowledgeable and engaging. There is one short writing assignment that you do before each lecture. The readings for this class are a bit lengthy, but you can get by with skimming them as they're not too difficult to understand.
