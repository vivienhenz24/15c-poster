TAKE THIS CLASS! Prof. Yang is amazing, the material is amazing, everything about it is amazing. Can be a bit time–consuming during some weeks, but I'd say still lower than most other CS courses I took. PSETs are a good review of lecture material, though some problems felt more like tedious work than educational... Labs on the other hand were great (and you get to use a real FPGA board and program it from your laptop and see the results!!). If you have any interest in the hardware at all, you will enjoy this class.
This is an interesting, but difficult class. As a CS concentrator, it felt very grounding to understand the lower level of the stack (circuits, combinational logic, processors, chips, semis). After taking this course, I am much more appreciative (& in awe) of the innovation that goes on at the hardware level on processors (CPUs/GPUs) and memory devices (SRAM/DRAM/FLASH).

Computer architecture isn't very mathy or theory based, which is nice. It's very visual and spatial–reasoning heavy. So if you like puzzles or are more of a visual thinker, the concepts won't be that difficult to understand.

It is a fair amount of work with the weekly PSETs and FBGA labs, but doable. The textbook is actually really well–written, and they encourage you to use AI to help you through the materials. PSETs + labs would easily take 2–5x longer if you don't use any AI.

Lectures can be very dense, but Woody is one of the best STEM lecturers I've had at Harvard. It's very refreshing to see in the CS department, in which most lectures are not worth attending. He is clearly passionate and extremely knowledgable about the topic, and often relates course material back to the real world drama of the semiconductor industry.

Really glad I took this course.
I think Yang has done an incredible job revamping this course! Despite the massive overhaul, the course seemed largely smooth with very few rough edges. The lectures were really fun and as someone with little hardware knowledge, I learned a lot.
This course does take more time that I imagined though, sheerly out of the total number of things to be submitted. With 10 Psets, 8 lab submissions, there's a lot of deadlines and sometimes they are pretty close. The assignements are pretty helpful and fun, just a little more space between them would be appreciated!
1410 is an interesting course with very engaging and important content, even for CS concentrators. The labs are extremely well done and help you understand the content very thoroughly; for example, for the last lab, we get to build a MIPS processor from scratch on our own, which was both incredibly difficult and incredibly rewarding. It's also a relatively small class, which was refreshing as a CS concentrator and it was nice to get to know my peers and professor pretty well.

That being said, although it was Woody's first time teaching this course so this is somewhat understandable, I found 1410 to be poorly organized in a few ways that made it much more difficult as a student. First of all, there were frequent errors in the course slides, which is fine once again given this semester being Woody's first iteration, but the amount of errors on a regular basis often got in the way of doing psets (ex: trying to reference slides but different slides saying conflicting things). Additionally, there were a few times where the psets or lab skeleton code itself had errors, which was frustrating as it led to several hours of wasted/misguided work. I also found office hours (at least the ones I was able to make it to) to be quite unproductive ––– there was often no centralized answer keys and I would get conflicting feedback from different TFs about the same problems. If these issues are resolved, which I am optimistic they will be, the course has the potential to be great, so I do recommend this course for anyone interested in hardware or lower–level programming with minor reservations.
This class was great. Professor Yang is an engaging lecturer, and the real–world experience he brings to the class is invaluable. The textbook is a great resource for understanding the topics in–depth, and the labs provide relevant project experience. I believe anyone planning to work in tech could benefit from understanding how computers work under the hood, and this course provides exactly that.
This class seems to have had a massive upswing with Professor Yang teaching the course. The labs take a bit to figure out, but the course material is very fleshed out and understandable. I took this alongside CS61 and it was a fantastic complement to the programming side of systems.

This class was incredible. Professor Yang's teaching was honestly refreshing, as he explored not only the technical content related to digital design/computer architecture but also discussed the social, political, and economic forces that affected the field on multiple occasions. As a person he also just has a lot of relevant industry experience in different areas that he is more than willing to share with students. Outside of lecture, we had labs with weekly check–offs, and they were probably the best part of the course. The labs were challenging but incredibly rewarding because you did nearly everything from design to implementation to testing. These were then followed by the weekly check–offs/sections. Rather than just grinding out the assignments and turning them in, the check–offs actually forced you to explain your thinking and were a great way to get feedback (plus you could get points for other questions even if the lab itself was late). As a whole, I think all of the assignments were things that you could reasonably figure out with enough time and effort (and without going to office hours). There certainly were some issues with labs and assignments, but as a whole the course staff was receptive to ideas , were flexible with late assignments, and encouraged students to point out such errors and give feedback (rewarded it in fact).
Challenging but rewarding.
