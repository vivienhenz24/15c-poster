This was the best class I have taken at Harvard, and I am by no means the type of student to say this lightly. Prof. Vjay expresses the urgency behind the topics that he is teaching. He is the best lecturer I have had. Not to mention the textbook in this course is phenomenal. If you are the type of person that feels that you go by smester by semester not really learning. Take this class!
This course is so much fun and incredibly engaging IF you are interested in computer system design, and you do not have extensive experience with computer hardware yet. It is definitely an introductory–level class, but it covers a lot of material from the transistor–level all the way into the processor and cache architecture. Vijay is awesome and so enthusiastic about the material; he also bakes spectacular brownies. The course support is very helpful too and consistently available in lectures and on Ed. A legendary past TF named Dhilan recorded useful, engaging section videos. And I've heard the textbook is useful...

Some notes: First, this class does have a lot of moving parts, between lectures, section videos, problem sets, labs, and a midterm exam. The problem sets are all fairly short and very manageable. The team labs will take up the majority of your time in this class. The main advice I have: like in life, find a good partner, and get carried. These labs expect some familiarity with virtual environments and Git; they use a finicky coding language called SystemVerilog to implement hardware in a coding environment. Students are also given FPGA boards to program and test, which is quite neat. The labs are typically completed over multiple weeks, and especially at the start, involve a lot of setup and troubleshooting without extensive instruction. These labs will be fun if you enjoy the material; otherwise, they could be a source of great sadness.

This class is a lot of work and is often frustrating. The exams are longer and harder than they probably should be and, if you're anything like me, the labs will test your will to live. However, if you stick with it, there is definitely something to appreciate here. For me, it was the fact that these complicated machines, before a complete black box to me, have distinctly human fingerprints on them. You can UNDERSTAND the design of these devices and, if you're at all curious and are willing to delve into deep, you'll (probably) be better for it.
My best advice is to ask questions, ask more questions, and keep asking questions until you understand. VJ and the course staff are very supportive and the only way they can help you is by finding where you are stuck which only comes through conversations/discussions you have about the material.

If this course can be your top priority for a semester (i.e. the hardest class you take), I really think it is so much better than CS61 for the systems requirement. Finding a good lab partner is also a big component of being successful in this class. If you keep up with the textbook readings, it's such a great class and I learned so much!! This is a very challenging but rewarding course! It is really cool to see the levels of abstraction of going from circuits to computing systems, and be able to apply them in practical examples (like coding from logic gates a traffic light controller and a simple CPU). But to be able to understand this requires a lot of time, effort, and practice! There is a lot of work, especially in the coding–based labs, that helps reinforce the the concepts. And lectures go really fast since there is a lot of content to cover, so reading the textbook really helps clarify concepts. But at the end, you will have a good understanding how microcontrollers and computers really work from a hardware perspective, which is so satisfying!
