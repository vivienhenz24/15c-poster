What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

Comments
If you get into this class, absolutely take it! I wanted to take it as a fun elective, and it was so worth it. The class size is very small, and you spend a lot of time (~8 hours/week) working with your classmates, which will leave you very close by the end of the semester. If you want to learn a lot about the technical side of photography or post–processing (Photoshop, etc.), then this class is probably not what you're looking for. We would have conversations during class about things that felt very philosophical at times: the "role" of the photographer in society, the importance of photography as art, finding one's individuality and honing a "unique perspective" that "only one person can bring into the world," etc. The teaching staff this semester was phenomenal; if Patrice and Navid are teaching, take this. They feel like close friends to me. They are so understanding, compassionate, and genuine. This course isn't anything too overwhelming (assignments usually consisted of 72 photos/week, printed contact sheets, printed works for critiques, etc.), but you will definitely get more out of the class if you really commit.
TAKE THIS CLASS! Patrice is a wonderful person and more than a regular professor. She genuinely cares about her students and our well being. Every class, there was a pantry of snacks, tea, coffee, mandarins, and granola bars. She even brought back Alaska smoked salmon and crackers for us when she visited. I think apart from learning to be a better photographer, Patrice ensures that we also learn life skills, giving us practical advice. She is also a real person and has a personality, unlike some other professors I have taken classes with. Navid is an expert when it comes to technical skills. He will teach you everything about editing your phots to obtain the best print possible. I really enjoyed this class and would love to take another class with Patrice.
Beyond the time and effort it requires, which in themselves are often signifiers of some positive outcome by the end, this course was a failure for me. I learned relatively little about the craft of photography, as someone who came in with everything to learn, with little prior experience.

I would say I come away with this course confident in:
1) managing aperture, shutter speed, and ISO
2) how to use flash and white balance
3) file and hard drive management and conversion
4) printing prints of diverse sizes the proper way

While those are great and substantive skills, they are vastly less than what should have been learned in a semester of this class, which had nearly 6 hours of class time a week, on top of a 2 hour lab. We never learned composition –– as just one example, the "rule of thirds" was something I heard about only through a friend in a different intro to photography class. Color theory and editing were nominally explained, but the instruction was primarily a demonstration that, if you wanted to, you could choose to adjust, say, exposure or highlights. I never understood how to look at a photograph with a discerning eye and make those edits productively; to the very end of this class, I was merely pushing a slider on the screen to see what number made the photograph look nicest, frustrated that we had never meaningfully learned to technically analyze and edit a photograph.

In class, there was minimal instruction, and time was spent meandering between watching miscellaneous videos the Prof liked, taking extended breaks, or spending extensive time discussing general life lessons. Patrice Helmar is a professor of genuine wisdom who shared many thoughtful and moving things, but the difficult fact that those things were essentially all I got in regard to learning anything about photography from them –– besides periodic instruction in the lab from our TF –– made it hard to appreciate them as a teacher. The primary advice given by them when asked questions like how to improve the visual appeal of a given photograph, or decide whether to print it in color or black and white, were responses like "sing the song that only you know," and "show us your world." They were positive and empowering sentiments for a teacher to say, but those ideas cant work when they're replacing real, concrete instruction that we needed to get better.

I left this class feeling defeated and a bit embarrassed that I had so little to show for so much time spent in class, time that could have been spent doing a million other things. ALL THIS SAID: this course seemed to work wonderfully for people who were already trained in photography; it gave them an opportunity to practice, experiment, and get a bit of feedback on their work, and engage with people who were true experts in the field. Those kids in my class very demonstrably improved by the end and I imagine will have positive things to say about this course.

If you do not know photography, do not take this course to learn it.
If you are interested in taking pictures and want to learn the basics of photography, definitely consider taking this course. It is a LOT of work (two 2.5–hour meetings each week, plus lab time and time to take pictures outside the course), but it is completely doable depending on your schedule––and in the end, I am sure you will learn a lot and be very proud of the work you end up creating. Professor Helmar is an incredible instructor, and Navid is a very dedicated TF. They were very accommodating, understanding, and gave great feedback. They are always willing to help the students when needed, even outside the class schedule. Overall, this course was one of my best academic experiences at Harvard, and I would recommend it with great enthusiasm.
