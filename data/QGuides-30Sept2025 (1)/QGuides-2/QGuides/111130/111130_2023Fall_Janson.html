This class probably had the highest average level of math/stat aptitude any class I've taken as an undergrad. Since grade distributions are roughly fixed (about half of the class gets A–range, half gets B–range), you likely need perfect/near–perfect exam scores to get an A as everyone is very good at math. Everything in the class is difficult, but my biggest frustration is that problems (especially on the exams) are often difficult because they require clever algebraic manipulation or spurts of inspiration, which aren't a function of understanding the content and can be difficult to coax out in a limited time frame.

That being said, I actually really enjoyed the course content when I could read and think at my own pace. I found selective inference and the associated problems to be really enjoyable. The lecture notes and Prof. Janson's lecturing are both excellent. While the outcome–based aspects of the course were frustrating and demoralizing, the actual teaching and content were pretty enjoyable. I may have just been out of my depth, but as long as you are comfortable with Stat 110 content, maximum likelihood, and pretty good at math, it is definitely a doable class.

I'm an undergraduate, and I took this course with MATH 112 (Real Analysis), but no STAT 210 (I also was not enrolled concurrently).

1. Regarding the workload: I feel like past Q–guides exaggerated the assigned workload for the class. PSETs are bi–weekly, so if you procrastinate it probably feels like a lot more than it is. However, if you space out your work effectively, I think it is about 1.5x the amount of work in STAT 111.

2. Regarding the course: there are a lot of really, really smart people in the course, and the class is graded on a curve. In all honestly, this is the first course in my life I think I will get below an A– in. I did really well on all the homeworks, went to every office hour, and did all the midterm/final practice materials. Despite that, I was on the 25th percentile for the midterm, and probably (?) did similarly on the final. Only about 1/3 of the class shows up to lectures, yet 1/3 of the class will ace the exams. There are a lot of really smart PhD and master's candidates at Harvard (who consider this course a review) who you will be competing against. In my year, the instructor announced that only the top 1/2 of the class will be in the A/A– range, so the curve is fairly steep.

3. Tips: I would take this course after STAT 210, or at least take it concurrently. The tests are structured as 16–hour exams with only 2 problems, but you need a lot of Statistical creativity and mathematical intuition to solve the problems, which is why I think 210 is helpful. Also, advanced real analysis or functional analysis will help a lot in this course. I think those courses but without STAT 210 are probably also enough preparation. Besides that, start the PSET early, and if you're not sure about a solution then you should ask during office hours. I would take short 5–minute breaks during the tests and pause to ask "Do I think this strategy is right, or am butting my head against a wall?"
The teaching component of this course is very good: Prof. Janson is an excellent lecturer who explains why we should care about the topics and how to apply the proof strategies beyond the theorems we covered. The lecture notes are very clear, and the homeworks are relevant and helpful. One minor frustration on the homeworks was how long the grading took: homeworks always took at least 2 weeks to be graded, and many weren't graded for over a month.

The exams were bad. The midterm was a 24–hour take–home exam that took the full 24 hours. Half of the points on the midterm came from two questions that were barely related to the course material and essentially required lucky creativity. If you figured out the trick, you got all the points, and if you didn't you got almost none. Put another way, over 10 points of our final grade came down to a high–variance game of chance mostly removed from the class concepts. The final was supposed to be 24 hours but was shortened to 16 hours because someone complained that 24–hour exams prevent them from sleeping (if you want to sleep, please do so, but don't drag the rest of us with you). The material on the final was more relevant to the course, and the questions were less about pure creativity, but the exam was still far from a representative survey of the class material. Additionally, these exams are ripe for cheating since working together is not allowed. When the point of the exam is to see how many "Aha" moments you can generate or research in 24 hours (since writing up the answer is usually trivial), having 2 brains is at least twice as helpful as having 1 brain. It strikes me as suspicious when a quarter of the class earns a perfect score on what should have been an extremely difficult exam, and this harms everyone who took the exam alone since the class is curved.
In general, this is an excellent class and a rewarding experience. The homework is a little long given that we only have 12 days and problems are very difficult. The take–home exams are also difficult.
