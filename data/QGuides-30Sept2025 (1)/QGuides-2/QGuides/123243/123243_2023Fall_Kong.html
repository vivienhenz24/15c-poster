This course is very rewarding. The material is significant, but this largely picks up where 131 leaves off with alg top. I would highly encourage anyone who enjoyed 131 to continue with 231 after taking some more algebra. The workload is hefty if this is your first time seeing the material. But if you are interesting in topology this is a must take.

Hana is a wonderful teacher and even though the class was 9am it was a joy to attend lecture! She cares immensely for her students and is always there to help if we need it!
This course covers a strong set of material in algebraic topology, with well–paced assignments. Furthermore, Hana is an excellent lecturer and a joy to be in class with! I would recommend this course to anybody with a strong background in topology up to fundamental groups, but I would be wary of the May textbook if it is used in the future.
This class introduces you to algebraic topology, which is a very interesting and important field of pure mathematics. However, think twice before taking the class if the reference textbook is JP May, as this textbook is very densely written and makes it extremely difficult to gain a comprehensive understanding of the course material.
May's book, which was used in this iteration of 231a, is weird but decent. I appreciated how the course heavily used Category Theory. If you took 131 or 55b, you should definitely take 231a. It's foundational and totes doable.
This class made me and most of my friends like math a lot less. I came in wanting to go to grad school. That is not how I came out. The lectures are actually not bad if you know what is going on, but the textbook ensures that you won't because it is unreadable. The chapters are 2–6 pages, with semesters' worth of material presented without any explanation. The structure of the course was also strange; apparently it is usually structured the reverse of how it was this year, which seems to make a lot more sense. I do not know anyone who followed the content of this course all the way to the end, or even the 2/3 mark.
Check the textbook in advance! There are several approaches to this subject, and different methods are more appealing to different people. If you like category theory, May's book is great! The class really builds on and returns to categories at each step. (I've also heard that the active areas in algebraic topology use a lot of category theory, though I don't know much at all.) However, if you want a more intuitive approach, Hatcher might be preferable. Just make sure that you choose to take 231 when it's taught in a way that appeals to you.
i really liked algebraic topology in 55, which was why i took this course. i thought that this course wouldnt be as bad as 55 but i was sorely mistaken. to be fair, hana does a good job of talking us through the material and giving us nice pictorial intuition behind the key concepts in algebraic topology. however, there are many occasions where she just skims over the proof of more key concepts in the class. during those occasions, i was so lost that i couldnt even ask good questions. when i try to go back and turn to the textbook for answers, i get nothing because peter may's book is actually unreadable. I have not heard of a single person that could read the book and make sense of it. this guy draws diagrams with 0 pictures in an algebraic topology textbook. also i never thought i would see the day when i would actually encounter 1 line proofs of a very non–trivial result in a textbook. even other people who have taken alg top are amazed that we start with a discussion on homotopy theory before eventually moving on to homology later on in the semester. i think a very common consensus is that if we were to use some other textbook, this class would be much much better

furthermore, this class gave me an epiphany: at least for me, i should probably take a class in category theory and homological algebra if i really want to fully understand this material, which i really want to. most of the time when doing assignments, i find myself spending more time searching up basic facts in homological algebra than actually doing anything topological. at least this was the case for me. however, doing the problem sets and spending lots of time thinking about the material actually made me feel like i understood the material fairly well at the end of the day, which was really worth it.
This was an excellent class.
Prof. Hana is a great teacher and the work is hard but rewarding, especially with the category theory heavy approach. Try to use Hatcher as well to supplement you understanding throughout the course and the material will make much more sense.
Math 231A was an interesting and beautiful course. Professor Kong does a wonderful job of explaining concepts during lecture, and TF Lev Kruglyak also deserves a special mention for his clear exposition of alternative perspectives. P–sets for the course were appropriately demanding, albeit some of the problems felt slightly peripheral to what was presented in lecture (maybe this was the intention however as the powerful tools introduced in the course definitely took some playing around with to get used to). The textbook (May) was definitely a strange choice – it did not present the topics in the standard order (which in my opinion was actually a good thing), but it was just way too dry and terse especially for such a beautiful subject. A minor detail, but I personally think a lecture dedicated to explaining the motivation for homology and beginning with more concrete constructions of the abstract concept would have cultivated a better understanding of the idea and a more nuanced appreciation for the beauty of results such as Hurewicz's theorem and Knneth formulae later down the line.
To those who enjoyed Math 55 or Math 131 (students from the former would benefit hugely from the careful review of modules and tensor products introduced in 55A, and those from the latter would benefit from self–studying these two concepts reasonably thoroughly), this is a great next step in the world of topology: personally I feel that the key takeaways from this course – namely a greater exposure to and appreciation of category theory, and an experience using tools from various levels of abstraction – will indeed be very beneficial for any future study of maths in general.
I did not enjoy my time in this course.

Mostly, this was down to some of the structural choices made for the class. I think that the choice to use May as a textbook, in particular, really sunk this class for me. While May might be easy to understand for someone who already has a thorough understanding of the subject, for those of us who had not really seen much of higher homotopy/homology theory, May was a challenging and unrewarding read. Further, the choice to have biweekly (rather than weekly) problem sets was also questionable. I think it just made me feel more and more distant from the material itself.

I thought that Hana was a passionate and highly knowledgeable instructor, but in the end, these two structural choices made this class unenjoyable and unrewarding for me.
The reason why this class did not go very well with a lot of students this year is because of the textbook. Hana seems to be a pretty good teacher who gives a a lot of intuition. Let me give you the following advice: make sure your iteration of the class is based on Hatcher, if it's based on May: RUN
