TL;DR: If you want to take this course, be prepared to practice, practice, and practice some more. This course is definitely not easy, but it is rewarding.

As someone who had taken AP Physics C: Mechanics, most of the topics are the same, but the material is covered at twice the pace and it certainly felt more difficult (although, mileage may vary with that statement; it's been about two years since I took it). However, it would have been far easier if I had taken the time at the beginning to actually do the reading for the class. I often found that the lectures, while having cool demos and providing a serviceable survey of the material, were not adequate for properly understanding the material. There's a difference between saying "yeah, okay, that makes sense" and having a strong grasp of the material. The latter requires a ton of practice (I learned that the hard way with the first midterm). This course was a rewarding experience that I recommend to anyone with the time to devote to it. There's no better feeling then, after hours of struggling, having the ideas coalesce in your mind. If you stick with it, you'll experience this for yourself.

Now, for some specific feedback:
– The labs were garbage; I really think that they could be cut from the course and I would have lost nothing but the opportunity to sink 20+ hours over the course of the semester.

– The exams were difficult. I went into the first exam thinking "I'll be fine" without doing any of the textbook problems. Predictably, I left the exam room thoroughly humbled. Professor Morin says that the key to exams is to know the homework and textbook problems "backwards and forwards" and I definitely agree with him. He puts some of the homework or textbook problems on the exam, so there really is no reason not to do the textbook problems (there's no worse feeling when you go into the exam and you don't recognize any of the problems...). Don't memorize the numbers in the problems, memorize the steps and reasoning you used to get there. Knowing the process is far more important than arriving at the correct answer.

–Section is required (although, attendance is 2% of the grade) and I recommend going for the guided practice.

–Please go to office hours. The amount of help you get will vary (some TFs and CAs will just walk through the whole problem for you, others will only check to make sure your process is correct), but they are useful across the board.

IF YOU'RE LIKE ME...and took AP Physics C: Mechanics in high school and sort of understood what was going on read this carefully.

This class is going to be challenging, BUT IT IS DOABLE AND I RECOMMEND YOU TAKE IT JUST READ THIS.

There will be times when you feel so confused you might question your self–worth, but read the book and go to office hours. I think almost every lecture I went to I had no clue what was going on, but when doing the problem sets things became much clearer. There are example problems in the book that are INCREDIBLY similar to the homework problems (use that to your advantage by using the example problems as a guide to how to approach the homework problems. There is an explanation for each problem, so read them and understand why certain decisions were made when problem solving). Sometimes, I felt that the book didn't really answer all my questions so you know what I did? I WENT TO OFFICE HOURS AND ASKED THEM SO DO THE SAME!

If you're like me, start the problem sets early. If they're assigned on Thursday, start looking through them on Friday and actually getting some stuff down. It's okay if your answers are wrong, but the important part is that you're actually trying to understand the problem while you solve it (that way when you're in office hours asking for help, you can see WHY your answer was wrong, basically learning from your mistakes. If you don't make the mistake, then how will you learn?).

Overall, take the class and do not worry if things don't make sense in lecture. The problem sets will be your main learning opportunity, but you have to do your part and try to actually learn from them using the book and office hours.

The midterms are alright as long as you study for them. You do that by redoing the homework problems and perusall problems that were assigned –– it's some work, but you'll be prepared.
