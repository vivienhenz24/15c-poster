This is a great class if you are interested in challenging your understanding of certain issues, in addition to learning a lot about historical and prevalent issues. There is a lot of reading, but class discussion helps you gain a complete understanding over topics that you were a bit confused about.
I highly recommend this class, it is a good mixture of history, social theory, and government. Although there is a lot of reading, it is very manageable.
This class is purely discussing the assigned readings + three papers. I suggest looking into other freshmen seminars that are more engaging and provide more comprehensive resources to discuss
Take this course! It's a really great survey of a lot of different groups throughout modern history, and you'll find that what you learn about can often have applications to today. My one piece of advice is to force yourself to talk in class. Not only is there a participation part of your grade, but if people are engaged in conversation rooted in their actual beliefs with some healthy debate here and there, it makes the course that much more fun.
This course has potential but not living up to it. There will be a lot of reading every week; often very hard to get through and at times feeling self–explanatory. The course is based on mostly discussing your reading every week, meaning you spend two hours talking with other students about topics that, at the same time, are obvious but also impossible to conclude on. Without having much background in philosophy or social sciences, having some understanding of moral frameworks etc., they become hard to discuss. The instructor is a really smart guy, and prepares the course structure well, but engages too little with the class. Pick only if you're gonna be interested in the readings and wanna do them at some point anyway.
Whitham is an interesting character – he is kind of uncomfortable to be around. The course itself is very white savior–y and doesn't touch on anything in Latin America or Africa. This seminar is definitely not what I thought it would be and it would have been better to take a real class. There were sometimes 300 pages to read for one week which was terrible to do. I got away with not doing the readings by just finding a random quote and making a comment on it or asking a question.

Another thing about the course that I didn't appreciate was that Whitham changed the readings with one week's notice to reflect the the israel–Palestine conflict. The readings were very insensitive and during the discussions he would shut you down if you tried to speak on the context of the entire situation.

Overall, I wouldn't recommend this class unless you are a white history buff.
Dont take this class. It's incredibly boring and the conversation is bad. If you can take a different seminar, I would really recommend doing the other seminar. If you want a pretty easy class for freshmen year then maybe take it but note there is 3 written assignments
This course is great, but enjoying it is highly dependent on doing the readings. There are a lot of readings (which is kind of a turn–off), but discussions are heavily based off of them.
Don't take this class. The lecturer is quite pretentious and sees this class as an opportunity to humble students for no reason. Readings can be interesting, but there are so many it becomes overwhelming. He won't fail you but prep is a lot. If you are interested in terrorism look into EMR.
This class is incredibly eye–opening, engaging, and rewarding. Some may think that the assigned readings are a weighty weekly assignment, but I truly regret the weeks when I skipped a few readings for time reasons. This class will make you want to not only participate in discussion but actively prepare for discussion prior to class.
