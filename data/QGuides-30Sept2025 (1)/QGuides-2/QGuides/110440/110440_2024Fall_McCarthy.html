This class is very fast–paced and can feel overwhelming at times, but I 100% recommend taking it. When I was deciding whether or not to take this course, I weighed 3 important factors: (1) I had always wanted to read some of the texts on the syllabus after hearing about them through so many history classes in high school, and I didn't know when else I would get the chance to read these texts (2) None of the expos course options particularly excited me and I thought it would be better to spend a year engaging with content that actually excited me rather than a semester with content I was not passionate about (3) I am thinking of being on the pre–med track, for which I would need 2 semesters of English classes, and this course would fulfill that relatively early on in my college experience (as opposed to doing expos now and another elective later on). Given these factors, I felt that I should take this course and I wanted to share this thinking in hopes that it can help other students when making the decision of whether to enroll in Hum10. You will definitely be spending a LOT of time on this course because you have 3 meeting times for this class (which is significantly more than expos I believe) because the reading is very intensive. So that could be another factor to consider when deciding whether to enroll. I won't lie, this class can get out of your hands very quickly –– if you miss one book it is very hard to keep going and catch up simultaneously. While it is possible to rely on SparkNotes to know just enough about the texts for lecture and seminar, this will hurt you in the end for the final (which is a pretty intensive exam), and you may feel unfulfilled not having read all the books which were the reason you decided to take this class. Thus, I recommend making a schedule for how much reading you will get done and when (since Lectures are on Tuesday and Seminar Wednesday, view your week as starting on Wednesday and incrementally work through the books until Monday night / Tuesday). I feel that because there are no actionable assignments tied to reading, it can become easy to forget / overlook until the night before lecture (especially when you have a million other assignments/psets/deliverables/things going on freshman year), thus I think this schedule could be incredibly helpful. The lectures are very cool because you are learning from world–renowned professors, but they can be a bit broad sometimes and I feel don't always do complete justice to the text (which is okay given the fact it is only 75 minutes on some of the most iconic texts in history). However, I feel that the seminars are where the real edification happens. It can be daunting to initially start speaking in seminar, you will probably feel that your thoughts/insights/ideas are not as profound as everyone else's and thus not speak. BUT I promise, everyone else feels the exact same way in the beginning (we all ended up talking about this in the beginning), and the professors/TFs are very open to hearing your thoughts –– they want to hear you speak so they won't judge you negatively for whatever you say (unless it is horribly far off from the text/shows you didn't read). It's always best to give it a shot and raise your hand, share your thoughts and questions and actively engage in both seminar and lab. I have become a much deeper thinker from this, I found that after the first few seminars, I approached the texts differently and began to ask much more acute questions while reading –– helping to not only prepare me for the next seminar discussion (and eventually, the final exam) but also extract more meaning from the texts. I have definitely learned how to ask better questions and become a better thinker. At the end of the day, make sure to speak in seminar because (1) you will feel better about actively engaging from the texts / getting your questions answered (2) other students may be thinking the same / could benefit from your points / questions (3) participation MATTERS. To that end, don't put too much pressure on yourself to speak either. The paper assignments can be scary, especially since they are worth so much of your grade, but I 100% recommend start planning early, go to your TF office hours, and go to the writing center so that you can plan your writing around when those meeting times are to ensure you are making progress and on track to revise/edit/flush out all your points in time for the deadline. I also recommend signing up for a hum10 mentor and asking them for help! One thing I regret is not going to office hours of other profs/TFs, it is a rare opportunity to learn from these acclaimed professors, and I wish I went to OH more often.
I hope this information/advice is helpful in deciding (1) whether or not to take this course and (2) how to approach this course once you decide to take it (you definitely should!)
I was very apprehensive initially, but I am very glad I took the course and this is all information I wish I knew ahead of time.
HUM10 is an excellent choice if you're excited about the prospect of reading the foundational texts on the syllabus and finding connections between them. While the course is not perfect, and may not feel especially new if you came from a high school that emphasized literature, it is special in its focus on the pace at which you move. Though you clearly can't expect to gain a truly in depth understanding of each book on the reading list (and though the lecture quality will vary), at the end of the course, if you have put in effort on your part, you will find you've gained both an expanded lexicon of literary terminology to draw upon and an array of cultural references that will contribute to your understanding of the humanities and their evolution. The people you will meet in this course are fantastic––most are people who are truly excited by the humanities, and by their educational experience in the class.
The main aspect of the course I feel could be improved is that of the section. From what I had gathered by the end of the semester, sections were run very differently and to varying degrees of success; there is a chance you will end up with a section leader who, though a great lecturer and fantastic thinker, is not the best at facilitating focused discussion. This is one thing the course does not do; for an introductory course in the humanities, it is oddly devoid of at least a standardized list of suggestions and expectations on how to engage with the texts, which means everyone comes in with different levels of confidence, and that that is never really remedied.
I would recommend this course because of the reading load and the people. I do feel as if I've grown as a consumer of literature, and the environment was one in which I've met some of my best friends to date. I think this is a better option for an expository writing course than expos. The professors and TFs are great and willing to engage with you––definitely attend as many office hours as you can. The only caveat is that if you are hoping to take a hard English class in terms of writing and new thinking, you may be better served by taking a class in the department itself.

This is by far the best class I took freshman fall it is excellent in (almost) every way. The professors are brilliant, kind, and thoughtful; the students are smart and genuinely engaged by the material; and the texts are ones I'll be thinking about for the rest of my life. I've become a better reader and thinker, and more than that, I've been able to understand foundational texts via lectures from intellectuals who are renowned for their work on said texts (think Stephen Greenblatt and Shakespeare). I looked forward to lecture every week; whether providing historical context or close–reading the texts, the professors pulled together something truly wonderful. If you're thinking about concentrating in anything in the humanities, I would recommend taking this course.

My only reservations:

1) Even though this course fulfills the Expos requirement, you will not learn to be a better writer from it (or your experience will vary greatly depending on your TF). There's no writing practice outside of two essays; I have not improved as a writer over the course of the semester.

2) Be warned that some students are, yes, overeager to show off their knowledge. There will be people who stroll into lecture asking you why you haven't read Purgatorio or Paradiso in Dante's Commedia; there will be people making oblique connections to Nietzsche not because they know what they're talking about, but they want to *seem* like they know what they're talking about. Be ready to distinguish between people who are genuinely smart and interesting and people who are simply "well read" (or think they are well read). That being said, I've met some of my closest friends through this class; there are some truly amazing, brilliant people, and they far outweigh the irritating ones.

3) At Visitas, they will tell you this is not a "great books" course. It is 100% a "great books" course. While it's not advertised as the Western canon, it essentially is... it's a bit saddening considering that equivalent courses at other universities (think Stanford, for example) will read texts like the Epic of Gilgamesh and the Quran, but we don't. Course leads have said that it's a faculty/staffing issue, but that's even worse, in my opinion you don't have enough non–Western scholars to have non–Western texts in the course? That isn't to say that you won't learn from the course you will but just know that you're going into a Western lit class.

Overall, however, the advantages overwhelmingly outweigh the disadvantages. This is a class I'll be thinking about through winter break it's where I have learned the most, and seen genuine "intellectual vitality" (if they're still throwing that buzzword around when you're reading this).
If you're hesitating, take this class. Yes, it's a lot of reading, but most of it is genuinely enjoyable and offers a break from your other work (especially if you're in a lot of STEM classes). And honestly, watching my friends suffer through expos... I wouldn't say it's that much more work. The final is definitely a ton of material, but you'll look back on the studying and be happy you did it –– it really reinforces concepts and brings your class closer together. On that note –– yes, some of the people can be a bit snobbish/pretentious, but they're also some of the most thoughtful readers you'll meet and a lot are genuinely there just because they love to read and talk about books in a serious way. Which should not be a bad thing –– in fact, it's probably the coolest part of the class.
