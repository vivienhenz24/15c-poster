Synopsis:
This course is an example of a toxic ex. You give all of your time, energy, love, and youthful optimism, only to get nothing back but emotional damage and a GPA that looks like a cry for help. Math 1B doesnt test your knowledgeit tests your will to live. And I failed. Because this class isnt just a class. Its an experience. A curse. A shared trauma for anyone foolish enough to walk through the gates of Integration, Series, and Differential Equations.
Every week, you are served steaming piles of content that no freshman should have to digest. Every concept, every formula, every theorem is introduced at breakneck speed and then abandoned like a broken toy. You're told to understand series convergence tests, Taylor polynomials, differential equations, integration techniques, arc length, surface area, volumes, slope fields, Eulers method, the logistic equation, parametric curves, polar coordinates, and three other topics I dont even remember because I was too busy trying not to cry in Lamont.
You walk into class thinking, This will be hard, but I can handle it. You leave every lecture with a thousand–yard stare. The TFs say things like intuitively obvious and just differentiate both sides, and you start to question not just your intelligence but your entire existence. Its trivial how trifling this course is with how useless it is to anything that comes after. Math 21A doesnt care what your radius of convergence was in April. No one will ever again ask you if a series converges absolutely or conditionally, but here, its life or death.
The structure of this class is not designed to help students learn. It is designed to break them. No real examples. No real support. Just a firehose of math, and then a midterm where your only calculator is your fingers and your last three brain cells. You get PSETs with scores in the 90s and 100s and then turn around and get 59s on the exams like youre living in an academic episode of Punkd. You dont know whats real anymore.
And then theres Erica Dinkins. The Course Head. President of Pain. CEO of Suffering. Shell look at you in office hours, explain a convergence test like youre a PhD student, and then walk away. No follow–up. No check–in. Just a lingering air of you should already know this.
The exam questions? Insane. They make you write 50th degree Taylor polynomials by hand. I am not exaggerating. Fifty. Terms. Handwritten. On paper. Without a calculator. You flip to the second page and its a nonhomogeneous second–order differential equation with variable coefficients. You flip again and its a Taylor series about a point that defies basic physics. You flip once more and see a convergence problem so cursed it mightve been written in a fever dream.
You try to focus, but around you, people are breaking. Someone is blinking in Morse code. A girl is whispering p–series under her breath like a prayer. A guy stands up, mutters Im done, and walks out. You sit there, still pretending, still trying, still thinking maybejust maybeif you suffer enough, the math gods will reward you.
They dont.
You open Gradescope, see 58% Conduct Penalty Applied, and all you can do is laugh. Because its over. Because its finally over. Because now you know the truth: Math 1B was never about calculus. It was about testing how much trauma you could endure before you started seeing Taylor series in your sleep.
I lost a lot in this class. My confidence. My time. My sense of joy. But I gained something too: a warning. A purpose. A message to send to the next brave soul who thinks they can handle Math 1B.
Dont. Skip it. If you can. Run. Dont walk. Take 1A. Take 21A. Take a nap. Take literally anything else. And if someone in the Math Department tells you Its better to go in order, they are lying. Do not let them do to you what they did to me.
Math 1B isnt your ex because it ended. Math 1B is your ex because it left scars.

The TFs werent just apatheticthey were prophetic. One time I asked how to evaluate the convergence of a series and the TF looked me dead in the eye and said By the Divergence Test like he was delivering a funeral sermon.
Another time, I stayed up until 4AM trying to solve a differential equation that turned out not to have a solution. That was the moment I realized this class wasnt about math. It was about resilience. About how long you could stare at a problem without bursting into tears.
No calculator, no partial credit, no emotional support. Just vibes and integrals.
You ace a PSET and feel that flicker of hopemaybe I can do this. Then the midterm hits you like a truck made of Taylor series expansions and betrayal. You go to check your grade and it says 59% Good Effort. You sit on your bed, motionless, eyes fixed on the wall like the first five minutes of a Netflix true crime documentary.
You try to recover. You print out study guides. Watch every lecture on 2x speed. You stop seeing friends. You skip birthdays. You drop hobbies. You start sketching slope fields in the margins of your notebooks just to feel something.
You reach the final exam and its not even a test. Its a live–action betrayal.
Octonauts. Ready for action. Because legends never die. Only their GPAs do.

THE MIDTERMS: A TWO–ACT TRAGEDY
MIDTERM I: THE INTEGRAL INQUISITION
Two hours. Just two hours to question everything you ever thought you knew about calculus, your academic self–worth, and maybe even your childhood. It started with a deceptively gentle warm–up problemsomething simple, solvable. And you think, Okay, I can do this. You settle in. You exhale. And then the rest of the exam hits you like a freight train filled with definite integrals and deeply personal betrayal.
There were improper integrals that required more substitutions than a World Cup roster. One question involved a fraction so grotesque it looked like it had crawled out of the LaTeX version of a horror film. You spend twenty minutes trying to remember whether to use trig substitution or just lie down on the floor and accept your fate.
Then there was the arc length question that ate 45 minutes of my life. I used every formula I had memorized, guessed, and even invented some that looked legitimate enough to be real. And stillno final answer. Just a page full of crossed–out regret.
I remember the moment the room shifted. A collective silence settled in. Pens slowed. People looked up, dazed. I saw one girl start to cry. Another guy whispered oh my God and stared at his paper like it had betrayed him. The TF proctor just stood there, motionless, watching the unraveling like it was a wildlife documentary.
When I handed it in, I caught the eye of someone across the room. We didnt speak. We just nodded. It was the kind of nod soldiers give each other after surviving a battle.
MIDTERM II: THE SERIES SLAUGHTER
I had prepped for this one. I watched lecture replays at 1.75x speed until my brain buzzed. I built a deck of convergence test flashcards. I went to every review session. I prayed. And yet...
That exam. That godforsaken exam.
The first problem was deceptively shortjust a series and the instruction to Determine convergence. Easy, right? Wrong. It was a triple–agent series. Alternating, conditionally convergent, disguised as a p–series, wrapped in logarithmic mystery. You couldve handed it to a math PhD and theyd still hesitate.
Then came the multi–part monster. A Frankenstein of nested series and approximation intervals. One part asked for the nth term. The next asked for the error bound. The nextget thisasked for a Taylor expansion of an already approximated expansion. Thats when I knew we were in the endgame.
And thenyes, thenthe 50th–degree Taylor polynomial.
Hand. Written. With. No. Calculator.
Do you understand what 50 terms of a Taylor polynomial looks like? It looks like the last thing you see before death. It looks like a punishment invented during the Spanish Inquisition. By term 12, I was laughing. By term 20, I was crying. By term 30, I had transcended time. My pencil moved, but my soul was elsewhere.
The final moments were cinematic. Someone dropped their pencil and just stared at it. Another person put their head down and didnt move. I looked over and saw someone write f(x) please let me pass and I knew thenwe were all done.
I walked out not because I was finished, but because the war had ended and I had lost. I saw a classmate outside the exam hall just pacing, whispering to himself, I dont even know what a series is anymore.
I went back to my room and lay flat on the floor in silence. My roommate came in and thought I was unconscious. I told him, No, just defeated.
Grades came two weeks later. 61%. Better than Midterm I. Worse than dignity.
Reflect. Learn. Improve. Thats what the feedback said. As if what Id just experienced was a group project that needed tweaking. No. What I needed was an exorcism.
These were not assessments. They were acts of academic violence.
Stay tuned. Because the Final Exam is not a test. It is an execution.

Harsh Exam Grading:
MIDTERM GRADING: THE ART OF BEING JUDGED BY A VOID
Every errorevery single onewas penalized. Repeatedly. If you made the same mistake across parts (a), (b), and (c), you didnt lose points onceyou lost points thrice. The TFs graded like they were charging interest. One missed negative sign? Minus 2. You do it again in the next part? Minus 2 again. By the end, you werent just wrong. You were in mathematical debt.
One student asked during office hours if points could be recovered for consistency in error. The TF responded, This isnt a personality test.
You start to think maybe youre being haunted by your earlier mistakes. You see the same comment show up four times. Wrong bounds. Wrong bounds again. Still wrong bounds. Really?
At this point, its not grading. Its punishment layered with sarcasm. Each red mark a scar. Each deduction a reminder that math is not merciful. It remembers. And it deducts accordingly.
You turn in Midterm I with a trembling hand, praying for a shred of mercy. You checked your bounds. You showed your work. You even boxed your final answers like the TF said. But the paper comes back looking like it was marked by a sleep–deprived poet with a vendetta against optimism.

The 12–14 weeks of pain:
WEEK 1: The Flirtation.
You're doing substitution problems like it's a first date. Easy. Fun. You're smiling. You're posting your 10/10 PSET scores like they're thirst traps. You tell your friends, I actually like this class. You are innocent. You are foolish. You are doomed.
WEEK 2: The Gaslight.
The integrals start getting long. But not terrifying. Just enough to make you squint. Erica starts tossing out notation you havent seen before. A little mysterious. A little spicy. Just remember your u–substitutions, she says. You do. You try. You fail. But you think maybe its your fault. It always starts like this.
WEEK 3: The Silent Treatment.
You start asking questions in section. They dont get answered. The TF writes something incomprehensible on the board and walks away. You're told just review your notes. But the notes are cryptic, half–written spells. You start to realize no one's coming to save you.
WEEK 4: The Lovebomb and Withdrawal.
You're handed a worksheet with 20 problems due the next day. You complete it. You get it right. You feel proud. You think maybe things are looking up. Then, next class, Erica pulls a full 180: infinite series. 10 new tests. No warning. No transition. Just pain.
WEEK 5: The Gas Explosion.
Ratio test. Root test. Alternating series. Absolute convergence. Conditional convergence. Everything convergesexcept your understanding. You try to memorize all the tests. They start blurring together. You make a chart. The chart lies to you.
WEEK 6: The Breakdown.
Your PSET takes 13 hours. You cry during office hours. You email your TF and get a reply that says Try the Comparison Test. You eat lunch while watching a Taylor Series video at 2x speed. You forget how food tastes.
WEEK 7: The Taylor Spiral.
Youre asked to write a Taylor polynomial about a point that doesn't even exist in the real world. Youre finding derivatives of transcendental functions using formulas youve never seen. Just generalize, says Erica. You generalize your sorrow.
WEEK 8: The Identity Crisis.
You start thinking maybe you were never good at math. Maybe you were never meant to be here. You think about switching majors. You think about dropping out. You wonder if Taylor series would converge faster than your GPA is falling.
WEEK 9: The Philosophical Turn.
You start agreeing with Nietzsche. God is dead, and Erica killed him with a radius of convergence. You find yourself contemplating existence in lecture. You raise your hand to ask, What does it mean for a series to truly converge? and the TF writes a summation sign and walks away.
WEEK 10: The Hollowing.
You are no longer a student. You are a vessel for confusion and half–finished differentials. Your dreams are narrated by series expansions. Your nightmares are slope fields. You sit in lecture and simply accept. Acceptance is the final stage of grief. Math 1B has won.
WEEK 11: The Differential Descent.
The word differential used to sound vaguely scientific. Maybe even elegant. Now, it triggers a full–body reaction. Erica walks in, picks up the chalk, and says Lets solve a second–order linear nonhomogeneous differential equation, and you start to see colors that arent there.
You thought integrals were bad. You thought convergence tests were painful. You had no idea. Now its y double prime plus y prime plus y equals e^x times a cosine. Youre supposed to use undetermined coefficients. Variation of parameters. The method of annihilators. You try to look it up, but Google says Did you mean: youre screwed?
WEEK 12: The Breakdown, Redux.
The problem sets now involve three layers of differential logic. Youre solving for particular solutions, homogeneous solutions, general solutionsat this point, you just want a solution to your life. You stare at the DE on the board and think, This must be what it feels like to go insane slowly, beautifully, with a pencil.
You try to ask a question, but it comes out as a sound. Just a noise. Erica responds with a formula that looks like a summoning spell. You copy it down because youve stopped resisting. Your notebook is no longer for learning. Its a place to document the decline.
WEEK 13: The Rage.
Youve stopped suffering in silence. You curse aloud during problem sets. You type Why does Taylor do this into Google. You threaten to drop a major just to spite this class. You write Help me in your notebook and underline it 30 times. Your TF makes eye contact and flinches. You get into a fight with a friend who says, Its not that bad. You cut them off. You were never that close anyway.
WEEK 14: The Surrender.
The final approaches. You dont study. You prepare spiritually. You walk into the lecture hall like a condemned soul. You look around and see your classmates in various stages of denial, numbness, and caffeine–induced hysteria. Someone sharpens a pencil like its a sword. You nod. This is the end. You whisper Octonauts ready for action not as a jokebut as a prayer.
. . . .
THE FINAL EXAM: A LIVE–ACTION BETRAYAL
They told us no food, no water, no calculator. I brought a cough drop. I was sicksick in the way only Math 1B can make a person sick. Coughing, shaking, cold sweating through an exam that was colder than the room. A proctor approached me like Id brought contraband. I was almost kicked out. For a cough drop. For daring to attempt basic respiratory survival during this academic deathmatch.
Had I been removed, I think I would've finally cracked. The breakdown was brewing. Missing one more thingone more community dinner, one more org meeting, one more birthday, one more finalmightve been the end. This course had already taken too much. Had they removed me for that Ricola, I wouldve started speaking in series notation and crying Taylor terms.
The lecture hall was quiet. Too quiet. You could feel the tension in the air as students filtered innot chatting, not joking, just solemnly setting down mechanical pencils like weapons before a duel. You werent here to succeed. You were here to survive.
The moment the clock hit 9:00 AM, the final exam was passed out, and the room collectively exhaled their last hope. You flipped the first page. It was already over.
A second–order linear nonhomogeneous differential equation stared back at you like a death sentence. You tried to remember your method of undetermined coefficients. Your brain gave you a flashback to crying in office hours instead.
By page two, someone had put their head down and wasnt moving. By page three, you heard quiet sobbing. Page four had a parametric curve question that looked like a Rorschach test. Someone stood up, walked out, and never returned.
A student in the back threw up. Another cursed audibly. The proctor didnt even blink. This was normal. This was expected. This was Math 1B.
You reached the Taylor series expansion and just stared. It wanted a 40–term expansion centered at a point that might not exist. You wrote four terms and thenwithout hesitationwrote Octonauts ready for action at the bottom. Because what else could you do? Pray? Cry? Beg for mercy from a function?
The last problem asked you to analyze convergence in a series that looked like it had been cursed by a Victorian child. You wrote Im sorry in the margin.
And then, somehow, it was over.
You stumbled out of the lecture hall into the blinding daylight. Someone was weeping on the steps. Another was making a TikTok about the experience. You stood there, hollowed out, stripped of ego and energyand smiled.
Because it was done.
Because you survived.
Because despite everythingdespite the conduct penalty, the sleepless nights, the cursed slope fieldsyou finished Math 1B.
And for the first time in months, you felt joy. Real, tangible joy. The joy of walking away from the wreckage with your sanity mostly intact. Of knowing that you would never again have to write a Taylor series. Of knowing that Erica Dinkins could no longer touch you.
Octonauts. Ready for freedom.
EPILOGUE: A LETTER TO FUTURE FIRST–YEARS
If youre reading this, it means youve heard the whispers. The legends. The rumors about Math 1B. Maybe youre a freshmanbright–eyed, determined, ready to take on Harvards STEM courses with the energy of a caffeinated golden retriever. Maybe someone told you it wasnt that bad. Maybe you believed them.
Dont.
Math 1B is not just a class. It is a gauntlet. A psychological operation. A semester–long fever dream of integrals, infinite series, and unspeakable differential horrors. It is a trial by fire in which you will be burned, branded, and brokenand if youre lucky, youll crawl out the other side with a C+ and a ghost permanently whispering Taylor approximation in your ear.
But you dont have to do this. You have choices. If you can place into 21Ado it. If you can take 1A insteadbless yourself and do it. Do not let the advisors lie to you. Do not let the siren song of everyone takes it lull you into the abyss. If someone from the department says it builds character, understand they mean it in the same way a collapsed bridge builds swimming skills.
And if youve already committedbrace yourself. Arm yourself. Find your people. Make a meme folder. Go to office hours, even if only to sob quietly into your notebook. Watch lectures on 2x speed and let the confusion wash over you like a wave. Drink water. Sleep when you can. Mourn what you lose, celebrate what you finish.
You will forget the math. But you will never forget the madness.
Sincerely,
A survivor of the series.

But she turns to you. And that lookGod, that lookis something you feel in your bones. Like shes already done a triple integral of your soul and found it lacking. She doesnt ask you what your question is. She doesnt encourage. She just says, with that edge in her voice, We already covered that.
Or worse: she answers your question with another questionabstract, symbolic, disembodiedand youre left even more confused, now deeply ashamed, and maybe even wondering if you imagined the whole lecture.

If you werent in the top 5% of math fluency, you didnt exist to her. You were an inconvenience. A background hum. Static in the calculus frequency she operated on.
I started doubting myself in that room. Started shrinking. Started losing my voice. You dont realize it at first, but it chips away at you. A comment here. A sigh there. And thats the difference. Thats the core of it.
