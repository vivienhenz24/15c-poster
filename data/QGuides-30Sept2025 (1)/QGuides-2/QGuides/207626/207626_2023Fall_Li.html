Overall: Controlling systems via feedback, i.e., taking mathematical models of physical/biological/mechanical/economic systems and using feedback of the output to create inputs that allow us to control the output. Useful for a range of concentrations where stability/feedback are important. The first half is modern control theory with the state space model and lin alg; the second half is classical control theory with the Laplace transform and frequency domain analysis. Also teaches some basic MATLAB, Simulink, and MATLAB ControlSystemDesigner.

Professor: Lina is a wonderful teacher. The lecture notes are extremely well written and are great references both inside and outside class.

Textbooks: Not critical but very useful for understanding. 1) Astrom and Murray, Feedback Systems: An Introduction for Scientists and Engineers. Second Edition.

Topics covered: State space model, difference equations (discrete); equilibrium points, phase portraits, stability, linearization, LTI systems; matrix exponentials; stability and Eigenvalues; state feedback control, reachability theorem, reference tracking, placing Eigenvalues, controllers; observability, Luenberger estimator; impulse/step/frequency response; Laplace transform, transfer functions, block diagrams; Bode plots (<3), zeros and poles; Nyquist plots, Nyquist criterion; tracking/steady state error, phase/gain margin; PID, lead/lag control, sensitivity functions, performance limits, robustness.

Course structure notes: Pset due on Fridays mostly. Group design project with project milestone assignments (replaces pset that week) and final report writeup, actually quite fun and extremely educational. Two short lab data collections with short writeups. Two midterms.
You learn a lot, and the workload is very manageable. If you feel like you start to get behind, don't be afraid to ask questions on Ed or go to office hours. The exams are similar to practice exams, which is great in a stressful exam period.

ES 155 is an excellent course: Lina is excellent, the teaching assistants are excellent, the course material is excellent, and you will learn a lot from taking this class. I think it is straightforward to do well in this class, e.g. for exams, the practice exams are very similar to the actual exam. It is a great class for any one interested in control systems. The material is well taught and thoroughly broken down.
Lina is an excellent instructor, and the course feels very organized until halfway through the semester.
If you take this class then make sure that you have access to a computer than can properly run MATLAB or the online version. For some reason my computer had a host of issues with the former and I did not know of the latter. This cost me hours on psets and definitely some late points because my system would get stuck for upwards of twenty minutes trying to generate a bode plot.
For an introduction to control theory course, ES155 does an excellent job covering the essentials. Lina's lectures get straight to the point, and she distills the information necessary to intuitively understand problems and concepts as well as the best guidelines for solving problems. The textbook by Astrom and Murray follows the class material faithfully, especially in the first half of the course. I would definitely recommend doing the textbook readings before the lectures so that Lina's teaching will reinforce material you've already been exposed to and have pondered on.

In terms of assignments, there are roughly 8 psets, once each week or every two weeks, and they are fairly written, covering the material according to the lectures. Most of the time, the psets will have MATLAB integrated, so it would be helpful to at least cover a crash course into MATLAB and gain some familiarity with the control toolbox before or at the start of the course. There will be sections that will help provide this on–ramp, but it's honestly easier and more effective to take care of it yourself beforehand. If anything, you can do the training earlier and ask any lingering questions in section the first couple weeks.

Besides the psets, there are two short labs (very guided, don't require more than 30min to conduct the lab and roughly 1–2hrs to do the lab report) in each half of the semester. The other assignment is the course project. This is done in about ~4 stages, called milestones, throughout the semester. The teams are picked by Lina, but you do get to send her your preferences for partners. The project objectively is time–consuming and difficult since it requires the knowledge acquired in just the last couple weeks to be applied to a control problem you study with your group. Now, depending on the commitment and work distribution of your team, the difficulty can range from challenging but manageable to extremely time consuming (i.e. expect a few all–nighters). Lina is very understanding and will provide a lot of help and guidance throughout the project, so please don't feel intimidated. By the end, you will feel that the project is probably your proudest accomplishment in the course. The project truly does provide a healthy challenge and is going to greatly help your understanding and confidence in the material you learn, but like anything you study, it will take time to digest and there will be a lot of mistakes along the way. Try to stay optimistic and know that all groups end up doing very well by the culmination of the project!

Lastly, there is one midterm and a final. I think the format of it changes from year to year, but in this past year the midterm was done in class with a handwritten cheat sheet. The final was a 3hr take home exam. Both were very reasonable. Be sure to look at the practice exam Lina will send because they represent the exams very well. For a few last notes, the class definitely will feel like a math course at times, but the math is based on a few key linear algebra topics like rank and finding eigenvalues. It's nothing extensive, so even if you don't have the best math background coming in, these are concepts you can still pick up. Plus, if you are an engineering concentrator, having concrete examples and applications will greatly help in your understanding, in ways pure math classes were lacking. For EE concentrators, this class pairs excellently with ES152. Truly, I cannot think of a better class pairing in the same semester as ES155 and ES152. The concepts overlap so nicely and the knowledge you gain in one class will help reinforce what you learn in the other. The best benefit is getting to learn bode plots and transfer functions in ES152 just before they are covered in ES155. Overall, ES155 is a very solid course and if the appropriate time is taken to understand the material, you will most likely have a great appreciation and fascination with controls that stays with you beyond the class.
