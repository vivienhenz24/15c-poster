This course is interesting and useful both for those interested in Soviet history and culture and for those who want to better understand how films work more generally. As Professors Khitrova and Weir pointed out several times, early Soviet films themselves are works of theory, both political and filmic, and learning to read them also teaches students how to approach both film and Soviet history.
Very cool overview with sharp professors! And a cool final project option
This is two courses.
One is taught by Daria and is primarily about the films themselves. This course is great. Daria is one of the most learned scholars on these films in the world, can answer questions very well, and generally lectures well with a great deal of knowledge.
The other is taught by Justin and is primarily about theory. This course is taught fine, never particularly interesting, often filled with pointless asides, and isn't great.
Try to focus on the former and ignore the latter and you should do well with this course.
Take this class it is amazing!! Prof. Weir and Prof. Khitrova are incredible and have so much knowledge to impart to their students. Seriously, if you're at all interested in film or the Soviet space, this is the perfect class for you –– I promise you'll get a lot out of it.
I would highly recommend it for students of Soviet literature! You can see the interaction of film with other mediums of art during the early Soviet period!
This was a great course! Expanded my film horizonswould recommend!
Set aside a specific time each week to watch the films for this class, that way you're not watching them the night before and can enjoy them.
I enjoyed this class but definitely make sure you're interested in silent film (and a lot of Eisenstein). I thought there would be more of a range of Soviet filmmakers but we didn't leave the 1920s until the second to last week of the semester. This was a pure elective for me, though, and I learned a ton about film theory and how that impacted Soviet silent film.
This course is great! The professors are very committed to it and you learn about the masters behind some of the most important pieces of cinema in history.
This class is very fun, especially if you watch the films carefully before class. The assignments are often creative, so it's a nice change of pace from essay–heavy courses, and equally pedagogically satisfying.
This course surveys the history of Soviet film through engaging lectures and noteworthy films. I particularly enjoyed the class visit to the Harvard Film Archive and studying the theory behind these incredible films.
I don't think this is such a great coursethe last two weeks were the most interesting and the course focused far too much on a small subsection of the history of Soviet Film. It is the history of early soviet film and not of soviet film overall.
Take this class! Great vibes, excellent professors, and you will learn a lot. The course load is reasonable and you are likely to be captivated by the banter between Prof. Khitrova and Prof. Weir
If you are interested in the topic, definitely take it. Though I did want more a more non–silent film heavy syllabus (we only got post–wwii for the final two weeks which kinda bummed me out). Don't take this class if you are not actually interested or curious to become interested cuz nothing stinks more than a tuition–paying Harvard student in class goofing off on their laptop when they are in the middle of an opportunity for great learning and intellectual enrichment. It's definitely a good intro film course for those who have not taken any and want to because it covers a lot of basic concepts of film theory that are useful in all other worlds of film and visual art (though maybe start with a less specific class like AFVS 70 just to get a wider range of films).
You should absolutely take this course! It was super interesting and well worth your time.
