take this class; Kaden is amazing
The lectures are somewhat boring for me. Many people did not go to the lectures since the lectures might not be the most helpful thing to learn the materials. However, Kaden is very good at making the lecture notes. Reading the notes and going to sections are the best ways to finish the assignments and prepare for the exams.
Take this class. It makes you think about language and meaning in a totally new way, and even for non–Ling concentrators it introduces you to logic and mechanical approaches to language. Prof. Holladay is fantastic, and provides great feedback and amazing lecture notes and materials for psets.
I think this can be a really fun class for those who are interested in humanity (language) and STEM. There are also connections between semantics and programming languages.
Great class. I had no prior linguistics background whatsoever so everything was very new to me. It is interesting stuff, and has some real (conversational) applications. If you are trying to figure out what types of assignments we had and what you will be learning, imagine doing math proofs for sentences like "I hit the ball." you have to basically write out all of the steps to make that sentence make sense. It is interesting, but gets complicated when the sentences get harder.
This class has been one of my favorites at Harvard. I study linguistics because I like languages and math and this class is the perfect combination of the two. Semantics is all about applying logic to language to derive meaning. The teaching team was super knowledgable and kind. The material was very much manageable (think 10–40 pages of reading and one problem set per week). The exams were fair and open note. Kaden wants students to learn and enjoy the course and it shows. I recommend this class to anybody with a vague interest in linguistics, math and/or logic because I ensure that you will find it enjoyable.
I would only recommend this course if you love linguistics in a purely academic sense. The class is essentially about creating an algorithm that can determine the meaning of a sentence from the words used, and it was more mathy (for lack of a better world) than I expected. It isnt especially difficult, but if you arent a concentrator or passionate about semantics, you probably wont find it worthwhile. I, for example, wanted to take a linguistics because I was interested in the social and comparative components of language, which is not what this class is about. As long as you know what you are signing up for, its a good introduction to semantics.
Semantics is a striking discipline mixing logic notation and human speech (and cognition?) (and philosophy?). Kaden is a great steward as a class leader, and in office hours he will make sure the material is drilled into your head. James, if you have him as your TF, is just about as good as section leaders get in all aspects.

Semantics is lowkey just about assigning truth values (0 or 1 or N/A) to sentences. If you're into learning concepts and learning how to apply them in the subject of what it means for things to mean something, this is a tasteful course for that aim.
GEM GEM GEM. If you are a STEM student, this class is so unbelievably easy for you to fulfill your humanities requirement, and you'll learn a little bit about semantics too. You can pay attention to no lecture, read the lecture notes, then finish your weekly homeworks, all in less than an hour. The midterm and final are open note as well (though this might change), so you literally don't even have to study for exams.
For anyone with any kind of mathematics or logic background, this class is not very difficult. It covers a decent amount of ground and some pretty interesting concepts, but not in a lot of depth. Most time, especially in assignments and exams, is spent on pretty basic notations and definitions.
