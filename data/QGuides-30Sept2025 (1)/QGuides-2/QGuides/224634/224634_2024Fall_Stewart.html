This is a very interesting course and William Stewart is AMAZING. He is engaging, passionate and so nice. The sections itself are very interesting and the topics covered are very relevant. HOWEVER, the readings are significantly longer than other freshman seminars, so be careful adding this as your "easy" course. Additionally with three essays it is more work than many other freshman seminars. I recommend this, but only if you are comfortable with the workload.
There is a lot of reading (I learned the hard way, you don't have to do it all) and 3 papers, which are not graded harshly at all. Easy to do well (also pass fail) but sometimes hard to stay awake in class. Professor is so enthusiastic so overall pretty good experience.
Id highly recommend this class to anyone interested in understanding the complexities of burnout and its connection to societal expectations. The course is unique in that it blends philosophical, historical, sociological, and psychological perspectives on burnout, offering a comprehensive view of the topic. One of its standout features is the diversity of materials, which range from German philosophers like Max Weber and Karl Marx to contemporary thinkers like Joan Didion and Byung–Chul Han. The course also explores a mix of ancient texts, books like Bartleby, and even films like, Slacker, and Koyaanisqatsi. While this variety is intellectually stimulating, I do think we would've benefitted from focusing on fewer readings to allow for a deeper, more meaningful engagement with the material.

Professor Stewart is an exceptional instructor whose passion for the subject is contagious. His enthusiasm fosters a dynamic seminar environment, making each class both engaging and thought–provoking. However, be prepared for a challenging weekly reading load, especially with the extensive reading list. Some of the texts, particularly those with economic or capitalist perspectives, might feel a bit distant from the more personal side of burnout. But if you can navigate this, the insights you gain will be incredibly valuable. By the end of the course, youll have a much deeper, more nuanced understanding of burnout and its far–reaching impacts.
Stewart is skilled at what he does, and theres a lot to glean from observing how he interprets a text, consolidates ideas into arguments, and communicates through writing. He provides helpful feedback and lots of enthusiasm. However, I really warn against taking the seminar if you are more drawn to the hard sciences, as it has turned me away from humanities instead of encouraging me to broaden my coursework.

This class may be a good fit if you are interested in examining burnout from a more historical and economic lens rather than a psychological one. Otherwise, I would suggest looking elsewhere. Basically every argument abstracts from social phenomena without referring to existing scientific explanation whatsoever. With a topic like burnout, I was frequently unsettled by and dissatisfied with this approach.

I did not usually find in–class discussions that productive or interesting, and the readings were generally not to my taste. A good number of texts were supposedly deep or insightful, but I was just confused or not enthused. Even when I did find ideas interesting, I often did not think they were presented cogently, and I felt like very few people would be convinced of them without already believing in their merit. The course did offer some productive ways to think about burnout, but mostly in peripheral ways.

Regarding style, the more modern, free–form texts came with a melodramatic, woe–is–me, look at how eloquently and tragically I can phrase things to cloud the fact that Im overgeneralizing without justification and being egregiously imprecise tone. Others use generic, obscure, or overly convoluted language to bestow upon you the exciting task of picking apart what they actually mean. I did not have the patience, and being averse to engaging with the delivery really harmed my ability to appreciate the ideas. This is a harsh review of many aspects of this seminar, but I think alignment with existing academic interests and how you think about the world is almost required for enjoying the course.

Another warning: this course expects you to make time for it. With tons of weekly reading and three essays, the workload is a lot for first–year seminar, (Id imagine its about as heavy as expos 20). Stewart lightened the page requirements for some essays and made optional the class that was going to be held during reading period, which changed my verdict from borders on unreasonable to heavy, so be prepared. Grading (especially with the 80% requirement instead of your usual pass–fail metrics) is taken seriously, which does mean you receive productive feedback, but the amount of stress this class induced for me was far less than ideal for a freshman seminar. In hindsight, this is all basically laid out in the syllabus, but with no prior experience with humanities at Harvard, it can be hard to know what youre really getting into.

If you are like me and are picky about which flavors of humanities you enjoy, or are not already primarily aligned towards humanities in your academic interests, the course may be a frustrating experience. Or, if youre unsure what aspects of humanities resonate with you, taking the seminar is an opportunity to find out the hard way.
TAKE THIS CLASS TAKE THIS CLASS TAKE THIS CLASS!!!!!!! This was undoubtedly my favorite course of the semester. Dr Stewart is so unbelievably kind, and you can tell he really loves what he does and cares about his student's learning. Readings were super interesting, but could be difficult if you have no experience to college–level reading. This shouldn't scare you though because you don't have to read everything (I certainly didn't...) since discussions will go based off what you have to say about what you did read. He also made it clear he didn't expect us to read absolutely everything (Stewart does not want you to burnout taking this class).
Also, a lot of the assigned "readings" are artworks, films etc. (We watched one film on the big screen in the carpenter center and another in Barker with Joe's pizza :)) This class helped with my "addiction to achievement", and I think warded off the potential for imposter syndrome that could develop at this school. The environment was really open and I always felt comfortable sharing my opinion. Dr Stewart is very accessible outside of class time. Overall, this class is everything you would want a Harvard class to be! Interesting, stimulating, prescient and enjoyable.

TAKE THIS CLASS!!! I don't know how to express my appreciation for this class enough. It has changed how I see the world and my understanding of the effects economic systems can have on society. I do not think I have ever learned so much and learned to change my way of thinking as much as I have over the last few months. Taking this class has reminded me why I love learning so much and makes me glad I chose to go to a school which values intellectual vitality. I feel better equipped to understand the "why's" of the world now, and hold more empathy for those who live outside the confined systems dictated by capitalism. At an institution which is so rigorous and seems to take in brilliant minds yet pump out the same archetypes, I am incredibly grateful to have had the opportunity to take a class which questions this to be the norm in my freshman year. The perspectives I have gained through this class are no doubt going to be fundamental in shaping who I am.

Some things to note about this class:
–The title can seem deceiving or the first half of the class... you may be asking yourself "why did I take this, its not what I signed up for". Stick with it and you will be rewarded
–The course work is heavy for a FYS. Our class discussed this issue with Dr.Stewart so things may change next time but as of this year, the work is not necessarily light
–Some weeks are difficult. Some texts (Marx, Smith) are tough to understand and feel pointless. They are not pointless and remember not even Dr.Stewart is expecting you to read every sentence in detail
It definitely isn't the most rewarding class I've ever taken and much of the topics are very up to interpretation so it's a very "take what you will" class. I definitely think there are more hands on, rewarding first year seminars to take but ultimately it is up to someone's interests. Personally, this class didn't study as much modern culture as I liked so that's why I found the course materials boring, not what I expected, and often all over the place.
Please take this class! Or anything with Professor Stewart! He is such a great instructor and has incredible insights. You leave the class feeling like you came to your own conclusions even if you needed some of his guidance. Everyone by the end of the course was gushing over how good it was, so stick with it even though the beginning is a bit hard.
