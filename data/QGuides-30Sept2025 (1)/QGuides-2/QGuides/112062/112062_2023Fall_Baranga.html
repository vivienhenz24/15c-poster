I came into this class very excited because I loved macro and thought it would be a great building of theory on top of the foundations I learned in EC10B. However, as I began the course, there were so many opportunities to get lost and become confused with the course material. My first issue with the course is that lectures are not recorded. I understand that some professors do this because it is a hassle and/or they want students to attend class in person, but I found it literally impossible to keep up with lectures in person. Usually when I do the readings for a class, the lectures make a ton of sense. However, there was so much disconnect between readings to lecture slides to what the professor was actually teaching. Lecture slides were often extremely confusing and unclear and there were several times that the professor would go off on random tangents deriving random equations on the board and it was really difficult to follow along. In classes, it sometimes felt like he was talking to himself because no one was understanding what he was saying. Im not sure if lecture recording would have helped much, but I think they definitely would have been a useful tool to look back on since there were so many additional details in lecture.

Next up is section. I would go to section every week, and every week my TF would start the class saying that he was super tired and didnt want to be there. I get it, I am tired too, and I dont particularly want to be here either, but sometimes we just have to push through. It is evident that my TF put effort into preparing for section, but again, maybe because of how section was structured, I didnt get much out of it. It was difficult to follow along and it felt like a lot of bouncing all over the place.

My biggest issue with the class is how it is graded. As an Econ concentrator, I am used to exams being weighted very heavily on the final grade. It sucks, but it is how it is. However, almost every component of this class is based on multiple choice/ fill in numbers??? That means if you mess up one little equation, you get the entire answer wrong and there is no opportunity for partial credit. Going with this, questions often built on top of each other. So if you got one multiple choice answer wrong, there is a good shot you would get some of the subsequent ones wrong as well. On the midterm and the final, I found these exams extremely difficult for no reason. Usually for Econ courses, understanding the big picture ideas is the most important part of the course and you should be able to figure out most other things if you understand those overarching concepts. However, this was not the case in this course. Thomas pulled out random graphs that he has maybe showed once in class and put them on the exam. He would ask questions about authors from readings we barely talked about. Numeric questions all built off of each other, so if you got one question wrong because of a silly calculation error, you likely got several in a row wrong. This type of model makes it seem like the course is testing your ability to do perfect math and memorize silly little details instead of actually gaining a solid understanding of macroeconomic theory, which I feel like defeats the entire purpose of the course. That being said, exams were extremely difficult and no matter how much I studied, I still didnt feel prepared.
