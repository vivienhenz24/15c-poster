I was originally hesitant to take this class, but I am extremely happy with my decision to take it. Here are some things I wish I knew before taking Chem 20:
– To succeed in this class, you do not necessarily need a super strong chemistry background (AP/IB Chem should suffice), but you will need a lot of discipline.
– Keep a set of flashcards with every single reaction learned in class. Every time a new reaction is introduced in lecture, annotate your blue book, and then go back to it after lecture to include it in your set. Review these flashcards. Every. Single. Night. You don't have to be crazy about it. Just for 10 minutes. If you maintain this habit, studying for exams will be significantly easier.
– PSETs are due on Tuesdays (which makes for a rough schedule), so here's what I would recommend. Start with Aktiv Chem as soon as it is released (Wednesday). Even if you initially get many things wrong, it will help you develop a stronger chemical intuition (which will, in turn, make the lectures easier to follow). After you finish the Aktiv Chem, GRIND the purple book (Thursday–Saturday). Purple book exercises usually get at concepts that we don't dive deep into class, so mastering them is essential to succeeding in this course. Once you have done this, do the PSET (Sunday–Tuesday). I think this is the best way to ensure you stay on top of everything and make use of all materials this class offers.
– Go to lecture. Even if it's only 10 people. It just makes the class much more meaningful and fun.
– Don't worry about the lab. It is so much fun, and TFs are very kind.
– Don't be discouraged by exams (I definitely struggled with this one). Exams for Chem 20 were very...interesting. They felt disconnected from the material covered in lecture, focusing on exceptions to the principles learned in lecture rather than the principles themselves. The Q is probably going to be filled with a lot of complaints about this class; I actually thought it was a fair challenge. By nature, organic chemistry is such a difficult subject that even the best students don't understand it without putting in a TON of work. It's essentially like puzzle–solving – even if you have a great knowledge base, sometimes things click and sometimes they just don't. Prepare to be pushed way out of your comfort zone, go to office hours and beg for explanations, and be disappointed with your exam grades.

That being said, though, I think Richard gives you all the tools you need to succeed. There are so many office hours, extra practice problems, and opportunities for help that if you put in the work, you can feasibly understand what's going on. He's a very good lecturer and I thought the concepts were pretty clear when he explained them. I especially appreciated that this class gave a bit of a crash course in molecular orbital theory, which most orgo classes skip. It really helped my conceptual understanding.

Tips for success from someone who did very well in the class: (a) GO TO LECTURE AND SECTION. VIDEOS DO NOT HIT THE SAME. I KNOW IT'S AT 9AM BUT YOU'RE STRONG. (b) GO TO OFFICE HOURS. (c) DO ALL THE PRACTICE PROBLEMS, either each week or when you're studying for the exam. (d) Know your reactions early; the course is very cumulative. (e) Your knowledge needs to be plastic, not based on rote memorization. If you don't understand molecular orbital theory, or why certain reactions happen and others do not, then you won't do very well. If you're struggling with conceptual explanations, get them straightened out – it's really worth it.

I actually ended up enjoying this class because it pushed me out of my comfort zone without ever feeling unfair or impossible. It's expected that you won't know all the answers the first time around, or the second or the third, but that you're always learning and always growing. It was a good workout for my brain. I would recommend taking it over Chem 17 simply because there's more puzzle solving and it's fun!
