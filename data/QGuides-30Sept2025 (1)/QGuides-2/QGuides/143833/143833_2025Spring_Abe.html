What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

Table for [QTitle].
Comments
You will learn a lot about Daoism, Confucianism, Buddhism, and how the three are integrated in the context of modern East Asia. But you won't learn it from secondary sources or being told about ityou read the texts yourselves and engage with the material, so you come away with a deep understanding of them on their own terms. Prof. Abe does a great job of filling in the gaps to make the readings approachable, while also helping you interpret them without imposing an opinion on you.
TAKE THIS CLASS! It is absolutely fantastic, low pressure, and INCREDIBLY REWARDING. It feels like a book club where every single book is enthralling to read. I thoroughly learned the fundamentals of Buddhism, Daoism, and Confucianism from a classical doctrinal lens to a modern literary lens. It made me really reflect on my own values and how I hope to keep a sense of peace and flexibility while living my life. Professor Abe is an incredibly knowledgeable and worthwhile teacher to learn from. You cant go wrong with taking this class.
Strengths
The course encourages a slow–food culture and deep immersion in very insightful books. However, the reading load is heavy and sometimes, so this is not totally possible all the time. The professor is warm and welcoming. The format of the assignments is very flexible. For instance, I tried out to write a short story for the final assignment. The class content in general challenges your conventional way of thinking about learning and the world, and you may notice that learning how to unlearn and forgetting may actually be useful.

Ideas for improvement
The professor does not explain concepts very well. In class, he would scribble ideas on the blackboard and when confronted with questions, I often struggled to understand his clarification. However, the professor is super knowledgeable and shares some of his insights on the text he himself is researching (e.g., Lotus Sutra). Sometimes, the professor talks more and the students listen. This is very pleasant, but class discussions become very stale.
