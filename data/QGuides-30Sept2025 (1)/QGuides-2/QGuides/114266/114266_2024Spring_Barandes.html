Recommended prereqs: a good familiarity with E&M is very helpful, as we refer to it a bunch throughout the course as a representative (and simpler) field theory which we use to apply a lot of the concepts we eventually apply to GR. It's also really cool to see Maxwell's equations rewritten 5+ times using more and more powerful formalism. I think having good familiarity with Lagrangian and Hamiltonian mechanics was incredibly helpful as well. Quantum mechanics is honestly quite useful as well, because (at least in this iteration of the course) we first introduced the Einstein Field Equation in the linearized limit, where the equations represent the dynamics of a massless spin–2 bosonic field. Honestly, I feel like in general this class only gets better the more physics you know, because you'll be able to see all of the connections so much better.

Lectures: Jacob's lectures are (in my opinion) pretty fantastic. He does an incredible job of explaining things very clearly and telling a coherent story in each lecture that keeps you engaged. I was never bored in a GR lecture, and the last couple of lectures are some of my favorites I have ever sat on. There are some downsides though; ESPECIALLY IF THE LECTURES ARE NOT OFFERED IN THE 3–4:15 TIME SLOT, be aware that Jacob goes overtime *every* *single* *lecture*. Expect lectures to end on average 10–15 minutes overtime. He went 30 minutes over in the last two lectures. It's more tolerable when it's the last lecture of the day, and I was lucky in that I usually didn't have pressing commitments right after 4:15pm. But it's honestly a little disrespectful to people who do have commitments. Be aware.

Problem sets: These are as long and torturous as advertised. Unless you are a genius, find a study group early and make sure you like the people; you will spend a lot of time with them over the semester. The actual problems ranged from being very interesting questions that built intuition on abstract mathematical and physical concepts to absolute bashfests that felt completely pointless.

Conclusion: GR is one of my favorite classes I have taken here. It is also one of the most flawed classes I have taken here. There is so much mathematical formalism to get through before you get to the actual physicswe basically built up (less rigorous) differential geometry from scratch during the middle half of the classand I found myself many times incredibly frustrated after finishing a pset that was all math and bash and no physics. The payoff is incredible, though; black holes, wormholes, Penrose diagrams, etc. etc.. However, this payoff happened in the last two weeks of the class, after we were tortured for the rest of the semester with math. Is all of this formalism absolutely necessary to appreciate the theory and structure behind GR? Maybe. Probably. But if you actually want to study the *results* of GRgravitational lensing, black hole formation, etc. etc., you will not get a lot of that in this class. I don't think I could honestly say that I understand GR after this class, but again, maybe that's not the goal. Despite all of this, however, no other final lecture has topped this one for me so far. The idea that these crazy spacetimes and mind–bending objects are somewhere out there in the Universe is absolutely mind–boggling.
This class is mostly math, differential geometry (Math 136 and maybe even a bit further), BUT it is encapsuled in a physics context. Prof. Barandes made it seem like a story, like a truth we were uncovering together throughout the semester. His lectures can be very fast (I often times couldn't follow or felt overwhelmed), but take notes, and try to pay as much attention as you can. Things will make sense at some point. He also manages to make the material so interesting that all I want to do is GR after attending a lecture. He is also open to questions after class, which is a great resource. I would note that he also always goes overtime, so try not to schedule a class far away right after. I would leave an empty slot after class. An amazing thing about Prof Barandes is that he never follows lecture notes: he just writes on the board and speaks. He know or derives all equations. It is an insanely difficult skill, but helps him make lecture so much more engaging and interesting. Section was also VERY useful, so go to it!
Barandes is very good at teaching physics. He's fairly clear, but his greatest strength is his passion. You really get to feel every experiment and understand why GR is such a marvellous theory through him.

Annoyingly, not a lot of the course is actually physics. We spent April doing physics, but for 6 weeks between February and March we were just doing math. It seems to me vastly more efficient to require students to learn Math 136. I think it is not necessary to present the formal definition of a topological manifold to students in this class, and really all the students need to get used to is this coordinate stuff (which may be absent from a more theoretical treatment of differential geometry).

Also, Barandes has this QFT section at the beginning of the class where he talks about how GR fits into this big theory. It's kind of cool to hear about it but I understood none of the details and wish it were much more limited. I would rather listen to the physics that I can understand.

Finally, the exam is hard. But it was probably one of the most informative experiences of my Harvard career. You have to think about GR very physically and I wish more of the class were like the exam (although maybe not as hard lol).

Where do I begin...

My second–favourite course of all time, Physics 210 was a truly fantastic experience. I came in to the course with high hopes and little intuition, just purely interested in the course to learn about "the most beautiful scientific theory of the 20th century", and let's just say it did not disappoint.

Let's start with the star of the show. Prof Jacob Barandes is an outstanding lecturer who clearly cares an incredible amount about his students and physics education as a whole. His endearing and respectful personality, excellent board–work and fantastically engaging presence just made lecture an absolute joy to be in. Combining this with how much he cares about presenting the historical development of the physics and his emphasis on instilling within us a genuine, intuitive conceptual understanding of the material has resulted in GR lecture actually giving me goosebumps and leaving me in awe of the material on very many occasions. Informal chats with Prof Barandes at the end of lecture were great opportunities to gather further perspective on the material, learn about his fascinating research and hear what follow–up questions other students have, leading to a wonderfully vibrant academic climate in the course that I will look back upon fondly. The course staff in general were excellent, but a well–earned special mention and thank you goes to TF Rui–Zhe Lee, for his great patience, knowledge, humility, kindness and approachability.

The material itself really lives up to its legendary status in physics. I really enjoyed the discussion of relativistic field theories that leads up to the derivation of the linearised Einstein Field Equation in unit one, which I understand to be something that is uniquely covered in Jacob's course, as it really nailed home the similarities in construction between Maxwell's Electrodynamics and General Relativity. The third unit (on the physics of curved spacetime), was the heart of the course and possibly my favourite section of any class I have ever taken – the final two weeks, an incredible collage of the astrophysical phenomena that the theory allows us to describe, was genuinely instrumental in keeping up my excitement and curiosity for physics through what is normally an exceedingly stressful time of the year. Overall, there are one or two lectures that are in my opinion need a little rethought, and depending on one's level of mathematical maturity the middle section of the course might feel a bit out of place pace–wise, but otherwise I am in strong agreement with the course's current structure. In general, Physics 210 just does a brilliant job of acting as a very necessary confluence of all the little streams of knowledge flowing in a student of theoretical physics' mind (Courses on Analytical Mechanics? Electrodynamics? Quantum Mechanics? Pure Maths? Pop science exposure (desperately in need to be rephrased rigorously)? They all combine here to make one beautiful unanimous whole).

Physics 210 is in general known in the department as a course in which the coursework is famously "bashy". Whereas I will not attempt to dispute this, I will say that Professor Barandes does make the experience far less painful than it would otherwise be by making most of the quite involved computational problems be key steps in results that we establish in lecture, so that even when we are in agony bashing tensor calculus on a blackboard late at night in Jefferson, we are constantly reminded of the significance of what we are doing each time we review the course material. In addition, unlike in some pretty infamously glaring examples of physics courses on this campus, one does find that in Professor Barandes' problem sets that staying close to one's organic mathematical intuition when solving problems does give a clearer picture of what is going on – always is a great plus to see a course be built upon solid conceptual understanding in all its aspects. Just a heads up – please do have a TF around when you try getting off the ground for the first time with Einstein notation, I was fortunate to have it be carefully walked through the first time around which was very helpful as I got the chance to integrate it very naturally with my structural intuition of linear algebra quite well early on before things started becoming too involved.

As is typical in my Physics department Q guide reviews, I feel a need to give my take on the prerequisites of the course. I am of the opinion that one will benefit the most from Prof. Barandes' Physics 210 having previously done Physics 151 and 153, and have the mathematical preparation of Math 22 or equivalent. None of my recommended prerequisites are formal requirements of course (besides like basic mechanics including Newtonian gravitation, and of course Linear Algebra and Several Variable Calculus – just a heads up these are most definitely completely necessary), but I am of the view that 210's narrative will make the most sense for those with the background I have just described – the physics discussions of the course will assume familiarity with the language of classical field theory with Maxwell's Electrodynamics being frequently revisited as the de facto example, and the mathematical discussions of the course will assume familiarity with the notion of an abstract vector space, for example, and will present mathematical ideas at the aforementioned level of exposition. Minor detail, but I will however put a word against having done a differential geometry course beforehand, however, as one would put themselves at risk of not enjoying the middle unit of the course as much as they would otherwise – not saying that one would find it dull (I have heard that Prof. Barandes' GR teaches Math 136 better than Math 136 does, and I can imagine that this is true), just an "I wish I learned things the other way around" kind of feeling if you get what I mean. This is definitely specific to Prof. Barandes' 210 though – I have heard that other versions of the course tend to do a less thorough treatment of the mathematical side of the subject, in which case it would probably make more sense to disregard what I just said. Definitely speak to more alumni of the course to get their views on this however, as I feel like this is both a subtle and a subjective thing to discuss – I'm just presenting my view on the matter.

Overall, a phenomenal course that I would recommend to everyone interested. I strongly believe that every student of physics will be at a stage in their education where Prof Barandes' GR would be an excellent addition to their catalogue; if you capitalise on this and take the course at your "proper time" (no pun intended), Physics 210 truly has the potential to blow your mind every lecture and remind you of how exciting life can be as a student of our wonderful subject.
