Honestly one of the worst classes I've taken in the chemistry department (also one of the worst science classes I've taken), and I've taken chem20/30/40/160/161 and ps2/3. The class seems easy with only 2 psets and 2 take–home exams. The problem sets were okay, but the take–home exams took up basically the entire 24 hours we were allotted for them which is unacceptable. The TFs also graded the exams and problem sets VERY harshly. Problem sets and exams weren't even related to material we learned in lecture. There's no final which again, seems nice. But there was a group project in which our group members were assigned to us. People's experiences of course will vary, but everyone I talked to in the class had negative experiences with group members either not wanting to work on the project at all or changing up the entire focus of the project the morning of the presentation without speaking to anyone about it. I don't understand why we couldn't just choose our own groups or have a final project that could be completed individually or in a group with the group option being more work in total since there are multiple people.

Lecture was incredibly boring. Professor Woo isn't a great lecturer and the TF who lectured when she was out, Hannah, was even worse. Again, the questions on the problem sets and exams weren't things we learned in lecture, which was mostly poorly explained theory, and instead were really practical applications and experimental design. I don't understand how this kind of a class can even happen in the chem department, every other class I've taken has been significantly better than this (not saying they've all been amazing, but even mediocre classes are more manageable than this). Section was useless. We attempted to go over the assigned scientific papers for the week but never finished. Everyone was checked out and I felt like it was just a waste of my time. The TF also wasn't super engaging in section. I'd rather we just reviewed lecture concepts (or actually learned something that would be on the test) as opposed to analyze figures in papers that I would forget the moment I stepped out of the room. Every other chemistry class uses section time to go over course material I don't get why this class should be any different.

I often felt, during lecture especially, that this course is NOT for undergrads and should be taken by 2nd year graduate students who've worked in a lab for years. There's a weird amount of basic BASIC stuff that's explained to you that I felt was unnecessary, but then other really complicated stuff that was just assumed knowledge and never gone over. I got the same sense when I went to Professor Woo's tenure talk. I figured that was just because she was lecturing to such a wide variety of people and that she'd be a better lecturer when she's teaching, but after taking the class I realize I was wrong. Maybe Professor Woo should look at how other chemistry classes, like 160 and 161, are taught. I was baffled by the fact that I could be more successful in courses on quantum mechanics and statistical thermodynamics no problem but have a negative experience in a chemical biology course. But really, what makes the most impact on a student's learning and their success in a class is the structure of the course. Having easily accessible office hours, having consistent, MANAGEABLE problem sets, exams that don't suck the life out of you and consume your every waking minute for 24 hours, engaging lecturers, useful sections. These are all things that chem 170 did not have but that I really wish it did. Kinda sad that this course sucked, I had higher hopes but here's hoping it gets better.

It's a good survey of chemical biology methods and Christina is a great public speaker. However, do consider how applicable / useful you will find the content, especially as an undergrad, or many of the methods will fly over your head and/or feel like an irrelevant deluge of information. Section was kind of a bog because some papers were very long and trudging through every figure was not that productive of an exercise to do every single section could be interesting to change up what section activities are or make clear what parts of the paper to focus on. Pset and exams felt like busy work in that it felt like a hunt through the lecture recordings/slides for specific things that were said, and word–vomiting everything we know about a method, and then still losing points for not mentioning something random. I did not feel like they added to my understanding of the course content. The final presentations kind of felt like a hit–or–miss since it was usually undergrads presenting a half–baked research idea in a field they didn't know a lot about, without any feedback from course staff, and was more an exhibition of poor public speaking. I think final presentations should get more feedback and start earlier, or even be changed to an individual project proposal we work on throughout the semester that can replace or be part of one of the psets or something, so that we can receive feedback on the idea and practice actual proposal writing. Or, presentations can be done within sections so it doesn't take four entire days. Or, instead of section being a group discussion the whole way through, we can have assigned paper presentations by one or two people each section to present the figures, and then we can discuss the questions, which 1) allows people to get public speaking practice and 2) means we can get to the interesting part of the discussion faster.
