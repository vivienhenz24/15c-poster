This class is an awesome introduction to human history from the dawn of human ancestor species to the present day! Dan and Matt are both really engaging lecturers, and the class encourages open discussions among everyone. Also, all the sections are awesome, and being able to work with artifacts at the Peabody museum is a neat touch – it gives you a very low–level understanding of how object analysis in history works, which is a fairly unique experience for stem folks like me.

The readings are nontrivial but not too stressful, and it's likely that you'll actually enjoy a fair few of them. The quizzes are also not too much stress. You definitely should be taking notes in lecture however the course material is *not* designed for being self–contained to review, and most of the actual content is delivered verbally.

The final project involves making a museum–style gallery on a topic of your choosing. Some advice on approaching it
1. When you pick a topic (early in the semester), be cognizant of how to find material objects to represent it.
2. Get creative! The sky's the limit when it comes to *how* you organize your presentation, so use it as an opportunity to try learning a new skill.
3. Start working on it early a lot of the writing involved requires some deep reflection on your topic and selected objects, so it's hard to spoof by doing all at once at the very end.

In all, this is a great Gened, with faculty who care a lot about their subject, on a topic that's fascinating. Definitely recommend!

I highly recommend taking this class if you want to gain an appreciation for how far back human history and culture goes. Solid readings, very interdisciplinary, encouraged to discuss. I'm impressed how much the professors actually care about the class. Coming from CS, this is not the norm. Dan and Matt put a lot of effort into their lectures and even read and respond to each student's short written assignments.

Imo, it's exactly what a GENED should be: makes you think about an interesting topic, fosters interdisciplinary conversations, and isn't particularly difficult. I highly recommend this course.

My only critique is that some of the units can feel a bit one–sided; only one side of the argument is presented in earnest, and the other is not presented or straw–manned. This felt a little dishonest or narrative–driven, but by no means malicious. They foster an open environment where well–reasoned contrarian opinions are heard and engaged with. I think they could do a better job in the future by presenting more varied readings.
This course is interesting in ways good and bad. Lots of the content is very interesting and I certainly feel very grateful to have a better understanding of the deep past. And not only was the content engaging for its own sake, but the professors did a good job of emphasizing the stakes of understanding the deep past and the relevance it has for our contemporary moment. I really appreciated this.

The instructors are very invested not just in the content of the course, but also in the pedagogy. Generally, this is a great quality in professors, but some of the pedagogical tools deployed in this course (really big emphasis on in–lecture participation, phone–a–friend option on quizzes, etc) made the class feel kind of gimmicky, and could get on my nerves. If you enjoy the more traditional lecture format, or really dont like speaking up, this class might not be for you.

I will also note that, as a senior who has written a *lot* of papers at this school (and has done fairly well), the previous q–guides werent lying about the grading being tough. Not that its unfair necessarily certainly all the feedback I got was warranted but it is harsher than most other classes. The quizzes can also be a bit tricky. That is all to say, if you take this class letter–graded and want an A, you will really need to be prepared to put your best foot forward.

Also shoutout TF Grace! She was lovely and always so thoughtful.

Overall, I recommend with reservations a lot of people took the course P/F, which probably makes it more enjoyable.

I loved this course so much. I came in not knowing how much I would get out of it, since I had already taken an HEB Gened and had read "Sapiens," a long–ranging history of humanity. However, even if you have a strong background in the topic, I believe you will love this course.

After taking this course, I look at the world in a new way. Each week, we look at a topic's past (examples include marriage, religion, and happiness) in order to better understand it. What was so cool was the number of angles through which Matt and Dan looked at the topics. They showed me how you can derive a deeper understanding by using history, anthropology, AND evolutionary biology to understand the past. If you've ever been frustrated by how siloed department can be, you'll love this class.

We talked about issues most people care about deeply, so I felt like I really got to learn from my classmates, especially those who come from different backgrounds than me.

The one warning I'd give is that Deep History is not an easy course. Throughout the semester you have three quizzes on lectures and readings. In addition, you have three papers, which culminate in another assignment due during finals period. I did actually enjoy the process of writing the papers. You get to choose any subject you want and trace its deep history, so it's a chance to delve into your personal interests. Matt and Dan are not easy graders, but they are fair. If you actually try and make your papers good, you can get an A a standard that not all Harvard classes uphold, apparently! If you're worried about the course being too challenging, I'd still recommend taking it, but doing it as your one pass/fail Gened. It's such a good class that it's worth it!
