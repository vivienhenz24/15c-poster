It's probably not wise to do all the readings on the day the discussion post is due (even though Professor Applbaum doesn't mind if you post a bit late).
TAKE THIS SEMINAR! It's a great way to take an intellectually stimulating course your freshman year without worrying about the pressure of grades. I would ESPECIALLY recommend it if you're unfamiliar with the topic. Though the title is long and interesting, the main theme in the course is social contract theory and when resistance is legitimate. If you've never read Locke, Hobbes, Rawls etc in high school, this is a great environment to do because you're not doing it alone. The weekly writing assignments are very doable and you do two short presentations. Prof. Applbaum is wonderfully passionate about the subject in a way that is almost t contagious; he was my favorite part of the class and is open to advising his students beyond it. I also got to meet great people and have great conversations. The only downside I would say is the length, but if you are down for 2.5 hours of interesting, light–hearted history and political philosophy, TAKE IT.
This class is a great way to get acquainted with political philosophy. Be prepared to do a lot of reading!
This is a good intro to Western philosophy class to get a taste of a mix of classic authors and just an introduction to what the field looks like.
Definitely take this class if you're even a little bit interested in political philosophy. There's around 75–100 pages of readings per week that are easier to manage if you divide the workload up and do a little each day. Taking notes on the reading as you do it (about what you find interesting) makes writing the discussion posts a breeze. Be prepared for on–the–spot questions in class. Applbaum is super interesting and knows this subject inside and out.
If you like thinking about political philosophy and big, revolutionary ideals, this is the right seminar for you. You read lots of fascinating political philosophers, and we covered most of social contract theory thinkers over the course of the semester.
The beginning of the course is slightly tedious, but you will be grateful if you push through. I feel like this class really made me confident that I have a good grasp on political theory through the ages. I like the format of the course: you read challenging material (which you probably don’t understand fully initially) but once class is over, Arthur has made sure that you understand all of it. So, this class really pushes you to read complicated texts without it seeming impossible to understand.
Arthur is overall a good professor, but sometimes can get bogged down in details, or feels very passionately about one or two words that we can spend almost an hour analyzing. He’s a fascinating resource though, super knowledgeable about an6 kind of world politics, and incredibly,y kind. He invited us out to dinner, and has insisted on getting to know everyone.
Overall, I’m glad I took this course because it goes over foundational material that is well explained.
