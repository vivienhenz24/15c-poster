What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

Comments
Take it, you'll love it. The content is fascinating and you get to learn about it through engaging readings, fun trips, and the incredibly knowledgeable Dr. Macklis.

Honestly, I felt like this course was my "introduction" to Harvard, in that it was exactly what I hoped my experience here would be like.
Overall: TAKE THIS CLASS!
Pros:
You get to go on a field trip almost every class, so this seminar is a great way to get acquainted with all of Harvard's museums and see some really cool stuff (ie: Phineas Gage's skull and an og Descartes book).
You will also learn a lot about the history of neuroscience!
Cons:
Class meetings are 2.75 hours
Most of this class is focused on pre–1900 neuroscience, so if you're looking for an overview of current neuroscience this is maybe not the best course :)
Work:
About 30–50 pages of reading and a one–page essay per week, as well as a 1–2 page paper at the end. You can definitely get away with not doing all of the work "well" and still getting a lot out of the class.
It is truly a transformative experience! You will learn to critically analyze the past and learn so much that most other people do not and will never know. Professor Macklis is truly kind and helpful should you ever reach out to him. You will learn so much more than you think you will!
The readings were interesting and the "field trips" were very cool, but the discussion part of the class can feel a bit dry. Be prepared to spend 2–4 hours reading and compiling a short 1–page essay on your thoughts every week, and then spend another 2.5 hours discussing your thoughts with the class.
I enjoyed this course and Professor Macklis was amazing! The field trips were my favorite part. Be prepared for a lot of discussions on readings. If you aren't a fan of long readings and discussions, this might not be the class for you, but it was a truly great seminar that opened my eyes to the long road of scientific advancement our world has experienced.
A good class for those interested in neuroscience. It is more of a history of neuroscience class than a typical neuroscience class, but it is nonetheless interesting.
This course has a multitude of history–heavy assigned readings that really build your appreciation for the people, context, and reasoning around scientific discovery. Note that this is not a Neuroscience course; while we did discuss some neurological concepts, we really focused on the timeline of discovery leading up to our current knowledge. It reframes the way you think about and adds humanity to science. Sometimes the discussion component can have lulls or seem extensive, but the class trips were always energizing. You'll be exposed to all different resources here at Harvard, including museums and brain technology, and that within itself is an invaluable experience.
This course allowed me to engage with ideas and analyze them. I think it provides a better background to medical concepts that I would have not been able to gain elsewhere. This is a course that synthesizes ideas and concepts in a way that you may not otherwise find elsewhere. Readings are chill and Professor Macklis pushes you to flesh out your ideas more. He is genuinely passionate about the class and passionate about learning about you.
Please take this course! The readings, sessions, and field trips are all extremely engaging and unique. Even if you believe you have no interest in this material, the course will help you find it! Apart from the wonderful opportunities of exploring Harvard's resources through field trips, this course opens your mind and encourages curiosity! The highlight of my first year at Harvard.
