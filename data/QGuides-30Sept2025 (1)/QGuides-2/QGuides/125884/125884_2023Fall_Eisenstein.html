This course is a very basic intro to astro. It can be boring at times but Eisenstein always has good intentions. He's a very sweet person and truly cares about his students, although classes can be very dense at times and honestly very boring. But, don't let that turn you away from astro!
Overall, this class was really good. The content is extremely interesting and the assignments are manageable (although due at a very annoying time). The only difficult part of the course are the exams which are VERY specific on the details briefly mentioned in the readings and lectures. The entirety of the midterm and half of the final were small details from the readings. Even if you're concentrating in astro, I would highly recommend taking this course later on after some physics or other astronomy experience.
Only take if this is a requirement. If you are interested in Astro, this will give a rather myopic view into the field. Would instead recommend taking more specialized higher level astro or physics classes. This class is doable but kind of takes away the sparkle of astrophysics. Focuses too much on instrumentation and observational astronomy (loves spectra). Lacking in the "physics" of astrophysics and often just describes the phenomena without really discussing the implications and what everything really means. Unless you put in the extra effort, you just have to take everything as given. If this sounds less appealing to you, would just recommend taking as much physics as possible until you can take Astron 130 or Physics 212. If you're an astrophysics concentrator, you have to take this course but as a concentrator myself lowkey regret taking the 16/17 intro sequence and would have preferred to have concentrated in physics and just take higher level astro classes further down the line. Modules were somehow long yet not super informative. There was just sooooo much material yet so little substance to it all. It's incredibly doable but just rather disheartening. Theory could have been probed so much deeper yet it wasn't.
– Don't skip lecture. This course covers a lot of material, and even if you don't think you'll be able to put 100% of your energy into the class one day, you will absorb more material by sitting in the room than you will staying in your room.
– Taking even 20 minutes a week to skim the modules posted on Canvas will help you immensely.
– The textbook for this class is incredibly dense, and while you may find it insightful, it may confuse you more than it helps and reading it is not at all necessary to be successful in this class.
–This class is primarily focused on big picture phenomena, so don't get bogged down in details, just try to absorb what you can and focus on understanding the major concepts.
I would only recommend to take this course if you are interested in Astro or are concentrating in it. This class does a great job of covering a lot of important topics within the realm of cosmology and other parts of large–scale Astronomy. My only advice is you get from this class whatever you put into it, so if you really care about the topic make sure you're locked in.
Take the class! It is one of the most valuable courses I have taken a Harvard so far. It gives you a perspective of the beginnings and workings of the universe that not many people will ever learn. I'd encourage everyone to take it for the valuable knowledge, but I would advise you to take a mechanics course beforehand and stay on top of the readings. The class makes a lot more sense when you read the material beforehand.
Great course with a great professor! You will learn a lot and have a lot of fun doing so!
This course starts out very easy but quickly ramps up and gets very difficult by the end. Professor Eisenstein is great and knew the answer to basically every question asked in the class, and the TFs were extremely helpful and flexible, in my experience! There is a lot of reading in this course that is essential for group discussions in class and for both the midterm and final exams. There is a great emphasis on the qualitative parts of extragalactic astronomy that you have less practice for in preparation for the exams. Be wary going into this course, as it can get difficult, especially if you're not passionate about the subject!
As an astrophysics concentrator, I really appreciated all that this course had to offer. I think that prospective astrophysics concentrators would gain valuable exposure to the fields of galactic and extragalactic astronomy and also cosmology as promised (i.e. the universe at large scales), but would moreover come out with somewhat of an appreciation for how modern optical astrophysics research surveys are conducted, which is valuable exposure to gain! Furthermore, Professor Eisenstein is just incredibly knowledgeable and very supportive and invested in answering questions and making himself available to students. He has led or been involved with several impressive astrophysics missions / instruments /surveys, ranging from SDSS to DESI to the James Webb Space Telescope; Prof. Eisenstein is very interested in instrumentation, and so you learn about the instruments themselves, too. I appreciated the effort this class makes in giving you the chance to actually work with real survey and spectroscopic data, and think about the instruments that gathered them, not just absorb (cool) astrophysics theories and knowledge. Definitely a good class to take to learn about modern astrophysics and the universe at large scales, especially if you are interested in astronomy!
Professor Eisenstein is one of the most knowledgeable people you will meet and has a great way of being able to answer every question about the universe that we do not know very much about.
