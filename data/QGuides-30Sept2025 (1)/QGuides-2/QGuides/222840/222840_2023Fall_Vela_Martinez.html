This class is one of my favorites ever at Harvard! Professor Vela Martinez and TF Maria made this course so great. This class changed my perspective on what defines "Mexican literature." I learned that Mexican literature does not necessarily have to be written/produced by someone born in Mexico. We had the opportunity to read authors from Chile and Chicago, for example, who produced works that related to or pertained to Mexico/Mexican audiences. I also loved how we discussed what is means to be a woman, a writer, and a woman writer in ways that were productive. I felt very safe to speak my opinions in class as the environment was very conducive to the sharing of ideas. Some of my favorite texts were Las ojas muertas, Sensacion termica, El extraño caso de Santi y Ago, The House on Mango Street, and Las niñas bien! I think that the rubrics were very fair and clearly defined expectations for our work. I also really enjoyed the liberty we were given to tackle the essay and final project! I had the opportunity to be very creative and approach the course from my own perspectives. You will definitely love this class!
This is a phenomenal course. The vast array of literature you read will change your perspective about what it means to be classified as a woman, author, Mexican, etc. Prof. Martinez is absolutely wonderful, please get to know her!! Don't skip out on the reading – plan in your schedule for a hefty reading load because each voice deserves attention and respect. Additionally, ensure that you are able to read Spanish literature, as the texts are dense in the language. This has been one of my favorite courses that I have ever taken, and I cannot recommend it more highly.
A classic Spanish course – better than some of the other upper level courses because the assignments aren't simply essays, etc. but the final project worth 40% of the grade was creating a unique map representation of women's writing, a concept I enjoyed very much.
TAKE THIS COURSE!! ALEJANDRA IS THE BEST! She is so ebullient and energetic, and we had such interesting discussions about gender, race, nationhood, citizenship, consumerism, and so much more. The class is well–structured, and it never lulled or felt like we ran out of things to talk about. The final project may seem daunting, but the course staff really reward creativity and outside–of–the–box thinking, which is a rare opportunity at this school. I came into this course as a non–native but fluent Spanish speaker, and I was super nervous about my ability to keep up. There was definitely a period of adjustment in terms of my language comprehension, but Alejandra was super understanding and reassuring — it's okay to make language mistakes! After the initial hump, I was perfectly fine and was able to communicate complex ideas about feminism in Spanish by the end of the course. There is far too much reading for you to reasonably finish before each class (about a book per class, which met twice per week), but we always had plenty to discuss, and even in weeks where I had only read the wikipedia page and skimmed the first fifteen pages, I was completely fine. This was an absolutely outstanding way to finish my language citation, and as a Hist Lit concentrator, it honestly felt like one of my concentration classes conducted in Spanish. I recommend this class (and any class with Alejandra) with my whole heart.
Take this course!! Alejandra is fabulous and generates a ton of enthusiasm for the course material. She was very engaging and classes were interesting (largely discussion–based with some contextualizing information from Alejandra that allowed us to go deeper into the texts). This class pushes you to think more deeply and critically about literature, gender, "Mexico," and the nation — so definitely recommended with anyone in an interest in those things!
