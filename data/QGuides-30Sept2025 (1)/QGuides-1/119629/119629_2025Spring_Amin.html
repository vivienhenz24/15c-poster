This has been a crazy (in a great way) class, and I've really enjoyed it. The material is very, very abstract, so it may be a bit unintuitive at first, but there are a lot of tidbits here and there that make for very interesting material –– some really weird math is sometimes sprinkled in to formalize things, and it's always quite fun. I think this class plays a complementary role to CS121: if your CS education is a brick wall, 121 is the grout, and 152 is the finishing coat, filling in the few details that really make everything work together. As someone who has never cared at all about systems, for instance, the framework given in 152 has really inspired me to go think about the implementations of programming languages in compilers, etc. in ways I'd really brushed off before.

The class itself is not a huge amount of work: to be sure, it's backloaded, so be careful about the amount of work at the end of the semester, but the exams were fair, the TFs very helpful (shoutout to my personal GOAT, Jonas), and Prof. Amin is incredibly knowledgeable about the material and friendly. Her knowledge of the material is a bit of a double–edged sword: she's clearly incredibly smart and a fantastic researcher, but this means she assumed we all are, too, so a lot of the examples given in class are a bit too difficult to pick up on the fly, and I think they could benefit from a tad more hands–on time with the material over pure powerpoint lecture (she live–codes in a really impressive way, though, I have to give her that).

I thoroughly enjoyed this class, and would highly recommend it to folks looking to round out their theoretical CS education or looking for a good bridge between theory and systems, because you really see both come alive.
There are not many good spring classes, so this one is one of the only ones that is relatively chill. Not a lot of good options tbh.
This course is great if you are really into programming theory; I didn't love it but still learned a lot. There are very few office hours/section times for this course, so make sure to work with your friends to maximize your availability for them.
The knowledge in this course is valuable for those interested in Programming Languages. Don't be intimidated by the materialsyou'll ultimately grasp the concepts by studying the lecture notes and utilizing AI assistance. Nada is nice and approachable, and the workload remains manageable if you have strong mathematical and logical reasoning skills. This is a great course if you want to know more about computer science. This course may not be ideal if you're seeking direct job market advantages or exposure to trending fields in computer science.
This course is a good alternative to 121/124/any other option for a third formal reasoning class. Nada and the course staff are friendly and helpful and the course material is interesting. The psets are challenging but not impossible, just make sure to start on time. Definitely still a difficult class, especially if you've struggled with other formal reasoning classes, but doable nonetheless.
This is an interesting class but it moves very quickly. You have to be very proactive about what you don't understand, or else you won't even know what questions to ask. As long as you do this, Prof. Amin and the course staff are very kind and helpful. Most of the topics are very niche since theoretical programming language semantics are relatively obscure.

Late hours are very generous, but the grading is harsh on psets so don't let them sneak up on you. The midterm is easy but the final is significantly harder so be careful. Psets are pretty fun as long as you start early.

If you already know you're interested in PL topics this course will probably be great for you. Experience in math proofs (mostly induction) will be very helpful. OCaml is useful to know in advance but I did manage to pick it up on the fly.
If you are on the fence about this class or are considering this class because the Q–Guide says it easy, don't take this class.
