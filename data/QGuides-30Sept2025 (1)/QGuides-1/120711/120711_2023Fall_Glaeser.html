Take the class prerequisites seriously – having a strong understanding of multivariable calculus (especially when it comes to optimizing with constraints) is crucial to understanding most all the math that happens in this class. In the first few weeks, you might feel like an overwhelming amount of confusing content is being thrown at you, but a month in, you'll start to realize how related all of the things this class covers truly are and how the primary mathematical process is really just constrained optimization with refinements depending on the type of policy or phenomena we're studying. I found lectures really interesting and engaging (especially the first 15–30 minutes where we focus on policy discussions), and though it can be hard to intake a lot of the math that Ed breezes through on the slides, you'll still get some sense of the intuition of it all. Don't procrastinate on modeling projects, and don't be afraid to take creative approaches to them. Through the process of double–checking your strategies in the textbook, lecture slides, and with your peers, you'll find the gist of the content really does start to seep in, and you'll walk away from the class having learning a lot about the world of microeconomic modeling! I am assuming that everyone else will tell you how hard this class is. I want to offer some words of advice from the perspective of a student who is not super cracked at math but still got a lot out of the course.
1) Take this class if you are thinking about going to grad school for economics. Ed is a fantastic lecturer, and he introduces you to graduate–level economic models. But even more so than the content of the class, 1011a is a signaling mechanism that indicates that you are serious about econ and care a lot.
2) The material is very learnable. You will not understand everything in lecture. Hell, you might only understand the contents of the "policy discussion" (this is what Ed does in the first twenty minutes of class before diving into math). But make sure that you read the CA notes and the textbook on Canvas. If, after every week, you look through this material, it becomes quite digestible. I may not have had a strong math background, but I was very willing to put in the time.
3) Treat the course like a bunch of different variations on a single theme. Basically, the course teaches you how to set up a bunch of different models and then solve them. The formula is this: write down your utility function or profit function. Take a derivative to determine the optimal quantity of a good / quantity of factor inputs that you want. And that's it! The tricky part is setting up the model in the first place, and then having enough intuition to interpret the results.
4) Re: point 3, try to takeaway economic intuition from the lectures, not the complicated math. You can pick that up later. Just try to grasp on to two or three big ideas ––– they will serve you well when the modeling projects, the midterm, and the final roll around.

Finally, you can do it! The class is hard, but not impossible. The TF, Akaash, was absolutely fantastic ––– he broke down the topics that Ed whizzed through in lecture into much more digestible bites. Ultimately, I hope that you choose to take the class, and I can guarantee that, if you do, you will come out of it with a much stronger understanding of micro. Good luck, folks!

For context I took Econ 10A/B and 21A/B before taking this course. Read if you have a similar background.

Generally there's just a really steep learning curve in the first half of the class as you get used to taking messy derivatives super quick all the time, don't let that discourage you since the content of the class ends up being quite analogous to the first few units, just different models. If you can hold out through the first half, then it gets easier and I would recommend focusing on the economic intuition behind everything really deeply as compared to getting caught up in the math longer than you need to. I definitely got scared off by the math in the lectures and psets and regretfully didn't have as great of an understanding of the economics by the end of the course as I would have liked to have because I spent too much time worrying about the accuracy of my math.

1. Lectures: Ed is great at what he does but my eyesight sucks for reading the board and honestly even if I could see I did not understand any of it when it came to lecture. Don't try to note down everything, just try to listen to him in class or annotate slides. CA lecture notes come out after class and are really nice. You'll get yourself psyched out trying to keep up with the exact details of what he's writing and miss out on understanding the intuition. Topic lectures are super fun.

2. Section: CAs are great, Luca is great, and Aakash was saving my grade in the class for sure. Try to go to his review sessions in person or at least watch the recordings if you can't make them. Makes everything broken down much easier mathematically.

2. Psets: The first few will probably have you in disbelief. My first pset I was dying in OH and spending like 10–15 hours. You'll feel alone, but then you'll find friends and you'll all bond over the semester. And you get used to the pset style! Then you realize it's completion based, and that you shouldn't spend too much time on accuracy. Solve problems well for the ones where you think you can because it's good practice, but the monstrous ones you can't figure out either go to OH or just wait for the solution key. Easily takes off a few hours you can use for studying, and I knew people who got away with just spending a couple hours writing it and turning it in (truly completion based). Just focus on learning course material and turning in something for psets. Check the solutions later. Often times the kinds of questions you did on psets are the setup on exams or modeling projects too. It becomes more familiar the more you see it. The second half of psets are so much easier. They are unfortunately timed though (ex. thanksgiving, during modeling project, before midterm, etc) so save your drop for one of those.

3. Exams: Way too long for the timeframe lol, and that's by design unfortunately. They grade harsher than you think for TFUs. But honestly anyone who scores above around 25th percentile in the midterm was projected to get an A–, which is great, because by the time you hit mid semester almost everyone has dropped out of the class and you're left with 50–70 extremely smart people and it'd be impossible for someone like me to score above half of them with my math background. Final was also hard. Just do a lot of practice, go to review sessions, practice TFUs, etc. and trust your partial derivative taking skills. Pour the rest of the time into stat unless you want to stretch yourself for an A in econ (I'd be the wrong person to ask).

4. With Stat 110: in the first half of the year, you might forget you are taking stats because econ will seem much more scary, whereas stats homework is like fun counting problems. Don't let that mindset stay the entire semester. Stat will get more long and tedious and you will benefit greatly from understanding the course material on a deeper intuitive level. Every hour spent studying stats will almost certainly help you on the psets and exams. Even though your grade in econ will look incredibly uncertain don't reduce your hours on stat too far down. Modeling projects seem intimidating but really you just have to get around the mean/median and everything is curved. Just hang on in econ since psets are completion based and continue trying to keep up with economic intuition once the math settles down. The economic intuition is NOT hard to learn, but you DO need to put some time into understanding it.

TLDR: if you have my background, you are aiming for an A–, and you can spend 10–15 hours a week on this course (add 5–10 for modeling project weeks and exams), just hang on, don't let the math get you, go to sections/review, try to understand the economic intuition, and you will very likely get an A– (or maybe B+ depending on your final). It's honestly a great class if not for the grade/time pressure, use it like a challenge for your sanity and you'll be proud of how much you could do. 1010A is always an option, so if you're here it's for the challenge anyway. Good luck!

LECTURES
Ed is a passionate lecturer who enchants easily–impressionable sophomores and ambitious freshmen while delivering little pedagogical value. Given a lecture, most of the time is spent by him talking through derivations on the slides. That is a red flag right there. Avoid lecturers who teach math on the slides. Sadly, Ed’s handwriting is terrible as he admits, and it seems to be a dead–end for Ed to be a better teacher in math derivations. The only useful part is when he stop talking about the math and he talks about the history, applications, motivations etc for the theory. He has good stories for those.

Recommended strategy: Skip all lectures except the cities I, cities II, crime and riots, moral hazard, and political economy where Ed spends more time in giving his valuable insights and well–motivated presentations. If you do go to the other lectures, then you might want to do your own work when Ed goes on a math derivation that you don’t find helpful. You can also attempt flipped classroom where you go through the slides on your own before the lectures. Anyways you can always get the relevant content delivered to you via the good sections of Luca or Aakash. Also don't be convinced by your classmates with perfect attendance, your classmates are simply impressionable sheep who don’t gain anything from the lectures but their brain is drawn to listen to the passionate Ed but learning minimally of anything useful.

PROBLEM SETS
The current design of the problem sets does not assist you in your learning but has the side effect of waste your time by choosing functional forms that are hard to manipulate, which obscures you from learning the economic intuition behind it. They could have designed more pedagogical psets by setting concrete values or better functional forms so that we can focus on applying our economic analysis instead of wasting our time bashing a disgusting expression. Perhaps the author wanted to make the student spend more time on the pset, but they should instead add more interesting problems instead of making an interesting problem non–interesting and hard to solve and in the process losing its pedagogical value.

Recommended strategy: It is crucial to learn by doing but it is simply unfortunate that the psets are bad. The learn by doing can be substituted by section materials, which have pretty good exercises especially those written by Luca or Gabe.

MISC
The lectures, textbooks, psets, exams and sometimes lecture and section notes have extremely poor expositions, in the sense that they don’t define their variables explicitly like how a math problem would. For example, this is from the 2022 finals

Suppose there are a gold coin factory and a pineapple plantation on a tropical island. Manufacturing coins generates waste that affects the profitability of the plantation. The profit of the coin factory is Ak^\alpha –rk.

Then you are asked to do a bunch of things with that model. But what is A, r, k and \alpha? Much of the course will be you figuring out this puzzle of notation which is not necessary, since the person who proposes a model should explicitly define its variables!

They also don’t define their vocabulary. For example, they will show the Prisoner’s dilemma and say “this and this is a Nash equilibrium”. But what is exactly a Nash equilibrium? The example helps but the teacher should give the exact definition.

Textbook and lecture notes are written by the students, of which I am heavily grateful of because I rely on these materials heavily for this course. But they are too bloated and only 10% of the words in those are useful. Sadly, it might not be exhaustive too so you need both to complement each other, in the sense that your best hope is that when the textbook is missing that subsection the lecture notes have it, and vice versa. Keep them in handy and ctrl+f is your friend.
