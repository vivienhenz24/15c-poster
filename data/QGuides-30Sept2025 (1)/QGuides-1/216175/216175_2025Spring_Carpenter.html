This class is extremely important, especially in the current context. I was able to learn a great deal from the group projects we completed. I do think this class was a little different this year since the class is usually meant to be with a smaller group, and I don't believe Professor Carpenter was expecting the number of students to enroll in this class that it did. At times, the projects we were working on weren't very clear, and there was a lot of confusion among students about exactly what we were expected to do. The highlight of this class for me was the weekly project meetings we'd with our tribal partners, where I learned a great deal. I'm not sure if Professor Carpenter is planning to cap this course in the future. However, I would still recommend this class nonetheless, especially if you have never learned about tribal governments and how they operate as sovereign entities in the U.S.

TAKE THIS COURSE. The guest lectures and course materials are excellent. You will gain extensive knowledge of the diversity of governance and culture of Native Nations and how important strong institutions and culturally matched governance structures are to economic development and by extension cultural heritage reclamation. For those interested in law, the course also covers lots of landmark cases of federal Indian law. The highlight of the course is being able to work collaboratively on a semester long project with a tribal nation and being able to deliver a tangible project that will hopefully tangibly help those communities

I loved this course, but think it can be even better. Strengths: Carpenter is just awesome, so friendly and SO smart. The guest lecturers were consistently incredible and were probably the highlight of the course (LOTS of credit to Dan for this pedagogically, too, it just makes a lot of sense for such a topic centering the work of Native Nations). Opportunity to work with a Nation rather than have a discussion section, incredible. WHERE TO IMPROVE: I think the project I was working on was thrown together semi–last minute, and as a result, the work that my small group did over the course of the semester was incredibly minimal, and was something realistically we did in 3 hours. I think other projects seemed more engaged, but ensuring a similar level of depth in each project I think could help really make this course shine in helping students to more deeply apply what they are learning in lecture (granted, I think this course had a surprisingly high enrollment which probably contributed to this). ALSO, I really appreciate how central the lectures were, but if there is a better way to make the readings more tied in, I think that would be great. The readings are kind of general/abstracted Native history stuff that is helpful context, but you did not need to do at all. Maybe just have less readings but make them much more central?

All in all, this was a very pragmatic/grounded course which I loved, I just think the student participation part could have been a little more fleshed out. To that end, I would suggest LOOKING at the HKS Native Nations Course (I think you can take it as an FAS class) because it seems like that structure is much more built out and the engagement is probably a little deeper.
This is a great class! You will have the opportunity to hear from so many different native speakers, and you really will learn so much. This class is very relaxed and not stressful. We had a midterm, which was only a few short questions, and you could use a cheat sheet, and your grade is mainly about how well you do the semester–long project you're assigned. The projects you get to do in this class give you a chance to work with Native nations and make a real difference. We also had a final five–page paper that wasn't stressful either, just talking about what you've learned from the project and connecting it to readings. Professor Carpenter is great, and this class really immerses you in the history/realities of Natives and will teach you everything you never knew.
