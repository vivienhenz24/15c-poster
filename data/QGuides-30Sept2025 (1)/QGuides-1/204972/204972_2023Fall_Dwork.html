What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

Comments
Found this course because it satisfies a lot of CS requirements, and was pleasantly surprised with the course material. This is probably some of the most interesting applied mathematics in computer science. As a bonus, Prof. Dwork is part of a hotbed at Harvard for the study of Differential Privacy, and it was satisfying to have questions answered directly whenever. That is also a small downside, though, as finding additional explanations online only yields scientific papers. Definitely take this if you are interested in secure secret sharing mechanisms, cryptography, and privacy as a whole. It is theory–based PSETS due every two weeks that shouldn't take more than 10 hours each.
This is a great course, especially if you have any interest in the intersection of CS and law or the applications of theoretical CS. The format is incredibly unique, and you will learn a lot. Prof. Dwork is also a leading researcher in differential privacy and algorithmic fairness, so it's great to learn these ideas from her. However, this course is not easy, and the theoretical parts can get pretty tricky without enough background. In many ways it almost felt like a CS graduate class, with challenging biweekly problem sets and a long take–home final. If you're willing to put in the work you should take it!
Very good class. Conceptually interesting. A better name would be Applications of Indistinguishability. Indistinguishability is why fairness, privacy, and cryptography are in one class. Homework problems can be difficult to understand, but TFs and Cynthia are super available and grading is pretty easy. The discussions in the law class are sometimes rough, but it's cool to see the issues lawyers are concerned with. I had never thought about designing regulation before.
This is a highly relevant class given the recent advances in AI, and the joint sessions with law students offers a chance for inter–disciplinary discussion! However, the CS–only classes are often overly technical with difficult problem sets to follow, so be prepared to do the work!
