My opinion is overall positive but definitely below expectations. The topics are interesting but sometimes do not get fully flushed out. Yannai will make you stop, think, and discuss, but sometimes he overcomplicates things with confusing notation, wording, or just by abstracting away an otherwise intelligible concept.
This is a really interesting, collaborative–based course where the main assignment is a group essay that allows you to explore any interesting observation you make in society. Since the group project is the majority of your grade, I recommend finding group members early on, and get started thinking about ideas right away. Aside from the project, there are a few problem sets that are very manageable to complete as long as you go to lecture and take good notes. For the graded notes assignment, take your time and really think about how the material can be applied in other areas. Overall, a good class where collaboration is key and lectures are really interesting.
The course content was really quite interesting, especially if you like math, algorithms, economics, etc. That said, this course does not hold you accountable to learn all the content that deeply, since there are only three psets and the psets are quite a bit easier than the lecture content. Yannai is a great professor, but the TFs don't particularly enhance the class. I would not expect to get much out of going to the TFs' office hours and I would expect very little guidance on your final project until the very end of the semester. The grading is also pretty weird: the cutoff for an A is a 95% and every assignment is graded extremely strictly. The TFs take off points for the most minor errors.
In my opinion, this class should be taken only if you are very interested in game theory and have a strong mathematical background. I was a bit thrown back at first about how much math was required for the course, but it truly was nothing that you couldn't understand if you had taken the required pre–requisites. I just felt like there was a learning curve if you had not taken much beyond what the pre–requisites require. Overall, I'm happy that I took the course and the final project was fulfilling to explore, but I wish there was more real–time feedback on our paper as we submitted the milestones.

I was so excited to take this class, and I ended up so disappointed. After reading glowing Q–guide reviews, I submitted a preference questionnaire to get in; the class was capped at 75. We were constantly told to reference the syllabus, which was extremely unclear on every assignment description and needed more detail. PSETs felt like busy work that were devoid of relevance to lecture, and grading was extremely stringent. The lecture notes assignment was particularly bad. For the class being about "incentive structures," the incentive structure to go earlier in the semester was nonexistent, as students who opted to complete it later benefitted from hearing about the mistakes of their earlier peers. A better syllabus could have solved this problem, as no one had a clue how things were going to be graded. Office hours were always too packed, and the class was understaffed. The TFs were curt and tough to get in contact with. I have no clue how the final paper is going to go, as we received minimal feedback on our milestones.
