Although I thuroughly enjoyed the course, it is almost an art history course. You spend most of your time on materiality of books. If that interests you, definitely take it. I wasn't aware of this going in, but I enjoyed it nonetheless. Prof Stern is super knowledgable, and it's truly a special experience.
This is a unique class that will redefine how you look at texts and their presentations. Learning to engage with Harvard's extensive collections is incredible, and if you are even just looking for the chance to see some unique objects (Gutenberg Bible, Noble Fragments, First Folio... the list goes on), then this is certainly the class for you. That said, the lessons from this course reach so far beyond the realm of libraries and collectionsyou may never look at the written word the same way again. If you're excited about books, but perhaps not a humanities concentrator, then no need to feel discouraged: students from a wide range of academic backgrounds composed our class, and the only real requirement is a sense of curiosity about this aspect of our world that we often take for granted.
This class is easily the best class I have taken at Harvard. If you have a chance to take it you absolutely should, regardless of background and prior experience. It has changed my perspective on not just books, but the history and development of modern life. Professor Stern is an incredible professor and puts so much time each week into curating an amazing selection of books, readings, and lectures.

Every week you get to spend time in Houghton looking at some of the most pivotal and incredible scrolls/manuscripts/books from throughout history (and you're actually allowed to flip through them and examine as you wish!). The assignments are manageable and well thought through, allowing you to deepen your knowledge and engagement with the material. This year we had weekly readings, weekly short reading responses (completion–based), and two papers at the middle and end points of the semester.

The class was held in different locations each week including the Divinity School, the Law School, Dana Palmer, and more. As well as giving you a great opportunity to explore the libraries of Harvard, it means the breadth of material on offer is vast. There are also really cool extra sessions such as the printing workshop where we learnt to use a printing press and a visit the the book restoration building where we learnt how books were bound. Additionally, we were able to speak to so many cool guest speakers and auditors from a whole range of different backgrounds.

A nice extra bonus of this class is learning how to navigate and make the most of the Harvard libraries. Whether you are hoping to do some research/thesis or just want to make the most of everything the Harvard collection's have to offer, this course helps you build great relationships with librarians and learn how to make the most out of all the resources on offer.

Truly an incredible course
This is a bad class. I cannot recommend it to any student, even if they were fascinated by book history, like me. Many of the sterling reviews for this course will come from students who are more wide–eyed than myself, and perhaps underclassmen who are still in the awe of seeing what Harvard has to offer. This course provides you fantastic access to Harvard's amazing book treasures. You really do get to see everything from the Gutenberg Bible to Shakespeare's First Folio; but the little–known secret I've learned as a humanities major is that you can request to see any number of these fantastic books all on your own which is a much better use of your time than this class.

The books you review are the highlight, but the pedagogy is poor. As a result, it is a far better use of your time to talk to the librarians and check out these books on your own time, instead of as a part of the course. Prof. Stern while both earnest and passionate about book history has overlong, meandering lectures that could benefit from tightening and active student discussion. For a class that runs 2 hours and 45 minutes, we often find ourselves being lectured at for the vast majority of the class session with little active participation, application of the readings, or clarity in the material being taught. Moreover, this class would frequently run well over time, and the TF would often be relegated to a timekeeper just to ensure Prof. Stern made his way through all of the material. It's very disorganized. Prof. Stern has a habit of asking open–ended discussion questions but will go down the line of participating students until he hears the desired answer for his question and will move on in the slideshow. Rather than sparking discussion, the occasional moments of discussion in the course are something more like plug and play. Furthermore, Prof. Stern's feedback on the writing assignments in the course is questionable; it gives the impression of capriciousness in grading style, because of minimal or odd feedback on why he docked points from your essay.

The only way I was able to follow the book history content of the course was to follow the obvious chronology that made itself apparent week to week (e,g. last week we talked about handwritten manuscripts, this week we'll talk printing presses). But beyond that, I cannot say that I learned much of anything new about book history. The course, in its best moments, feels much more about the wow–factor of seeing cool, old books rather than rigorously learning anything about their history. Not to mention that, at several hundred pages of assigned reading a week, it was very difficult to keep pace with the course's demands. Many students were demotivated by the high number of readings we were assigned and their apparent irrelevancy in subsequent classes.

TLDR: if you want to see cool old books in Harvard Library, go look at them yourself outside the course. This course is not a good use of your time to learn about them or meaningfully engage with themor even just to look at them if you were curious.
take it!! Can be kind of chaotic but in a still deeply interesting and engaging way, not a stressful one
I have to admit that this is a cool class. It is a very Harvard class in which you get to explore all the rich collection this university has to offer. And it is a class where Houghton and other rare book collection will give you the green card to see precious books (they literally pulled out the Gutenberg Bible for us, among other crazily rare books). HOWEVER, I would recommend people to think twice about taking it. First, it is an extremely time consuming class where you have to do a lot of busy work. Each week, there are a near–3–hour lecture, 2 hour section in Houghton where you look at books, one group presentation, one reading response, and about 200 pages of reading assigned. And you don't necessary learn a whole lot other than random facts about certain book, print culture, or a specific culture background. And if you want to take this class to talk about literature this class is NOT about literature. It is about "material text," so essentially we spent most of the semester learning about printed books. I came in expected to talk about how printing culture or manuscript culture changed the content of the book and the significance of that change, for instance, through discussing the literary different of King Lear's two endings. We DID NOT do that. Instead for that week, we looked at a lot of covers of printed Shakespearean plays. This course had a lot of technical reading about manuscript or print techniques, technologies, and terminologies, as well as lists of examples, which I do not think I was able to retain much, since we barely talk about readings in class. Also, since this class is pretty much made up of endless lists of books and readings, I am not sure what exactly I really learned other than some detailed facts about manuscripts, printed books, Bible, etc. It is a really cool class, but I am not sure what I took away from it. This class feels like a thesis that only lists the body paragraphs but lacks a central argument(s). The content of this class is also extremely European/Judeo–Christian centered, which makes sense as the professor is a Jewish Studies professor. But it does makes the class a bit boring and biased for those who do not read Hebrew or do not have a Bible/Torah background. This class can be improved in so many way. But as for now, I would think twice about taking it.
