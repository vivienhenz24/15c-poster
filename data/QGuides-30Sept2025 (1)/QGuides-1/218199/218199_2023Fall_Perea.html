What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

This class discusses a lot of important topics in health equity which is what drew me to it. It also had a very cool course that allows you to work with community organizations in Boston and apply the knowledge you've learned in the classroom to tackle some very important issues/projects. However, it is also very difficult. A lot of the work is group/project based so your grade will largely rely upon the engagement of your group. The TF also plays a large role in how you are supported and graded so it is important to communicate actively with the TF or professor to facilitate feedback on assignments before submission or discuss questions you may have.
Be prepared to deal with some very difficult group work. For the sake of your group members, please don't take this class if you aren't determined to put in the work to make a great deliverable.
I would tell them that if you want to learn about public health with a normative lens, and if you see community based projects as helping consultants and other elites, then this class is for you. Some aspects of the class are useful, but if you want a liberatory or imaginative perspective, then I would look elsewhere.
Talk in class sessions. Your collective voices make the class special and informative. Even if you have trouble speaking out loud the intimacy of the small group makes it easier.
I WOULDNT RECOMMEND THIS CLASS! At first, I was excited by the idea of a project based course with community engagement. It was awesome to meet organizers who cared so much about niche but super important issues and see why/how their passions manifested through public and justice work. The class has a break about half way through and Flavia even brought pizza a couple of times. BUT the class is just not worth the time and unnecessary stress. There is so little scaffolding for students to know community best practices and have meaningful project ideas that the whole class felt disingenuous. Several groups, mine included, had deliverables that were just connecting their partner organizations to other organizations. However, for the sole purpose of completing the course, it was a long drawn out process of conducting interviews and making things look pretty. Our final presentation thus felt like preaching to the choir. Youre essentially giving a 20 minute lecture to community experts and 20ish students who already probably knew the information because the group projects are pretty similar/have overlap. Towards the end of the semester, everything was just disorganized. The class just became reviews of the professor's research and forced discussions based on questions students had on readings. Among other flaws in the class, some readings are unnecessarily long, we had several challenges being able to see/access materials, and there were several mistakes with assignment descriptions, submission deadlines, etc. on canvas. The feedback is short, vague, and doesn't reflect the grading on assignments/participation. Grades took at least a week to be updated, if at all. I received only 2 grades out of multiple assignments by the time the class ended. The syllabus also gives vague descriptions of the assignment components and it is assumed that you should just know how to complete things to an unrealistic, unspecified standard with no rubric to back anything up. It also doesn't list any concrete information on the expectations in the class on participation, attendance, etc. so if you aren't keen to ask for specifics early, be prepared for a whole rollercoaster of unexpected mess. As a student with both DAO and Title IX accommodations, this class was a nightmare. If you have accommodations, please dont take this class or do so with extreme caution and keep EVERYTHING in writing. Firstly, the class is in PBHA on the second and third floors with no elevator. When I had mobility issues, getting zoom access was restricted and an unnecessary deal was made out of moving class to the first floor. Almost every time I asked for help from Flavia or Kamille, but especially Flavia (Kamille often just felt like an enabler/bystander in much of this, which wasn't much more helpful), their condescending tone and poor communication styles made me, and even other DAO/Harvard staff who were witnesses, feel uncomfortable. They were very unaccommodating to physical and mental accessibility needs and quick to shut down conversations on how my accommodations could be met. The irony of the situation was truly unfathomable and traumatic given the supposed intention of the course. Although Flavia preached the importance of listening to people who are experts on their situations and how unethical health practices are harmful–it appears her personal values and teaching style are misaligned. I shared my concerns on several occasions and she was wholly unperturbed. She was incredibly shady in emails and obviously held grudges (even if I owned up to and/or explained something due to disabilities/illnesses). I often DREADED coming to class. The class is small, so everything you do is observed on a microscopic level and that sort of punitive scrutiny can't be productive to anyone's learning experience. Dealing with all these course issues, the staff's poor attitude, and navigating disabilities, was exhausting. I'm worried about repercussions but hoping my honesty will prevent other unassuming students from blindly taking this course. I'm no quitter, so I finished and did my best, but at what cost? For future students, even if you dont have disabilities or other issues, I would suggest that you take a different course or join a community–based organization and learn through more organic, hands–on experiences. I think Flavia and Kamille are superficially nice, but are deeply flawed and so is the class. Dont expect the content or name of the course to guarantee that you'll have a good experience or even really learn anything.
The content of this course is great and the experience completing a real project for a community partner is invaluable. The course will change your understanding of how health interventions should be created and delivered. However, be prepared for a lot of self–directed work.
take this class if youre interested in participating in something hands–on at Harvard!!! I am a GHHP secondary and took this class to explore how we use theory and principles to actually help in our community. You get to know an organization outside of Harvard that you can continue to check in on once you graduate and understand what it would be like to get involved in this type of work post grad.
