Great class. Teaches you how to break down complicated equations and find approximations, in a way you won't learn elsewhere. Builds a great intuition for how to understand phenomena that is often brushed aside because it "needs numerical solutions". Fun class with really good lectures from Brenner. That said the teaching style is very chaotic and jumps around a lot. That is just his style, some like it some don't. I recommend having taken 21a & 21b and some differential equations (or to have seen ODEs in physics. You won't learn traditional ways to solve diff eqs in this class directly, so it is good to have that knowledge beforehand or at least some basic exposure). The only major downside was the organization of the class and sections. Although the lecture notes are thorough, the canvas organization was not always great. Section were quite useless, the undergraduate Tfs that were leading the sections basically just read from section notes from previous years that were on canvas anyway. Office hours were really disorganized and each Tf would give a different, often contradictory answer to the same question. Overall, I recommend the class.
If you're at all thinking about a career as a STEM researcher, this class is indispensable!
Fantastic class!
Course is rather interesting content wise, but not structured super well. Brenner is a great professor who really cares about the subject matter and the students and it's wonderful going to class and section. But, sometimes the flow of the class was a little jumpy from place to place which made it hard to follow and made it difficult to catch up if you fell behind. Section was helpful as it related to the problem sets.
Interesting ideas, and a useful and fun class! Prof. Brenner is an engaging instructor.
It helps if you can connect what you learn in class to real applied mathematics problems. For example, a lot of the course content is applied in fluid dynamics, and seeing how it is used there helps motivate the need for the techniques that are taught.
I LOVED this course, it's the best I have taken at Harvard. Prof. Brenner is engaging and knows the subject matter well, and the course really makes you think. Everything I learned was new or provided new ways of seeing existing concepts, and these methods will be useful in research in many fields. The psets take quite a bit of work, but if you start early and go to OH / speak with other students, they're actually fun to work on. Take this course!! Cannot recommend enough.
I think the class is a collection of very useful methods that I can definitely see myself using in my research in applied physics, however, I think it is not in itself particularly interesting.
There aren't really any formal prerequisites for the class, but the material gets progressively more challenging and is quite difficult to understand by the end. The problem sets are always pretty hard, but the interplay between theory and computational tools is interesting. Professor Brenner's lectures are exciting
Teaches a cool kind of math.
Take this class if you want to get an intro about approximations. Bare in mind that it may not be the best structured course you have taken though.
If you are serious about learning applied mathematics, I strongly recommend that you take this class with a pinch of salt. This class is much more a class on concepts, than a course on how to actually solve problems in applied mathematics (i.e machinery). I recommend auditing this course after you have completed a more standard introductory course to applied mathematical methods in science and engineering.
AM201 is an incredibly useful course. The lectures are interesting and the course note are a really reliable source. I really appreciated the new perspective this class provided one that promotes trying different approaches to problems and "having courage". Overall I learned a lot an gained confidence in my problem solving skills.
I think this class is very useful in the sense that it teaches you approximation techniques that may make analytically–intractable problems solvable up to arbitrary position. I really like Prof. Brenner's philosophy of Applied Mathematics as a no–holds–barred endeavor of trying everything to solve a problem. I do wish that the most–recent lectures were recorded, however, as sometimes the material takes a few passes to grasp fully. Overall, excellent course with excellent teaching staff!
This is a really fun class! Brenner is a fantastic lecturer and the course material is pretty interesting. My only complaint is that the course was kind of messy logistically besides the first two psets, all assignments were graded in the last few days of finals period, and we received very minimal instruction or guidelines on the final project.
The class will teach you how approximations behave. This course changed the way that I look at so many systems, a perfect example is looking at dominant balance between terms in a PDE. It's a concept that seems so simple but I am surprised I had never seen before; it is the ideal introduction to applied mathematics for an advanced undergrad. The psets are pretty tractable with office hours, and there is a lot of time to do each one. Section materials are incredibly useful for solving problems in the class.
This class is all about finding approximate analytical solutions to complex problems that can't be solved exactly (eg ODE's, PDE's, integrals etc). It gave me a lot more intuition about how to approach these types of problems. Brenner is a very engaging lecturer and seems to really care about his students. This year there were 5 psets (which were good but occasionally tedious), a takehome midterm, and a final project that you can do on pretty much whatever you want. Overall a great class and I would recommend it if you ever see yourself doing research that involves approximating complex numerical problems.
