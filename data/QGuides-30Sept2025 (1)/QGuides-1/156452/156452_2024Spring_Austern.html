This course was faster and harder than Stat 210 but more interesting. Compared to Stat 171, Stat 212 focuses more on the theory and construction and not applications. Morgane does a great job of introducing enough rigor without getting bogged down by measure theory; as the prerequisites note, Math 112 is sufficient for analysis background (surprisingly), though the arguments/proof techniques definitely come more naturally with more math background.

A few things to note:

This course is self–contained, though reading a few chapters on martingales in Durrett would help.

Morgane is an amazing professor; ask lots of questions.

Don't be scared by the exams. Morgane is very generous with partial credit.

Overall, you get out what you put in.
One of the best courses I have ever taken!

~ course material ~
The class was a very well–taught introduction to probability theory and a glimpse of some cool ideas beyond. It begins with some basic concepts from stat 210 with some more rigor (e.g., review of conditional expectation and convergence theorems); then, one discusses the Martingale Convergence theorem, uniform integrability, and convergence in L^p spaces. The classic 0–1 laws are discussed, with applications, e.g. in LLN or U–statistics; exchangeability is introduced. Some concentration inequalities (azuma, efron–stein) are proven. The first half of the course then ends with the martingale CLT, which is quite a cool result! The second half then begins with a discussion of Brownian Motion (intro, construction, uniqueness, properties), etc. One then moves to Stein's method. Finally, there is a final project, in which one reads a paper (typically somewhat recent research output), and gives a presentation. Overall, the material is quite fun, and there are a lot of cool theorems and proof methods!

~ comments ~

The course is organized in a very good manner, as to provide both fundamentals and a bit of an idea of the field. Most courses tend to do only one of the two (or neither). However, the basic material (first half and BM) are done fairly rigorously; occasionally measure theory is swept under the rug, but it can be justified with some (and not too great) effort. The book recommendations are very good: Durrett (PTE) is satisfactory for most of the rigor in measure theory (I spent a good amount of time reading details outside of course–time; it also covers some useful concepts not done in 212), Morters–Peres (Brownian Motion) is a good intro to BM and the later chapters (I read a few extras for fun) cover some surprising and cool facts, for instance geometric properties of BM and its relation to random walks); lastly 'Fundamentals on Stein's Method' (cf. arxiv) provides rigor for the Stein's method parts. I find that the very important fundamentals are done quite rigorously and well; some results are introduced to give a sense of what types of results have been proven, and this latter type is usually not justified, but rather intuition (which is good) is provided.

The lecturing is fantastic. The usual way I like lectures to go is to either (1) explicate the material in a clear and engaging manner or (2) provide some intuition otherwise not found. These are both done; the first half of the course especially, Morgane encourages participation and engagement by asking questions about how to proceed; following closely in the course was helpful both in understanding the proofs better and being able to come up with and understand the main techniques of proof (e.g., 'definitions!'). Lecturing speed varies; I found that it started quite quickly and then in the end slowed down a bit. As noted in the last paragraph, I think intuition for the unproven results provided was very useful, whereas the basic facts were justified rigorously (and for these, the intuition often comes from going through the basic ideas and really thinking about the math over and over again).

The assignments are fairly chill and fun. There are about 5 HW assignments, with one drop; there are a large number of additional problems and HWs which are very useful when studying the exam or for gaining more familiarity with the concepts (so one has enough problems to work with, but there is minimal stress). The final project was a fun way to see what sort of methods and ideas are being researched in probability (e.g., random matrix theory, Stein's method, etc.). Admittedly, it was pretty difficult to comprehend the presentations, but I enjoyed it because one gains a viewpoint of how the course can lead to later research.

Now, a few miscellaneous comments the exams are not easy, but contain many fun problems. Studying is certainly necessary but the review process is helpful in going through the main ideas of the course, and there are a number of problems and past finals which are enjoyable (there are a lot of 'cute' problems). I would advise one to read the reference materials, both for review purposes, and because they are good classics, which provide a lot of extras (concepts or 'measure theory' etc!)

Lastly, Morgane is very approachable outside of class; I went to a few office hours to ask questions, in addition to our final project meeting. She provides helpful wisdom, both in respect to the main concepts of the course as well as life advice, e.g. experiences in grad school, which I appreciate. I came into this semester with a heavy math background and stat 210 deciding that I wanted a change from heavily abstract mathematics to learn more stats this course has genuinely built my enjoyment and interest in probability and was important in helping me settle my focus in the field; in this way it has been a very important course for my personal path. It also has provided a satisfyingly large collection of resources for further exploration (e.g., there are the three course references mentioned; a collection of papers from final projects, resources for random matrix theory, etc.). If you are considering this course, definitely take it!

Stat 212 was an absolute ride.

The course began with advanced martingale theory that uses Durrett as reference, which I enjoyed thoroughly. Morgane starts off this half of the course with such blazing speed it forced me to spend many extra hours to review stat 210 material until they are all fairly instinctual. The speed in the beginning of the course almost made me cry, it was definitely faster than 55, but after reading Durrett became much more manageable. I would say this is definitely the highlight of the class, I felt like my brain was absorbing ephemeral forms of peak human imaginations, and this was basically until the midterm.

Now we talk about the exams in 212. They are basically freaking impossible. If you are thinking about taking 212, you have probably done fairly well in 210. In 210 we had 3 hours take home on 3 questions for the midterm, and 6 hours take home for like 5 question in the final. Imagine doing them in person, 5 questions in 75 minutes for midterm, and 7 questions in 3 hours for final, and each question harder than the 210 questions. I remember trying so hard for the 212 midterm and felt a sense of mastery over Martingales, and yet I was still crushed by it, without a doubt some of the hardest exams I have taken.

After midterm we started covering Brownian motion, which was done with slower speed and slightly less clarity. We utilized Morters–Peres, which was a pretty good textbook and an enjoyable read. After Brownian motion when the weather at Harvard finally starts to get better, we covered Stein's methods and related topics, which was fairly bad. The lectures were barely understandable, and the scribed notes had a good amount of typos, definitely my least favorite part of the course (I also felt like I barely understood any of it).

We had Morgane Austern as the professor, who is one of the best lecturers at Harvard, if you have a chance to take a course with her 100% do so.

The homeworks are biweekly and fairly doable. The first of the course I spent more time on the course just reading through textbooks etc, and on average probably took 10 hours per week. One not reasonable thing is for most of the semester the homeworks do not have solutions and even the past practice exams do not have solutions. Lecture was a highlight so you should definitely attend lecture. For exams one should probably read through the scribe notes, do past problems, and read through the textbooks, especially Durrett and Morters–Peres.

Overall one should probably sit into the course for a week or two and if one can handle it one should most likely take it. Objectively one of the best stem classes at Harvard.
If you feel comfortable with real analysis and have any interest in probability, you should definitely take this class. Morgane is one of the most phenomenal, talented, humorous, engaging, and inspiring professors I have ever had the pleasure of taking a class with. She goes above and beyond to ensure that everyone feels welcome and supported in the class –– which, if you have taken any math class here, you will know is anything but the norm. Even though lectures were at 4:30pm and in a windowless room in the Science Center basement, they were regularly my highlights of the day: the content is interesting and beautiful, and the environment created by the teaching stuff makes it even more fun.

I had heard before taking this class that it is challenging and the overall low number of assignments make you feel underprepared for the exams. While the content of the class is certainly challenging, it is not overly so, and the assignments are carefully chosen to ensure that you learn maximally without unnecessary busywork. There was a huge number of optional assignments provided (maybe in response to previous years' concerns), which I did before the exams and which made me feel very well–prepared (and I did end up doing reasonably well on all of the assignments). The final project was no more stressful or time–consuming that a normal problem set.
