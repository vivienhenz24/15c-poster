Brilliant course, with a wonderful teacher. Please, please: pay attention to the historical narrative Gordon is constructing. It is not by accident that one starts a course on "French Social Though" by starting with Durkheim and the Existentialists before getting into the height of properly French theory in the names of Lvi–Strauss, Derrida, and Foucault. You will also notice the notable exclusions of the syllabus: Deleuze & Guattari, Derrida's very minor presence, Lyotard, ... This is a course that focuses on the movement from the thinking subject who celebrates the advent of the republican state to the unconscious in the latter part of the 20th century and how much of a performative contradiction that creates. It is brilliant. Take it.
Don't gloss over Durkheim, as so many do in this class. He is the key to entering the course.

Absolutely take this class if you're interested in political/social theory or philosophy generally. The structure of the course is very basic and gets right to the point: you do the readings (or skim if busy), contextualize them in lecture, discuss them in section, and then write about them in three papers throughout the semster. Prof Gordon is amazing and knows so much about the subject matter, but I've heard he can be hard to connect with unless you study with him directly. Essentially, if you think you can handle long readings and three papers, take this class

This was a great class that made me regret not having taken German Social Thought. Professor Gordon is a captivating lecturer and makes the material very easily digestible; the handouts are a very useful companion both during the lecture, and after when trying to make sense of the material again. It can be a little hard to sit in a lecture hall for 2.5 hours every week just listening to Prof. Gordon speak without any visual references, but the content is always interesting enough that this feels worth it. It is very gratifying to struggle through the readings, then have them explained clearly in lecture and in section; if you're at all interested in critical theory but daunted by how opaque it tends to be, you should definitely take this course!
Take this class! Prof. Gordon is an excellent lecturer who manages to make extraordinarily complicated texts by authors like Derrida and Foucault accessible and clear. This is a course that will change how you think about society and our relation to it. The readings, while not short, are fascinating and remain relevant today. This class is a must for any social sciences or humanities concentrator who wants to think about the larger questions underlying topics in their own work and for any student interested in social and political thought.
