It's the required econometrics course. You just gotta take it.
This was an abysmal class. I didn't write down anything on the final that I didn't already know before I took the class. Section was great, though, and my only source of learning.
Need basic econometric knowledge. May need to work a lot on your own to fully grasp topics.
The visiting professor frequently made mistakes while teaching the course material in lecture, would sometimes disparage students or make comments that seemed off–beat and disheartening about the class, and decided to veer into material completely beyond the scope of what should be taught for a first–year econometrics course. By the end of the semester, over half of the cohort completely stopped attending lectures because of the pedagogical approach in lecture. The visiting professor taught the course in a way with a design that almost seemed to gleefully mock students when they would not understand the material, which is antithetical to teaching. Many students have talked about suffering because of the almost hostile, mocking tone that the visiting professor would take on at times when speaking to them. This course notably damaged the cohort experience and dynamic, and I would not recommend this visiting professor. Elie, who usually teaches this course, I've heard is a much better professor, so for future students, do not worry if Phillip Heiler is not teaching this course again.
My understanding is that often time we need to estimate something for something. Thus, we can come up with all kind of estimators. We can then impose assumptions (write a model) to prove some properties of the estimators we came up with. Then, there are certain properties that we like and certain assumptions that we don't like, so there is a trade–off. Finally, it appears that vector space optimization techniques are very useful in all this business. Indeed, "everything is an orthogonal projection".
This can be one of the hardest core classes because you need to be very fast on a very technical subject to do well on the exams. Before the exam, make sure to do the practice tests so you are warmed up.
Lectures were so unhelpful that less than half of the G1 cohort regularly attended.
Even if you don't understand in the 1st or the 2nd attempt, you'll probably understand it later. Practice makes perfect!
