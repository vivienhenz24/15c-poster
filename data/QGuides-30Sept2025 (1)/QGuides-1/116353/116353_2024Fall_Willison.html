The first third of the year, I really didn't like this class. I truly could not understand some of the linguistic concepts. However, by the end of the year it became one of my favorite classes. Rob/ Dr. Styles is slightly unhinged– but always entertaining, and you can tell he wants you to succeed even if his feedback is hard to hear at first. When the content switches to meaning in literature and then meaning in life– the class gets really good!!
This course is enjoyable if your classmates are wonderful and invested – which they were this semester. However, the class itself needs major work: it seems to not abide by the traditional Expos protocol in any sense, in terms of the way class is run or the writing skills taught. If you are excited about the nuts and bolts of the philosophy of meaning, I would take it. For those interested in taking this class in hopes of cultivating their passion for philosophy, I wouldn't.
Rob Willison is a really awesome guy. He's genuine and cares about his students and he tells really funny stories. I really enjoyed this class and would recommend it if you're interested in the meaning of life, or a little bit of linguistics.
This class is very, very, very philosophy–heavy, and the subjects are very complex. If you have no interest in philosophy or discussing philosophy, this Expos is not for you. The readings are dense and very long, and skimming is not even a possibility when the writing is often so convoluted that you have to read it ten times to understand (such is the nature of philosophical writing I guess). Grading on essays is also really harsh for the subject being so difficult. That being said, if you do like talking about things like truth and the meaning of life, you will enjoy discussions in this class a lot. Rob is such a good facilitator and he genuinely cares that everyone's opinions are heard. Class discussions were the most fun part of this course for sure.
This is a great expos if you enjoy philosophy. I like philosophy of language and of meaning, and as a result the chance to read Frege, Wittgenstein, Grice, Putnam etc. was a wonderful opportunity, and we really got to get into the texts in question. Professor Willison very clearly knows what he's talking about with regards to these texts and it was really wonderful gaining a greater appreciation of them. I also felt that the workload of the course was very reasonable, with it consisting mostly of reading responses to each of the readings to hold us to account on the readings and then structured work on the three essays. The classes themselves are mostly very engaging, with a lot of student led discussion (although sometimes so much that it felt a bit unguided, but this was not often that big an issue).

One thing I will warn however: if you don't have an interest in philosophy of meaning, the readings can get very dense at times and at those times it is very hard to stay motivated unless you have an active interest in the material, so if you do not enjoy philosophy – especially if you don't like things that don't seem to have immediate practical relevance to the real world in some sense – then the course might not be as interesting for you. I still think that Professor Willison does a good job of making it interesting and giving you reason to care, but if that's not your area of interest I don't think it makes too much sense to place this that high up on the expos lottery,
I would strongly advise you against taking this course. Unless you absolutely love philosophy and are ready for grad level readings, you probably won't enjoy this class (even if you are a philosophy lover you still might not enjoy this class because it is poorly organized)

I think the material of this course was so challenging that it felt extremely difficult to really grow as a writer. Expos is meant to be a writing class taught through the vehicle of some other field. In this class however, the philosophy is the focus. I could see this class being a philosophy course for philosophy concentrators, not an expos class. He told us on the first day that this class would cover grad level material, which again does not make sense considering this is an introductory writing class.

Additionally the lack of quality feedback and difficulty communicating with the professor made it extremely difficult to find direction in the course and grow as a writer (his feedback was usually incredibly vague, and he rarely replied to emails).

On top of everything else, things were incredibly poorly organized. The professor would forget to put assignments on canvas, or would randomly change due dates.
This is not an easy expo. It was emotionally and intellectually taxing. However, I did love the class because of the professor and the class. I wasn't in love with the content we were learning every day, but there is so much to learn from the incredible people I was surrounded with. It definitely teaches you about writing, philosophy, and just expands they way you think about problems. Take this if you want to do something meaningful with your expo requirement, but expect to struggle.
Only take this class if you're willing to put the time into the readings. They are extremely dense philosophical texts, and much of my time in this course felt like it was devoted to decoding and understanding those texts rather than actually improving my writing. If you absolutely love philosophy and you think this course would fulfill that love, I would take it. If you're even a little bit uncertain, I wouldn't this is one of the more challenging Expos courses and you will save yourself stress by choosing another one.
Great class if you are interested in philosophy at all. The professor is amazing but if you hate philosophy the readings can be tedious.
Do not take this course unless you really like linguistics. Grading is strict, readings are long and reading response for each class.
