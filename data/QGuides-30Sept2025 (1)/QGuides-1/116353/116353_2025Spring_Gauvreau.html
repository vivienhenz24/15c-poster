Its a great topic and Joseph is a cool guy, but the class is slightly deceiving. You do a lot of time analyzing the texts in section, and most people in the class are experienced in the type of texts you will analyze, so this class is not the one for people who are not the most humanities–oriented. The essays are not too bad, but sometimes it seems like the timelines hit quick (reading an entire book in a weekend). If you stay on top of things, like any expos class, this will be much easier. Getting an A seems to be difficult, but ultimately you will get what you put into the course. Not a terrible expos requirement course, and would be great if you like literature or anayltical writing.
Take the course if you don't have a particular affinity for any topic or area. It's a writing class (expos) that focuses on literature and media itself, so it turns out to be pretty generic in terms of analysis and essay–writing. The third/final essay also offers lots of creative freedom in terms of what you're going to write about, so if you like that, take it into account. Overall, the grading is focused on improvement, it seems. You don't have to write something magnificent to get an A, so it's chill. Just focus on getting those writing mechanics down, and you'll do very well.
Honestly, this was my 6th choice Exposbut it ended up being my favorite class of the semester! I gained such an appreciation for different types of literature and general publications as well. This course made me think in a new way about how authors form ideas and reuse content in their own vision. The books we read were especially engaging and my final paper was a lot of fun to write. Professor Gauvreau was incredible and the class had a great time in sections getting to know each other and hang out. Highly recommend!
This course is definitely a time commitment; we read all of our texts in a very short time frame (Hamlet in one weekend, movies in a handful of days, etc), and because we spent a long time on our first essay, we had less time for the other two. The material was engaging, feedback was helpful (though somewhat last minute), and the final essay prompt was open enough to write an essay about anything. Grading was strict (compared to what I've heard from friends in other expos courses), but as long as you are willing to spend the time on writing, this class is definitely interesting and effective enough to warrant taking. I would highly recommend reading the texts in advance of the class and having an idea of what you want your final paper to be, though.
This is a better expos than most. None will be very easy, but this specific class and Joseph will support you all the way through it.
This course just casually expects you to read the entirety of Shakespeare's "Hamlet" and Anne Carson's "Nox" in a week each, so if you can get a head start on reading, DEFINITELY do that. Also, I'm like pretty sure you're graded on improvement rather than actual content, so keep that in mind when you're writing essays. Honestly, this definitely isn't one of the easier expos courses, so I'd recommend taking another one unless you're willing to put in the work.
