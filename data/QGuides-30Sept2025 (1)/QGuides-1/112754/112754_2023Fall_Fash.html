TAKE THIS CLASS!!! I am a CS and Math concentrator, and even though I love my concentration, this class was by far my favorite class this semester (beating a CS, a Math, and a Stat class).

Pros:
– Extremely interesting and engaging lectures + wonderful guest lectures
– The professors are genuinely passionate about this class
– Virtually no work for most of the time (there are assigned readings every week but you can get through by just skimming or not even doing that)
– Take–home midterm and final whose format is easy even for a stem concentrator (10 definitions of 5–6 sentences + two 3–4 page essays) + you have more than enough time to complete them
– A very fun creative project for which you can do virtually anything (a painting, a poem, a clay sculpture, a cooking video, a crochet doll, etc.)

Cons:
– Some of the readings are just photo–scanned so you cannot copy from or search in them
– Some of the lectures might feel a little disorganized, but then you cover a lot of interesting topics!
– The midterm and final can be time–consuming, but then you do practically no work during the rest of the semester!
This course doesn't require much work outside of class, but that's one of its only redeeming qualities. Though the professors are giants in their field, the lectures tend to be disorganized, unconnected to the readings, and delivered soporifically. Lecture attendance is also required. That said, if you're very interested in the topic, you might get something out of this. The professors are very enthusiastic about meeting with students.
Pros: this class is easy. Like, except for the midterm week and the section presentation week, I never did any work for this class. There is an ungodly amount of readings, but that's actually a good thing–––if there's too much to read, nobody will do the readings, which means everybody is equally clueless during section discussions! So... yeah. You don't need to do the readings unless you want to. It's really a "you get out what you put in" sort of class.

Cons: It needs a much greater emphasis on the "now" part of Moctezuma's Mexico: Then and Now. It really felt like I was learning history for no good reason beyond Harvard having a deranged philosophy that we all need to take courses irrelevant to our field of study with no importance to daily life. PLEASE teach us about modern–day Mexican culture more than just saying "here is the history, and yes, it is still important today." I felt like what I learned was at best loosely related to Mexican culture and politics today, which is really all I have any incentive to care about.

The biggest issue I have is that I found it really really REALLY boring. That said, the easiness makes up for it. Gem in a workload sense, absolutely miserable in a content sense. Obviously, not everyone will feel that way: if you're interested in Mexican history, it will probably be an amazing class for you.
This class is interesting. You learn a lot about Mexico's Aztec history, and the two professors are some of the top in this field, so no better place to learn than here. I enjoyed lectures most times and section was interactive as well. However, it does feel like there are some biases in this class about what they believe is morally right vs. wrong in terms of the ethics, religion, and readings given. I understand teaching about religion and cosmovision can be a tender and delicate subject that must be properly balanced giving respect to all people, which I do think that the professors did well and are not perfect. But sometimes I feel like it can paint Christianity in a bad light, which doesn't feel the best if you are a Christian in the audience. I understand that there are certain blunt facts about history but its important not to overgeneralize people in modern times, especially to present–day Mexicans who identify as Christian or Catholic.
