This truly opened my eyes to how poorly organized a course can be. On the first day of class, the course website was not done being set up. We were assured that this was because the professor had just arrived, and that it would be finalized soon. It was not until Thanksgiving break that the schedule was even close to being completed. (Fun fact: as of today, 12/18, there are still some labs that are marked "TBD" on the schedule. For full clarity, the course is over.) Until then, we were left guessing as to what the content of each lecture would be, when the next assignment would be due, when the next quiz would be, and what the quiz would cover. The lack of a course schedule made it very difficult to understand how each session's content fit into the overarching trajectory of the class, leaving the lectures feeling very discontinuous. It was also near–impossible to plan ahead because of this lack of information. On one homework assignment, the Gradescope autograder was not properly set up until the day before the deadline. This left us with less than 24 hours to fix errors in our code, and the instructor waved this off by claiming that if our code was correct the release of the autograder wouldn't make a difference anyway. That's all very well, but then why publish the autograder at all? Furthermore, many of the autograder checks were specific cases of exception handling that were not clearly communicated in the assignment. Many of us had small errors that stemmed not from a lack of understanding of the material, but from minor discrepancies in implementation.

That all being said, the crowning failure of this course was the final project. Over the course of the semester, the description of the project changed from one of open–ended software development to a hyper–specific delve into spectral analysis (conveniently chosen to suit the professor's background). Despite the niche topic, we were given little to no background information/material to help us understand the extensive. jargon–heavy requirements. My group ultimately shelled out 40–50 hours each working on this project, concentrated heavily toward the end of the semester. Better yet, we spent the majority of our time not actually developing software, but trying to understand the meaning of terms like inferred continuum, emission lines, and flux quantification. The pacing of the project was supposed to be split up by six milestones over the course of six weeks, but the release of these milestones (particularly the later ones) was so delayed that it actually inhibited our work. Instead of having a thorough understanding of project expectations and end deliverables from the start, we were literally living deadline to deadline –– we didn't know when milestones would be released, or even how many milestones there were in total, until late into the project. It was not until Milestone 5 that a sizeable list of clarifications and changes to the project requirements were released, which meant we ended up having to change some of our initial work (it should be noted that we had to take the initiative to do this, as we did not actually receive feedback for any of the milestones despite being required to submit them). To cap things off, Milestone 6 (which was released TWO DAYS before the original deadline, in the middle of finals period) revealed several new deliverables that had not previously been announced, the most unreasonable of which was a seven–minute video that had to include all team members. While the instructor agreed to make this deliverable optional after extensive pushback, it caused far too much unnecessary stress given that many students were juggling multiple projects as well as pre–arranged travel plans. The instructor also granted a three–day extension to facilitate these additional requirements, but failed to take into account that we might have already planned our schedules around what we believed to be final deadlines for multiple courses (not just his). I still can't comprehend what could have justified such a last minute release of new work in the instructor's mind, especially given these requirements could EASILY have been communicated early on. But of course, "there are no mistakes."

I will note that I don't blame the TAs for this course for its poor organization, as I believe they made a genuine effort to maintain communication with students and fight for accommodations (Kimon especially was carrying the Ed board at times). Unfortunately, confusion was just too great and I doubt they got much support from the instructor to be able to perform their jobs to the best of their abilities. Overall: the course content and assignments were pretty reasonable if you ignore the horrendous organization and dumpster fire of a final project (and if you have sufficient background in programming to make up for the lack of proper teaching). If no changes are made to the teaching situation, though, I can confidently say this course will take years off of your life :)
