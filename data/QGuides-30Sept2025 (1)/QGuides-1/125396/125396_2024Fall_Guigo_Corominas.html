Definitely take this class before or simultaneously with Ec 1010A. I took 1010A before this class and a lot of the content started making sense only after I took this class. It is also fun to see the application of multivariable calc in economic situations.
If you need to learn multi and you're in Econ, absolutely take this class over 21a. Roderic is the best, and he makes himself available for office hours, even outside of the ones he has posted. The PSets start out pretty easy (less than 1.5 hours per PSet), but some of the later ones took me a few hours, so make sure to at least budget a good amount of time and work with a good group/go to OH. Our CAs were also great and provided a lot of times for us to work on PSets.

My only criticism is that the class gets a little confusing after the first and second midterms, and I wish Roderic spent more time explicitly teaching some of the more difficult techniques in class. Otherwise, if you don't understand the new ideas when you work on problems with your table at the chalkboards, you won't understand anything for the rest of class. This isn't terrible, since I think Roderic is really amazing at explaining things one–one in office hours, but something to watch out for.

Overall, though, definitely a super painless math class. I came in as a sophomore, having taken BC Calc in HS and nothing Freshman year. I reviewed basic integration techniques and calc concepts over the summer (just on Khan Academy), which was very helpful but not strictly necessary. If you're worried, like I was, that we'd use more advanced calc class, don't be. You don't need trig–sub, series, etc. I think we might've used integration by parts once or twice? But most of the time when we integrated it was just reverse power rule, very simple. The hard part of this class is understanding the concepts, not actually doing the math (since you don't even need a calculator for the class).
This class is, at least from what Ive heard about 21A, a relatively lighter workload multivariable class, with a great professor! The information we learn is definitely applicable to many different contexts, and the smaller class size is a bonus! Obviously it will be somewhat time consuming, with 3 psets a week, 2 midterms, a final and a mini exam, but there are a lot of resources and I think the grading is very fair.
Roderic is great! Its really nice to have a class with 20–30 people. Definitely feels a bit like high school (many freshmen, class 3x a week), which can be nice in terms of participatory learning/the class dynamic. There are always psets (although not too long) and concepts can be frustrating/hard to understand but ultimately its not too difficult to do well in this class. Theres also a strong set of course materials. Cant say much about 18 vs 21 theyre probably pretty similar, but I enjoyed 18 a lot!
Stay focused in class, take good notes, and take full advantage of office hours as well as MCS to get all your conceptual questions answered prior to exams
Take this class! It is so fun and Roderic is a fantastic prof. It was a manageable amount of work, but a good amount to properly prepare me for the course.
Its a banger. Roderic is the goat.
Despite the name, this class really does not defer too much from 21a. 18a does cover differential equations and some economic applications in optimization which 21a doesn't, but there isn't a huge economic/social science application in 18a.

The course is small (21 people), so it feels like how a high school math class is taught as there is a lot of groupwork. Roderic is a great teacher and really does get to know each of the students in the class on a personal level. There are 3 CAs (course assistants) that are sophomores/juniors that took the class before. They are really helpful during office hours in helping with PSETs and hosting review sessions before tests. Both Roderic and the CAs are easily accessible if you need help.

The 3 PSETs a week is not that bad as you have 2 days (or 3 days on the weekend) to complete each PSET. The PSETs really do help you reinforce the material you learn in class, and there are a lot of support during office hours to complete the PSET. There are videos for each topic that you can watch too if you need clarification on a topic.
I strongly recommend this course! Roderic is a great TF and he teaches concepts in a very comprehensible manner. If you're deciding between this and 21, take 18!
This is a great math class. You not only learn how to do multivariable calculus, but how to apply it to the social sciences as well. For me, the most interesting aspect of this course was the lessons that explained the multivariable calculus behind important economic models and theorems.
