This course is a good introduction to reinforcement learning principles. Psets aren't that bad if you're strong with Stat 110 concepts (especially Adam's Law) and Python. Be prepared for some pretty nasty notation and a lot of time dedicated to the final project though.
A thorough introduction to reinforcement learning. Lectures could be a bit dry and pacing was off sometimes (a lot of time spent answering questions and going on tangents, which meant we didn't get through all the intended material). That being said, the teaching staff was super open to feedback –– they had an anonymous suggestions form at the end of each lecture and would share responses at the start of the next one –– which I really appreciated.
This is a good class to take if you want to learn about the various aspects of RL and enjoy doing Stat 110–style problems. While Stat 111 is certainly not required, some basic ideas from the class (what is an estimator, what is bias, what is Fisher information) may be helpful. It has a good balance of theory and application. The class is certainly not perfect yet but Lucas Janson is an excellent instructor. Would recommend.
184 was HARD. The midterm was probably the most difficult exam I've ever taken in my life. But the course content is extremely interesting, so I would still recommend students to take it, especially future iterations of this course.
Be prepared for a statistical theory class. Although this is listed as a CS / STAT class, think of it as a pure stat class. It's theory upon theory, which can be great if that's what you want. Homeworks and the final project offer a good practical sense, but I wish lectures had more balance. However, Sham and Lucas are amazing. Having both teach the class is a great combination. At the very least, you need to have completed Stat 110, but more background in ML would be helpful
CS 184 was a pretty good course! I especially liked the first half of the course and thought it was well–taught and well–structured. The workload is not super high, though I think this means it's easy to coast without learning that much in the second half of the course. This year we had a final project replace the final exam, which I personally did not like as much since there was not enough time to produce a project I would have liked, but this is probably just a personal preference.
If you like working through sometimes tedious proofs, this class is for you. Sham and Lucas are great lecturers but this class was too much at times and midterm was tough.
Effort required for the class does not feel to be reciprocated by professors
This class teaches you all about the theory behind reinforcement learning. Before this class, I always hesitated to get into the weeds with reinforcement learning algorithms, but now after this class I feel comfortable with the workings behind many of the popular algorithms and how they are motivated/work. You get exposed to lots of material, covering everything from MDPs to control, bandits to supervised learning, policy gradients to imitation learning, etc. In the psets, you get to work with gym simulations and implement these equations in practice, which feels good. The course gave me more confidence in my reinforcement learning foundations both in the wild and in theory, and made me appreciate the overall hardness of the field.

The course was very organized and well–structured, with clear themes and big ideas that were elucidated upon in a straightforward way. I appreciate that the instructor pauses for questions fairly often during the lecture and answer the questions well. It really helped us digest the material better. The recap at the start of the lecture also helped us recall concepts from the previous lecture, which is essential since the material almost always builds upon itself. The lectures slides are sufficiently dense that you can follow along, but it might be hard for, e.g., pre–reading or working on it yourself. The lectures moved a bit more slowly than I wouldve liked. The notes are dense with notation. Feedback on homework assignments was delayed quite a bit.
This course gives a very good overview of popular reinforcement learning algorithms. You get to cover the shiny exciting realm of deep RL, but also some classic algorithms that turn out to be surprisingly useful. Although this class does go over the statistical justifications behind most of the algorithms, I wouldn't let that scare you. The course does a very good job of giving intuitive explanations for each algorithm and why they might do as well as they do, and I found that understanding these explanations could carry me quite far. Problem sets are pretty chill since they're across multiple weeks, so as long as you don't leave them for the last minute they are very doable. They also give you a lot of freedom for your final project, and it turned out to be the least stressful part of my finals period.

Overall if you are interested in machine learning and want to see what reinforcement learning is about, I think this class does a great job of showing what is being used in practice today.
This class is OK. Midterm was a bit difficult, so would recommend taking some time to study for those. Start the final course project early and come up with a concrete idea when writing your proposal.
This class is relatively decent. RL is an interesting topic and the class gives a good overview of it, though it's a bit poorly organized because the class overviews a lot of math that doesn't necessarily get used. Understanding what's happening in the theory and understanding the material at the class level are totally different. The former is actually hard, the latter is not. I counted about 5 hours of work outside of lecture each week, and that's inflated from the final project, so maybe 4 hours a week? I would recommend probability at the level of Stat110 and mathematical/theoretical CS maturity at the same level required for CS124. These aren't hard requirements but if you find yourself unable to manipulate the math in pset0 then you're going to be in for a tough time, but it's not impossible.

Favorite CS/stat class I've taken here so far. Starts off quite basic, but by the end of the semester, I found myself thoroughly understanding all relevant aspects of RL. Some people don't appreciate how proofy the class materials are, but I think it does help build important foundational understanding, and if nothing else, you get better at taking expectations and recognizing inequalities through osmosis. If you are interested in machine learning at all, I'd highly recommend this class (potentially even over 181). Sham and Lucas are both heavyweights in their fields, and provide rigorous and clear lectures, and the teaching staff was pretty decent as well. Problem sets were the perfect balance between practical and theoretical, and I found office hours to be quite helpful.

The midterm is somewhat challenging but I would say very fair, as long as you study it is manageable. I enjoyed the final project.
