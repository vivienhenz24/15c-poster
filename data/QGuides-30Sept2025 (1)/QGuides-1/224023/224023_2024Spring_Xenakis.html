This is the first time that 114 is being offered, so logistically it was a bit of a mess but I suppose that is to be expected. James really cares about his students and their learning and I highly encourage you to engage with him if you choose to take this class. I think this class is well–conceived and really flexible in terms of your learning outcomes. The final project is basically a chance for you to work on whatever your'e interested in, so if you're doing any bioinformatics research this class is perfect for you. If Jay is still on the teaching team next year, he is a GOATed TF so for his sections and office hours to make your life a lot easier. I do think that James' lecturing style is not for everyone though and the slides were not very helpful to learn from and lectures were not recorded as well which was not ideal. The labs + chatgpt are probably your best bet at learning the material.
S24 was the first edition of this class, and it showed. professor was disorganized, though he was also extremely flexible which was nice.
This is a great course if you want to understand the statistical and computational components of the RNA–seq pipeline. There are so many different tools out there for different types of analyses (ATAC–seq, spatial transcriptomics, etc.) and this course really prepares you well to understand those analyses. Like now I am able to pick up computational methods and understand their analyses. You will also learn about bash/linux, and learn some bioinformatics tools. Overall, great course!
very hard class––it's doable, but difficult. James is a wonderful prof and very accommodating, but he kind of operates as if we have prior experience in the bioinformatics programs and topics he teaches about. granted, a handful of students DO have that experience from working in labs, but I found myself lost and confused a lot of times as someone from a purely statistical background. assignments are pretty long, but you have a lot of time to do them and sections/office hours are very helpful.
This course had amazing potential when I took it, and if James is returning to teach it, I recommend it! Being that this was the first year this course was offered, I found instruction to be a bit rocky sometimes; nonetheless, James is an amazing and kind professor who does a great job of accommodating students of all backgrounds. I went into this course having taken the only pre–req listed for this course (Stat 110) and I was concurrently taking Stat 111, and to be quite honest, I think I was underprepared: this course did demand a higher fluency with coding than I was expecting, and some Stat 139 knowledge would've been helpful (but James/Jay were still very understanding and didn't just leave me to struggle in that regard; both are great instructors and super helpful people, and I learned some Stat 139 stuff along the way). This course gives excellent exposure to the field of bioinformatics itself: the tools, software packages, and the statistical foundations and motivations underlying the types of analyses done not just in big–data life sciences research but in the life sciences more generally. It will expose you to very useful methods and tech you will likely encounter (and need) in grad school. This course is made for both statistics and life sciences concentrators, so if you feel like you're only strong in one and not the other, you aren't alone.
Take this class! Not too stressful, but definitely teaches applicable content that is widely used throughout modern bioinformatics.
This class is very chill, but the psets can creep up on you, and they're usually quite challenging and take a substantial amount of time. When psets are due, be prepared to dedicate a heavy chunk of time to it, anywhere from 8–15 hours. The lectures can be a bit boring at times, but section and office hours are super helpful. James is one of the sweetest professors I've met here, and he is extremely invested in your success. He emphasizes his availability to meet with you and answer questions, and he's super understanding about everyone's background. He's an incredibly kind person all–around. However, the course did feel disorganized at times, and the lecture content felt a bit scattered and not directly related to the psets. Overall, I would recommend for you to take this class if you want to learn the basics of bioinformatics in a low–stakes environment.
Because this is the first iteration of the class, it was quite disorganized: the lecture schedule changed, the assignment number changed, there were unexpected hurdles, etc. BUT James tried really really hard to circumvent that and help students learn as much as possible about bioinformatics. A lot of things went well and a lot of important concepts were taught well, some less so but the feedback about that was requested and well received so it is very likely that things will be better next year. James and Jay both worked very hard to help students and it was very clear how much they cared about the course. James is always around in his office meeting with students and helping. He's very accessible and open to feedback. He's also very competent, friendly, and approachable. I recommend as a bioinformatics course but honestly the more stat you took, the more you'll get out of this course. If you never took 111 or 139 you will have some catching up to do but it's definitely feasible.
James is a wonderful professor. Very laid back, very eager to help. Super accessible and fun to talk to! Very knowledgeable. I had a great time in this class even if the homeworks were a little long and a bit difficult.
