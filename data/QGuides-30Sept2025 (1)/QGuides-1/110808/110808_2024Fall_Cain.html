If you're reading this, you're choosing between Math 22, 25, and 55. I shopped 22 and 55 in the first week, because I was always told that '25 is 55 without the clout, at that point may as well go to 22.' Welp, here I am months later, and I'm so glad I ended up choosing Math 25. I came in with a bit of proof and zero linear/multivar background.

People shit on 25 because there's so little people taking the course and it seems like 55's less popular younger brother, but trust me, it is bloody intense. Wes is a great lecturer but also has no mercy when it comes to deadline extensions, pset lengths, punctuality etc. But that helped us grow as people and as mathematicians. We also did Classroom to Table with him, and he's a legend.

That said, some of my best friends made this year were from this class. The intimacy of the class led to amazing bonds, and I would take it again in a heartbeat. 25 has some of the most humble, hard–working, smart, and genuinely interesting people you'll meet at Harvard. Hours in Lamont and Jefferson, even extending through to the morning – this was definitely a defining experience in freshman year.

Start your psets on Thursday. Read the textbook (Axler) before and after class. Go to every OH. Expect to 'lose out' socially because you can't go to parties. Screw that, psetting is genuinely way more fun anyways.

In the words of Wes, 'You can write for the Crimson if you'd like, but just know that there will never be an extension on any of your psets... Math is now your extracurricular."

Bite the bullet, do some interesting stuff this semester. Take this class, o mathematically minded ones. "Perhaps, one day even this will be pleasing to look back at." – Virgil. And it was.
Great course. Wes is my favorite professor so far.

It is hard but very rewarding. You learn Linear Algebra in a way that feels very complete, and when you formally prove every theorem you surprisingly also get an intuition for everything. So although the course is difficult, it is worth the effort. Here are some more arguments:
– Wes is a very serious and inspiring professor and everyone loves him;
– The class size is small, so you can ask a lot of questions and make friends;
– Each homework feels like a challenge and it is very fun finally getting that one problem right;
– It is a theoretical course, but one of the weeks is fully dedicated to solving and studying differential equations that you could encounter in physics or chemistry for example. As a CS concentrator, the applications of the concepts we studied felt very natural.

Warning: the course also takes everything very seriously, so there is no curve, the exams are difficult, and there are no extensions for homework. However, the fact that people still enjoy it so much should then say even more about its high quality.

Math 25 is somewhat infamous amongst freshman here for being "about as hard as Math 55, just without the clout". Honestly, that just about sums up the course. Be prepared to work very hard, since while you spend less time on the class than 55, you need to actually know what you're doing instead of having an overview idea like you could in 55.

Before enrolling, look closely at the syllabus. Wes has a very particular style in his pedagogy, and each aspect of the course is designated in a way to ensure you understand the content and can rigorously expound upon it. Unless you have extensive background on linear algebra, i.e. you know what Jordan normal form or something of that level of math is, you will need at least 15 hours each week outside class to process the material and do the psets. Computational linear algebra will help for the first 2/3 of the class.

Something particular about 25 is that almost all psets are done in Latex. If you haven't used it to type up your solutions, start immediately. This will save you hours of writing your solutions. Eventually, many people in the class started to type directly into Latex and started to "think in Latex". Don't stress too much, though – problem sets are graded fairly generously, and that's because the exams are not.

With exams, when Wes says there is no curve, there is no curve. You will need to have a deep understanding of what's going on to do well on exams, and practicing Axler questions for the midterm/final is useful. The slightest misstep in your thinking and you will be punished accordingly. In a way, though, this is a good thing that forces you to be hard on yourself and is great preparation for the rest of your math career here at Harvard.

Math 25 is a special experience.

Lectures are dense, fast–paced, and excellently organized. Wes is an extremely accessible professor and excels at establishing an intuitive understanding of the material. The community aspect is incredible (only 22 students after dropping ended!) and the intense workload means that everyone bonds over psetting and office hours. Without a doubt, Math 25 is the best class I took this semester. Everything about this course is optimized to ensuring the students learn and become better mathematicians, and I have grown tremendously from going through this course.

I think Professor Cain (Wes) is one of the biggest draws for 25. He's strict in an incredibly caring way, and his lectures are incredible. The class is small enough where everyone feels comfortable asking questions, and Wes basically always has an answer.

The community is also fantastic––partially due to bonding over psets––but we got to know each other quite well by the end of the year. The CAs and our TF were both extremely helpful, and I never felt any pset or test were unreasonable.

The one part that I would caution about is the grading. Wes is quite strict with pset submissions, and there are no extensions or drops. In addition, tests are in–person (compared to 55's take–home tests) and are uncurved. The partial credit is quite generous though, and with a high enough pset grade it's possible to get an A. I wouldn't let the grading policy stop you from taking this class, and I found that the benefits absolutely worth the drawbacks. However, if you're extremely concerned about grades, a different math class might be better

For context, I came in with Calc BC and minimal exposure to multi/diff eq, as well as a decent competition math background (AIME). However, I had never written a proof in my life, and LaTeX was a foreign language. I don't think anything past AB is necessary to this class, but it would definitely help to have written proofs before.

If you're debating between 22, 25, and 55, I think 25 was a fantastic choice for me. While 55 might be a better choice for anyone interested in pure math, I think that 25 is great for understanding linear algebra while also having some real–world applications. While this class isn't for everyone, I can't praise my experience with 25 enough and would highly recommend it if you are willing to put the time into it.

Fantastic course with a fantastic professor! Wes is a very enthusiastic (after all, he ends his emails with Enthusiastically, Wes xD) and very caring professor. He might seem harsh or strict, but this is for your own benefit to keep you locked in and on track. In reality, deep inside of him, he really cares for each and every one of his students. He is also an incredible lecturer who is able to effectively convey difficult mathematical concepts in lecture and always stops to answer questions from students. As for the course material, its pretty standard bread–and–butter proof–based linear algebra, but still very beautiful and very satisfying. The PSETs and exams were all challenging but very reasonable, and with a bit of effort, you can get an A in the course even without prior proof–writing or linear algebra experience, so definitely try it out!

A few words about 25 vs. 55: I started out in 55a and then dropped to 25a because 55a was going to fast for me. I had no prior experience with proof–writing (I knew, in theory, how proofs are written but had never actually taken a formal course that involved proof–writing), and as a result 55a was my first foray into abstract math, which turned out to be too big of a leap. In hindsight, I think I could have stayed in 55a and survived, but I am not sure if I would have learned as much as I did in 25a. My advice for you would be to shop both classes and then decide. But please keep in mind that 1) if you havent seen the material before and/or you have zero experience in proof–writing, youll probably learn more in 25a than in 55a; and 2) contrary to what many veterans of 55a might tell you, 55a IS NOT easier than 25a. Out of personal experience, I can tell you that my first 55a PSET took me 40 hours, whereas my 25a PSETs took on average 7–10 hours. The take–home exam format of 55a, while very seductive, actually leads to a much higher level of difficulty of the problems. A lot of my 55 friends had a torturous experience with the final, as many QReports for 55 will probably say. Bottom line: dont be lured into thinking that 55 is easier than 25 just because the grading is more lenient and the exams are take–home.
