Without hesitation, this was one of the best courses I've taken at Harvard. Dr. Pia Raffler is a phenomenal lecturer who consistently finds unique ways to keep students engaged and contributing to the course content. The class is conducted in a "seminar" style, which allowed for the opportunity to share my own personal insight while simultaneously immersing myself in the knowledge and perspective of others. Sections with Shelby were fantastic and went beyond the surface level of core concepts discussed in class. Overall, this course completely changed my perception of the concept of "government" and what it means, and inspired me to continue to pursue development–related opportunities.
A wonderful class, even for those not interested in African or development politics. Prof Raffler and Shelby are wonderful people, and they made the learning fun.
This is a wonderful course. You will learn about how Africa's history (slave trade, colonialism) has shaped its modern politics, how various structural factors (ethnicity, resource wealth) shape incentives to help or hinder development, the role of the West, and more. If you are interested in the prospects of developing countries generally, you have much to learn from Prof. Raffler, who is very passionate and very approachable. I now have an informed opinion on pathways to African development, and am more interested in careers in development. There are few assignments, all of which are fun and intuitive (although the exam questions were hard due to their breadth). Lecture slides have all you need to know for the exam (along with the readings, of course). You will succeed if you pay attention. Definitely take this one!
Take this class!!! Professor Raffler is incredible. She is an expert in her field and facilitates a lot of great discussions during class. This is one of the most well organized lecture courses Ive taken at Harvard. The focus of the class is on retention rather than memorization and it shows – the readings and assignments are very interesting and you will learn so much about development in Africa. If you want to know more about Africa (which you should!) then take this class.
A genuine interest in development in Africa (both from a historical and contemporary view) is needed to succeed and gain anything from this class. The ability to understand research designs/extract key information from sometimes dense research studies is also needed. Participate, and do not be afraid of asking questions. Stay on top of readings.
Professor Raffler is amazing. She not only has real experience on the African continent, but she has the unique perspective of actually doing research on the continent to help improve governance. She is clearly well–informed about Africa, and the pre–class readings she assigns are enlightening and critical to learning in the course. It's not a Gem–like time commitment but it's not huge either. It's also a generally forgiving course in my opinion. There were times I didn't do the pre–class readings and it was fine, I just read them later. Would def recommend to take this course if you're interested at all about the current condition of Africa.
One of the best classes I have taken at Harvard. Not only is the course material very interesting, the course itself is very well taught and run. I really enjoyed the participatory nature of the course and the ability to get to know/work with other students. Professor Raffler and Shelby are also deeply committed to supporting their students after the class, and I deeply admire that!
I think it you already have a genuine interest in the continent, or maybe you are African and you have family back home there is nothing in this course that you will pick up that you haven't already picked up in life. A lot of this feels like common sense, and I think you will find that you've heard a lot of these things before. You may not learn new content, but you will interact with many academic papers to validate the things you may have picked up before. Whether this is or is not extremely important to you may inform your decision to take this course––it's up to you. I don't think this is an extraordinarily bad class. I don't even think this is a bad class at all. But I do think that is indicative of a larger trend that I see in a lot of GOV department classes and have heard when talking to other concentrators––tedious classes where you may not feel like you're learning anything, a dearth of listings in African politics, and a section that doesn't challenge or provoke thought. This wasn't a *bad* class. But it wasn't particularly good either. And you're not going to feel like you came to Harvard to take classes like this one.
Professor Raffler does an amazing job summarizing the various facets to development in Africa, starting with historical context in the trans–Atlantic slave trade and colonialism and ending with the various barriers to governance in the relationships between voters, politicians, and bureaucrats.
This class is a great introduction to development politics and has a great professor.
Pretty good class– professor raffler is very kind and enthusiastic. *
An actual transformative course. The readings are fascinating, the projects/tests difficult yet manageable, and discussions so so so compelling. Professor Raffler is a fantastic lecturer, who always makes sure there slots of time for discussion, yet also covers material at a perfect pace. She understands that theres a lot of material in the course and that it moved at a fast pace, so there are recaps at the beginning of class and in sections that are so, so helpful. You choose a focus country that you follow and analyze for the rest of the semester, which was an amazing way to learn about course topics but also to engage with present day Africa. I am so grateful to have taken this class, it has informed my long term career interests more than any other class Ive taken.

A great class to take if you are interested in the subject material, a lot of the students who take it are personally interested and that leads to a more involved classroom environment. Relatively low work commitment. Would highly recommend!
