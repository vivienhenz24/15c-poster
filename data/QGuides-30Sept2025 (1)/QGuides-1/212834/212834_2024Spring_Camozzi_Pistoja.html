If I were to summarize this class with one phrase, I'd probably go with dumpster fire. The positive part (before the fire starts) is that assignments aren't really bad and you don't really have to do the readings. The lectures were kind of boring, but Ambrogio is really passionate about teaching satire. The problem was, though, the syllabus was constantly changing and we never really knew what we were supposed to do, so if things didn't get posted on Canvas, even if they'd been mentioned in lecture like two days before, we just assumed we weren't supposed to do them. The two quizzes we were supposed to have got turned into one right before spring break, which got turned into the week after spring break, which then got pushed off so much we took it two weeks before the semester ended. This caused the grading scheme to change to 60% participation. At one point we had to wonder if the class was a satire itself. I don't think it was.
It will always be unexpected – sometimes very good sometimes mildly amusing sometimes a little boring
The teaching is staff is very nice. You'll get out of lectures what you put into them. Try to engage with the material! But be prepared to potentially not get feedback on assignments, or have due dates be changed frequently.
This course is solid in terms of content and workload overall, but you have to be prepared for some disorganization. Our quiz was continuously postponed for over a month and the assignments were due on Saturdays at 9pm, which was not ideal and was only done in order to give our TFs enough time to provide feedback before the following Tuesday lecturewe didnt receive feedback for a single assignment throughout this course. Section was also totally unnecessary, we rarely did anything productive and both Prof Pistoja and my TF often ended up showing late/not at all to class. It often felt like I was wasting my time waiting for things to happen, but when we did get to the material it was usually pretty interesting stuff. I personally would still prefer it over a super work–heavy gened but pick your poison I guess.

This course was disorganized and poorly run. I would highly caution anyone from taking it in future semesters, unless you have a STRONG interest in the theory of satire/violence.
1. The professor is very passionate about satire, and his enthusiasm comes through in the lectures – but unfortunately, his lectures are quite disorganized and often go on long tangents that leave students very confused. I know several friends who have taken this class, and we all agreed that it was very difficult to take away a single coherent concept from most lectures.
2. The course has 2 real graded components: a midterm quiz and a final project. The midterm quiz was originally scheduled for week 6 or 7, and because the professor did not have a quiz or study materials ready in time, it was continued being pushed back last minute each week until around week 10 or 11. The resulting quiz was EXTREMELY difficult compared to the limited content that we actually learned in lecture.
3. The final project was also pretty fake. The professor allowed some students to opt out of completing a final project and instead help him run an in–class event. Many of these students were required to do VERY little work for this event, and still received a free perfect grade on their final project while the rest of the class was forced to put in many hours of work. The entire situation showed poor planning from the teaching staff.
I've never had a poor experience in a Harvard class before, but this class was truly unfortunate. I genuinely enjoyed getting to know the professor and the TFsthey're lovely peoplebut the class itself was not well run.
This is a good gened honestly. The workload is light, but if you pay attention and do the readings, then it is pretty interesting stuff. There are very subtle differences in terms like irony, parody, caricature, etc. and this class leads you through how to think about these differences, what society is doing, and what problems might come up. It is a little disorganized, but that was fine honestly. You just check the homework like the day before it's due and do it for completion.
I think this course has a lot of potential, but unfortunately, it was quite disorganized. Our quiz (20% of our grade) date was moved back four times due to delays in posting a practice quiz and other study materials. Further, the Canvas was often not up to date, which made it difficult to keep up with the weekly readings.

Nevertheless, I think the course was quite interesting. Professor Camozzi–Pistoja is an excellent lecturer, and his enthusiasm for the subject is contagious. The class and section discussions are very interesting, and I don't feel like you need a ton of background in literature and classics to understand lecture and be able to participate in discussions. However, I do think the readings were at time a bit inaccessible (although the professor did provide "reading guides" in order to aid our understanding). The course difficulty and workload was not that bad overall: there are weekly assignments, weekly readings, one quiz, and a final project/paper, which is about average for a non–gem gened. Overall, I would say that if the topic really interests you, and you are willing to put in work and put up with a little bit of disorganization, then go for it. However, if you're looking for a gem, this is definitely not the class for you.
