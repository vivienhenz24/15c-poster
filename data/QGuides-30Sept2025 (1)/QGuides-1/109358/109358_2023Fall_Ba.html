Extremely interesting and engaging, and the topics are relevant and applicable. Professor Ba is an amazing professor––very thoughtful and knowledgable, takes all questions seriously even when they go beyond the scope of the class, and genuinely cares about his students and the course material. Take advantage of his office hours, not necessarily to go over psets and other assignments but rather just to have conversations with him about the material and your final project, because he knows so much about the topics and is sincerely very interested. This course teaches a lot of useful data analysis skills and provides great background into timely issues such as AI and neural networks. However, it can feel dense and confusing, especially if you don't have much background with probability and statistics (as I did not). Try not to get too bogged down in the details during lectures; it's more important to pay attention and absorb the high level stuff, and you will get a lot out of this class.
This class is very math–heavy. As soon as Professor Ba started using linear algebra and matrix operations everything went downhill. But, you get used to it somehow. The concepts he introduced to us were important and heavily used in Data Science and Machine Learning. If you are BioE/BME and are coming from taking BE110, know that this is not that, and I think it is much more well taught and organized version of systems.
Demba is a great professor who explains complex topics very well in different ways. I especially liked his visual representations of processes, such as PCA and LDA. However, the take–home exams can be very tough and time consuming and some of the problem sets were difficult. Overall though, it was a good course.
I had the impression that this class was STAT 111 but with applications in biology. You need strong Statistics and Probability background to do well.. Even though Slack is the main tool for communication, don't expect to get a response to your question in the general chat. The class didn't foster an environment of learning, openness and growth so one teaching fellow had to answer all the private messages on Slack who was helpful in times of stress. There are not many office hours and you can't ask questions during the lecture because this apparently breaks the flow of the lecture and then by the end, you will be left confused and feel discouraged from asking further questions. In addition, lectures notes were often out of order. I also had the impression that Demba is only welcoming to his favorite students which made him come off as selective in student interactions. Though, one positive aspect of this class that I'd point out is exams are graded very fast, typically you will hear back about your grade the next day. Overall, I wouldn't recommend taking this course to my fellow peers. If you want to learn how to analyze signals, take 6.3000 over at MIT instead.
If you are interested/ have experience with signals and systems, this is a great class to build on from those skills and explore your interests. You are allowed with a great deal of flexibility to work on a project related to the class content, which can be a great way to get outside of your comfort zone and apply the content of signals and systems. Additionally, if you are interested, this class connects very nicely with ML and AI, and I feel I was able to learn about ML and incorporate it into the assignments.
Definitely take this course! Very helpful and overall just a cool opportunity
Main pros: Demba cares a lot about his students, focuses on student learning, and is passionate about AI ethics and AIxsociety. He's a great listener too, and is always open to feedback. He genuinely seems to try to incorporate the feedback he's given. He's a very personable, passionate, and principled professor. His class structure is flexible, so you will be treated like an adult. You will feel free to explore, be curious, and prioritize learning over external expectations.

Advice: Take statistics before you take this course. Previous Q guides say Demba teaches all the math you need to do well in this course –– while this is technically true, it's still hard to understand Demba unless you have a background in the concepts. Within the first week I found myself at a disadvantage because I did not know complex analysis, and my classmate expressed that she was glad she had taken complex analysis before, otherwise it would've been difficult to understand the lectures and pset. By the middle of the course, I found myself saying the same thing about statistics –– I think on the course catalog it says that only multivar and linalg is needed –– I was grateful I had taken a year of statistics before this course, as it is very heavy on linear algebra and statistics (multivariable not so much so don't worry if you don't have much of a multivar background; Demba himself believes that calculus will be dead soon). Set aside lots of time to self–study if you are not confident in linalg or stats. In general, this course is HIGHLY unstructured: assignments are pushed back, grading is delayed often, and also it feels like the assignments are constructed last–minute, so while they are not time–consuming, they feel like they are not thoughtfully made. Expectations for final project are unclear. Also, although the course has a final project, this is not a project–centered course, so don't go into it expecting to be able to focus on a cool final project. Finally, Demba's quite hard to hear as an instructor. Sit close to the whiteboard at lecture.

Great Class! I would definitely recommend this if you're curious about biological signals and want to get more of a computational understanding about how many signals are processed.
Definitely make sure you have taken stats. I did not have stats experience beforehand and I was lost throughout the semester and it was difficult towards the end.
Professor Demba might seem slightly hard to read, but he's a phenomenal human being, go to his office hours!
