If you read Kyla's notes in their entirety, but don't do much else (including understanding everything discussed in class), you will learn a lot and be fine.
This is a very challenging class! The 24 hour exam took me 19 straight hours to do. But it's also very rewarding and a very well–resourced course for a class of its size. Kosuke and the teaching staff are all great and patient.

I took it as a undergrad with STAT 110 and STAT 111 previously and 139 concurrently (as well as lots of R experience) and was fine for the GOV side of the course, though experience with more advanced regression implementation would certainly be useful. The distinction between the GOV and STAT side can sometimes be blurry, but in general the GOV side is more focused on empirical implementation while STAT focuses on analytical derivations.

286/2003 covers a lot of ground. Regardless whether you're STAT or GOV, the course tries to teach both the theoretical underpinnings of various inference methods while teaching you how to implement them, which... is a lot! But it's generally pretty successful in this endeavour.

I'd recommend doing all the PSETs and be sure to note that the second half of the course gets much more difficult and has far more content.

Also just an aside but the STAT TF Kyla Chasalow is easily the best TF I've ever had. She made the material so clear in her section, even when it could get a bit muddled in lecture. Also she wrote a 130–page note document for the class, effectively serving as a course textbook.
Stat 286 offers a very comprehensive overview of the causal inference literature. Kosuke covers a lot of material, as we have two video lectures each week plus in person lectures which usually go over new material as well. The midterm and final are long (12+ hours each) but are instructive. Section was great, and the resources provided by the course staff are plenty and helpful. While I am personally not a huge fan of lots of math on slides, Kosuke took time to go in depth where necessary. Outside of class, Kosuke is extremely responsive and accessible, and it is great to get to know such a renowned professor on a more personal level, so do make use of this chance!
This class is very difficult, especially in its current form where it's taught simultaneously with Stat 286. As a Gov student, you should probably set the expectation that about a third of the material is designed for Stats students to understand and Gov students will probably be lost. Also, it requires at least two courses' worth of time, so keep that in mind if you plan to take it.
This course is an excellent and rigorous introduction to modern causal inference techniques. The assignments and exams are well–written, useful, and fair.
The structure of the course is very weird. I really don't like 1. the flipped classroom structure and 2. the attempt at teaching a Gov and a Stats class at once. Kosuke is very helpful when you meet one–on–one, and the TFs were great, but overall I don't feel like I learned a lot.
Excellent overview of causal inference, highly recommend this course.
I think the problem sets and review questions are good preparation for the exams, so it is good to keep up with those as much as possible, which means not falling behind with the material if possible.
The course may take up a lot of time (I spent more than 15 hours a week on the course) so be prepared to assign enough time for this course.
This is a challenging, but rewarding course. The problem sets are worth a very small percentage of the grade, which should be changed moving forward, because they are all very useful practice. The exams are challenging but fair. Be sure to have significant R coding, probability, regression, and statistical inference experience prior to enrolling this course (i.e., GOV 2002, GOV 50/51, STAT 110/111).
I thought that Stat 286 was an interesting class, though there was definitely room for improvement (in my opinion) with regard to the course structure. I thought that pre–lecture videos were engaging and well–made, though I sometimes wasn't super sure on what content I needed to know. Problem sets are really useful for cementing your understanding of the material, but some of them can take quite a while and you're allowed to drop a few, so it doesn't make sense to do all of them, even though doing all of them is probably the best way to understand the content; I wish that all of the problem sets were required but that they were a bit shorter.

I felt that exams are more time–consuming than they were difficult. At a certain point, it felt like they tested endurance, rather than understanding of the course content.
This course is very challenging and you have to invest a lot of time. But your efforts to understand the materials are proportionate to the feeling of acheivement. The course is very demanding and if you have 0 background in statistics, this course will be very difficult.
This class is most useful course if you want to do research with causal inference. There is no option not to take.
This course is challenging, and yet, extremely rewarding. It is simply the most helpful, AND enjoyable course I have ever taken in graduate school. The course instructors are responsive and helpful, and the students in the class are all brilliant and friendly (you get to form study groups and work with one another on course materials). The course content is very practical and provides a quick introduction to causal inference without sacrificing mathematical/theoretical rigour. Students interested in either substantive (in social sciences) or methodological research of causal inference are highly recommended to take the course. The exams are challenging and yet a good motivation to study. But students should take the class with a solid understanding of probability theory (Stat110 level) and statistical inference, intermediate econometrics or linear models. Regression theory is extremely important for the course, otherwise students might struggle or have to spend larger time to study in the latter half of the course.

Thank you Kosuke for the amazing class!!
