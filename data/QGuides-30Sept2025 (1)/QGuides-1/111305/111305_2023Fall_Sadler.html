This class is a great introduction to astronomy, little preparation needed, great as an elective. Professor and TF are both extremely helpful if you take the initiative to approach them and ask them questions. Three–hour lab at night might throw some people off but it's mostly really fun and useful. You get to practice a lot of hands–on things like plotting, using sextants, etc. Mid–semester p–sets get much more time–consuming: expect maybe four to six hours but the beginning and end of the semester are pretty light. Highly recommend the class as long as you're willing to put in the work and learn. Everything's cumulative so if you fall behind on material, the midterm and final aren't going to be fun. Also a tip: get good data in your journal early before things pile up. You really only need two or three weeks' worth of good data, predictions, and models to get a high score on it.

*This class was, overall, very enjoyable and the most science–y science class that I have ever had that combined simply learning about the world with meaningful testing, labs, journaling, measurement, and such. I feel that I have gained a very tangible knowledge about how the sky works and the Earth's position among the celestial objects surrounding it. Phil and Max were both so engaging and made the class fun, fostering a culture that made this, as a very much non–STEM person, my favorite course this semester. There are some issues that I feel need to be worked out with the way that feedback is given and with the problem sets and exams. Simply put, there's a good bit of contradiction at times in two ways. First, the course head and TF sometimes gave contradictory advice or feedback on our assignments or general questions, and we were often confused as to how something ACTUALLY worked or what was ACTUALLY going on. Second, and what was more frustrating, was the sometimes inconsistent, unclear, and inaccurate information for the questions on our assignments and exams. Sometimes, we would have two different numbers for the same position, or a question would be worded so oddly that we would have to guess whether it meant one thing or another. Rarely, when someone asked, the answer would not even be very satisfying or would itself be contradictory or unclear. In spite of these issues, though, I recommend that anybody take this course. While this course is lauded as an easy way to get your QRD out of the way, I'll warn that this is not the case. It's easy math, sure, but it's difficult in terms of map–drawing and celestial–reduction–taking, two things which you likely have little experience with. We've all taken a math class before––there exists a frame of reference for that material which does not exist for, say, cartography in most cases. I found this class empowering in its hands–on nature, but often discouraging, too. It's really a remarkable time commitment.
This class is a classic choice to fulfill QRD –– I would say that the course is by no means less work than other math classes, but is much more engaging than other math classes. The work I was doing for the class was fun, interesting, and unique. The course was one of the most hands–on courses I have ever taken at Harvard. I loved the ability to learn by doing –– whether that be by taking the T up past MIT to learn about acceleration, going whale watching, or going up to the roof of the science center. This class also required me to work with partners in ways that I previously did not (coming from a humanities background) which I loved. Celestial Navigation is a course worth taking.
