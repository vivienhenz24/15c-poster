Meritocracy is overall a great GenEd. The course is definitely not as gemmy as before (grading on the essays is a lot harsher than you'd think, there is a final exam, etc), but the course does a great job at reducing busy work and make sure you are learning both the concepts of the course and more importantly, your ability to develop normative arguments. The two–hour lectures and not being able to use a device during lecture in Sever is rough, but the debates between Sandel and the absolutely superstar panel of guest lecturers make this one of the courses you really can only take at Harvard, and I think every undergrad should try and take it one time during their time here. As Harvard students, the topics are extremely applicable, and the manner in which the course progresses fundamentally changes the way you view the world. Also, the course really focuses on developing your ability to form logical normative arguments, and this is a skill that I think is the greatest takeaway of this course. This means that tbh, the essays are pretty harshly graded, but the feedback is really great for Essay 2, and the fact that participation is such a large portion of your grade is built so that they can be more honest with your writing. Brian, the head TF, was really helpful in breaking this down and I highly recommend him if you can because he is great at distilling all of the concepts in an understandable way that contextualizes them within the broader message of the course.

That being said, I do have some caveats. The course can be relatively preachy at time and be very dismissive of opinions that might disagree with Sandel. Especially around the first essay, you might have an instinct to disagree with some of Sandel's arguments, but not have the right framework to properly argue your viewpoint or make a great argument, thus making you feel as if you are elitist and/or feel pressured to write an argument that agrees with Sandel to avoid a bad grade, but by the end of the course, you get exposed to enough materials so that you can take either side comfortably. MERITOCRACY IS NOT AN EASY A. I took this class freshman fall, hoping that it would fulfill a GenEd requirement, introduce me to philosophy/government (as someone with no background in either,) and challenge me well as I adapted to my freshman year. I was not particularly looking for an easy class, but, based on the previous reports, thought that this course would be on the lighter side. That was completely wrong. Not only is this class decidedly not a 'gem,' it feels as though it was made much more difficult than necessary. For example, our final was an oral exam over 21 required readings; I spent more time studying for it than on any of my other classes (Expos and Concentration reqs.) It felt a little overboard, especially for my first semester of college.

On the content: Sandel is an okay lecturer with no real ideas or solutions to addressing the issues he finds with meritocracy. Guest speakers are really cool and engaging and the topic itself is definitely interesting to look at, but at times can be repetitive.

I thought meritocracy was a good class, but much of it did not live up to my expectations. I'm not big on writing paragraphs, so here is a bulleted list:
* Lectures are early and long. Sandel is very intelligent but speaks slowly, making lectures seem even longer. At the end, you have to write a journal post, but mine were sometimes just gibberish because I was about to fall asleep.
* The guests Sandel brings in are AWESOME. Chief Justice of the Indian Supreme Court, to name one exceptionl one. Definitely a cool experience.
* The papers are a little bit harshly graded and there is not a whole lot of guidance and standarization between what each TF is looking for
* The readings are informative, but long. You could totally get an A without doing any of the readings, as long as you have a friend with an extensive study guide. I would do them, though. Especially the first few weeks with Hayek, Rawls, and Mankiw. Sandel's book is also pretty good (and very readable) but he repeats the same general points a lot. I listened to it on audiobook on 3x speed while playing Minecraft and caught most of it.
