Hi guys! Wanted to give an honest answer to this question.
Overall: this course was not too tough at all and strikes a great balance between handling material and extrapolating on the concepts.

Prereqs:
– STAT 110 is useful for understanding random variables
– EC 10A or something similar is useful for general supply/demand knowledge
– Coding experience is useful but not required. You'll learn STATA or R. No need to know these beforehand as they give examples and allow you to use online sources/section help.

Topics Covered:
[important foundational knowledge]
– statistics and probability introduction
– t–tests, p values, F–tests, J–tests, QLR + Chow tests, and more
[data types]
– cross–sectional data, regression, and inference
– panel data, regression, and inference
– time series data, regression, and inference
[variable types]
– instrumental variables
– control variables
– binary variables, both dependent and dependent

Exams:
– one midterm and one final
– generally a split between MC and open–response.
– they provide tons of practice MC per topic and about 1–2 practice exams
– if you review the PSETS, do the MC, and the practice final, you'll do totally fine

PSETS:
– generally 3–5 questions in length
– you can go to office hours to get help
– each is given about a week to do, and you don't feel rushed
– my year, you could drop one of the lowest PSET grades

I would highly recommend coming in–person to learn as I loved the atmosphere there and just being able to absorb so much information. You got this, and you'll love it! For CS–majors, this is EXTREMELY useful for understanding machine learning regression algorithms on a deeper level.
There is the option to do the course in R, but most of the help available is in Stata, so if that is going to be the case, people only comfortable with R should be prepared to switch to Stata.

Pros:
This course is a solid introduction to econometric models, with a good selection of models that are useful in both economics research and industry.

Cons:
In one word, this course was confusing. There were so many different – and unfortunately conflicting – places to get information. Often, the lecture notes, lecture slides, section and the book told us different ways to do various tasks, which were often different. There was no clear indication as to what the "official" way to run a certain model was, since one thing was said in class but the problem sets were based on the book. The addition of both section notes and slides (which we didin't use) was very confusing as well. Additionally, notation was quite confusing as well, and it wasn't really clear what various letters/symbols meant, where often the same letter would be used in completely different contexts, and it was never clear why that was, or if there was any connection. Finally, the psets were also all very confusing, where the "correct" answer to the (generally) extremely vague questions could only be found through specific consultation in office hours or on the mismanaged ed forum, where a TF would often respond after a few hours with one very specific subclause from the book's appendix that we were never officially supposed to read and also didn't discuss in class, but were somehow supposed to have known somehow. Overall, this class could be improved with a complete overhaul of the teaching material to be more consistent and more effort put into the psets to have clearly formulated questions about material that the class actually covered.
