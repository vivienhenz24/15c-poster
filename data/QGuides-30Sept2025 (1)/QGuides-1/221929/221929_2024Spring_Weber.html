The course is well–organized with lectures that give a high level overview of the topic and only one problem set per module (3 total), in addition to the group paper presentation and a final project. The small number of assignments made the courseload very manageable, though some psets were overwhelming and seemed very far removed from the content covered in lecture/far too theoretical. The lectures can also feel disorienting unless you've read the background materials, as Melanie skips over a lot of math and just states it as fact without going too deep into intuitive understanding of the concepts. Overall, I think this was a very manageable and rewarding way to satisfy the AM requirement for CSE Master's, as I've learned useful concepts about graph neural networks and got to work on an interesting passionate project, though I've suffered through some obscure theoretical psets that I felt like taught me nothing useful.
Melanie is great and the fusion of differential geometry with AI/ML is super cool. Would likely recommend some background though to get the most out of the lectures.
Start the homework assignments earlier than you think is necessary and go to office hours
This class teaches you all about manifold learning, graph neural networks and exploiting symmetries, and manifold optimization. Youll gain valuable geometric intuition about manifolds and how to think about notions such as curvature and tangent spaces. The course gave me more confidence in geometric machine learning, and is overall a good course to take. The course was accessible, and the psets were strongly related to course material but forced you to engage in a deeper manner, which was helpful in giving you experience and practice with the course material without feeling too lost. The materials of the class are well–organized, with slides, psets, and project expectations clearly delineated at the start of the semester. The class gives you both the theoretical and application oriented knowledge about geometric machine learning, and overall presents clear motivations and generates enthusiasm for the course content.
The class provides a good overview about various aspects of geometric deep learning, from dimensionality reduction techniques, to graph embedding techniques and manifold learning methods. The workload is manageable, with an emphasis on the class project where one can apply the techniques learned in this class to a topic of one's interest. Would highly recommend this class if one's interests align.
Take this course! You will get a pretty broad + thorough survey of many topics in the exciting new field of geometric machine learning. Prof. Weber is great!
To truly absorb and understand the material, I'd recommend studying with great consistency. Thoroughly reading through the recommended papers is an essential part of the learning process.
Go to the lectures and start the problem sets early. Definitely go to office hours for the homework.
I was super excited for this course, and while it fell short in some aspects, it was overall engaging and thought–provoking. The problem set questions were sometimes not the most instructive, and since this is somewhat a survey course, there were times when I think lectures without further reading / discussion didn't provide as much new insight as other lectures that could stand alone. That being said, the teaching staff was extremely attentive and helpful, and the course project is a great experience to apply some of the ideas from the course to a larger scale project related to your personal interests!
I was really excited to take this class because of its glowing reviews last year, but it wasn't quite as interesting as I had hoped for. It still is a solid class that teaches some unique ML material, but be aware that it is theory–oriented and most of the material is not very applicable outside ML/data science theory. Be prepared for the homework to be either quite easy or quite hard. Prof. Weber was nice and helpful with the course project. I would recommend much more math background than linear algebra to get something out of the classperhaps math 25a or math 122 (abstract algebra).
If I had to go back, I probably wouldn't take this class. I think this an issue with grad–level researchy classes in general, but the theory isn't presented in a way that is easy to 'grok' or really understand well. Melanie is a poor lecturer imo (hesitant to answer questions, very monotone and struggles to generate enthusiasm for the subject), and I could have gotten >99% out of this class by looking thru the lecture notes on my own accord. The last third of the class is also virtually inaccessible/useless without a decent differential geometry background. I'm a cs–math joint with pretty strong mathematical maturity and I didn't find myself walking away with any amazing insights about manifold learning/optimization.

I will say, the final poster presentation and the student–led paper presentations were a good way to build community. I met some cool grad students and enjoyed seeing other ppl's projects.
