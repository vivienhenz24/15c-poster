Great class! Yes it is quite a time commitment, but you will genuinely learn a lot. Important note: get with a good team for the main robot final project because that basically decides your grade.
This class can be confusing because it tries to cover a lot of topics in very little depth. I would not recommend taking this class if your aim is to grasp general mechanical engineering topics. However, I would recommend this class if you would like to build your CAD skills and learn to use the tools in the SEC machine shop. Try to solve the problem sets using only what you remember from class, and anything you don't remember add to your cheat sheet. Build your cheat sheet throughout the course of the semester and use it when doing problem sets in order to prepare for exams. This class is about understanding a lot of topics at a very basic level, so the cheat sheet is very important. Put key terms and equations on there that you might remember now but not later. Practice good CAD techniques when building your robot and when doing your psets. The better you are at CAD, the better! Also, keep your robot design simple and communicate often with your team to ensure you have a working product by competition time.
This class is great! Lecture can be confusing and pedantic at times but there are also some very interesting classes. Lab was my favorite part of class but beware that lab takes A LOT of time, especially near the end of the semester as you prepare for turf wars. In the last 2 weeks, especially during reading period my team and I were in lab everyday for 5–8 hours in order to get the robot working. Additionally, start your psets early, especially any pset with CAD. The psets offer interesting questions but they can often be complicated to solve so go to office hours. The TFs for the class are incredibly helpful in solving the problems, helping with CAD, and explaining any content from class.
This class was one of the most stressful, frustrating, and draining classes I have every taken, especially since it required so much outside work, with having problem sets and lab work that had to be done outside of class every week. The lecture and lab components of the class often seemed disconnected in material, so making the robot (the final project) required a lot of outside knowledge and experience that the class does not provide, which is already tiring since the robot required a lot of outside trips made to the SEC, on top of two midterms. Professor Hasanov is also a great person and super nice, but the lectures were also not structured well, as I would often leave lecture confused over the concepts and with a bunch of equations written down that I did not know how to use or apply.

ES–51 definitely makes or breaks people, and it made me question if I really want to do engineering in the future; however, applying engineering concepts to making and building the robot was a cool experience, as well as getting to use the machine shop and other tools. The TFs and the teaching staff in the lab were also very helpful, and they were always willing to answer questions and help with anything. This course teaches you to be independent, resilient, collaborate with others, and exercise critically thinking skills, but I would definitely say that if you want to take this course on, learn to dissociate because otherwise the stress can be crippling. Your robot team also makes or breaks the lab group, but thankfully, most of the grading for that is based off the final report and the peer evaluation rather than robot performance, so hopefully that alleviates some stress.
If you want to know whether you want to do Mech E or not, this class is going to help you the most to decide. The professor is an amazing person as a teacher and as a person

Personally, I loved this class. You learn great hands–on skills working in the lab that can extend beyond engineering into just useful life skills that can help you for making/fixing stuff in the future. Professor Hasanov and the CAs are always available to help, and office hours are extremely helpful for completing psets. HOWEVER, this class also does have some drawbacks. At some points, it feels a bit disorganized, and often you WILL need to reach out for help on psets (SolidWorks can have a steep learning curve). The midterms and assignments can be a bit vague in terms of how to prepare, and if you're not on top of things, the final project can sneak up on you very fast. Also, having an Apple–brand laptop can be a disadvantage at some times. But if you're willing to put in the work and deal with some annoyances, the outcome of this course (the final robot project) can be VERY fulfilling and satisfying.
This course will challenge you. Be sure to use your classmates and Lab TFs as resources, as the lectures were not very conducive to learning the content. Take physics before taking the class, if possible. Get a mouse and use the computer lab as a resource if you have an Apple computer (Solidworks is not Mac friendly!!). The robot/lab component is a hit or miss – it depends on your teammates and work ethic. However, you can get the robot built and functional if you put in the effort, regardless of your teammates. Communication and proactive robot planning are essential. Although the lectures usually make minimal sense, you will come away from the course feeling accomplished and proud of what you have completed.
This is a great class! Seymur and Hanna are great and you'll learn a lot from this class. It is not a hard class but definitely has a lot of assignments and is a really big time commitment.
The teaching staff is very engaged and helpful and the lab component of the course is fantastic (you gain a lot of valuable hands–on skills), but the structure and content of lectures leaves a lot to be desired. This course will require that you put in a lot of hours, especially as you approach the end of the project. Your project group will make or break your experience. Overall, the course is manageable and valuable, but be prepared to put in a lot of time and for it to be difficult, and even frustrating (if not because of the content, because of the workload and group work required)
