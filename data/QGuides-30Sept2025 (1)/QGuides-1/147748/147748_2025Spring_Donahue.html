What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

Table for [QTitle].
Comments
If you have a deep interest in the law and the more technical underpinnings of its development, then take this course. The course actually has some powerful messages about what law is and how we arrived at the legal institutions and norms we have currently. Paying attention and doing the primary source readings (along with pre–recorded lectures) is key as the direction of the course will be very hard to track if you're not on top of things. Professor is very nice and goes out of his way to engage with students' questions.
Professor Donahue has a truly unique expertise –– there really might not be anyone else alive who knows as much he does about this topic area (or at least it feels that way in class). Beyond that, he is also a very careful and talented historian, and he does a good job of breaking down his thinking process for the class, rather than just present his conclusions; I learned a lot from observing the way that he reached historical conclusions and from his critiques of historical theories that he thinks are incorrect or lack sufficient proof. He also is very attentive to student interests: he builds every lecture around students' questions and devotes a truly unusual amount of time and care to giving feedback on papers. It's a manageable amount of work.

That said, this class is not for everyone. Not everyone finds the minutiae of medieval legal rules interesting, and this class might be rather dry for such people, even if the big picture conclusions are interesting. The materials and recorded homework lectures are online (https://amesfoundation.law.harvard.edu/CLH/clhfas/lectures/index_lec_CLH.html); I'd recommend perusing them (especially the recorded lectures) to get a feel for the class before deciding if it's for you.
