A lot of the topics covered in this class are quite fundamental, though I don't think Neil is the best lecturer. In particular, the class tries to cover way too much; a lot of results are stated without proof, and it's hard to fully understand everything being taught. That being said, Neil does keep the homework and exam reasonable. I think that a lot of people looking to take time series are looking for a more applied class, and the class does involve some more applied aspects (we implement some basic time series models). However, the models implemented are fairly elementary/basic, and most of the class is spent on understanding theory.
If you wish to do analysis with macroeconomic data, I strongly recommend the course. Else, it may be good to look elsewhere for a grad–level course.

The lecture content also wasn't strictly helpful for psets, so most people stopped going to lecture early in the semester, despite lectures not being recorded. I don't feel like I learned very much, partially due to somewhat ineffective lectures, although maybe I would have learned more if I had entered the course with more background for several other grad Stat courses. Neil's very nice, but the class was fairly disorganized and I probably would have learned more from other classes had I taken those instead.
Do not get left behind by the material because there is so much that it will be impossible to catch up. It is pretty hard to get by without going to lecture (speaking as someone who got by going to some lectures) I think it should be a priority to go.

Neil is very accessible, cares a lot about the class and us students, and is very reasonable in terms of expectations. This class is challenging but extremely applicable and interesting. In my opinion, you need to be comfortable with statistics above the level of Stat 110/111. My only statistics background was Stat 110 and CS 181, and I struggled to keep up with the theory in lectures (even though Neil is a pretty entertaining and informative lecturer). I think Stat 210 would help, since we referenced results and ideas from that class pretty frequently. You don't need any economics to understand what's going on, but a lot of the application questions Neil gave on psets were macro–related.

Assignments had 2 theory questions and 2 applied questions each, which I thought was a pretty good balance. They helped solidify why we use certain models and why certain properties and assumptions matter. There were only five psets, and we were able to drop 2 of them and use extensions on 2 others.

There were no midterms, but there was a final that had an applied section that we did at home and a theory section that we took in person during the scheduled final exam time. The final seemed fair to me, even though I didn't do very well (lack of understanding and preparation on my part). If you keep up with homework, attend lecture and office hours, and really understand what assumptions are carried by each time series model, then you should be fine.

The class is fair and the content is useful. Take it! It's tough but worth it.
