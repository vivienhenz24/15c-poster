Fun trip, really cool taking and working with your own data. Be prepared for pretty tedious if not difficult data reduction and a pretty apathetic professor but who knows his stuff.
This is the worst course that I've taken at Harvard. The flaws are numerous:

1. Should not be taught in IRAF. Modern astrophysics is done in python, leaving this course is very outdated.
2. Course structure: it was always very unclear what tasks were required/graded, and most assignments were submitted through email...?
3. Final project is very undirected. No assignment requirements or rubrics are provided, despite it almost entirely determining our grade.
4. Final project support is minimal, but not at fault of the TFs. After completing data reduction, groups are left to do data analysis on their own (and all groups chose to do it in Python, as this is the modern coding language of astrophysics), but because each group is doing a different type of project, the TFs are not able to help very much.
5. After spring break, class time becomes equivalent to office hours, but attendance is still mandatory.
6. Introductory lectures do not offer any educational value as they are, largely, covering material that is a prerequisite for the class.
7. First half of the course is spent going through IRAF "documents," which are outdated, riddled with typos and errors, and impossible to go through individually. They do not incentivize learning as they require dependence upon TFs – you get too bogged down in the outdated software to learn anything meaningful.
8. I have not received any written feedback, in any form, for a single assignment this entire semester.

The spring break trip to Arizona is pretty fun, especially if you have friends in the class. However, with the numerous glaring issues that this course presents, I do not think that it is anywhere near worth it.

If you are considering taking this class to satisfy astro requirements, take any other course in the department before this one. If you are planning to take this class for some other reason, don't.
Please reconsider taking this class unless there has been a substantial structural change. IRAF is not helpful with modern astrophysics research (to my knowledge) and if it is then the TFs at the very least should be familiar so they can help troubleshoot. In the midst of the course the server was updated and some commands just did not work anymore. There were no clear expectations for the last third of class (including the final project and presentation) despite the professor being asked multiple times to provide some guidelines. Plus, the 9 am lectures in northwest were very frustrating when the professor himself wasnt present or treated them as work sessions.

This class is good for the trip, but the course materials are poorly organized, the assignments and grading are unclear, and the teaching is uninspired. I don't know that the trip would necessarily even be worth the class for most students. The course is primarily taught using an outdated software that barely works on most computers, and most of my time was spending trying to find ways to fix coding issues that were only problems for me (and I have a Mac, Windows people had it even worse). This was a difficult class for me, but I think others would have a much easier time –– just assess whether you feel ready to put a lot of time into some really frustrating software issues.
This class spends about a quarter of the lectures before the field trip going a little bit more in depth on instrumentation than what is covered in Astro 17, and the rest of lecture periods are spent working on homework assignments, which focus on learning to navigate a software called IRAF.

IRAF is 40 years old. IRAF is sometimes incompatible with modern software, and there may be parts of your homeworks left incomplete because your laptop simply cannot run the function and theres nothing anybody could do about it. These are fundamental structural issues in IRAF that would require rewriting the software to fix because, again, its forty years old. Any spectroscopy or photometry stuff you can do in python you can probably also do in IRAF, it will probably just take about eight times as long if you want to use IRAF, which you have to for this class. If something doesnt work, there isnt necessarily a reason why. You could run the same thing again and it will suddenly work. Also, if something isnt working there is a good chance you will need to exit out of everything and start over again, which could mean exiting out of an hours worth of work.

After the field trip, all of class time is spent working on processing the data you collected using IRAF (which, again, would probably go much faster in python), with course staff there to answer questions.

The vast majority of the grade in this class comes from a paper and a presentation with no rubric and very hand–wavy requirements, but (and I cannot confirm this because grades arent released until after the Q guide closes) past years have said that these assignments are very harshly graded.

The field trip for this class is its redeeming quality. We went to places we would never otherwise get to visit, and for a week we were got to experience life like real observational astronomers, choosing our own targets and collecting our own data. This field trip is a highlight of my undergraduate experience. It is an incredible experience, and one that I will never forget, and I personally think it made the rest of the semesters coding struggles worth it, but remember that it is one week out of a semester of working with IRAF, and only you can judge if that is worth it to you.

Most days were just us silently struggling through one page of a worksheet of IRAF and it wasn't until the very last few weeks that I actually understood anything :(
