Math 21a is a good course overall, and one I would recommend for those who enjoy small–group exploration of math material as opposed to large lecture–style classes (that are typical of the Math 22 series). From my personal experience, I really liked my TF and CAs, and I greatly enjoyed grappling with the material in small groups. However, I would say that if Math 21a still has 3 psets a week at the time of reading this, do consider whether that is more your speed than 1 big pset a week (typical of the Math 22 series). Committing time for 3 psets a week was kind of an unnecessary hassle, but if they change 21a to being 1 large pset a week (like 22 is), it will be much better. The exams were somewhat inconsistent in terms of difficulty (first midterm was quite easy, second midterm was very hard, and the final was quite straightforward and not too hard), but the teaching team provides plenty of practice tests and extra practice that are very helpful for studying. The only objectively bad part of the course was the workshop section which is pretty much useless, just show up and do the problem that you need to write up on the next pset and then do other homework if you want to optimize your time spent. Despite these drawbacks, definitely take Math 21a or 22b if you are planning to do physics or other math–intensive science courses that could involve multivariable calc or vector calc, as Math 21a was super helpful for me to take concurrently with physics 19 and was helpful for many of my friends taking physics 15a as well.

TL;DR: Math 21a is a solid course that is moderately difficult with a decent time commitment to it, but one I would recommend for further STEM studies regardless. Take this over Math 22b if you like small groups more than big lectures.

Dear Future Math 21a student,

If you received your placement score and it said that you were placed above Math 1B, I strongly recommend you take Math 21a if you have a general understanding of Calculus 1 + 2/AP Calculus AB/BC. I will tell you from experience, this is not an easy course, but it is doable: You just need to put in the work. First, I will talk about the course structure (assignments, exams, etc.) and then I will tell you my personal experience about the class.

Lecture – Math 21a is organized into small class sections rather than a big lecture hall like the other STEM classes. You'll have a class of ~20 –25 students. Each class, you'll have to bring your workbook to complete the questions regarding the course concepts. But here's the catch: You actually have to write out WORDS in math. Yes, words... The worksheets are structured so that you won't be able to finish them all on time, but there are few occasions where you will finish. Don't worry though, the solutions to the worksheets are posted on Canvas the same day later in the evening.

Problem Sets (psets) – Like Math 1a and 1b, 21a has three psets a week, one due Monday, Wednesday, and Friday. Now, your first instinct would be to take Pset 0 and head to the library to figure it out on your own. That is fine, but, if you're spending 1–2 hours on one question, you are wasting time. My strongest advice is if there are office hours (OH) after class, go to them as soon as you finish class so that the material stays fresh in your mind. The TFs are helpful as they can provide clarification on questions you feel lost on. OH are held on Mondays through Fridays. The other resource that is super helpful is Math Question Center (MQC), which runs from Sundays through Thursdays in the evenings. They are held by course assistants (CAs) who have taken the Math 21a already and can help you knock your pset. My warning is that Sunday nights are usually the most chaotic since so many people are trying to submit their pset before Monday morning; so try to arrive as early as it starts so the CAs give you as much attention as possible.

Workshops – The section is honestly not too helpful at times. They are headed by CAs and you are required to attend these sections every Tuesdays, except the Tuesday(s) during the week of your math midterm, and they're meant to show you how multivariable calculus is applied in the real world. While they were interesting at times, I felt like they could have been used more for reviewing topics that build on one another (similar to how discussion sections in science classes are meant to review content). Other than that, you'll have to complete your workshop activities diligently as few of the questions in certain workshops are in your problem sets.

Mini–Exam + Revisit – The mini–exam only covered the first 5 topics. It is meant to give you a taste of what a math 21a exam looks like. The good news is that you can retake it for a higher score. The bad news is (1) that if you want to retake it , you would have to take it the same day as midterm 1 (the revisit takes place after the midterm) and (2) it only accounts for 5% of your grade. Then again, every point matters in your grade, so it is up to you.

Midterms + Final

You'll be provided with practice exams with answers and solutions for each test. My biggest advice for this is to do them as early as possible and truly reflect from them. Just as a disclaimer, just because there is a question on the practice test, this doesn't mean it will be on the actual test. The practice exams are to help you identify concepts that you need more clarification on. So make sure that after you finish them, make a tally of all the questions you got wrong, write down which concepts are being tested by them by looking at the solutions, and then bring your questions or concerns to MQC/Office hours. From my experience, I remember that the second midterm surprised us because the wording of a few of the questions was so weird. But regardless, you need to (1) figure out what they want you to solve, (2) discern the possible concepts need, and (3) execute your solution.

For my background, I had taken multivariable calculus in high school when I was a senior. However, we did not go as in depth into the topics 21a covers (partly because the class in high school was brand new, so it was a pilot course). Regardless, I still struggled in 21a during the first couple of classes because we needed to think in terms of R3 (the third dimension) and reason with derivatives and integrals in R3. The psets were a pain at the beginning since I made the rookie mistake of trying to figure them out on my own instead of seeking out for help. Luckily, in the second half of the semester, I went to OH regularly after class to knock out a good chunk of pset questions and then went to MQC to verify my answers.

Do not be afraid or embarrassed to ask for help in your 21a class. This is not meant to be an easy class nor are you meant to do it alone. Even though most students will have the same/similar math background as you, some may comprehend the math topics quicker than you, and that is okay. It is not indicative of your capabilities or skill set in math, it just means you have more to learn. Definitely, Math 21a helped me grow as a problem–solver at Harvard in terms of recognizing how concepts build on one another, and working collaboratively with others. This class is a journey, and just like any journey, you've got to embrace the process.
I highly recommend Math 21a to anyone looking to fulfill a requirement or get an introduction to math at Harvard, it was one of my favorite classes of the semester! The class is a sizeable commitment, but it is not too difficult as long as you put in the work. Make sure to do ALL the practice tests, as they carry over greatly to the exams.
