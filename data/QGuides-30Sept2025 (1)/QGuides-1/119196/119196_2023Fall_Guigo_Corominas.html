This course is perfect to take after high school single variable calculus. It is fast paced but very reasonable. There is ample support for the class and a great culture. I would highly recommend taking this course if you do not want to sit in a lecture to learn math.
Math 21a seems like the most natural choice for strong (but not genius) students interested in applied math, honors econ, bio, physics, engineering, etc. But I would strongly recommend taking either 22b or 18a instead. The class was very challenging, but not necessarily in the right ways. The tests were full of bizarre and unnecessary computation that had little to do with testing the important stuff. After the second midterm, 3 separate people told me they were dropping their STEM concentration, which is honestly depressing. I could complain, but it would be more helpful to give advice.

1. Find a great section. Roderic was wonderful. I'm not sure how the others were. But your experience in this class will depend mainly on your section leader, so make sure you're in a good section. Find an instructor who teaches in a style that works for you.

2. Go to MQC. The Psets are often too difficult to do on your own. In high school, I would have been embarrassed to go to something like that because I thought I was too smart for those kind of supports but that was a remarkably stupid perspective. Please try it out or go to office hours or work in a good pset group.

3. DO NOT let this class influence your views on STEM or even math at Harvard. Harvard is an amazing academic environment with a wide variety of classes even within each subfield of study. If you struggle in Math 21a, do not think it is because you should switch to econ or sociology (I love econ and sociology so no offense if you have a genuine interest in those). If you do not enjoy the content in Math 21a, do not think it is because you just lack that passion for STEM that some people have.

The reality is it is a strange course with many good components and many weaknesses. Put effort into it obviously, but don't let it influence your views on math in general too much. Also, don't be discouraged if your classmates pick up on the ideas quicker than you or already know the theorems before you learn them in class. They have already taken multi before and wanted an easy class. Simple as that. To people with a multi background already, take math 22 for the proofs or something else.

I hate to be so pessimistic because parts of this class were very interesting. But overall, I found it to be quite strange.
This class is worth taking even if it isn't required for your concentration. I took it in my freshman fall after taking BC calc. in high school and did well and found out that I liked math way more than I thought. The small class size and manageable work load help contribute to this feeling.

For 22, at least you learn proofs, which are often needed for concentrations that require this anyway. How section goes is highly dependent on what TF you have; I had a great TF, but some of my friends struggled due to their section TF. If you take this course, find a pset group early on and don't leave studying for the tests until the very end. Yes, I learned a lot about multivariable calculus, but I have a feeling I won't remember most of it after the course is over.

MATH21a was tough... First, the positives: there is always help available, lots of Office Hours led by CAs, generally supportive teaching team, you learn SO much throughout the semester, and it is quite lovely to see how far you have come at the end of the course. On the downside, there's a lot of assumed background knowledge that left me scrambling as someone who hasn't done math in a while; I spent most of my time playing catch–up with my classmates and felt mostly unsupported (for example, some extra worksheets or practice problems for Calc–I would've been helpful). The active learning style didn't suit me, and class was often stressfulI think understanding the concepts first before diving into practice would be more effective. Group–style learning could be great in theory, but I believe it struggles at Harvard where the competitive environment and everyone's tight schedules don't really support the teamwork structure. Lastly, having to complete three problem sets every week is excessive. I'm usually someone who appreciates doing work outside of class and never found homework burdensome in other courses. However, the requirement of submitting three p–sets weekly limited my time for revision and hindered my ability to start preparing for the exams early.

Overall, take this class if you need to. It is not the worst course out there, and you can definitely be successful if you put in the time and effort. However, be mindful of the high workload and incredibly fast–paced teaching, and pick your other classes accordingly!
