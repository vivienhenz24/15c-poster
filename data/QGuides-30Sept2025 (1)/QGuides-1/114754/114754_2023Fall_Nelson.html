Unless you have read most of the material I would not take the class. The readings are extensive around 600 pages a week. But overall it's an interesting class but it requires a lot of time. I recommend this course to anyone, including those outside the Gov department, with even a slight interest in early political thought in the Western world. However, I do so with the following recommendations: read strategically. You're not going to be able to go through every page of reading assigned to youâ€”in fact, you probably won't even get close. And if you try, it'll be harder to tease out the most important takeaways from each of the texts. I would recommend either skimming the texts AFTER each corresponding lecture, paying close attention to the passages that are quoted and discussed in class, or simply looking up the most important parts of each text online and then focusing on those in your reading (though this strategy is less reliable with some of the more obscure texts). If you focus on reading and thinking about the most important parts of each text (which, honestly, is a pretty slim proportion of the total assigned reading), go to lecture, and attend the review sessions, you'll be just fine. If you don't get bogged down in the reading, this course will be fascinating and manageable.
