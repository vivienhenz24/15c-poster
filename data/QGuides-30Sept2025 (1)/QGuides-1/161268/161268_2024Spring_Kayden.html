I have mixed feelings about this course. If you're a pre–med please read this! The grading for this course is harsh!!! Over 50% of your grade is based on team "presentations". First, these "presentations" are graded on whether you used the right visuals (use slides carnival). Second, your presentation grade is dependent on your team work which is based off a curve that not even the TF understands. So your grade can be completely destroyed if your teammates think you're doing "average" for the team. Third, they take off points for very small things on your presentation (such as formatting issues, speaking quality, etc.). This class felt more like a presentation/speech class than a humanitarian course because I was more worried about how I presented my powerpoints than I was about the ACTUAL humanitarian work in the course.

While the other comments will tell you that this is a course that most people should take, I wouldn't recommend it. Too much stress is placed on the grading of the "presentations" that it takes away from the actual subject being taught. Grading and feedback should be constructive – not destructive.

TAKE THIS COURSE YOU WILL LEARN SO MUCH. I knew nothing about the humanitarian sector before and came away thinking I might want to begin a career in this field. The professor give great lectures (especially prof. kayden) and you will learn SO SO much through the hw and the lectures. Some topics that you come away knowing are specific "sentinel" events that changed the humanitarian field (aka Rwanda Genocide, Srebernica etc.) The community in the class overall was great. The group projects are probably the toughest part of the course as they are graded quite harshly and your experience can very significantly based on the group members you get assigned. That being said, I would still take this class again and strongly recommend it if you are interested in international humanitarian law, learning about refugee camps, and learning about major historical events that deal with issues of global health, humanitarian access, and intervention.
This course was genuinely one of the coolest ones I've taken at Harvard. I was one of few who took it who weren't premed and still found it extremely interesting. You'll learn so much about how humanitarian aid functions from a very technical and matter–of–fact perspective. It's super applicable to the real world, and I find myself reading news articles about conflicts and disasters with so much more appreciation for the work nowadays. Workload is super manageable! Your weekly workload will pretty much consist of a short response over some readings which usually took about 30 minutes or max an hour and then a sentence about what you've struggled to understand in class that week. Workload really only ramps up around midterm and final presentations, where you'll present topics in randomized groups. You will be grilled harder during presentations but you are given a lot of prep time. The EOY simulation is super fun and was a great opportunity to bond with classmates. Overall, the class gave me such a unique set of knowledge I don't think is covered in any Harvard classes. Take the class!
This class will teach you so much more than you'd ever expect! It has inspired me to think with a global perspective, constantly ask "why" when discussing the crisis of the day, and consider a humanitarian career. This was doubtlessly my most applicable class at Harvard in addition to being the most engaging one.
