Comments
Prof. Sellke is a wonderful lecturer and you should take anything he teaches! This course gives really good intuition and has very instructive problems. That being said, some familiarity with high–dimensional probability (e.g. basic concentration of measure type–inequalities, such as Lipschitz concentration of Gaussians) and Stat 212 (e.g. Ito's lemma) would be very helpful, as well as a very strong willingness to get your hands dirty with calculations. Other than that, the course is fairly accessible and enjoyable!
This class is a great companion to Stat 217. That being said, I think to understand more of what RSB behavior "looks like," it may be better to have taken that course first. In any case, this is a wonderful course that dives much deeper into spherical spin glasses, and Mark is a great instructor. The homework is quite interesting. I think it is amazing how the intuition that physicists have developed for the "geometry" of these spin glasses emerges in all of the rigorous proofs that follow.
Stat 291 is definitely one of the hardest and one of the best classes I've taken at Harvard. I feel like I've learned so many useful methods/techniques from Stat 291 for both statistical physics and other stat/CS related problems. Mark is so knowledgeable about the content from the class, and, other than occasional mistakes on the board, his lectures are as clear as possible (it seems impossible for anyone to lecture completely clearly on some of the topics). If you've taken/are taking/will be taking Stat 217, Stat 291 is a really nice complement to the course: knowing basic information about RSB is really helpful for the latter part of Stat 291, and Stat 291 helps fill in some of the gaps in rigor from the Stat 217 heuristics. Strong probability and math/analysis is a must for the class, and knowing some differential geometry is really helpful for understanding a lot of the math.
One of the most unique courses I've had here as a Harvard college student and a statistics concurrent masters' candidate. There are only three homeworks (with lots of opportunity for extra credit!) and a final paper + presentation, so workload–wise, it is extremely reasonable (relative to other statistics graduate classes which have really harsh workloads). The content itself is also mind–blowing if one is seeing this type of thing for the first time, and it really trains one to think well about high–dimensional probability and formal algorithms. Overall, really well taught, and I would wholeheartedly recommend this class.
