This course is overall much lighter than 110/111. You probably won't have a choice in whether you take this course or not, since you'd probably only take it if you're a stats concentrator (since it's required) or taking it as an elective for your secondary. If you're doing quant and you're AM/Ec or CS, I'd probably also recommend taking this course due to its applicability and relatively light courseload. If you don't know what you want to do with your life and just want to take a moderately easy stat course that might potentially be applicable, this could also be the course for you.

The professor, James, is really sweet and enthusiastic about the material; he is also very fair with assignments/exams and final grades in the class. He's always happy to help in office hours.

The first section of the class (start of the semester through mid–October) is quite similar in content to 110/111, mostly applying the concepts from those classes in similar algebraic derivations , understanding distributions (normality, i.i.d assumptions), summations (such as those you do in 111), and expected value. You really only use a small subset of 110/111 material though, and it shouldn't be too bad. Psets are almost entirely completable without office hours, even for those who needed OH in 110/111. These psets also had a bit of coding in R which was quite easy with basic experience. You can use AI tools such as ChatGPT or GitHub CoPilot (integrated into RStudio/Posit Cloud) if you're struggling with coding. Midterm 1 had some time pressure but was quite fair, similar to a 110/111 exam but much less tricky. Make sure you review your concepts and make a good cheat sheet to prepare (and don't forget random distribution facts from 110 such as the relation between chi square and gamma distributions!). This portion of the class also uses some very basic linear algebra, so make sure you brush up on that (know what an eigenvalue is and how to invert a 2x2 matrix...). You also delve a bit deeper into the Multivariate normal distribution in this class than you do in 110 (useful for multivariable regression), so maybe try to brush up on that a bit (especially covariance matrices).

The rest of the class is a lot more application–based, testing less theory and more coding ability and data wrangling. You cover topics such as regularization (LASSO, RIDGE) and other forms of model selection and ways to transform a dataset to fit the assumptions required for regression. You'll be tested on this material in Midterm 2, which will probably be a take–home test. A lot of people did poorly on this test because of a strange question that James provided "hints" for, so make sure that you read all of his Canvas announcements closely if you don't want 18 percent deducted from your test for not including interaction terms...

Overall, you probably don't have a choice in whether you want to take this class, unless you substitute the requirement with 244 or 149. If you really prefer theory to application, you might want to take 244 instead, which is more linear algebra intensive but should be doable for anyone who did decently well in 110+111+Math 21b/22a. 149 covers generalized linear models and is meant as a sequel to this class, so I probably wouldn't recommend substituting it (especially since 139 is far more applicable for most tasks) unless for some reason you need to take it in your spring semester instead. Also, if you got an A in 110/111, you will almost certainly get an A in this class, and with probably half as much effort. If you got an A– in 110/111, you can probably also get an A in this class with not too much effort. Just remember that you'll take away from this class whatever you put in: if you ChatGPT all the psets, you might not actually learn a lot of the R skills required and miss out on conceptual understanding, even if it will get you by grade–wise. Try to enjoy the experience and don't sweat this class too much.
