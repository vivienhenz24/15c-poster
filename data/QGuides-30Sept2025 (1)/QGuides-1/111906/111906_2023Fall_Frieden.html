There is definitely a lot of reading, but it is all interesting and worthwhile reading that you will learn from regardless.
A very worthwhile class if you are interested in government. The material is substantive and provides a good framework for analyzing international politics and relations. Great class! Very little work week–by–week (nothing besides readings), but the 2 weeks in which essays are due feel quite intense. Prof. Frieden is awesome and extremely accessible, and the only TF (Mara) is a huge asset to the class.

This class is amazing! Prof Frieden is that kind of professor you would expect at the world's best university! He is so nice and so wise! The lectures are very freely taught (no slides) but Prof Frieden completely makes up for that with his great ability to explain complex topics and he follows a very logical and easy–to–follow order. The topic the course teaches is interesting in itself, but Prof Frieden makes this class unforgettable and even more interesting! My best class at Harvard so far!
I would recommend this class to anyone interested in understanding why economic or political theory alone does not guide real world outcomes. Professor Frieden seems to have an encyclopedic knowledge spanning concepts in both political science and economics, and I truly enjoyed conversations with him outside of class (he genuinely seems to really enjoy getting to know students). This class has a *very* good reading listthere is a core textbook (Oatley) that does an excellent job teaching the core concepts of IPE while another textbook (Frieden Lake Broz) provides a lot of anecdotal examples and analysis of how these concepts play out.

As a freshman, this course is likely to feel daunting due to the college–level writing practice necessary to succeed on the essays and the fundamental economic knowledge required to understand some of the class concepts at a deeper level. That being said, the course could feel like a bit of a review at times if you are an upperclassmen, particularly if you have already taken the globalization gen ed (while the syllabi are clearly distinct, there is some decent overlap around concepts like the MF Trilemma, Bretton Woods history, current/capital accounts, etc.).
Not going to lie, this course will kick your butt if you are not self–disciplined and self–motivated. You need to stay on top of those dense readings every single week and be diligent about taking good notes. If you don't, you will struggle with the exams, especially the final which consists of a closed–book essay section. I would be cautious with this class if you are not ready to dive into somewhat technical material about IPE and macroeconomic policy
This class is full of interesting information about economics and government. The lectures are very information dense, and you will walk away feeling you learned a lot. Doing the readings will ensure you get ten times more out of the class.

However, I would most recommend this class to first years or sophomores rather than seniors studying economics or government , as a lot of the material will be redundant with what you have likely already learned.
I would recommend taking this class if you have a strong interest in the field of international political economy and would like to connect, for example, with the professor. The reading load is extremely heavy, and the assessments (both papers and exams) are also challenging, with a strict grading approach that deducts points for any minor errors. The professor is very friendly, kind, and approachable, showing a great deal of concern for students. However, the lectures can be tiring. For students with extensive knowledge of history and economics (such as someone who has taken Ec10B, for example), much of the material may not be entirely new, only approached differently. I feel that I didn't learn many new things; instead, I reflected on what I already knew in different ways with new implications, which is also valuable. I was very frustrated with the grades and expected more, given my dedication and involvement in the class.
