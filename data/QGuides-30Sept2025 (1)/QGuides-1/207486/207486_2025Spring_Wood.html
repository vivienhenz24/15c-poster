This course needs a lot of work in terms of the teaching and the section structure. First, the lecturer and TFs/CAs almost NEVER responded to the Ed Discussion, which made learning the content and digesting it extremely difficult. Additionally, the lack of support was apparent in every step of the course. The CAs were not required to do office hours, and many who ran them did not know the answers themselves –– including some of the TFs themselves. Second, the answers that were given to them were often times littered with errors and hard to understand twists and turns that students beginning to learn proofs should not be expected to know. Third, the exams often tested on content that had either been taught/mentioned once in class, with no further mention ever again, or they were obscenely difficult and tedious mathematical calculations –– I'm talking about integrals, things we had learned in high school, which we were suddenly being asked to do for an entire page, rather than spending more time to give us more proofs in class. I think most of the content tested was fair, but the true/false questions were rather unfair, because we were being asked to prove statements that we had barely considered in class. Fourth, the lecturer would not post practice midterms or finals designed for the course until only 1–3 days before, and he didn't even post an answer key to the final practice exam he designed –– meaning that we had no way of knowing if our practice was correct before the final.

If you are really confident in your math abilities + self–teaching + proofs, then I think you should take this course. Also maybe if you're really against completing 3 psets a week in Math 21. If you're anything else, heavily reconsider your choice to take this course.

This class was great! 22a got a lot of hate last semester, but keep in mind that was Prof. Wood's first time teaching the course. That being said, he made a significant number of improvements based on feedback. Here are a few; there are not longer random extra days of class, section is optional now, lectures feel better paced with a bit more time for problems, attendance sheets were simplified and make more sense, the random extra coding/math projects are gone, and so on. It is also clear from the syllabus that Prof. Wood read through the Q guide, identified resources that were particularly helpful to students last semester, and emphasized them this semester.

Perhaps the only note I have about this course is that students should really take 22a first, as a lot of 22b draws on concepts from 22a (especially at the beginning of class). It helps to brush up on 22a materials also, especially if you may have forgotten over winter break or took 22a longer than a semester ago. Other than that, this course is pretty straightforward – grading is to your benefit, classes are easy to follow, and the prof cares a lot and so makes the material interesting (therefore making it easier to learn).

This course has 3 midterms (they weren't bad, were graded pretty leniently and the questions made sense). It is proof based, so much of the homework involves writing proofs instead of explicit calculation. Especially compared to 21, this class has only one pset and one brief online assignment per week, making it by and far less stressful (source; the pained looks on the faces of my friends in 21). Teaching staff are nice and available when needed.

I felt that I took a lot away from this course, and I would definitely recommend it!

(Bonus note; if you are free, the best office hours to go to are Wood's own – he explains stuff better (more effectively and more efficiently) and is super nice. I had classes during his OH this semester, but if you can, definitely go and say hi)

The first thing I will say is that Math 22 and 21 are very different classes. The learning experience in Math 22 is fundamentally different from 21 – it is. much less computation/applications and much more concepts and theory. You will develop an intuition for mathematical principles from the ground up (is this a good thing? See below).

This past year (2024–2025) is the first time Professor Wood has taught this class. Dr. Wood is a kind, knowledgeable, and genuinely very engaged lecturer, but the inexperience really does show in the operations side of the class. Dr. Wood's participation and engagement–based teaching style works well in a smaller setting (and is great in office hours) but is downright awkward in a massive lecture hall where everyone is very spread apart. Dr. Wood loves to go in–depth with proofs and concepts, which is great, but that sometimes comes at the cost of learning the application or how to actually do the problems. There are frequent errors in the lecture notes, online homework, and practice exam solutions, and often delays in returning homework, responding to questions, or posting practice exam material. This class has great content and is genuinely interesting if you engage with it – but be prepared to shoulder the operational headache that comes with it.
A background in multivariable calculus or proofs is really helpful, but the course is doable without it. Just be prepared to put in the extra work to become proficient in both multivariable and proofs.
