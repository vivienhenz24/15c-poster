This class was a wonderful way to understand art as a lens through which to view pertinent social problems. The course staff are very supportive, and anyone with an interest in art and social issues should consider it. I will say that assignment instructions can often be unclear and some changes aren't clearly announced to students.
This class is a gem that seems to not think it is a gem. There's not much feedback on written assignments, which are occasionally graded harshly. If you've seen modern/contemporary art (I had been to the MoMA and was mostly unmoved, but a little intrigued) and hated it, I would probably not say this class is worth it. You will have to write meaningfully about art you do not find compelling, all semester. That said, this class does teach you some valuable tools for interpreting art. If you've never taken an art class before, this would be a great one to start with just for those interpretive tools. Spending each section in the art museum is relaxing and fun, and the assignments that ask you to seek out art are also fun. I think I would have enjoyed the class way more if I actually enjoyed the art that we got to see. That said, there are some artists that I found really cool (Yinka Shonibare, Kerry James Marshall).

End of the day though, still a gem.
Only take this if you are truly interested in contemporary art. Prof. Lambert–Beatty honestly is not the most effective lecturer, though I hate to say it... Lectures can be very dry and not engaging. Sections aren't much better either, usually we are given a work to look at and just blindly discuss for the whole hour; I feel like discussion could only be fruitful if we are provided more background info about the work (which is so important for contemporary artworks) and instructions/prompts. During the term workload is very low, you write observation lists and museum–label–like essays for artworks. However, final essay is difficult and the class does not prepare you properly for it.
Professor is awful (like really awful at teaching/engaging, i don't believe anyone takes her seriously) but the class is somewhat made up by content, little workload and lack of exams.
The professor is super chill and very understanding although it can be somewhat difficult to get a hold of her at times. The teaching staff is incredible and super interesting – I would recommend taking this class as an elective since it will help foster a genuine interest in art which tends to push outsiders away instead of welcoming them in.
I still think you should take this class despite what I'm about to say, because being able to understand visual art is really important, and this is a pretty comprehensive introduction to what's going on in the art world right now. You encounter a ton of cool art from the last 40 years in class, and some of the pieces I saw in lecture will stick with me for the rest of my life. The workload is never unmanageable, and you can re–do pretty much every assignment with feedback from the TFs, who are great, if you want.

This course has the content of an intro class, but Professor Lambert–Beatty does not have the attitude of someone teaching an intro class. As pretty much everybody knows, there's a big debate about what "art" actually is, and it's pretty much impossible to start from zero knowledge about post–modern art and contemporary art without hitting the wall of, "does this still count?" But Professor Lambert–Beatty, maybe because she's been in the art world for so long, and maybe because she's simply sick of the central debate of any person encountering art academically for the first time, sort of refuses to engage with this question. Genuine curiosity from students is met with defensiveness and accusations of thinking the course material is unserious. Sometimes Professor Lambert–Beatty would even acknowledge that some people in the class might be skeptical of certain types of art, but every time somebody took the bait and expressed that opinion, it would turn into a one–sided debate between a professor and somebody who had only just learned what "contemporary" means a few weeks prior. I'm only expressing this so long–windedly because I hope that in the next iteration of this class, there's a bit more of an assumption of good–faith curiosity rather than an expectation of cynicism. As a humanities student who took this class as a pure elective, my heart sinks when I think of people taking this class for the humanities distribution requirement thinking that this is how we all are. I really do understand the impulse to protect something you love as much as Professor Lambert–Beatty clearly loves art. But I fear that she's just protecting the art from genuine interest.

One more thingas you learn pretty quickly in this class, contemporary art is mostly about and/or related to social justice issues. Professor Lambert–Beatty often comes across as a bit uncomfortable discussing these topics in front of a lecture. Again, this seems to come from a desire to get it right more than anything else, which you have to respect. But it's a bit awkward to sit through the anxious throat–clearing before we can get to the heart of these discussions. Whenever it was something related to my identity being discussed, I felt sort of othered a lot of the time because of her anxiety discussing things that are normal to me. In my opinion, this isn't a reason not to take this class. But something to note.

tl;dr take this class but don't raise your hand in lecture unless you want to be argued with
