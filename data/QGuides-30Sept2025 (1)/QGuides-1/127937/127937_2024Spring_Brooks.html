I would recommend this class. Manageable work load is paired with interesting material. Plus the professor is top–notch.
The content is interesting – I really liked learning about how modern CPUs use all sorts of algorithms to squeeze as much resource usage as possible. The problem sets are also not bad – they generally don't take too long, and the exercises are often just running through the steps of an algorithm covered in class, which is more tedious than difficult. One issue is that the midterm was REALLY long this year – it's a take–home midterm so you get as much time as you need, but it was also like the length of three problem sets and was an absolute pain to complete. But other than that, lectures are recorded, problem sets aren't particularly long, and it's overall on the lighter side.
146 is a great course! It's pretty high–level course, but it's really great to see how modern processors are designed and architected.
Great class, lots of very useful material. Assignments can be a little tedious at times but they do help with learning.
CS 246 is an absolute gem of a class, especially if you have already taken CS 141. It serves as a natural extension, building upon the concepts covered in the prerequisite course. The syllabus is comprehensive and engaging, covering a wide range of fascinating topics.

The first half of the course lays a strong foundation, while the second half delves into more contemporary subjects such as GPUs and datacenters.

The problem sets, while not particularly challenging, serve as excellent practice for the ideas and techniques taught in class. They are well–designed and reinforce the learning objectives effectively.

The only drawback of the class was its 9 am timeslot. As someone who is not a morning person, I found it challenging to attend the lectures in person consistently. I had to rely on the recorded Zoom lectures, which were sometimes muted or of poor quality, making them useless.
This class is pretty solid and not too much work. David is also an excellent instructor.
Make sure that you come in with basic knowledge of data dependencies and pipelining. Much of the content that you may not understand in lecture can be reviewed online (it can just be hard to find sometimes), and walk through the more complicated algorithms with the slides until you understand them.
