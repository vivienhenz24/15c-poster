Relatively easy class that acts as a non–intimidating intro to big data through economics. However, for the most part, this class requires no understanding of economic concepts and is more about quantitative social science in general and introducing you to quasi–experimental methods and basic machine learning concepts. Labs are very easy and will take you less than an hour per week. The empirical project (both parts) are not terribly time–consuming and are generously graded as long as you follow the instructions. The only challenging parts of the class will be the midterm and final exams, which are all multiple choice questions. Half the questions on these will be basic concept application from the class (e.g., calculate a certain value, vocab) and the other half will be brutal rote memorization questions that can be incredibly niche: you will be asked to recall study results that you learned months prior that you definitely do not remember. Don't make the mistake I did: MAKE A CHEAT SHEET! Write down as many study results as you can in the hopes that you write down answers to questions that get asked. Add pictures of graphs from lecture, even the most random ones, as they will show up. Basically anything on a lecture slide is fair game, so budget your space wisely. That is the only way to get through these exams. However, the class supposedly has a generous curve, so as long as you do all the assignments and follow instructions and study a decent amount for the exams (and make cheat sheets), you will probably get an A in this class. Also, don't worry too much about the readings: they probably won't help you anyway. Just watch the lectures (they're recorded) and do your assignments on time. Lastly, choosing between Stata and R labs: if you have R experience, you should probably do an R lab. If you have no CS experience/you're too good at R/you want to be an economist, perhaps take the Stata lab. Either way, most of the code is done for you for each lab, so it really doesn't matter.

Content–wise, you'll probably enjoy some of the topics. The education section was particularly interesting (they included college admissions and Harvard final clubs), and the health/judicial decisions algorithmic bias was also pretty fascinating. However, the lectures are quite a drag (mostly just discussing study results), so it might be more efficient to watchrecorded lectures on double speed.
