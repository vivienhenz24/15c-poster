What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

Comments
Don't do two semesters of it, just do research to do research, not for a class.
Definitely take the course to give yourself time to do research and explore your interests while still having some structure and support from course staff through workshops that help guide you through your project.
SCRB 91R is very convenient for people who are serious about lab work, but don't discount it as "just a class." With travel time and the unpredictability of lab experiments, taking this class can be pretty exhausting with the time requirement. Try to set clear expectations with your lab supervisors to make sure you know what you need to do to succeed in this course. The course doesn't have any formal checkpoints in the middle, so try not to be afraid to ask your mentor what you can do to improve. The benefit of this course is that there is very little pressure to produce any "good" data. We spent a lot of time talking about how experiments fail during the workshops and it made the experience much less stressful, since we all knew that we weren't expected to churn out a scientific treatise in three months.
Amie is the best. She has such a command of this content and is able to teach with a lot of clarity, really pushing you to think further about your experiments (e.g. Why is this design better than another? When is a positive control unrealistic?)
Take this class if you want to do research! It is so helpful when fitting research into your schedule and for getting advice from the HDRB department regarding research.
This is a great chance to get to focus on your research before starting your thesis!
This course is exactly what it seems –– you work with your postdoc and PI to do research throughout the semester. Note that there are six workshops that you have to either go to or watch asynchronously. Make sure to communicate with your postdoc to set expectaitons because they determine your grade.
