7. Sometimes, the psets were released a day late and consequently, we would have one less day to complete them

I'm pretty sure the grading deadlines are released very early on and the teaching staff should've made note of this. This seriously interfered with students' travel plans

Constructive feedback:
1. Grade midterms and exams on time. It’s okay if there are delays sometimes, but it absolutely should not be the norm.

2. Provide solutions for the psets

3. Carefully think about the problems you assign

4. Hire graduate TFs who have more years of math experience and may have more time on their hands

5. Please be mindful of students’ time and end class exactly when it’s supposed to

6. Keep office hours fixed. Set up some times at the start of the school year and stick to them. Provide longer notice before you cancel or change your OH

7. Release psets on time so we have sufficient time to do them

8. Please do not change the dates of exams after you had finalized the dates on the syllabus.

I was under the mistaken impression that this is an easy class, because it is an anti–req to Math 25 / 55. As it turns out, the class was challenging – although not unmanageable. I'll split my feedback into 1. the actual content and 2. the logistics of this specific instance.

1. first, on the content of real analysis, since this is more consistent across course iterations. This course is extremely standard, and the same content is taught essentially everywhere (e.g. also at MIT). If you are really motivated, you can self–study this content from any textbook and you don't need to take the class. You will learn exactly the same thing. Obviously, you will be more challenged by taking the class in person. The teaching this year was solid, but you could also just enroll, study the class notes and do the homework off of those without always attending lecture.

2. second, on this year's logistics. The upside of the logistics was that psets were 70% of the grade, and the rest was a take–home midterm and a take–home final. I think it was really great to have the exams be take–home, because it took away the time pressure and was ultimately a more accurate reflection of my understanding of the content. For future iterations, I would look carefully at what the format of the exams are, because in–person exams in real analysis are notoriously difficult. The major downside this year was that the grading was really slow. Problem sets from October were being graded in finals week. This made it hard to know how you were doing in the course. It's really hard to know ex–ante whether this will be the case in the future, so I wouldn't base your enrollment decision on this . But I think you should be aware that grading could be very slow in the future, as well.

Overall, my take would be essentially as follows. If you have to take this class for some kind of requirement (e.g. for an Econ PhD), you don't have much of choice. It probably doesn't make much of a difference whether you take it at Harvard or at MIT– check the logistics of the specific offerings. Be prepared to put the work in (10–15 hours a week to do well). If you don't need to take this for a requirement, think seriously about whether you find the content of the course really interesting. If you do and you enjoy abstract math, then by all means enroll. If you don't find it interesting or you like more practical courses, look elsewhere – this is unlikely to be rewarding.
