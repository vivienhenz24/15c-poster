This class is not easy (I have not received my final grade yet so idk how harsh they are in the end). There are two lecturers each week, Stephanie and Mansi. Both lectures would stress me out because it felt like a lot of material with things you had to simply memorize on each slide. Stephanie's lectures really do feel like an exhaustive list of things to remember, but may be more entertaining because they are about comparative zoology. Mansi's lectures are about embryology and germ layer development, but I felt like they had more of a story and big picture things to understand rather than an exhaustive list. Definitely go to office hours because they are empty most of the time.
The workload is manageable (read light) and the content is interesting, however, the exams are stressful and often not reflective of your overall knowledge. There is a massive amount of content to learn and memorize AND apply. It is a lot. I did better on the midterm than the final, despite feeling better about the final than the midterm. It is a lot of work, and I am not sure I would recommend this class to someone with a weaker biology background.
This class is not a beginner biology course. It requires pre–existing familiarity with the foundations of animal biology since such knowledge is foundational to the course concepts. I would suggest another pre–med biology course if you don't have such background knowledge.
This course is definitely difficult but very rewarding! Each week covers a different system that contributes to the theme of what 'makes' an animal. There are technically two instructors, so Stephanie will lecture on the more physical/anatomical side of a given concept while Mansi will lecture on the evolutionary/gene regulatory aspect of the concept. So by the end of the class, you've basically sat through two lectures at 1.5x speed, which can be a lot, but they are so helpful and understanding and offer a lot of support. Would recommend!
This is a course that I recommend, with the caveat that there are lots of little deadlines throughout the week and everything is graded for correctness. Exams are doable but difficult. Lectures have a lot of material, it is explained well and slides are easy to study from. The labs are super cool. If you have the capacity to take this class, take it, it's worth it, but only take it if you have the capacity to give it time and energy.
As someone who doesn't have any background in evolutionary biology, it's a really interesting course and you definitely learn a lot! Comparing organ systems in species across different phyla can provide you a unique perspective viewing the different animals in the world; Stephanie and Mansi's expertise really complement each other well and build a more holistic understanding. There are 1–2 quizzes every week though to look out for, and it can particularly be more challenging for sophomores just in terms of lacking lab or general organismic/evolutionary biology experience, since most juniors/seniors do have confidence in these areas. Overall though, it's a great course, friendly environment, and the entire teaching team is really strong so don't hesitate to reach out for extra help.
This course is a great survey of many major organ systems from both a morphological and developmental perspective. At times, the content can feel a bit rushed and the exams cover a lot of material, but it's super interesting. There is definitely a vertebrate bias (as there is with most of the scientific literature), but I appreciated that we considered material from both the vertebrate and Drosophila perspective. The first half of the course was very well–organized and taught, with super cool labs, but the organization of lectures and material is a little more muddled in the second half (mostly because you are looking at systems where the line between morphology and development is a lot less clear). I would definitely recommend this class for anyone who is interested in how animals!
Make sure you prioritize studying for the midterms and try to find someone else in the class that you can study/run by materials with. There is a lot of material and the class is great, but it can be a lot to remember.
Fantastic class with VERY hard exams! The teaching staff is fantastic and very knowledgeable and available for office hours and extra help. This class has a relatively light workload throughout the week. The kicker of this class is the midterm and final exam, which are very detailed and very hard. It is necessary to study the material throughout the semester to keep up. Especially if you do not have prior experience with animal biology. The labs are super fun and the reverse classroom is a great style of teaching this large amount of material.
If you are pre–med and trying to take a class to fulfill a pre–med requirement, OEB58 might be enticing but I would think twice about whether or not it's the best choice for you just to fulfill the requirement. Overall, I think it is a good class and I definitely learned a lot about animals and organ systems, but I think I would've preferred LS2 instead of this class (but with no hate to OEB58). OEB58 can be extremely challenging if you haven't taken biology in a while, especially if you have no interactions with animal biology. The class has no psets, only weekly lecture, lab, and section, which is nice until the midterm and final come around. There are no practice exams or practice problems to help prepare for the exam, so you really just have to study and memorize everything for the exams. The exams are divided into two parts and are in–class, and they are SO long. Time is of the essence, and it's extremely difficult to keep track of time while also going through each question with enough detail. All in all, OEB58 was, by standard, a good class but difficult. Make sure you know what you're getting into before taking this class.
Do not take this to satisfy a pre–med requirement. It does not make sense. This class is pretty challenging. It is easier if you're interested in it and have some background, so if you come from a background that isn't somewhat biology–oriented, you will have to put in more work to succeed.
The amount of content that happens in this class is a lot. You have to sit through two lectures for one class meeting, so make sure to review the material as the term progresses. The other class meeting is for the labs. The strengths of the course is the labs. I think the labs are very hands on and allow for better understanding of the (huge amount) material that is gone over lecture. About the grading, it is really hard to know what is required of you in your responses for the exams. The exams say don't write essays, but then when we get grades back, we get points off for leaving out mini details. We are asked to not write long responses, but if we don't write thorough answers, we get points off. It is very confusing and frustrating for students. As a person who is not OEB/Bio related concentration and have other commitments to my concentration, this class has a very strict attendance policy that ends up hurting students who has other legitimate commitments since attendance is big part of the class. The professors advertised their class as trying to foster a diverse learning environment and not just having bio concentrators, but they are not accommodating to students at all. However, I do like the emphasis on participation; this class just needs to be more flexible. The professors besides their inflexibility are great, nice, and are proactive about reaching out to students. They are truly experts of their field. Their lecture materials are made available for students and is great studying material.
You might read that this course has a hard midterm and final but the grading on those was extremely fair in my opinion. I really enjoyed this course but since there is only one lecture a week as the other class meeting is used as a lab, each lecture has a high concentration of material. I found it helpful to review lectures every few weeks to make it easier to handle the amount of material in each lecture when the exams came around.
This is a really enjoyable class that covers A TON of content but it's worth it. Exams are tricky but study hard and you'll be alright.
I am so glad I took this class over LS2 or any bio/premed requirement –– Mansi and Stephanie are wonderful lecturers, the course content is quite interesting (although sometimes I had some trouble figuring out what content mattered for exams), and best of all, the labs are very engaging and during the Thursday section! Just by nature of the class structure –– labs on Thursdays, final exam on the last day of school, etc. I feel like I had a relatively worry free and very fun experience in this class. Not to mention you get to go on a field trip to the Museum of Natural History and you start to see animals entirely differently afterwards!
This class is excellent if you are curious about biology. I think that pairing evolutionary biology and developmental biology was a stroke of genius, and I feel like I understand life much more deeply. But, the assignments are pretty boring and meaningless, and the exams are just memorization – so proceed with caution.
Overall a nice class! The small size was a real boon and the teaching staff were extremely helpful and supportive. The lab activities were also really nice; I found them fun and engaging. One piece of advice I would give is to start studying for the exams early. They cover a lot of material in detail and require quite a bit of memorization. I would make sure to start studying at least a week in advance and go to office hours.
