What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

My message is simple: any time you have an opportunity to take a course with Dr. Lautua, or read Dr. Lautua's scholarship, or just listen to her speak: do it. She is a brilliant scholar and an incredible teacher, a combination that is rare to come by. I can't sing her praises enough, her syllabus and her teaching style made this course easily one of the best courses I've taken at Harvard. Her facilitation encourages students to share from their backgrounds and lived experiences, which for me as a non–Pacific student, helped me both learn more about my peers and their communities and encouraged me to reflect on how my own identity brings me to my work more than any other class I've taken. I am grateful to no end that I took this course.
If you can take any course taught by Dr. Lautua, you absolutely should. As someone with a background in Pacific Islander Studies, I found this course to be a breath of fresh air and offered a new lens through which to view the field. I have never taken a religion course or engaged in academic discussions of Indigenous Pacific religions, so this class was incredibly eye–opening. Professor Lautua rightfully favors the scholarship of Pacific Islander scholars, theologians, activists, etc., which aligns perfectly with her methodology and pedagogies. We meet twice a week for 1.15 hours and open class with very well–prepared slideshows on this week's topics, then open up for a class talanoa, activities, or discussions. The class has always been incredibly engaging, and I have never found myself searching for a clock to see when the class will end; if anything, I always wished we had more time! The workload is very manageable regardless of your semester workload; although the readings are a bit hefty, you can still thrive if you can only thoroughly read a few and skim the others. Assignments and class excursions are all great! Take. This. Class.
Please, please, PLEASE take a class with Therese Lautua while you can. Although I will do absolutely everything in my power to get her a permanent position at Harvard, she is only here on a fellowship contract, so her time with us may be unfortunately limited. I believe that we have zero tenured faculty of Pacific descent at this university, but that is another issue.

Taking a class with Therese will not only transform your approach to learning and discussion, but it will require you to engage deeply with some of the most pressing issues of our time from an indigenous Pacific lens. That is a once in a lifetime opportunity.

Talanoa, or to talk/unveil, refers to a collaborative discussion approach to complex problems. Through talanoa, we build community, challenge norms, and facilitate understanding. This class was taught using Talanoa, which means that each student must come to class prepared to fully immerse themselves in the week's texts and topic(s). It was an unparalleled learning experience, and one that enabled students to form genuine connections with both each other and the material.

Around 85% of texts that we read in REL 32 were by indigenous Pacific scholars. In most classes about topics in the Pacific that I have come across (at Harvard), the ratio is flipped. I think that in itself speaks volumes. Having an emic perspective on Pacific issues is absolutely fundamental to effectively and deeply engaging with it.

I come from a country in the Pacific, and for lack of a better word, this class felt like home. It felt like home in a place that can sometimes feel isolating. This class gave me an anchor, and it (or any class taught by Therese, for that matter) will give you one too.
This course taught me so much about how big, connected, and diverse Oceania is.
TAKE THIS CLASS! One of the best experiences I've had at Harvard. Great introduction to Pacific culture and language!
