Very well–structured class that really gets into the mathematical and graphical understandings of microeconomics. I found the graphical interpretations to be very useful

Learning
– Preference curves and utility
– Perfect competition models based on profit maximization and cost minimization
– Monopoly/monopsony models for input/outputs
– Cost types (MC, AC, AVC, AFC) and how they're graphed
– Oligopoly/duopoly models, including Cournot, Bertrand, and Stackelberg
– Short run vs long run decisions of firms

I would advise future students of this class to brace themselves for a challenging experience and to only take this course if absolutely necessary. The course demands a significant investment of time and effort, particularly because the tests are exclusively free–response, with no multiple–choice questions. This format requires a deep understanding of economic theories and a high level of mathematical skill and speed, as the problems are often more complex than those practiced in problem sets.

Despite the rigorous nature of the assessments, it's important to note that the professor is genuinely nice and approachable. He is willing to help and provide guidance, which can be a great asset when navigating the course's challenges. However, the bulk of your grade will be determined by a few high–stakes exams, so effective preparation is key.

While the course may fall short in effectively teaching economics, it excels in imparting crucial life skills such as discipline, resilience, and the importance of continuous effort. Youll likely discover much about your own learning style and how to study efficiently under pressure. I recommend starting early with your preparations, utilizing all available resources, and practicing under timed conditions to enhance your ability to quickly and accurately solve complex problems.
