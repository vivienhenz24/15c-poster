Prof. Matherne is the best ever! I took this class with no English or Phil background because a friend told me that she was great, and she did not disappoint. Not only a great lecturer, but a great discussion facilitator, and super helpful one on one for helping me work through essay topics, issues I was having with the course, etc. The course itself is pretty straightforward; it's a class on Austen, and you just read all of her books (plus some pretty manageable philosophy pieces). Definitely a lot of reading, but if you like Austen, it's a pretty enjoyable workload. Also, I came in hating Austen from when I read Pride and Prejudice in ninth grade, and I loved most of the books, so keep an open mind!
This is a pleasant foray into English literature with unexpected but interesting connections to philosophy
I cannot stress this enough: TAKE ANY AND EVERY CLASS WITH PROF. MATHERNE. She is a genius, incredible lecturer, and conversations with her in office hours are invaluable. I don't even study philosophy, but I wish I did after taking her class. Do not make the mistake I did and wait until your senior year to take a course with her. Take one as early as possible. And then another. And then another.

For practical information about this course: One of the reasons why this class was so great, was that everyone was so bought in that class participation was actually very productive, which allowed the 40–person class to feel like a community by March. This class is not a gem. I spent a good amount of my time reading every week, because you are practically reading one Austen book every 2 weeks, on top of philosophy readings. If you are going to take this class, know that you will have to commit to doing all of the readings in order to get the full experience of this course. This is not to scare anyone off – Prof. Matherne is so accessible with explaining philosophy and making it comfortable for anyone, no matter their experience, to participate in class – HOWEVER I would recommend that you do not take this class as a buffer/light workload class during a heavy semester, but as a class you expect to devote about 5–10 hours of intentional reading to each week. The more you have the time to engage with this course – doing all of the readings, supplemental readings, going to office hours, thinking about it in your free time – the more you will get out of it. You also shouldn't worry about papers. They are very clearly structured for you (similar to other Matherne papers, if you've taken a course before) so that you know exactly what is expected to do well. There are also multiple interesting prompt options, so if you want to avoid writing about the complicated mess of Mansfield Park, you can do that.

Final thoughts: This class is for people who wonder, "What makes philosophy, philosophy?" This isn't a phil course where a philosopher's work is reconstructed neatly by the professor, but one where the concept of philosophy is called into question. The project of understanding Austen as a philosopher is very alive and in process, so you may find yourself strongly disagreeing with a lecture from Prof. Matherne, or questioning if Austen is really a philosopher at all. This is the heart of what makes this class great. I was pushed to critically think in ways that I hadn't been these past four years, and you will feel so fulfilled by the end. I also now can say that I've read all 6 Austen novels!

I found myself applying the comments on philosophy and friendship to my own life. Professor Matherne's office hours were phenomenal. Please go.
