Take this class. Professor Sen is incredibly caring, an unbelievable lecturer (motivates the material better than anyone I've had), and writes fair and interesting assignments. Not the easiest class, but it's a time suck (less than 110 and 111) and is incredibly interesting and useful.
This is a great course that provides a solid foundation in the fundamentals of stochastic processes. I would highly recommend taking this course.

Background: The main background you need in this course is a solid understanding of STAT 110. Familiarity with linear algebra at the level of MATH 21B is sufficient. Multivariable calc isn't really that important, as the only thing you really use it for is taking partial derivatives, which you know how to take if you can do a regular derivative.

Lectures/Textbook: I found lectures easy to follow, as Professor Sen is a great lecturer. He follows the course textbook pretty closely, which is helpful. The textbook is the best resource to fully learn and understand the material in this class, and you should definitely read the textbook.

Homeworks: The problem sets were pretty manageable. I would recommend solving the problem sets on your own before going to OH or checking with a peer. They also aren't too time–consuming, and I think a student on average probably spends maybe 4–6 hours per problem set to do well.

Sections: Not really that helpful, as no one showed up (to the ones I went to at least, and the TFs would sometimes go really quickly over problems and concepts.

Exams: I found exams to be fair and reasonable, but they should be taken seriously, and you should do a lot of practice problems to prepare for them. The exams being 75 percent of the grade can be a bit scary, but I don't think that should disincentivize you from taking the course; if you get anxious about exams, you can do well on them by doing a lot of practice.

Almost everyone in the class did pretty well on the midterm (average was in mid 80s for both exams), so keep that in mind. Since I think there was intent to not make the midterms too difficult, the grade distribution on the midterms weren't really spread out and didn't really differentiate students based on gaps and understanding that much. For instance, on the second midterm the difference to getting the average and the third quartile could basically come down to getting one true/false question and perhaps a minor algebra error somewhere. But I think since the distribution of scores on the midterm is really narrow, I wouldn't worry too much about the midterms if you get around or a little below average, as I think the final exam can have more impact on your final grade.

I think there might be comments saying the final was really hard, and I expected the final to be quite difficult based on previous reviews of the course, but I think the final was quite reasonable, if you do sufficient practice with the material beforehand. This also might be due to the final testing stochastic calculus and Brownian motion, which can be the hardest topics in the course, but you can get really comfortable solving problems if you do lots of practice.

Here's how I prepared for the exams:

1) I would create a cheatsheet. This helped me review the specific formulation of definitions and theorems and their assumptions. This year, Professor Sen allowed us to bring one–page one–sided cheatsheet to midterm 1, a double–sided one page cheatsheet to midterm 2, and a three single–sided pages to the final. You can fit all the course content (definitions, theorems, main results) from the cheatsheet, and should still have some room left over to include other stuff.

2) I did the practice problems given to us. Professor Sen gave us about 6 practice problems for each of the midterm sand final. I did them under timed–conditions, but they're not really a practice exam, more of a collection of practice problems. I didn't feel as if the problems were all super relevant to exam problems, and some of them were a bit weird, but you should definitely do these problems.

3) I did section problems and textbook problems. The section problems also include problems from the textbook. You should be able to do all the section problems before the exam. I found the textbook problem actually really good practice for the exam, and I think doing a lot of them helped me do well especially on the second midterm and final exam. I think if you block off enough time before the exam, you can probably do a lot, if not all, of the non–R textbook problems in the chapters leading up to the exam. I actually found the textbook problems even more helpful then the midterm and final practice problems that were given to us.

Overall, my main advice would just to make sure you do a lot of practice before exams, especially the final, as this class at times really does become an extension of STAT 110–style problem–solving.

So overall great course, would highly recommend.
