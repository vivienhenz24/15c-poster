In communist East Germany, the secret police had something called Zersetzung, where instead of disappearing you they would just gradually wear down your sanity. QFT has a similar effect, if not in intention then certainly in application. This course is a millstone that just grinds and grinds away at your soul as you question your life choices as you expand out your eight gamma matrix trace for the fifth time that night in your Sisyphean delusion that you'll somehow be able to spot your mistake this time.

I unironically believe this course took a week off my lifespan. The class is brutally difficult, but in such a subtle way that you don't even realize it as it occurs and while your grades are fine, you begin to understand what Socrates meant when he said "I know that I know nothing." What makes QFT difficult is how you can hide your lack of conceptual understanding behind your ability to bash out absurd QED trace calculations. I still don't know what a particle is. Plus, there are more fine details than a Rolex watch which are critical to your calculations, which are never explained in lecture or the textbook, and rather you have to divine them by channeling the spirit of Julian Schwinger. By the time renormalization comes around, you end up leaving lecture more confused than you came in, and that's certainly not a slight on Schwartz. It's just a really, really hard class that sucks up all your time. QFT is truly a mess, a Frankenstein sewn together from classical field theory, quantum scattering theory, special relativity, and a Russian nesting doll of harmonic oscillators.

I started evaluating matrix elements in my dreams. It will happen to you to.

Schwartz is a great professor. Truly, I think the most knowledgable professor I've had thus far at Harvard. For all the joking in this review aside, he has been so helpful in my trying to parse what on earth is going on. I especially appreciate how he reassures us that it's okay to not feel totally comfortable with the material, and that even he, practically the world's expert on QFT, doesn't feel comfortable about everything. He also infuses much needed lightness into lectures sometimes, like his side comments on Einstein's and Schrdinger's "unusual" home lives.

He wrote the textbook, which is both a blessing and a curse: it sticks closely to lecture, but if his presentation in lecture wasn't clear, the book will likely be similar. He also lectures at speed 1 – epsilon and I'm always amazed how he manages to write equations on the blackboard and lecture faster than my ability to copy down the equation from three lines ago. You have to be on your A game every single lecture.

The teaching staff is phenomenal. Rui Zhe Lee deserves special commendation. Rui would regularly over stay his office hours by hours, past midnight, to help us, while in the thick of grad school applications. Hats off.

I'm writing this close to 1 am after finishing the take home final, which I spent 30 of the past 36 hours working on. I feel a certain sadness, though, upon this course's conclusion. This was my favorite course I've taken at Harvard by far. I've always wanted to study QFT and particle physics, and I found 253 to be an incredibly fulfilling way to do so.
Matt Schwartz has a very distinctive lecturing style. He will breeze through calculations in class (because those are done out, correctly, in the book, which he will often refer you to) but then slow down to emphasize the main idea or main tool that the calculation gives you.
Having the book open during lecture is very helpful, and reading beforehand (I know, wishful thinking, when you are spending 20 hours a week doing PSets) would probably be even better, because then you would be equipped to slow down Schwartz by asking questions.
Some advice that I got about QFT is to find a group of people and talk about what is going on, because it is very easy to get lost in the math, do the calculations blindfolded, and not understand at all what you are calculating or why and of the formulas you are pulling from the textbook work.

Overall, Schwartz's QFT is good, but a little too fast and a little too much, so pace yourself and you'll make it out the other side!

The syllabus for this class is largely accurate. To succeed in this class, all you really need is a good understanding of quantum mechanics (Physics 143a + 143b at Harvard undergrad for example) and a willingness to work hard. However, depending on how much other physics knowledge you have upon taking this class, the exact amount of work required might range from very substantial to semester–encapsuling. This course is indeed its own prerequisite in that after one semester, I feel like I could retake Physics 253a and get as much if not more out of the second pass as the first one. Unlike (say) general relativity, which was largely developed by a singular figure and can be taught in a relatively linear fashion, quantum theory was built out of the collective genius of dozens of incredible minds over decades and decades, and you are as a result dropped in the middle of this beautiful mess and have to find your way around in the dark from basically day one. You'll be expected to not only learn but use a kitchen sink of mathematical formalism that sometimes has very little obvious connection to observable things (and what is even observable is sometimes hard to identify), and while learning the formalism and physics might be hard, it's using that that will take the vast majority of your time. The calculation of observables from quantum field theory consists of lines and lines of intricate mathematical bash, and problem sets will consist of this week after week after week until you feel like you're going crazy because you just spent eight hours calculating one cross section which consists of one part of one of three similarly difficult problems, and you now have to use this result to obtain incredibly nontrivial but often very profound and interesting physical results that make you step back and think that maybe those eight hours were worth it after all. All this to say is that this course is hard. It's both the hardest and most time–consuming course I have taken at Harvard thus far. You really will feel like you are going crazy sometimes, but I feel like it was all worth it.

A big reason why it was worth it was because of the person teaching it. Professor Schwartz literally wrote the book on quantum field theory, and it shows. His lectures are incredibly clear and concise, and despite the difficulty of the material and the fact that many days you'll leave the classroom after lecture and not know what you were just taught, you will get the impression that no one could have taught it better than Schwartz. He is very cognizant and understanding about everyone's situation and is incredibly supportive of everyone in the classhe often says after going through a difficult calculation or derivation that it's ok not to understand it on the first pass, and that was very comforting for me, who very often did not understand a lick of what he had just written down. I would however not recommend taking handwritten notes in lecture, because he goes too fast and makes too many errors on the board for it to be all that usefulinstead, closely follow his textbook during lectures, which he follows pretty linearly. In this way, you'll get a sense for what was not covered in lecture and should be reviewed, and where in a derivation he might have called a page of calculations "some algebra."

In terms of the actual content we do learn, Schwartz emphasizes the particle physics aspect of the course, which involves a lot of Feyman diagram evaluations and cross section calculations. I found this approach to be tedious but often illuminating, and Schwartz managed to tie the material to experiment a lot more than expected which was incredibly rewarding. The content is truly fascinatingas cliche as it sounds, this and general relativity are the two subjects where I've felt the closest to "probing the fundamentals of the universe." I believe that anyone who really wants to take this course and has the sufficient quantum mechanics background should go for it. It might take up your entire semester, and there may be many consecutive late nights spent pulling your hair out over endless calculations where you regret taking QFT, but I promise that at the end of the day you'll be glad you went through the experience. I can't wait for 253b.

The same general pieces of advice for every hard physics course apply to this one: try your best not to get behind, go to as many office hours as possible, and find a study group quickly and make sure you like the people you study with because you'll be spending more time with them this semester than potentially any of your other friends. While you technically only need quantum mechanics and a good comfort with the math, other courses I found to benefit from taking for the field theory intuition were Physics 151, 153, and 210 (210 also helps a ton for the comfort you obtain with einstein summation notation that helps with feyman diagram bash).
