The material in this class is interesting but it is also extremely dry. Most students are not engaged in the lecture which can create an awkward feeling when the professor opens up for questions. The lectures themselves are disorganized and hard to follow, which hampers engagement. However, the class was a good review of intermediate economic concepts and did teach me quite a bit about how the EU functions and makes decisions. The midterm paper was really fun to write.

Without weekly problem sets or response papers, it was hard to apply what I learned on a week–to–week basis. Section felt like a rehash of lecture and was not engaging. I started my final paper relatively early, but I wrote about a topic that was only tangentially related to lecture materials, so it felt difficult to integrate.

Overall, decent elective with some gem–like qualities, but there are better Econ courses out there.
Great class! Not a lot of work at all. The final paper looks daunting, but you have 3–4 months to complete it. I would recommend coming into the course with a topic idea, or at least have one by the end of week 1 so that you can start early.
Engage with Professor Kotz as much as possible in this course. Ask him for feedback about papers through office hours and ask him questions about the course. He is truly one of the kindest and friendliest professors in the Econ department (and a voracious reader). The course as a whole is pretty you get out of it what you put into it because its primarily based around two papers, but overall a fascinating course to take.
If you have any interest in the EU, I highly, highly recommend this course. It provides a great overview into the mechanisms of the EU (various policy issues, economic tools, relationships between member states) as well as current events. Professor Kotz is an excellent lecturer, genuinely passionate about the subject and provides a lot of support. The assignments are fairly straightforward and workload was never really a concern. Would recommend starting your term paper early. I really enjoyed this class and this was definitely the best Econ elective I've taken at Harvard.
This is a great class! You should take it if you're interested in European economics and macroeconomics at all. However, I wouldn't take it as a sophomore. Most students are juniors and seniors, so there is much less direction given on class assignments. There also isn't really as much of a class community because of its small size, so most of the work seems to be independently done. There are only two major assignments, the midterm paper and final paper. I went to office hours about my midterm paper and took all of the professor's feedback into account and still got a B+, which was a bit confusing and much of the feedback I received seemed to ignore the fact that we had a 6 page word limit, so I couldn't expand on things as much as he wanted me to. I didn't give myself enough time to write the final paper, and while the process of writing it was actually really interesting and I learned a lot about my topic, I didn't have enough time to ask questions about what is expected in the paper, so I'm anticipating a pretty average score (haven't received it back yet). If I had been a junior or senior, I probably would have felt comfortable with the little amount of direction in the class, and even though I don't regret taking the class as a sophomore because it is overall a pretty light workload, I do think I would have been able to perform better had I taken more economics classes and felt more comfortable with academic/economics style writing.
This is a great course if you want to learn more about the concept of economic integration especially within the context of Europe. Professor Kotz is an incredible lecturer and he has unparalleled experience in the field. One piece of advice would be to go to office hours whenever you have any questions regarding your essays and start the term paper early!
