If you are generally comfortable with taking classes out of sequence, PLEASE take this course instead of PHYS 143a. I took this class with minimal physics experience (15b and 181) and still found it very doable. The most important prerequisite is not any specific physics knowledge, but comfort with Linear Algebra (although it is reviewed fairly thoroughly in the first few weeks) and series manipulation.

The major advantage to taking this class is a view of quantum mechanics that goes much deeper into ideas of symmetry and conservation, and gives you tools to think about systems without needing to compute all the Gaussians. If you're curious about how theories in physics are constructed, you should take this class!

The exams are super reasonable given the PSETS, and the PSETS themselves often need to nice insights/solidify key concepts in the lecture material. Eslam is a great lecturer, and the notes are also super accessible. If you are a first–year or sophomore reading this, please don't be intimidated by the label of this course as a "graduate course" the pace might be slightly faster, but the difficulty of the material is fair, and assessments are graded generously.
This class is very difficult, but rewarding. I took it as a Sophomore after taking 143 as a Freshman, and it was quite difficult for me, but not undoable.
I think 251a is a great class and a very solid alternative to 143a for someone who wants a more rigorous approach to QM. Eslam is a great lecturer(though he can sometimes go a bit too quickly), and writes very good lecture notes as well so the material of the course is pretty digestible and PSETs are overall reasonable. My only real takeaway is that the exams in the course while generally reasonable were a bit poorly structured(in a partial attempt to reduce diffuclty) with our midterm(a harder exam than the final) being effectively dropped from the grade (despite this not being listed on syllabus) which was a bit strange but generally beneficial for students. Nevertheless though my experience through 251 was very positive and would recommend anyone with a serious interest in physics to take this course over 143a, or even for those who have already taken 143a but want a deeper understanding of QM.
It was very hard but learned a lot.
Overall wonderful course, it teaches all the important parts of quantum mechanics and does it very rigorously. The problem sets are interesting and challenging, and help greatly to test your understanding.

The teaching is more or less wonderful, and I think the course itself could be improved by making the problem sets test your understanding of concepts (e. through true/false) a little deeper.

The course assignments were challenging and fun to work with, and each had applications, was interesting on its own, or served to solidify concepts taught in class. Overall well done! The sets were challenging in the start but got much much easier towards the end.

I learned a lot about different formulations of QM that were all very exciting, and am happy having learned the variational and perturbative methods. It drastically improved my knowledge in quantum and I am very satisfied. For anyone willing to take on a challenging course taking about 10–12 hours a week, this is amazing. Dont buy the textbooks: they are extremely expensive and all material is covered in class.
Start problem sets early, and start studying for exams early.
Read lecture notes or textbook ahead of lecture if possible. It is much easier to follow lecture that way and more will be gained from it.
Prof. Khalaf was an excellent teacher and he was great at explaining an intuitive way to understand quantum mechanics. He also really knows his stuff and is very well organized coming into lecture. We covered a lot and the order in which we covered material made sense: everything blended together by the end of lecture. The homework can be tough sometimes, but it usually doable and you also have office hours with help. Although, since this is a grad level class there are few office hours, so I would make sure I can attend them weekly (not have other weekly commitments during the time). You gain a much deeper understanding of QM than after Phys 143a. Make sure you know linear algebra and some multivariable calculus and you should be fine with the math. Also Prof. Khalaf's lecture notes are GREAT, and usually enough to get ready for the exams. The exams include 2 long problems and one problem with many true/false questions. The long ones are usually doable and he tried to make them easier since the last time he taught the class the students complained (loved that he incorporated feedback, and also cared about it!), but the true/false are definitely challenging. In a good way though since they test deep understanding of material, which you gain by making sure you understand the lecture notes really well, since they include a lot of talk about why things work the way they do. Great class overall! Really loved it and learned a lot! Feel so much more comfortable now with quantum mechanics, and am so much more fascinated by it than I was before.
Physics 251 is a great class, especially is Prof Khalaf is teaching it! Though the pacing is pretty quick, the lecture notes are incredible and ensure that you have both a rigorous mathematical and conceptual understanding of the material. This class is great for anyone interested in condensed matter, or learning quantum mechanics more rigorously. I loved it!
TAKE THIS CLASS INSTEAD OF 143A!!!!!! Personally, I always felt that the 143a / Griffiths approach to quantum was extremely difficult to understand and takes way too long to get to the point (which is the conclusion that the Hilbert space is a model of quantum mechanics). 251a roughly follows Sakurai, and it begins with the Hilbert space hypothesis and builds from there. I would not say it is too much more difficult than the 143a approach, just different –– 251a is dependent on linalg and matrices, while 143a is mostly integrals.

The class overall is extremely well taught. Eslam's lecture notes are gold, and Guan our TF was really helpful and willing to answer questions via email at 2 am in the morning. The workload is also very manageable –– most people completed the psets in one day. Overall a pretty fantastic intro to quantum.
