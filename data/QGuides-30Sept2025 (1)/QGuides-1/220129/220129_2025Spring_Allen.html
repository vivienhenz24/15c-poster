Lowkey a G.E.M.

The class is divided up into three parts, which is perhaps not well–reflected on the syllabus: 1) old head political and economic philosopher readings (Keynes, Constant, Friedman, etc.) –– this was a nice intro and not too difficult, though the readings were intensive. 2) You read Allen's book Justice by Means of Democracy and understand her value framework. 3) you break up into teams of 3–4 and are assigned an interviewee who's doing work in a specific policy domain (education, tech, democracy, etc.) Each group conducts a 25min interview, 2 interviews a class, for roughly the last 1/3 of the course.
Course felt fairly easy and reading load was never too bad but we didn't receive feedback for really anything throughout the semester (course journals, group policymaker interview, etc.) so there's no way to really know how you are doing in the class until you get your grade.

This class is a good sampling of 20th–century political theory and then seeing how that could be applied to highly relevant policy issues. I wouldn't take this as a political theory class, per se, as it very quickly surveys many different thinkers and doesn't consistently think about how to push back against them. For example, the class seems to take Justice by Means of Democracy for granted and not engage as much with legitimate critiques, in my opinion. There were very few assignments throughout the semester, which I think led to course engagement lagging. I was one of the few people in my section who did the reading consistently, which was quite frustrating, as there was not that much to begin with.

Despite these issues, I loved Professor Allen's teaching style, and she has a real talent for framing complicated issues in fairly intuitive ways. The most exciting aspect of the class was the interviews for me, as it is rare you get to here from so many experts on so many different topics.

Decent course.

A lot of the content lives in a somewhat ideal plane, and it is hard to get a sense of how to translate the principles into the real world. Prof. Allen aims to dedicate the second half of the course to this by inviting interviewees who embody different aspects of power sharing liberalism, which I found to be a very nice idea! Yet Prof. Allen largely steered away from addressing this aspect herself concretely, instead pushing students to think about this for themselves. This approach has its benefits and shortcomings. All interviews were also conducted by students, which was a good experience, but as far as developing a strong picture of real–world power sharing liberalism, left it feeling a little disjointed.

If you are comfortable with a course where you can think about a philosophical paradigm, and then independently try to apply it to the world yourself, you will like this class a lot. If you want to more formally think about applications of power–sharing liberalism to current society, this may not scratch that itch. Also recommend briefly looking over her book "Justice by Means of a Democracy" –– the course is based upon it, and so if you like that style of philosophy, this course is largely a deep dive into the book, with the author, so will be great for you!

Note: this class in the past has been taught combined with HKS and the college, but this year they were separate courses. So be sure to check that before enrolling. I think it would have been nice for them to be combined, but you will have to check how it is offered year–of.

I think one's interest in the topics covered in this class varies. Some of my peers that I talked to really enjoyed what was talked about in the course, but I found that I enjoyed this course notably less than I had hoped. Perhaps it's just personal preference of topics covered or the manner in which they're covered, but I wouldn't take this course over another course that I'd possibly be interested in. However, if Justice by Means of Democracy is something that speaks to you, definitely give it a shot! It's not a difficult course, but it does require some work (such as readings) to stay caught up with the foundations being built and the overarching message being sent, especially since a lot of what is talked about in the end is quite novel.
To future students taking FAS–GOV 1041, be prepared for a challenging yet rewarding experience. The course dives deep into complex political theories, so its important to stay engaged with the readings and participate in class discussions. The professor leads thought–provoking debates that encourage critical thinking, and the material is relevant to both historical and contemporary issues. Make sure to think critically about how justice and democracy intersect and how different political systems approach these ideas. Also, dont hesitate to ask questions and connect the course content to real–world eventsitll make the concepts much more relatable and meaningful.
