What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

Comments
This class is a good introduction to waves (light, sound, etc.), however it is absolutely not a replacement for Physics 15c. To get the most out of this class, you should be very comfortable with calculus, mechanics, and some linear algebra. Classes sometimes get bogged down by equations and unfamiliar math, however (if you have a solid physics/math background) you will walk away with some intuition of what is going on. Problem sets are very light; all of the members of the class would go to Physics night and work on the problem together with Prof. Georgi there to help us if we had any questions.

I would recommend this class to students who have a very comfortable background in the natural sciences and are looking for a laid–back introduction to their time at Harvard. Also––you get to meet Prof. Georgi, a very sweet faculty member :), in a small group setting. If you are a prospective physics/STEM student, though, it may be advisable to not pick a freshman seminar and instead take a more common class (like Phys 15a or Chem 10) as it will probably be more beneficial for you down the road.
This class is very very advanced. You will almosy certainly pass, but you will probably learn nothing and always be confused.
This is a very hard course. It is hard to learn from it without background, and do not let it take a back seat; it may be a seminar, but it is very difficult and deserves as much attention as another course. That being said, it is a very interesting course and it is worth the effort.
Professor Georgi is a wonderful person that I really enjoyed being around this semester. The course content is DIFFICULT, but if you can gain some understanding of the content it's incredibly rewarding. Often the psets were much more accessible than the lectures, and I found much of my learning occurred there. Also, the demonstrations in the class were great.

The class is simply too difficult. I didn't have an understanding of linear algebra or multivariable calculus before entering the course, and Georgi mostly expected us to intuit these topics. I wish I could retake this course in a year or two, with more of a math/physics background. Also, a lot of the basic topics (such as wave functions) were never given good definitions in class and as such I had to self–teach quite a bit of the fundamental material.

At least calc II and calculus–based mechanics (AP Physics C Mech) are required to understand this course. This was my background, and I had to do lots of self–teaching in order to keep up. By the end of the course the lectures were incomprehensible to me. Realistically I think a person should have both linear algebra and multivariable calculus under their belt before taking this class, and probably also an equivalent of AP Physics C Electricity and Magnetism.

I learned so much in this course. It was a real trial by fire for physics at Harvard, but doing that in an environment with no risk (i.e. a letter grade) was nice. I feel like I didn't learn a lot of the content from the course, but I most significantly took away a new way of thinking about physics.

You will see some FANTASTIC demonstrations in the Science Center. Now I can say for the rest of my life that I've performed the double slit experiment!
Take it but be prepared that it is not a walk in the park as seminars go
