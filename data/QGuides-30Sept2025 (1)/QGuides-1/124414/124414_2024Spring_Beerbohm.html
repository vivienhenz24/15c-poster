It is clear that Avishay thought that, order to work at Harvard, he needed to be a 20th–century lecturer. Avishay took his role far too harshly and strictly and decided that his students needed to be graded as if they were PhD students. He chose clear favorites and least favorites, and he made it obvious, both in the classroom and on assignments, the way he felt about you. Regardless of the effort I put in, Avishay decided he did not like me and graded me as such. I have never felt so discouraged and helpless in a course. Prof. Beerrbohm is the kindest person, but you will be forced to face the wrath of Avishay instead. Absolutely terrible. Trust me.

I really enjoyed this class and always felt excited to attend every week. Professor Beerbohm brings incredible energy and enthusiasm and is an amazing individual to learn from.
This class is very interesting and a great introduction to political theory. You definitely don't need any background in the topics to be successful in this course. Professor Beerbohm is the highlight of this course. He always brought good vibes and kept the class moving which can be difficult for a 2 hour class. The class is laid out well and is easy to understand especially for someone like me who had little to know background in these topics. The readings were a bit heavy but definitely skimable if needed. The essays were not very difficult and the P–sets relied on the readings and were not too difficult either.
This was one of the most challenging courses Ive taken at Harvard, but Professor Beerbohm is truly the most knowledgeable person fit to teach this class.
Not a theory person, but I enjoyed this course. I punted the theory requirement until my very last semester but I ended up having a good time with it. Eric makes class fun and engaging.
Political theory obviously has limited good class options, but this class is pretty good. Lectures are pretty interesting, even with the 2 hour length, and Professor Beerbohm is always enthusiastic about the course content. This class is a great place to debate competing opinions and political philosophies, openly and without fear. The essays arent bad, but I dont think the peer review system is particularly helpful, and the final paper feedback wasnt very informative either. Youre also not really shown how to write a concise political theory paper, particularly since a lot of the readings are very wordy and repetitive. But I think overall, this class helps you think about a variety of ways of seeing the world, and it does so without a crazy amount of reading or writing.
Take this class! It's very chill with minimal work, besides readings. The readings are comprehensive and interesting. At times they can be long, but they're worth doing. Professor Beerbohm is very nice and I encourage students to go to his office hours just to talk. He's insightful and loves learning from his students.
If you are considering Gov but haven't taken a class yet, do NOT take this as your introduction to the department because unless you love political theory, you will not enjoy this class. Also, if after the first few readings you find yourself questioning whether you want to drop the class or not, don't be afraid to do so. The professor is amazing, it's all about whether you can handle the material.
Professor Beerbohm is such a kind and interesting professor. He's also clearly very busy, and doesn't have the most consistent office hours (eg, sometimes he misses OH he sets, or doesn't do a great job communicating if he moves them). However, this class covers some incredibly interesting material related to democracies at large. Keep in mind that the course, in this iteration, didn't include a lot of canonical works – no Plato/Hobbes/etc. There were times when the selection felt a bit Harvard–centric, though I imagine writers like Nozick or Rawls would be included in any theoretical course that covers modern writers.

Be warned – there is a lot of reading for this class. Even if it doesn't look bad on the syllabus, 30–40 pages of philosophical writing is dense. Be prepared to spend several hours on the reading each week, and try not to stuff it all in the day before class like I did. If you don't do the reading, the class will be useless, as the lectures primarily explain/connect the readings.
Excellent course to develop your thinking, even if you're not going to study politics or philosophy in the future. It offers a great overview of contemporary philosophy in the English–speaking world.
It really pushes your thinking!
Future students, you're in for a treat with Eric leading this class! His presence truly enriches the learning experience. While the readings might leave you scratching your head at times, don't let that deter you. Embrace the challenge, and you'll find the journey through this class to be incredibly rewarding. Plus, having Eric as your guide makes it all the more enjoyable. So, dive in, engage with the material, and savor every moment of this enriching academic adventure!
GOV 10 is a really pleasant introduction to political philosophy. The readings can be heavy, but they do all have a purpose and they are pretty recent (no slogging through the ancient canon), so I would strongly recommend working through them each week. The assignments are fair and not too long (the problem sets even have a short word limit, which forces you to trim off the excess in your arguments).
This class leans more towards philosophy while incorporating real–world issues, like poverty, injustice, and inequality. The readings are time–consuming, however you do not have to read all of them in detail. If you do the readings it will make it easier for you to participate in class discussion.
