Overall: GENED 1110 is a course in ancient Greek and Roman mythology. It uses the study of mythology to explore disturbing themes such as parricide, cannibalism, incest, misogyny, and adultery, as well as to understand the cultures and connections in the Mediterranean world in the centuries before and after the Common Era. The mythographic tools used in this course are tied to a variety of fields including history, psychology, philosophy, etymology, and anthropology. You will also learn to identify Classical gods and myths in pottery and sculptures!

Professor: Rachel is an enthusiastic and engaging professor. She discusses touchy themes in a light–hearted yet considerate manner. She is great at breaking down myths in more modern gen z/millennial vocabulary while still retaining depth and formality.

Textbooks: All readings are freely provided through Canvas, and are largely excerpts. The How We Read analysis report though requires you to find a source on Hollis.

Topics covered (including but not limited to): Hesiods Theogony, birth and overthrowing of primordials/titans/gods; Hesiods Works and Days, Ages of Man, Prometheus/Pandora, kalon kakon; foundation myths for cities including Thebes (Cadmus/Europa) and Rome (Aeneas/Romulus/Remus), hetero/autochthony, Ovids Metamorphoses; Pseudo–apollodorus, Theseus/Pasiphae/Minos/Ariadne/Minotaur/Daedalus and associated Cretan mythology; Euhemerism, historical rationalism; Apuleis The Golden Ass, Cupid and Psyche, Aphrodite, love stories from Ovid; indigenous Roman mythology and political associations, syncretism, aetiology; Structuralism, Levi–Strauss, Oedipus, Homeric Hymn to Demeter; Jason/Medea/Argonautica; Perseus/Graiai/Hyperboreans/Medusa myth, Terrae Incognitae, Medusa in ancient and modern times; Homers Iliad, full Trojan War from build up to return home, the Trojan Horse and Achilles heel are not in the Iliad, Homers Odyssey and Virgils Aeneid, nostos and xenia; Persian Wars/Aeschylus/Sophocles/Euripides/Peloponnesian War; Heracles; Psychoanalysis as a way of reading myth; Euripedes Bacchae and Dionysus; Hades and his (lack of) mythology; Campbell Archetypes, katabasis, and the Monomyth.

Course structure notes:
No psets. Short weekly discussion posts. Lecture attendance is required (enforced by PollEverywhere questions) but also extremely informative about the myths and How We Read lenses. Required sections discussing readings, but also include a fair bit of instruction. Five short Canvas quizzes, book report, How We Read analysis report, and a final.
