Moran is retiring so this probably won't apply, but lecture tends to be a little hard to follow. The Philosophical Investigations are NOT easy, and neither is the Blue Book. Read parts before you start the course it will make the semester much easier and your takeaways from the course much greater. The course states that the pre–requisite is one course in philosophy: unless you are up for a real challenge (both in terms of the difficulty of the text and the slightly hands–off pedagogy of upper–level courses), do not take this without some good background in analytic philosophy.

Unfortunately this class probably wont get offered again since Professor Moran is leaving, but it was really excellent! Wittgenstein is such a fascinating and important figure, and this class did a great job giving a nuanced view of his thinking. Morans lectures were super thorough and methodical, Emily did a great job in section, and the selection of readings was excellent. It was a bit more challenging and work–heavy class, but extremely rewarding.
This class is probably not the first philosophy class you want to be taking. Even experienced readers were struggling with Wittgensteins commentaries, but the intellectual challenge was well worth the squeeze. You really do become much more skeptical about the logistics of human language and of the mind – I found myself, many times throughout the class, realising huh, I never thought of that phenomena. I also found myself outside of class, remarking that Wittgenstein probably wouldve thought this way, or perceiving the ideas that Wittgenstein was discussing in our readings. Readings are quite dense, but it nice to get some abstraction away from the world.

The grading does seem to be quite harsh though. Section was more useful for me in terms of discussing my doubts about the content, versus lectures where it was often monotonous and hard to focus. Moran is retiring this semester, and I hear that this class will not be offered again, so Im grateful to have been able to have taken this class.
Everyone, everyone, everyone should take this course before they graduate. Wittgenstein's relevance to your thinking and your doing will be pressed upon you no matter your background.
Look, Moran is retiring, so this class is unlikely to be taught again. I'd just say that this class involves a willingness to sit through tedious lectures.
