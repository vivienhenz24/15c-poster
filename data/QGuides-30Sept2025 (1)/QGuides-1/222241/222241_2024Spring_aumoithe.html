What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

Comments
The readings for this course were great. They were very informative and taught me the history of public health.

However,

In terms of the skills and enthusiasm for history this course was meant to teach, this course was just plain bad. My writing didn’t change at all, and I learned more about historical research in my freshman year of high school than this class. I’ve heard from at least two classmates that they no longer plan to pursue the history concentration at all because of their experience in this course. While I’m sticking with history, I can’t blame them. Grading was inconsistent at best. TF would say to change one thing in my draft and then the professor would take points off for the change and suggest what I had originally done. It took 5 days to get our work back (we were expected to return peer reviews within about 48 hours of receiving our classmates’ work and we weren’t getting paid to teach and grade). Seminar was cookie cutter and predictable to the point of boredom. Teaching staff did not seem like they were interested in our enthusiasm for the subject at all. Maybe it was the 3 hour evening seminar, but very few people wanted to be there. After talking with friends in other 97 seminars, it was clear that our class was an exception. All other seminars returned papers directly after tutorial, especially the final paper which we had to wait until FRIDAY to receive comments on. I wouldn’t be surprised if many of my classmates had their grades negatively impacted by word count limits on papers because of footnotes (no other history class I’ve taken in my life has counted footnotes in the word count, and this was never communicated to the class. I only found out after a frantic Wednesday night text from a classmate). I can wholeheartedly say this was my least favorite class at Harvard
This class approaches an interesting sub–field of history through a discussion based seminar approach. Go into the class realizing that there will be a good amount of work throughout the semester and take the time to seek out ways to make the course go more smoothly. Talk to the course librarians, meet with the professor, and start your essays early and you will both enjoy the course and take a lot out of it.
Take this class, but don't be limited by the historical scope of the topic, use it to inspire the historical work you want to uncover or expouse, and use your ideas for future endeavors. This is as much a course as it is a stepping stone to your own work and research.
1. The history of public health is a very interesting topic on its own. However I don't see it as a good fit for a Hist97 class. The scope of the history of public health is very narrow compared to other subfields of history, and oftentimes I felt constrained by the public health focus where (as we mentioned in class) the historiographical cannon is just not as rich or complete as other topics. Be prepared to feel limited by the public health focus if you do decide to take the class.

2. The teaching on its own was good in seminars, but Professor Aumoithe at times felt hard to connect to. Whereas other professors in the department oftentimes take the time to get to know each student even in a larger lecture course, I found that Professor Aumoithe made little–to–no effort to get to know our class despite its small size, making the class feel more impersonal than I had hoped (I had heard from prior concentrators that they had developed a strong relationship with their professor as a result of taking 97. But talking with members of our class, I don't feel that many people had this experience). I hope that this changes in the future as if it did, this could be an excellent class.
This tutorial was definitely challenging, but incredibly rewarding. This class allowed me to explore another side of history that I had never engaged with before, and I'm so happy that I was given that opportunity. The grading is tough, but fair, and the participation grades at the end of the semester should balance out any less favorable grades on assignments.
