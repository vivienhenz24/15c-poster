I rate this class 6/10. Solid history class, but I want to make a huge caveat: You’ll only get one, biased viewpoint on the history of Black lawmaking in this class. The instructor has very left–leaning ideals, and so do the students. The only lawyers that were given case studies were far left–leaning. They were upheld as martyrs. There was no political diversity in this course, which made for a very boring and intellectually dull experience. There was no room for debate. Not all Black lawyers are liberal. I would’ve loved to learn more about conservative Black lawyers and their impacts. Liberals don’t have all the answers. Students self–select into this class, so beware of the class discussions. They’re very left–leaning and left no room for critiques of leftist/liberal ideals. I mentioned representation of conservative Black lawyers in class once and I got shunned. This class has no room for academic freedom. Discussions were long and monotonous and repetitive. It’s the same 11 “future lawyers/activists” and IOP kids spewing their half–baked thoughts on inequality and Black rights—when 80% of the class wasn’t even Black. One day we wasted 45 minutes discussing what pronouns we should use for Pauli Murray. I believe in gender representation/rights, but minuscule details like that are not important for understanding the depth of her impact. No one paid attention in class discussions. Maybe one or two students. Everyone else just did work on their laptops. Given the self–selection into this class, be prepared to meet and be irritated by the obnoxious social justice warriors. One student would never fail to remind us that she’s doing research at Radcliffe and has swipe access to all the buildings. That’s just the nature of people who’d be interested in this class—obnoxious people who think they know the answer to everything. In–class discussions and discussion posts were absolutely useless. Other than that, it’s a solid history course. It’s unique in that you get full access to Schlesinger library. Something that you should take advantage of during your time here — especially if you’re a woman. I enjoyed the research process. Professor Eatmon is a solid professor and accessible with writing/research assistance. She’s not the most organized though. She changed the final assignment halfway into the course, which was incredibly frustrating and unprofessional. We were originally given the option to record a podcast, which attracted a lot of people to the course—including myself. After the add/drop deadline. she changed the final assignment to only having the option of writing a 3,000 word paper….Baiting people into a class with a fun final project—just to change it at the last minute—is not cool. I didn’t appreciate that at all, and future students should beware of this behavior. The archival activities were interesting and unique. Outside of that, I wouldn’t recommend this course. Find another way to engage with the library archives and do research on campus.
It’s a wonderful course, just be mindful that you may need to have a lot of support from her if you want to do well and I would suggest that you go to the writing center 3 weeks before an assignment is due and bring it back to her for feedback
When all you talk about in class is your activist work on the topic of the week, we all know you didn't do the reading. Please stop.
A great class to fulfill your seminar requirement! Professor Eatmon is fantastic and you will learn a lot about Black women’s history and archival research.
This class has genuinely been one of my favorite classes this semester (though it does seem to draw a biased sample of people who are already passionate about the subject matter). The course content is super interesting and the opportunity to do archival work in Schlesinger Library IN CLASS is unparalleled. It is newer though and it isn't an easy class by any means; you must do the readings, weekly discussion posts, and the penalty for missing a discussion post will do a number on your grade. Dr. Eatmon is kind but also extensions are not guaranteed so you have to be on top of it. Overall, rigorous but so so worth it.
