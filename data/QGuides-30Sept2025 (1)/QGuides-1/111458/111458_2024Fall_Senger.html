Math concentrators: here is the lore passed down from the ages about Harvards department. Courses are not standardized like they are in other departments (chem or stat). The courses you take are going to have an insanely high variance if you do not condition on the instructor. That being said, this class is basically an algebraic topology class masquerading as a topology class, so dont let that introduction to part fool you. This class requires some serious mathematical maturity and if you dont have it youre going to develop it.

All that said, I cant say that senger was the reason the class forces you to develop maturity. Often times it is just the material itself. The problems from munkres on each homework were superb. I learned a lot from doing them. But the grading for this class was extremely bizarre. At first things were very similar to other math department classes but over the course of the class it got much worse. Points were taken off for extremely minor things like notation and things being hard to read (even though I latex all of my PSETs like what) luckily near the end I think the CAs stopped doing this in favor of being fast . Mind you I dont have a problem with a hard math class and many bonus points were allocated to us but these often felt nitpicky and strange.

In terms of the exams; my god they were insane. I felt like the midterm (average around 60%) was not super difficult but the grading sure was. I often felt like many points were taken off for proofs with the right idea and that things were extremely nitpicky once again here. Many of the people that I spoke to felt as though their understanding of the material was not reflected well by the exams as a whole. Additionally, post–final, I think that the last exam was not preceded by relevant homework problems or things in class. It was also extremely hard.

As for senger, hes an extremely nice guy and a very tough teacher. He grades hard and expects 100% rigor on every exam. We are begging for a curve. But the class was well organized and I thought every PSET he wrote was very fun and informative, I wish that they were relevant to problems on our exams. Senger also gives generous extensions and is pretty concerned with helping you get through everything time wise.

All in all, you know everything for this class was in place for it to be perfect: it was extremely organized; it had an excellent lecturer; homeworks were fun and engaging. But something fell apart along the way, I cant help but think that there was something behind the scenes that went wrong. Why was the grading so inconsisten? Why were the homeworks not good preparation for the exam? I dont really know. I think the class needed to chill out at some point but it ramped up like crazy over the semester. But its topology you have to take it right? Maybe consider 132 first and definitely dont let this be one of your first upper division math classes, I think sometimes that is ok if you know the teacher will be relaxed and focus less on alg top and let you handwave a little more, but otherwise come in expecting this to be rough. It was much harder than stat 110, 111 or any other stem class Ive taken.

Nonetheless, I think it should be taken by every math concentrator even 55ers. There is so much more detail here than you can get in that class and the math is overwhelmingly beautiful. Its like reading Ulysses, after it you can be like man, what an accomplishment just to have done it. But sometimes I feel like I sacrificed my semester for this class and this class alone.
This course is a great introduction to point–set and algebraic topology. The course is divided into two halves, the first half being on point–set topology, and the second half on algebraic topology, with the two being separated by the midterm. The first half of the class does a great job of setting up the language of topology and helps you work with abstract spaces and their properties. This part of the course is more straightforward, with many of the proofs simply involving manipulating the definitions. The second part of the course introduces algebraic topology and the fundamental group, which is where the course becomes super interesting, but also much more difficult. This part of the course involves a lot more clever spatial reasoning and visual arguments, and is therefore much less straightforward. The Munkres textbook is excellent. If you are concerned about not having any familiarity with group theory coming into this course, just know that the book does a great job of introducing as much as you will ever need to know.
