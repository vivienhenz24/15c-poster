All math concentrators should take 123. The material at the start is a little dry, but Galois theory is such a deep and beautiful subject it's worth all the maximal ideals you have to put up with at the start of the course. Just be sure you really know group theory! My impression is that most of the course was from 122, with a few 55ers.

Professor Kisin is a fine lecturer. He's not the clearest lecturer in the department and sometimes he makes strange notation choices, but on the whole I never felt like lecture was too hard to follow. Just be aware that it's often his style to state a result, derive consequences from the result, and only then go back to prove the statement. It can make the line between remark and proof a little blurry, but once you pick up on it it's not a big deal. Also, there were only 4 homeworks all semester and when Prof. Kisin says the exams should be completely straightforward if you do the homework, that's pretty much his way of telling you the exams will mostly be homework questions. Still, I feel like I learned a lot in the course, and I'm happy I took it.
Prof Kisin is so great! And the TFs were amazing. I love the subject matter too!
The course was organized in a pretty strange way. We spent two or three weeks on tensor products and we never used them again (and they are not on the syllabus). Anyways, it was very interesting and a good introduction to Math 221. The Galois Part was really cool, and prof. Kisin is a good lecturer once you get used to his style
The material is interesting in this course, but at least for me, the way it is taught did not do it justice. The structure of the class felt sporadic and chaotic; combine this with the fact that there were only 4 problem sets to gain hands–on experience with what we were learning, and you get a course that feels like a lot of results that you don't fully understand being thrown at you without much connection to each other and without much reason as to why you should care about them. This was a shame, as rings, fields and Galois theory are very beautiful subjects, but I did not develop much intuition for them in this course.
This class was taught strangely this year. There were only 4 psets, and we did a unit on Tensor products which took several weeks. These were the most challenging concept I have encountered in classes at harvard and do not think I really understand them now. Unfortunately we had to skip some material routinely covered in this course to make room for tensor products which I think would have been much more fun to learn and prepared us more for the Galois theory. The first, third, and fourth psets were manageable, but the second pset, That almost made me quit math forever. I am not joking. I have never spent so much of a week on a pset. Additionally I have never just turned in a pset knowing it was wrong, but being exhausted and hoping the CAs also didn't understand how to do it either (I was right they didn't know what to do so just gave full credit). Oh to make the Tensor section worse the CAs had no idea and gave some incorrect help.
Professor Kisin's lectures are very "orthodox": proposition, proof, remark, proposition, proof, remark, etc. His lectures are clear and detailed, and I was able to get a good understanding of "why" different theorems are true.

This year, professor Kisin taught tensor and wedge products in place of quadratic fields and the geometry of numbers. Number fields/geometry of numbers is covered in Math 129, and previously 123 was called "baby 129." I was concurrently enrolled in both classes, and I felt that the two classes feel very different this year. Understanding tensors and wedges was also very helpful when studying differential forms/vector bundles in 132.

Math 122 is a strict pre–req for this class. Not a lot of group theory is used until Galois theory, but the proof techniques you learn in 122 translate almost directly to 123. I thought taking Math 129 concurrently was doable, except it was tough in the beginning since I had to self–study basic number field stuff (quadratic fields) and Galois theory.

I expect professor Gammage's 123 to be very different from professor Kisin's: see math 132 course reviews from 2024 spring. Ben tends to focus more on motivation and "big ideas," with less emphasis on detail in proofs. I also suspect there to be more work. Professor Kisin's 123 only had 4 pset with 5–6 problems each, whereas professor Gammage's 132 this semester has weekly psets of 10–12 problems each. He is an excellent lecturer though, so would 100% recommend shopping!
Math 123 is a very important course to take in order to understand modules, tensor products, and Galois Theory. These are topics that come up in pretty much any higher level math class, so it's good to know. Some of the results from Galois Theory are also very cool –– such as the unsolveability of the quintic. However, I would check the q scores of the professor teaching the class before I take it.
Definitely take this course if Kisin isnt teaching it. The content is awesome, and when I learned from the textbook, it was splendid. However, Kisins lectures are unclear, he is a condescending teacher, and he is not organized. The whole semester, he kept referring to section despite us reminding him that his CAs didnt have section. Whenever I would ask him questions out of my genuine confusion, he would say I had a psychological problem instead of acknowledging that a concept was not as intuitive to me, an undergrad, than it was to him, a tenured professor.
Interesting material and good instructor. Would recommend this course.
This is a pretty fair class, only four problem sets throughout and a (very standard) midterm and final (like the problems were pulled straight from problem sets and class notes). I thought the class went a bit fast at times, but Prof. Kisin and the CAs are very helpful if you go to their office hours. Plus, the textbook is a really good resource. Galois Theory was super interesting (although hard to understand at first).
The course is great, whether you are using it to fulfil your concentration requirements or if you want to explore algebra more. Exams and assignments are pretty straighforward, but being able to replicate them on your own requires some time to understand and engage with the material which is useful and rewarding for future courses.
Most math people I know really like Galois theory –– unfortunately I cannot say the same. I did not find the material very engaging, and the fact that we skipped some chapters usually taught in this class leading up to Galois theory probably didn't help. That might be different in future iterations with different professors, so take it with a grain of salt. The only part of the class I really enjoyed was our deep dive into the tensor product (which is usually not covered in this class, I believe).
Definitely fundamental for anyone pursuing mathematics. Also, if you are taking it with Prof. Kisin expect to go over concepts that are not usually included in the course, like tensor products, and to skip or rush over with other concepts usually included in the course, like qudratic fields.
