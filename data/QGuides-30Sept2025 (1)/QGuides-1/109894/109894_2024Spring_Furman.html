Daron Acemoglu, David Laibson, and John List, Macroeconomics (3rd edition)

Topics covered: Wealth of Nations; money, prices, inflation; labor market and unemployment; macroeconomic fluctuations, time tradeoffs, bond pricing; credit markets, monetary policy; financial crises; fiscal policy; inflation and unemployment, Phillips Curve; government budget, debt sustainability; trade deficits, exchange rates, exchange rate policies, crisis management using monetary/fiscal policy under floating/fixed exchange rates; aggregate incomes, proximate and fundamental drivers for growth; household finance, asset pricing.

Course structure notes:
Weekly analytical psets, plus weekly multiple choice questions in MyLab. Required weekly sections. A relatively–chill section debate similar to 10a. Multiple choice midterm and final, which include questions about lecture–time guest speakers. Unit Review Program for extra points. Required attendance or written reflection for at least three of six Economics in Action seminars outside of lecture.

I took this class a sophomore as an elective and highly recommend doing so. I found it to be a light elective (more work around exams), as you could do the psets over the weekends and then go to the pset collab on Sundays for final questions. Here are my other thoughts:

Psets: I think the problem sets are doable, especially if you have friends to do it with and attend pset collab sessions (def go to these, they are so helpful for getting the pset done and answering questions). I also think having two drops is helpful, though I would not recommend using them until the end of the semester when you know if there is a pset you have done poorly on and would want to drop.

Exams: As long as you have a very thorough cheat sheet, you will be fine. I condensed my entire class notes (essentially) into my cheat sheet which was very helpful (size 4 font). I think this is a good way to learn the content as I didn't end up needing my cheat sheet then. The midterm is much more straightforward than the final, whereas the final is logic puzzles and wording to trick you it felt like. Either way, I think these are also doable.

URP: Go every week! You don't have to study at all or do well, but it is a good way to learn and get an extra 2% on your grade.

Time commitment: the in class, section, and URP time commitment is a lot, combined with one OH per week just to confirm questions on the pset. However, I think that this is worth it to do well.

Math: As someone with a decent background in math, I think having this is helpful for the logic and background of the models covered. Though a lot of the math is technically covered, I found it helpful to understand what was going on behind the scenes. Not necessary though.
