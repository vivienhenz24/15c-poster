This class was fun and mostly low–stress! You watch some cool movies, learn some cool things about film techniques and analysis, and discuss Japanese cinema in the context of certain critical lenses. The weekly discussion posts are short and fun, and the video essays are quite a pleasant alternative to writing long tedious essays! Prof Zahlten is super nice and knowledgable and cares about his students, too! The one downside to the class is that it's a 9 am class where attendance is mandatory, which can be painful when the semester ramps up. The mandatory film screenings also take up two hours outside of class time every week, which is something to consider in terms of time commitment. Overall, highly recommended Gen Ed class. It's interesting, fun, and relatively low–stress!

– Movies: awesome! These alone are worth taking the class. I got introduced to so many amazing films and directors this semester, and this class as definitely expanded my appreciation for film.
– Lectures: overall interesting. I think they could be made better though with more content about the specific films we watched and more in–depth coverage of techniques in filmmaking/editing/visual analysis. The lecture content felt more geared towards broad themes in cinema like ideas of national identity, the politics of global platforms etc., which I found to be interesting but I also wanted to learn more about the art of filmmaking itself. I wasn't a huge fan of the amount of student discussion during the lecture – this might just be my personal taste, but I tend to find lecture time that is filled with miscellaneous comments from other students to be less engaging than a more structured lecture.
– Time commitment: not a gem. The video essays will take a while, especially since they make you watch an entirely new film outside of the class screenings for the video essays. If you choose to learn Adobe Premiere Pro, there is a learning curve . That being said, the video editing skills you will learn are very practical and useful to have in the future; so, I personally found gaining this skill to be worth it.

This is one of my favorite courses that I have taken at Harvard so far. Through exploring Japanese films, you really deep dive into each of the words of the course title: GLOBAL, JAPANESE and CINEMA. With regards to GLOBAL, you explore how the relationships between countries and the distribution of products are connected. With regards to JAPANESE, you learn a lot about history and culture of Japan in a nuanced way in a way that feels natural and not forced. With regards to CINEMA, you learn how to analyze film techniques critically and evaluate their effectiveness which really changes the way you look at film. For the projects, you get to make video essays which is an awesome communication skill to develop.
