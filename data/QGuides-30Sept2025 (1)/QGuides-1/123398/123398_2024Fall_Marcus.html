This class is pretty much just memorization based. It won't take too much of your time all you have to do is pay attention and take good notes during lecture, do one out of class assignment (~10ish hours of work), and prepare for one in class assignment, the midterm, and the final (all memorization based). You also do a section post and some readings every week, which is a pretty quick process (1–2 hours). This class is pretty well taught, as the professors care about your success and understanding, but it's not super intellectually stimulating. You'll probably already have a good grasp of the information presented if you've taken some combo of AP Euro/AP World and APUSH, and the spin they have on the info isn't going to rock your world. As most people say, you'll probably find the latter half of the class more interesting, when material centers around energy, the Manhattan project, eugenics, etc. It's not a high stress class so Hist Sci concentrators shouldn't worry about it (but if you're not Hist Sci, this class probably isn't worth it for you).

HS100 is a fair introduction to the History of Science at Harvard, with some drawbacks. First, the positives: the teaching team was, for the most part, extremely kind and helpful. They were eager to answer questions and led great discussions. Furthermore, the wide range of material covered, I think, gives a good introduction to the variety of topics that can be included in the History of Science – from cosmology to the making of the atomic bomb. The assignments are not difficult and this course seldom caused me stress (it is fairly graded and the weekly responses are not terribly difficult). The readings, overall, were quite interesting as well! This said, there are certainly drawbacks. For one, because SO much material was covered, it was hard to feel that we did so in any great depth. Personally, I would have preferred that we covered fewer people, but in greater detail. For this reason, I preferred Professor Wilson's lectures because in each lecture, he covered many fewer people than were featured in Professor Marcus's. Additionally, while the lectures are overall good, the assignments are rather frustrating. The midterm and final exam (which is basically a feat in memorizing the entire course) did not feel productive. Given that the History of Science at Harvard is so much about thinking for yourself and generating original arguments, spending weeks memorizing dates, names, and key words for these exams did not feel like it would adequately prepare anyone to continue into the tutorials or upper–level electives, where you need to substantially analyze and find both primary and secondary sources. I think much of the class would have preferred a "DBQ"–style open note exam or assignment, where we could apply knowledge from class without having to memorize it. The professors also prohibited the use of computers during lecture (and did not record the lectures) and because of the speed of the presentation, I frequently missed important details or explanations while scrambling to write everything down. Finally, this year, the only substantial writing assignment was done in pairs – which, unfortunately, for many people turned out to be greatly frustrating. I would recommend against this in the future. Overall, this course is not bad, but if you are not a HistSci concentrator or secondary, I would recommend choosing an elective for a more fulfilling experience.
