Prof. Jones is a great lecturer who cares a lot about the course material, and lectures were always genuinely enjoyable. The readings will give you insights into different eras and issues in medical history and ethics and will push you to think and analyze while still being comprehensible to people without ethics/philosophy backgrounds. After this class, you will be much more informed about the ethics of medical practices and the history behind them, and you will feel better equipped to analyze and assess historical and philosophical arguments.
I highly recommend this course, regardless of whether or not you are planning to pursue a career in the medical field. This course is challenging but not hard, and you will learn about different perspectives on controversial issues and will learn to form strong arguments on either side of debates.

I did effectively learn about ethical issues and some basic ideas about philosophy. Whether or not you find this interesting or fun depends, but it's undeniably useful for understanding modern healthcare issues, whether as a future patient or doctor.

I know inevitably a lot of you come here to answer two questions, can I get a good grade and is it a big workload? I did the same. That being said, it is also not a silly little class. In reflection, the goal really is to help you better recognize, critique, and craft arguments. It really equips you with philosophical and historical perspectives to engage in debates and possibly contribute to dilemmas. Even as a stem–inclined student who has never debated, it is USEFUL. The professor is a WELL OF KNOWLEDGE. I regret not attending all his office hours and if you were to ask me for one single recommendation... go to the professor's office hours. For other authors... skim (idk if I'm suppose to say that). In terms of workload, there is only 4 assignments to turn in, papers.
