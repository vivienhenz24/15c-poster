Take this course!! 161 was my favorite CS course at Harvard. Every problem set actually taught me so much about systems because each assignment basically asks us to code a different part of the Chickadee OS. One thing that could be improved are the lectures: some felt disorganized and could benefit from more visual slides. Overall though, the course was an amazing experience with super accessible teaching staff, both during and outside of office hours. Definitely check out 161!

I don't think this review will give anything new, but basically if you're interested in OSes and felt comfortable with CS61, take this class! Beyond learning about the internals of an operating system and building one yourself, you gain a lot of confidence thinking about multithreading and synchronization, as well as systems programming in general. The problem sets can feel intimidating at times, but as long as you start early and make heavy use of OH, everything is very manageable (disclaimer: this year we did not have any exams, which may not be the case in future years if Mickens is teaching again).

TAKE THIS COURSE! It's time consuming, but it's not as hard as people make it out to be. If you enjoyed CS61, you should seriously consider taking this. If you didn't, probably don't (it's just more of the same thing, and more in depth, you will suffer). Whether Prof. Kohler or Mickens teaches it, you will leave the course with a lot of deep understanding (and hopefully appreciation) for how the systems you use on a daily basis work. ngl, there were some psets for which one had to grind (and debugging anything multithreaded if you didn't think out your synchronization invariants properly is pita) but I'd say most of them were rewarding, The grading wasn't harsh at all and the teaching staff is super helpful and answers all your questions. I think most people would say that this year's iteration of CS161 was perhaps not as difficult as folklore would have you think – I certainly suffered more in CS124 than in 161, although I suppose 124 has the safety net of somebody, somewhere will have the key insight or just the outright solution to a PSET, whereas in these sorts of classes, chatting to someone who has done the whole problem set might help you avoid some pitfalls, but you still have to put in the time to write the thing. I guess I should caveat that I took 165 the previous semester, and my experience there I think probably saved me a perhaps 1–3 hours on average per PSET, just from having some familiarity with GDB and instantly writing a good logging system (something I did way too late into 165, here it was the first thing I wrote and that was certainly a good decision).

Eddie as always is a wonderful professor; he generally makes you think he has some boundless knowledge vault locked away in his head regarding everything related to systems. And we don't even need to talk about anything that follows `emacs –nw`. Anyone who writes such fabled characters in 2024 should probably either be worshipped or feared.

Getting to build a reasonable sized portion of an operating system is awesome, the PSETs are obviously time consuming but honestly not even close to as overwhelming as I was expecting. I took this partly to build some familiarity with C++, and I definitely felt like I gained some of that, along with some good practice in GDB if either of those appeal to you.

Overall, very much enjoyed, maybe a bit burnt out taking this and some other moderately time–consuming stuff in senior spring, but I'm not sure there's a better final assignment to hand in as a CS concentrator as your operating system final project (even if mine was pretty meh) and be able to call yourself done with Harvard :)

At the start of the course Eddie was very transparent and told us basically that he wasn't expecting to teach this course this year but since Mickens wasn't he would step up. With this in mind, there were some aspects of the course this year that were a little disappointing. I though Eddie's lectures were overall fine though I did often find myself questioning why we spent so much time on certain topics. Mickens stepped in for a week at the start of the semester and I thought his lectures were a lot tighter. Psets 2 and on took forever to get feedback on (as of writing at the end of April still don't have feedback for pset 2 which was due in February..). I know trying to grade student's ugly code, let alone understand and give useful feedback, takes a lot of time especially considering there are always new questions on ed/in office hours, but from the student's perspective this was not ideal. Also both the midterm and final were removed once the course was already underway. I was kinda nice to not have exams but I don't think they were removed any pedagogical reason.

With that out of the way, I still 100% recommend this class and think that any student who plans on wants to be good at writing code and understanding computers should take it. There is no better way to learn than through doing, and the structure of the course ensures that you will spend a lot of time implementing and debugging kernel features. Course staff (including Eddie) devote an insane amount of time to helping students. There's a reason 161 consistency has both the highest number of hours and q guide score out of any cs class (any maybe all classes??), it really is the final boss of harvard cs and it would be a shame to graduate with a cs degree without taking it on.
