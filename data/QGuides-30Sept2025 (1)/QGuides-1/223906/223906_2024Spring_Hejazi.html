Comments
Despite being very enthusiastic about wanting to learn more causal inference theory, I did not like this class at all.

I recommend taking this course either concurrently or after taking Inference II.

Instead of producing slides for class, or presenting through a chalk–talk, the professor has opted to simply scroll through notes.

The quality of teaching seems to be very poor. The professor likes to say he "doesn't remember what it was like to be a student," or presents from notes that he just scrolls through, and when he does board–work, it's very messy and often he gets caught up in his own mistakes leaving students confused about what we're supposed to be taking away from the class. I think the professor thinks of writing his notes as preparation for class, but pays relatively little attention to how the ideas behind the notes will be delivered, making for a disjointed experience watching him scroll through notes, jumping around, picking and choosing what should be emphasized as the definitions, theorems, and lemmas fly by.

The "warmup exercises" are basically useless because the professor doesn't synthesize the answers clearly, so after 30 minutes of the classroom positing different ideas, there's no clear answers given as to which of the ideas mentioned were correct vs. incorrect.

It feels like the class vacillates between either covering material that is so simple it's repetitive and unnecessary, or is skipping over the more complicated theorems/proofs that he says are important but isn't spending enough time on to communicate clearly.

I think the professor's points about the higher plausibility of nonparametric models accurately reflecting reality are fair, but it felt like he largely used this as a point of moral high–ground / to condescend to the students about why the majority of the traditional theory is useless. I think a lot of people decided to just not engage further on this point because classroom discussions felt more like being shot–down or told why you don't know enough about the subject than they did a discussion.

The professor's main strength is that he knows the literature really well. At least point you to the major milestones in a coherent, largely linear fashion. I hope his teaching improves.
This course is a great introduction to causal identification and estimation for randomized trials, observational studies, and mediation analysis.
This course was still developing when I took it, but it is clear that Nima cares a lot about the content and the quality of the course. Expect a slightly higher workload than some of the other Biostatistics doctoral electives since, at least in this iteration, the class involved weekly readings, 3–4 homework assignments, a midterm presentation on a paper, and a final project. The plus side: no exams! Overall, if you want to learn the best, most efficient techniques for causal inference and estimation with somewhat of a machine–learning focus, I would recommend taking this course. It will also make you much better at reading statistical literature, as the assigned readings are extremely high quality,and Nima takes great care in selecting papers that teach you a lot about the subject and also give you practice critiquing scholarly work.
You will probably learn about causal inference more than you anticipate (especially if you have not taken any class before). Professor Hejazi is great at causal inference and created a course that encourages students to discover causal inference outside the classroom. The courseload is not light, but it is doable and aligned with the lectures. I think that this class will only get better in the future and it is a great addition to the Biostatistics Dept offerings.
BST 258 is an engaging course that strikes a nice balance between presenting the theory behind methods and their actual application. This is the first iteration of the course since its revival, so the contents/structure may change a bit in the coming years. If you're interested in causal inference, it's absolutely worth taking
