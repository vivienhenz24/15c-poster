Take this class if you are enthusiastic about physics and understand high school mechanics well. Regarding learning content anew, I believe that 15a might be more appropriate, as it also gives you more free time to study the content (in 16, you will probably spend most of your time doing problem sets). If you plan on concentrating in physics, this course will give you a substantial foundation and enable you to meet an excellent physics community, which is one of the most significant aspects of this course.
This class is hard. You should take it only if you really like Physics and want to challenge yourself.. Grades can be harsh, but if you do your work it shouldn't be too bad. If you like Physics, you have done a lot of Physics and you have a strong background in math (differential equations, Lin algebra and multi calc) definitely take this course. If you want to take most advanced courses (skipping the intro sequence) start taking this course. If you just want to be cool taking the course not having the pre req, don't do it, take 15 and you'll have more fun.
The course description says that you will do fine as long as you did well on AP Physics C. This is a lie. There are fairly complex topics from both multivariable calculus and linear algebra in this course, so being familiar with both is probably a prerequisite to feeling comfortable in this class. This is especially true for linear algebra, as if you have not had any introduction to it some concepts will make no sense and will never make sense until you learn linear algebra. Even if you know some linear algebra, it is probably best to take 25a or 55a concurrently with this course to keep up with the math. Even then, that will just get you ready for the math portion, if barely. Even with physics ability on the level of physics olympiad, this course might still be quite difficult. The only way one might feel truly "comfortable" in this class is having already done work on the level of physics olympiad and math 25/55. Even then, good luck.
Georgi is incomprehensible, however the content is engaging and interesting. If you enjoy physics (or want to see if you do) then take this class. Don't be intimidated by others, just go in and enjoy it and if you don't then don't feel pressured to take the class. Learning and thoroughly understanding concepts will require your own volition and independence and it is worth it if you want it to be.
It's awesome but a ton of work.
This is a very good introductory mechanics course if you already have a solid grounding in mechanics, since it uses the same skills but is a more in–depth look at a lot of pieces of the content than you might have had before. It's also a nice community, and a good place to start if you plan to take multiple physics classes in this department.
Please, before you commit to this course, understand what you are committing to. This class is NO JOKE. It is genuinely the most difficult and demanding course I have ever taken in my life. That being said, it is simultaneously the most beautiful. The physics you learn is genuinely incredible, and it makes you feel like you have superpowers sometimes. If you plan on having a somewhat active social life, maybe second guess registering for this course. It's definitely possible, but not easy by any means. However! If you know physics is your passion, and you have the determination to do great things, don't let this course and all its downfalls stop you. This course was made for the people who want to do great things, so if that's what you plan for your future, dive straight in, give it your all, and feel the physics in your bones.
This is an incredibly course and I cannot recommend it highly enough! The community of this course is incredible and there is nothing as rewarding as figuring out the problems (the problem sets tend to be insanely difficult...) with classmates and the help of CAs. It really changes the way you think about physics and how you approach problems.
What can I say about Physics 16? Its wonderful material, and the course succeeded at being fast–paced and intense while creating a sense of community. However, despite good intentions, many things about it were disorganized and demoralizing. I learned a lot, but I wouldnt say that I had a good time or would particularly recommend it to others.

Issues included: problem sets assigned late (like out Sat, due Tue); poorly vetted homework and exam questions; a low ratio of appreciation to offhand and rather grinding criticism; idiosyncratic and all–or–nothing grading with no drop lowest or realistic way to get points back; Mathematica–based lectures and pset solutions that tended to buried key details in big piles of code; some students flagrant reliance on CAs and stronger peers for answers; significant homework due just after each midterm; an interesting but huge final project that undermined the last month of course material and ate up time I would have liked to study for the final...

I feel strongly that a course that pushes people as hard, makes as many demands, and is as much of a lifestyle as Physics 16 needs much stronger administration and better vibes, or it can put off even very able and enthusiastic students. It's also just miserable to get halfway into the semester, realize that you're in a course that treats people poorly for no particular reason, and have no real option other than to slog it out.

Maybe you should take Physics 16 if youre serious about physics and have the time for it. Just be aware that its one where you can study beautiful topics, meet some amazing people, get an A, and still regret taking it.

That being said, I absolutely loved the course. Yes, it is extremely hard and yes, it takes a lot of time, but taking 16 was probably one of the best decisions I ever made. So, if you are even the slightest bit interested in concentrating in physics, take this course, see how it goes, and drop to Physics 15a if necessary. The lecturing is known to be incomprehensible, but that's honestly just how Prof. Georgi teaches and you get used to it. The problem sets are hard, but you do them with a group, and everyone gets them done so that's not to worry. The final project is super fun, and I hope Prof. Yao does that next semester! (probably not but oh well). The course content is really good, and you learn a lot of cool physics, but rotations towards the end of the course will probably be the worst thing you've ever done (cooked all of us).

However, the absolute best thing about this course is the people you meet here. I genuinely believe (biased) that Physics 16 has the best community out of any *hard* class here at Harvard, and I am so glad I took the class to meet the people I did. You bond with the whole class over impossible psets at 1am at Physics Night, and there is no better way to get to know people. Please do take the course, I highly highly recommend it. The scores are probably horrendous on this Q report, but that doesn't mean much. Even though it is hard, everyone gets through it, just try it out please!
If you are thinking of taking this class, be prepared for it to essentially take up your whole semester, including both thinking about the assignments you have to do and actually doing them.

I have no regrets taking this class––I met some of my closest friends, and, as with all the other years, the trial–by–fire bonding experiences you have with your other classmates throughout the semester is irreplaceable. Additionally, the people who take Physics 16 often stay together and continue to take similar physics courses, so you'll always have this community aspect after this class.

HOWEVER, coming in just with AP Physics C and AP Calc BC background, I had to work extra hard to understand the concepts taught in class and build my physical intuition. One of the biggest tips I have is to GO TO OFFICE HOURS (and physics night)!! I would've never survived the class (or been able to solve really any problem on the homework for that matter) without them. Do NOT be afraid to ask for help in this class, as you will meet some of the most intelligent, kind people, both within your peers and within the teaching staff (Jerry absolutely carried this semester––if he is CAing this class again next year, be sure to go to his office hours!!) It is also definitely worth it to go through the homework after solutions are released and make sure you actually understand the physics (although sometimes Prof. Georgi's solutions can be really confusing, but you can always ask your CAs).

As expected, problem sets are overall very difficult (some weeks were worse than others). Usually, though, after going through the painful process of figuring out the problem, you will understand just how interesting the physics is. For me, I thought that the second midterm and the final were especially difficult. Just make sure that you truly understand the physical concepts behind each problem, and you can truly apply those concepts––that will take you very far.

Overall, Physics 16 was such a standout experience––both the best and worst class I took this semester. Despite all the moments where I wanted to drop and was questioning my life, I do believe it was worth it.
This is a challenging course. I spent a lot of time trying to wrap my head around concepts (such as the difference between twisting torque vs. r x F torque and how to apply each). I also spent a lot of time trying to understand what problems were asking on the problem sets. Often, Professor Georgi would be looking for a very specific interpretation or insight. I relied heavily on classmates and CAs to guide me when I was stuck.

Professor Georgi is a great teacher one–on–one, but his lectures are very hard to follow. So if you have him, make sure to stop by his office and ask him any questions you have. Don't be scared off by his comments on your problem sets.

Overall, you learn a lot about mechanics and general problem solving in physics.
Professor Georgi is a poor lecturer who often gives highly deconstructive criticism on PSETS. Also, you can get negative points on exams for wrong answers.
Physics 16 is a truly one–of–a–kind course. It benefits from inspiring teaching and engaging curriculum that spotlights the most beautiful aspects of introductory mechanics (and beyond!). Professor Georgi is an amazing physicist, and being able to take a class with him (or really anyone professor who takes on this class with zeal) is an outstanding experience at Harvard. Professor Georgi's teaching is rigorous. It definitely demands prior reading and preparation ahead of each lecture to attain a full conceptual understanding as lectures can take unexpected turns or focus in on specific results or examples. Problem sets are very difficult, but from the collective struggle comes a strong sense of community that I did not fully appreciate until homework 8 came around and everyone was grinding away in Lyman Library at 2am, all working with a little extra sense of motivation. Midterms and the Final were very challenging, as was the final project, but these were all sources of wicked cool physics. Grading during the semester is fair, but I cannot speak to final grade assignment as I am filling out this form before receiving mine.

This course shaped my current understanding of physics and provided me with the tools to tackle extremely difficult problems. Throughout the course you are confronted with questions on problem sets that you struggle to find a way into, and from this struggle you reach two changes. The first change comes in your problem solving mindset. You slowly develop an understanding for how to think about physical systems, even the most unintuitive, in ways that can lead you into the equations. The second change comes in your collaboration mindset. You realize that working with others is not only essential, but beneficial if done in the right context.
If you want to learn physics, take another class. If you want to struggle for a semester and meet great people, take this class.
Be happy that you have Norman Yao.
