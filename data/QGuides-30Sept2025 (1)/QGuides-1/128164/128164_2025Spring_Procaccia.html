Only take this course if you can memorize theorems and proofs. There is no textbook or other preparation material other than the lecture slides. In order to complete the pset, you have to go to office hours every week.
You will learn fundamental results like the G–S theorem: that it is mathematically impossible for a voting rule to have all three following properties: free from manipulation, any candidate can theoretically win, and voters are incentivized to report honest rankings of their preferred candidates. Or that for two–sided matchings, it is mathematically impossible to have a rule that incentivize both sides to be honest–reporting, while resulting in a matching that is stable (where pairs wont want to switch partners). Or that for allocating schools to students, its impossible to have a mechanism that satisfy all the following properties together: incentivizes honest–reporting, equal treatment of students with equal preferences, and doesnt result in a subpar matching that a better matching exists. Incredibly fundamental results with extremely practical results.

To give it credit, it had interesting content that is applicable to pretty much every social setting where resources are being allocated. Think group projects in class, or splitting the rent, or food donation, or even any kind of election as you learn about strategyproofness.

One of the biggest recurring themes has been the interplay between fairness and efficiency in decision–making, particularly in social choice theory. The impossibility of designing a mechanism that is both neutral and anonymous for two voters and two alternatives highlights the inherent trade–offs in voting systemsa reminder that no system is perfect and must prioritize certain fairness criteria over others.

In resource allocation, the concept of envy–freeness up to one good (EF1) has been a powerful tool for ensuring fairness, especially when dividing indivisible goods. The "divide–and–choose" method, where one player splits and the other selects, ensures that no player envies another too much, reinforcing the practicality of designing mechanisms that guarantee near–fair outcomes even when perfect fairness is unattainable.

Studying online algorithms, particularly in bipartite matching, has emphasized the challenges of decision–making under uncertainty. Finally, the exploration of cascade models in networks revealed how ideas or behaviors spread based on connectivity thresholds. The mathematical formulation of contagion showed that even simple network structureslike a sequence of interconnected trianglescan lead to sharp transitions between global influence and local containment. To me, such is the beauty and resonance of geometry, guiding us to the structural properties in diffusion processes.

Across these topics, the common thread has been understanding the limits of algorithms and fairness in real–world scenarios and appreciating how theoretical models help guide practical decision–making.
