I recommend this course for people who are already comfortable with traditional classical mechanics problem solving as a relatively easy introduction to a more formal and abstract picture of physics that will be useful in one's future studies in quantum mechanics and QFT, although this course definitely does not teach one to solve standard mechanics problems. It is also possible to skip this course and pick up much of the relevant information from other physics courses and self–study if this doesn't fit into your schedule.
This was far and away my favorite class of the semester, and one of my favorite classes of my time at Harvard so far. I had already taken 15a my freshman fall, but I absolutely do not regret going back for a second pass at mechanics. It is a completely different subject when looked at more deeply, and far more beautiful than I gave it credit for before taking this class. The main content of the class revolves around symmetries and conserved quantities in Lagrangian and Hamiltonian mechanics, a subject that (as professor Jaffe stressed over and over) had very deep connections to modern–day quantum theory.

Due to the small class size, we all became pretty close by the end of the class. This was in large part fostered by the gem of a TF, Serhii––try and take any class he TF's in the future, not just 151! Professor Jaffe gave incredible lectures for the most part, and would frequently drop incredibly stories about his life and career. He is an incredibly physicist and a really sweet person––the class party at his house at the end of the semester was definitely a highlight!

The only downsides I found in the class were the problem sets and tests. Many of the problem sets (biweekly) were short, so the workload was very manageable for the majority of the semester. However, I found the pset questions to be very detached from the material we were learning in class, with much time spent on endless bashing without any noticeable benefit. The exams were stressful, with professor Jaffe not giving any practice exams or practice problems and forbidding cheat sheets (his teaching style is very old school in general). However, the two in–class midterms comprised only 20% of our grade this semester, so it wasn't the worst thing in the world.

We had a final project as our final assessment for the class, which was really fun, albeit quite stressful. It is incredibly how deep of a subject classical mechanics is, and I don't think you get to experience its beauty in any other class at Harvard.

Overall, I think that Physics 151 is as close to a gem of a class as you're going to get in the Physics department––relatively low stress and low workload, very interesting and relevant content, and an unforgettable experience with incredible people! I would highly recommend incoming freshman take this class over 15a, 16, or 19 to fulfill their mechanics requirement, especially if you have a decent linear algebra background. (Without a solid linear algebra foundation, it's very hard to appreciate a lot of the material we covered.)
I was super excited for this class, but we didn't end up doing most of what was on the syllabus. We learned Lagrangian Mechanics, Hamilton's Formalism, and some ideas related to Noether's Theorem, but many of us left the semester wondering what we had done. Do not take this class if you're familiar with those—we ended up spending a single day on all the other (solitons, connections to quantum mechanics) stuff. I don't think anyone really enjoyed the lectures this year.

This is a very low workload class, which was nice. We had five short problem sets, two midterms and a final project, all graded leniently. The TA, Serhii, is an incredible teacher and human being, though I'm not sure if he'll be teaching this class again.
My second exposure to classical mechanics (having done the equivalent of AP in the UK before uni), Physics 151 was a fun and rewarding experience. Professor Jaffe was great – both at teaching and at using the small class size to its advantage by making it as interactive as possible. TF Serhii Kryhin deserves a special mention for his accessibility, dedication, patience and great explanations. The class community is smaller than that of the typical physics course at Harvard, and as a result usually has a more informal feel to it which gives it the potential to be (and in my experience, was) something special. The material is a really nice breath of fresh air, especially when contrasted with one's typical ubiquitous and frustratingly boring first encounter with classical mechanics, and was both beautiful in itself as well as having many interesting connections with various bits of physics and mathematics such as quantum mechanics, lie algebras, differential geometry and more – definitely gained a totally new appreciation for something I once found so dry and lifeless. The problem sets and exams were a good opportunity to practice working with things that will undoubtedly be seen again and again in modern physics courses such as GR and QFT – sometimes the "bash" felt a bit much but it always resolved to something nice that is consistent with good intuition and felt like a good conceptual takeaway. One critique is that more preparatory material for the exams would be much appreciated – don't think anyone in the class was that clear on exactly what to study, especially before the first midterm. Overall, I find that the course does a great job of balancing beauty and rigour, and I greatly recommend it to all concentrators – especially to those who are interested in the more theoretical side of physics and to those who are looking for a fun and unique course.

(Specific groups of people to whom I would recommend this to especially:
Ex–Physics–19ers who found the snippets of analytical mechanics interesting
Anyone who needs a mechanics requirement but has gotten sick to death of Newtonian mechanics (I belong in this category)
Freshmen looking for a lighter course than 16 which teaches similar material (could imagine that this would be especially good to have under your belt before Physics 251 for more maths–forward physics students)
