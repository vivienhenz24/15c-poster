This was a really great class, and definitely changed me as a person. The format of the class is a bit unique, as there is no lecture component and is entirely student discussion led (although this means the quality of each class is dependent on a student discussion leader) Two hours of discussion can feel like a lot, so I would say to only take the course if you're really interested in the material and prepared to do the (manageable) readings. There is a great community around the class, and you can tell everyone is very interested in the topic. I was initially worried that the topic might get redundant week to week, but every class was super interesting and offered a look into different aspects of our relationships with animals. While the weekly work load is relatively low, writing the two papers and planning your week's discussion are pretty involved, and I would say the professor is a bit of a tough grader. I think my only real issue with this class is that the professor does not put grades into Canvas, so it's on students to calculate their own average and keep track of their grades (so hold onto your printed and graded discussion posts!) Overall, this is a really great class, and I think that everyone can take away something valuable from it!

I found this to be one of the most valuable and eye–opening courses I've enrolled in during my time at Harvard! I came into the class with a surface–level grasp of "human exceptionalism" as a term, and minimal depth of knowledge on its historical underpinnings and present–day impacts. This course offered enlightening lessons that delved deeply into how dominant cultural narratives have supported humanity's misperception of being separate from and superior to nature – distorting our proper role as humble stewards interconnected with all life on Earth.

In terms of future improvements, one area that could be improved is applying a bit more structure and facilitation within the student–led discussion sections. With the sheer breadth and depth of assigned materials each week, it was at times hard to ensure every reading, podcast, or video was covered with equal attention and care in our limited group discussion time. Adding some guiding reflection questions or prompts for each lesson could better help students in driving more productive discourse touching upon the essential points within the abundant content.
