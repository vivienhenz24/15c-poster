What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

I really liked this class–––it was I think the first time it was taught with the literature component, which meant that readings were often Really Heavy but at the same time incredibly valuable to engage with if you did manage to find the time. The archival work we were involved in was really powerful, if at times a bit unstructured, and discussions were fairly engaging. My one complaint is probably that this course tries to cover a lot of ground while also only meeting once a week, and that limited time together with the (often) hundreds of pages of readings assigned each week meant that it sometimes felt like we weren't able to dig as deep into the material as I would've liked. Regardless, this is a really good class taught by a professor who is genuinely engaged in the material and committed to helping us work through its complexities and translating the ideas we come across into practice in the real world–––take it if you can!!!!
Take the class if you are really interested, but not if you want to be super challenged and have a lot of "transformative" discussion. It is really cool for exploring literature written by individuals who were incarcerated, and definitely incredibly valuable alone on that merit. However, the discussion aspect of the course could definitely be more challenging and get more into the depths of abolition and its relation to what we read. Also, if you are hoping to learn a lot about the history of prison literature, maybe don't take it. We don't do a lot of historical analysis, but definitely do a lot of cool literary analysis so that is probably the larger attraction. Oh, the archival part of this class is like really dope if you are a history nerd or really any type that would get excited about that sort of thing, but definitely take the class if you are excited to get experience with that.
I came into the course wanting to learn more about prison abolition and its history, which we do talk about at the beginning, but then we pivot more towards general prison literature. The class is no solely focused on theory of abolition, but also the application and narratives from people on the inside.
This is a cool class, but you better be into prison abolition going in. There is not a whole lot of exposition before you're diving deep into the literature of the idea, rather than introducing the idea as a policy proposal first. Thomas is very passionate and very kind, but sometimes class discussions can be lacking.
