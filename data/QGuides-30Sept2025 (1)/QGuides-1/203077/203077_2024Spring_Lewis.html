This course changed much of the way I see the world and how central image–making is on creating American citizenship. If you are interested in race and art, I would highly suggest taking this class!
Sarah Lewis is a fantastic professor who has really struck gold with her area of research. This course is completely accessible to those without much of a background in art history, but it will also allow you to go deep into course content if you come with such a background. You will be given a lot of guidance on visual analysis – I came away from this course feeling like I could really look and appreciate artwork in a new way.

Lewis is a fantastic teacher, though she may come off as a little divorced from the contemporary moment. Her scholarship is really deeply crucial to understand the social justice strand of the art world that emerged in the wake of Black Lives Matter. However, this class won't leave you much if you're looking to understand how the art and visual culture is developing in the recent frame of the past 5 years. Similarly, we talked a whole lot about protest imagery and photographing catastrophe, but the conversation never seemed to extend itself to the Palestine moment that was enveloping the world. In that way, this course can be a bit myopic. I think Lewis's aversion has something to do with her political connections and fragile place in the curatorial world. Nonetheless, what you will learn from this class is important to reading these new events.
This was one of the most rewarding classes I have taken at Harvard. Professor Lewis is a great and genius professor who will change the way you see race, citizenship, and the role of visual art in culture. I am so glad I took this course.

This is not an easy class and not one to take if you are not actually interested in the course material. There are a bunch of readings and the tests are not easy (lots of preparation required). If you are genuinely interested in the topics, highly recommend this course!
take it
Excellent class. Workload was always manageable.
If you have the opportunity to be in the room with Professor Lewis, please take it. She is one of a kind and has an incredible perspective that adds so much to the course. You will learn more than you expect you could from a single course – I certainly did.
Although this is a lecture, there is a lot opportunity to speak during lecture, so much so that it is even encouraged. Because it is a visual analysis course at its core, this is very different from the normal lecture atmosphere. The course asks the students to be observant and creative, which is unlike many other courses. Professor Lewis is an incredibly engaging and encouraging educator, which creates a comfortable environment.
TAKE THIS! Professor Lewis is a tour de force. She may be an acquired taste, but she remains fair and brilliant alongside incredibly interesting material. She cares, and I think for any Harvard student taking a class on art that feelings spiritual, academically, and communal is worthwhile. She teaches with love and talks openly, bringing guests that will blow your mind.
STEER CLEAR if you are at all interested in the world of modern/contemporary American art or culture and have supplemented your coursework by investing in its study. It will be a waste of your time because the material is not novel or cutting–edge any longer. The material clearly has not been updated in a decade (despite the professor's assertion of its groundbreaking nature) and the course's central thesis (which she repeats every lecture) is totally surface–level and elementary. The only thing that kept me there were the two requirements that it filled (GenEd and concentration). There was an instance in lecture in which a student asked a question and Professor Lewis said that she would provide an "undergraduate answer" to the question, implying that we wouldn't be able to grasp the complexities of the fuller answer. There was another instance in which a student began commenting on more recent events as they pertained to that day's lecture material, and the professor cut her off, saying that she would "get there next week" and did not want her to continue so as to "spoil" the lecture. You cannot "spoil" of American history and culture. She provides no flexibility or opportunity for true commentary and dissent; calling on the same students who affirm your viewpoints does not create a fruitful learning environment. I am not a student with a marginalized political viewpoint in the slightest at Harvard, but the homogeneity of perspective and professorial reverence that happened in this classroom was nauseating and completely unprecedented. The fact that she is going to transform this into a seminar course is terrifying; section was this course's saving grace, and with that removed, all of the problems with the course's current iteration will be amplified. If this is the "cutting edge of scholarship," as the course advertises, then no longer would I like to be a scholar.
This is the sort of class everyone should take. It's a civics class, a methods class, a history class, a Black studies class, and an art analysis class wrapped up into one and tied with the beautiful bow that is the single most genius professor I have ever had! TAKE IT
