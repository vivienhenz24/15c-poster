It can be a good start for those who want to take an interdisciplinary course/path.
I would tell them to take a look at some readings beforehand to determine whether they're really interested in it, as I feel like there's a bit of a mismatch between the course title/description and the actual course content. I'd say if they're interested in it, they're going to have a really good time, but if they're unsure, then they might want to pick another elective.
This class was amazing. The course staff is great, and they care about the topic and teaching. The content is super applicable to different parts of life, and it is so cool how different disciplines come together. I truly learned so much about the past and how we can use knowledge about previous disease to help us in the future.
Overall a great class. The material is super interesting and feels very important, especially in a post covid era. Prof. Warinner is such a cool person hearing about her research is so amazing and she clearly has a lot of passion for the course material. The class is very interdisciplinary, and regardless of your background, you'll come out of the class with some new insights into your own field as well as a deeper appreciation of others. The guest interviews are also super cool and a great opportunity to learn more about research in different fields and different career options. Assignments and grading are very reasonable, thought I will say the pandemic preparedness project can be rough depending on your groupmates.
This course provides an interesting, interdisciplinary approach to the history and archaeology of illness. Professor Warinner is a very passionate instructor and is very willing to discuss the course material; I really enjoyed learning from her. The workload is manageable (though it is important not to fall behind on the annotated bibliography entries). The course has more of an emphasis on the COVID–19 pandemic than I anticipated, but it was interesting to see how studying historical diseases and pandemics could apply to modern issues.
If you are interested in anthropology and medicine at any capacity, I would highly recommend this class! There are a lot of readings, but each one is quite interesting and builds upon the course material in a meaningful way. There was never a time where I felt that the lectures were just regurgitating the readings. In addition, the guest interviews were very lovely!
I loved this class. I would take it. I would say it is a really good combination of history, epidemiology and a small amount of science.
This class was very interesting. I enjoyed the topics quite a bit and Dr. Warriner is an incredibly interesting and learned professor, but I felt as though the workload could be a lot at times.
This course will impress upon you the deeply impactful and devastating effects of disease, revealing its interplay with social structure, cultural and ethnic identity, religion, and colonial power. It will also push you to examine group thinking through the lens of pandemic events, tracing common fears and prejudices and identifying unique contextual influences.
