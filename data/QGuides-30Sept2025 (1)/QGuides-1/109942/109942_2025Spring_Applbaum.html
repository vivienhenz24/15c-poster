What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

– Though part of a fellowship in ethics, this class is, in reality, a class on political philosophy and, more specifically, a class on Rawlsian liberalism. The focus is so strong it can feel dogmatic at times, but don't be afraid to express your disagreement!
– I took this class as a sophomore, and while it proved to be a transformative and immersive experience (one of the best, if not *the* best class I have taken at Harvard), I know I would have benefitted more from it had I taken more philosophy courses first. Which is all to say, if I were to do it all over again, I probably would have waited and taken this class as a junior. By no means am I saying that sophomores should not apply, but if you are a sophomore feeling uncertain about your preparedness, listen to your instinct! You might benefit more from the course if you choose to take it in a later year.
– Make sure to meet with your team in person. It makes the experience so much better.
– Food is served and it is good. Fresh berries. Every. Week.
– Go the events!
Take this class!! The fellowship is a very unique program and you get a lot out of this class. It is more work than many other classes at Harvard – you do the readings (which sometimes can take a while to understand), meet with your group outside class before the class, do 4 written responses per week, and have the seminar once a week. Then, you also have the final paper and the presentation. However, each step enriches more your experience and allows you to engage deeper with the material and to take more out of the class. It's a great opportunity to develop an idea of your own and have a whole cohort of people supporting your writing and contributing to the thoroughness of your work. In class, you can engage in meaningful philosophical discussions applicable to real life in a very tangible way, and learn a lot and well.
If you're looking at this Q–guide, it's because you either already are an undergraduate fellow or are thinking of applying to be one. If you have already been accepted, congratulations! This is a wonderful course and opportunity to join a great community of students engaged in political and moral philosophy; I learned so much from my classmates (all of whom are interested in a diversity of topics and bring many perspectives to the table) and from Arthur and Bethany. Joining this course, expect to leave seminars feeling like you've deeply engaged with the content and its limitations, and sometimes, that you have no idea what just happened. But that's completely acceptable, because everyone in the fellowship is in the same boat, and over time, you'll grow more and more comfortable with the content. If you are thinking of applying to be an undergraduate fellow, you absolutely should do so. If you don't, you may be missing out on one of the best courses and experiences at Harvard!
Please take this course. It is not easy, and it is not all smooth, but it is a fantastic academic experience. You will learn a lot, including valuable academic skills, and you will gain an inquisitive perspective into many issues. Professor Applbaum will push your limits but he cares a great deal about his students doing well, is very insightful, and is incredibly kind. He challenges his students in a unique way that might feel overwhelming sometimes but it is incredibly thoughtful, thought–provoking, and helpful.
The Safra workshop is an island in the sea of Harvard's confusion about what a liberal arts education is for.
