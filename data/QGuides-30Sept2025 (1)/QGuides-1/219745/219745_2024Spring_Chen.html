This is a somewhat niche but very well–taught course! Yiling is an amazing lecturer, and the small class size definitely helps––she is very responsive to any questions asked during lecture and isn't afraid to re–explain a point if needed. It feels more like an applied math course at times (not surprising, given that this was originally in the applied math department), so be prepared to think a lot about gradients and not a lot about coding. You also stay squarely in the realm of convex problems, so if you're approaching this from a machine learning interest, you may not get a lot out of this course. That being said, this course wasn't super heavy, so I think it's a good second or third course to take if you just want to learn about different variations of gradient descent or finally learn what the dual of a problem is.
This course is very theoretical. If you are not familiar with writing formal proofs, this class will likely be challenging. In general, the problem sets are not extremely long but there is a lot of material to cover. To understand each lecture, significant amount of time is required outside of class to fill in missing information. Overall, it was a pretty challenging course and was very time consuming.
Yiling is great! Kind! The lectures are fun! Exciting! Take the class! If you have the change!
CS128 is a fantastic course (and a great follow–on to AM121). My proof–writing and computational skills improved across the course, and Professor Yiling led effective lectures. The hidden GOAT of the course, however, was the TF, Ali. He was always available, and his office hour sessions were incredibly useful. I can't recommend this class highly enough, and I think that anyone passionate about computer science, optimization, or ML should take it!
It's a fairly new course, but they did a pretty good job this year.

Lectures: Lectures are good. Professor Chen is a great instructor.

Assignments: There's like 6 homeworks. Honestly, all of them were pretty easy with the exception of the last one (that was due during finals period), so start that one early

Exams: Exams are not too hard, but you should take the time to study for them (I wet the bed on the second due to inadequate preparation). There's not a lot of practice problems, so make sure you have a strong conceptual understanding of the material (i.e. understand what's being taught in lecture and the readings on a deep level).

Would recommend the course. It'll probably get better and have a few tweaks next year, but wasn't bad at all. Honestly one of the most informative courses I've had at Harvard. Convex opt is a really cool and useful field in mathematics!
Overall, I enjoyed the course. I thought the lectures were compelling, and the problem sets were fairly interesting. That being said, it was a lot of work and frankly quite challenging. Most of the students in the course seem to come with a very strong theoretical math background and almost all of the content is abstract (very little coding) so you can feel a bit left behind if you are more of an applied / coding person with less math background like myself. I found the midterms quite challenging.
Yiling is a fantastic lecturer who is also super sweet, you should probably spend effort getting to know Yiling.

Material wise the lectures are all theory, and homework has some implementations, and exams are all theory. Homeworks and exams are somewhat difficult, probably similar to math 25 or stat 111 exam level. The classmates in the course are fairly strong so the average scores were high.

Boyd as the textbook was very good. We covered a lot about convex sets, functions, and transformations in the first half; duality, first and second order methods in the second half.

Overall the class has good classmates, good lectures, fairly reasonable exams and homeworks, totally recommended.
The course was pretty solid, but a little slow in covering content. The problem sets were more technically challenging than conceptually challenging.
CS 128 is a great course, and one that is arguably quite fundamental if you are interested in the linear combination of {optimization, machine learning, data science, statistics}. I personally came into this class with a machine learning bend, and since modern machine learning is effectively optimization, I loved that the course taught these techniques from a fundamentals perspective.

Now sure... modern deep learning doesn't really fit the traditional optimization paradigm. Nevertheless, a whole of things do (econ, operations research, statistics) + this technical view of optimization has laid a lot of helpful groundwork in my mind. CS 128 is also great practice with the "grittier" part of mathematics (eigenthings, hessians, matrix calculus, etc.). In it, you will learn how to 1) recognize convexity, 2) prove properties on convex optimization techniques, and 3) learn the actual grits of some cool & important concepts like duality.

My only gripe with the course is that it moved slowly at times. I estimate it could probably cover approximately 20% more content quite comfortably (with epsilon more work from students).

Yiling is a bodacious lecturer, and extremely cogent. She also is willing to answer any manner of question! Ali was one of the best TF's I've hadhe is patient, kind, and so well–versed in the material. It's a shame he won't be around in the course's next iteration :(
Excellent class, extremely well taught. The material is very mathematical but still very applicable to programming. Only 6 psets and they are all very doable, except I found the last one to be extremely time consuming. A great course for understanding more of the mathematical background behind how machine learning models are trained using optimization algorithms.
I tremendously enjoyed CS 128 this semester. Professor Chen is a fantastic lecturer with great insights to share about the course material in lectures and office hours, and more importantly, she cares a lot about the learning and wellbeing of her students. The teaching fellow Ali was similarly wonderful in his sections and office hours regarding illuminating the motivating concepts behind course topics. Overall the course is run quite well with problem sets that engage students well but are not unbearably challenging, and both Professor Chen and Ali were very responsive on Ed to the comments of students. I would highly recommend this course and attending all of the lectures if possible.
Yiling is a great prof and the course material is very interesting. Psets and exams are fair, and it's not a crazy time commitment.
I think this is a good class, and you'll learn about Convex Optimization, which is a very useful field to know. While this course gives a good review of convex optimization, I expected it to be a bit more rigorous given reviews from the first iteration of the course, so I'm not sure if they dumbed it down a bit this year. In lecture, we cover some proofs but the proofs aren't really that difficult, and we don't really follow things at the rigor of the B & V textbook. While I understand this course doesn't aim to cover all the content in the textbook, I feel as if for the content they do cover, they could match that level of rigor.

Lectures: Lectures were good and clearly presented.

Homework: As with the rigor of the class, I expected the homeworks to be a bit more challenging, especially the first couple. I think HW5 and HW6 were closer to the difficult level I was expecting. Though, they're reasonable, and they don't take too long to complete. Took me about 5–7 hours to complete each homework assignment.

Exams: the tests were fair, and they give us adequate material to practice. Practice problems in the B & V textbook are also good if you want more preparation.

Overall, this is a good course. However, it kind of felt dry since there were so few students in the course. I don't think this course needs to cap enrollment in future iterations, as the course is still more organized than other courses that have been for a long–time (like CS181). I think the course would actually be a better experience if more students took the course, as convex optimization is a very interesting subject.
Yiling is an incredible lecturer. She really knows convex optimization so well, and is able to distill the entire textbook into the most important concepts, which she goes over in lecture. I never felt like I came away from a lecture wondering "why did we learn this?" because she would connect it back to the overarching subject of Convex Optimization.
I was initially hesitant about taking the class because it felt more like a math class than a CS class. In the end, the class really grew on me; it may feel pressuring at times to rush all of Harvard's applied ML courses, but having a good understanding of optimization––arguably the foundation of ML––makes black–box ML algorithms less mysterious.
