Take this class (if you like math). I have lots to say, but if you don't read all of it, here's my most important point — more than teaching me math and preparing me for future studies (which it did very, very well), this course made me a smarter person. I can't fully describe it, and indeed it's somewhat uncanny — but after a semester of working through Math 55 problem sets, I genuinely notice that my mind is much sharper; I think more intelligently about the world (and I honestly didn't think that was possible) far outside of math. What more could I every ask from a course?

Math 55 has a reputation. But no, it doesn't take over your life; you can still take other tough classes, be part of some clubs, and have time for your friends (believe me, I did it, and many others alongside me). Don't be fazed by the "hours per week" — it's much more manageable than you think. And yes, it's hard. But that's the best part. It's more than a class. You make so many close friends and find a wonderful community with your classmates, CAs, and the math department as a whole. Think of it this way: The College claims the First–Year Seminar Program is designed to help you integrate into the Harvard academic community — and that's true, but Math 55 is even better.

The hardest part of the course isn't the content. It's the speed and the homework. But Joe Harris is amazing (and I'm sure whoever they assign to teach it in the future will be, too). I truly didn't notice the speed we were moving at — it always feels like he is moving slowly (in a good way), but he manages to cover so much content in a way that every person in the room understands. I have nothing but praise for him. Oh, and his lecture notes are the best. Side note — follow Joe Harris wherever he goes in his post–55 future. You won't regret it.

Now for the best part — the homework. Math 55 problem sets are, well, a rite of passage. Yes, they're harder — in fact, I'll admit that they're harder than they need to be for you to learn the content. But solving the problems (whether on your own, or, more often than not, with your soon–to–be best friends) and writing up your proofs engages you with the content in a new way unlike any other assignment in any other course. Believe me, the course is the way it is for a reason — for you to learn, have fun, and make friends. If you like math, take advantage of it while you're here. There is quite literally no better exercise for your brain.

You have to live it to know it, but Math 55 is a truly transformational experience unlike any other. In hindsight, I would pick Harvard over any other school just to take this course. There is no better way to start your Harvard journey. And you'll have so, so much fun along the way — from way too many inside jokes to memorable late nights in the math lounge. Enjoy.

Professor Harris is the GOAT. He is absolutely fantastic to have and I cannot recommend his courses highly enough. Last year on the Q, he got a perfect 5/5 in his evaluations and he ought to this year as well – he's that good. He is everything you want in a professor: enthusiastic, always has a smile on his face, knows the material inside and out, and very clearly loves teaching. Harris covers abstract and tricky material with such lucidness and clarity. He perfectly balances building theory with providing intuition and motivation for what he is writing on the blackboard – this is arguably more important, so pay attention in lecture. As a matter of fact, actually go to lecture! Many people get in the habit of not going to lecture and reading the lecture notes instead. No matter how smart you think you are, Harris is a world–class mathematician who has been doing this for 3 times as long as you've been alive, so his insight and intuition is something we can all learn from.

Enough of praising Joe Harris, let's get to the actual course. 55 forms a basis (no pun intended) of all your future mathematics education, with the fall course focusing on linear algebra and group theory. There's not much to be said here. We use the standard texts, Artin and Axler, and adhere to them rather closely. Read the textbooks! Whenever I found something to be unclear or I felt a little behind, just a half hour of reading was all it took to clear up any confusion. We will cover just about all of Axler and chapters 2, 6, 7 of Artin. One thing that unfortunately is not in either textbook that I know many of my classmates were confused by was the notion of tensor products – these are actually important and will show up later in the course, so if they don't make sense to you, be sure to ask for help.

For the last three or so weeks of the course, we study representation theory, which I found to be one of my favorite parts of the course. Representation theory is sort of a combination of group theory and linear algebra, so it's nice to see everything come together. We use Fulton–Harris as our text for this portion. Yes, that Harris is Professor Harris, another reason you should go to lecture for representation theory. Be warned, though, that the representation theory tends to be more abstract and terse than the previous material – it's at a graduate level, not undergrad. It's by no means impossible, but requires more dedicated effort to keep up. Fortunately, the problem sets tend to be less intensive here.

Now, 55 has a reputation of being infamously difficult. I have not found that to be the case at all. For my background, I was not a math prodigy coming into Harvard. I had qualified for AIME a few times and had seen a little bit of linear algebra and group theory before. I'd like to dispel the notion that everyone in 55 is an IMO gold medalist. Yes, there are IMO kids in 55, but honestly they seem to be on the same level as everyone else. With that said, 55 is certainly time intensive. Start problem sets early, go to office hours, and don't get in the habit of using late days unless you have to. Problem sets are graded fairly.

One final word, and advice for some of you reading this: while most people in 55 are able to do well in the course, I truthfully don't think everyone is best served by being in 55. There is, unfortunately but not unusually for Harvard freshmen, a contingent of students in this class who are taking it only to say their schedule is hard. Most of these students are either future stats or CS concentrators for whom the material in 55 is not particularly useful, which at times fosters a culture of one–uppiness and tryhardness among some circles in this class, rather than the more friendly, congenial atmosphere I have found while taking Physics 16. I do not mean this with offense or intend to scare anyone away, but I just want to point it out.

In light of this, I would say if you plan on concentrating in math or doing a joint concentration that includes math, definitely take this course. However, if you already know that you aren't interested in studying math and feel like taking 55 just to say you took it, I would impress upon you to reconsider. Best of luck :)

If you're on the fence between 25 and 55, take 55! Although the class is less difficult than its reputation from the 90s and early 2000s advertises, it is by no means trivial. As someone who enrolled in the class without any group theory and representation theory experience, I had to dedicate a significant amount of time at the beginning of the year doing catch up and understand the same concepts that my peers would understand quite easily. However, if you optimize your collaboration with friends on the psets, start working on assignments early, and review the lecture notes after class to gain a firmer grasp of the material, you will be just fine! The community is really what differentiates 55 from other courses in my schedule –– from the wonderful CAs to the number of brilliant, curious, and humble friends I've made thanks to this class, they have truly been the ones that made the class special. Although the psets can be very difficult at times and I've indeed had to decline extracurricular involvements due to the amount of time that I knew I needed to dedicate to this class, it is absolutely worth it.

Professor Harris is a phenomenal lecturer and professor, and at times I felt that his lectures have almost transformed into story–telling in the way that he steps through the motivations of various key concepts and then reinforces them with examples. His curriculum, consisting of four larger units, is very well structured, though because we are speedrunning through so many different concepts and units, it has been difficult to enjoy what you're learning at times, but getting to representation theory and seeing how previous units tie so nicely together is incredibly satisfying. Overall, it's a class worth taking and spending 20 hours per week on, but only if you are certain of how you can balance your schedule and social life outside of this class.
