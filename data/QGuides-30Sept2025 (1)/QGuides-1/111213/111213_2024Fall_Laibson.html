I took this class as an undergrad to satisfy the macro theory requirement. David Laibsons half was amazingnot too difficult, but still very, very interesting, especially as a math major. The second half was less great. Lagakos is amazingengaging, funny, and definitely knows/loves the content. That said, the content itself is not the most interesting basically eating your macro vegetables before getting to more interesting content in field courses. It is VERY repetitive, with each lecture and problem being a small variation on one of two setups. But again, not Lagakoss fault at all he is quirky and amazing and a pleasure to listen to.

That said, Lagakoss half had some badly–defined pset problems as in, not super clear what was being asked. Those psets could have done with a second draft thats my biggest advice for improving the course. Also, less of a focus on this monotonous defining an equilibrium, where I have to write effectively the exact same conditions over and over and over on psets and exams I must have written a nearly identical paragraph a dozen times for this course. Last bit of room for improvementsome of the second half psets were only solvable by quite creative algebraic manipulation, which is a bit annoying/a time sink. But not the end of the world.

You dont need a macro theory background to take this course, although it might be nice to have a bit of a macro background to avoid a little catch–up game in the second (growth) half. And some real analysis would help. Dont stress about studying for the midterm/final. The weighting is a bit scary, but youll be fineits all quite straightforward if you go to lecture and know the content. No tricks (:

Also, David Laibson is amazing. He has so many fun things to say!!
I think this class is a good if you are interested in understanding what grad school (macro) coursework looks like. The models you learn are used in macro research and it's super exciting to get to learn the extensions of stuff learned in undergrad macro.

That said, this class is definitely not easy it does take a significant amount of time to understand and work through the psets so I would budget a lot of time for this. Prof. Laibson's half was very clearly taught, with pretty clear lecture + section slides, and was very well–organized. He's also very accessible and happy to talk to students!

I found the second half a little tougher to figure out, especially because the pset questions were a little more vague and unclear (e.g. at times unclear what exactly we needed to solve for). Get ready for lots of tedious algebra / needing to find smart algebra tricks to answer the questions too. Lectures were still clearly taught, but a lot of time in class was spent on the set up of models in class, but I felt that the evaluation of the model and key takeaways were often rushed through at the end of lecture. Sometimes section time was also taken to cover mandatory material, which was not great for students who had time conflicts with section (since section was stated to be optional in the first half). The midterm was also significantly more of a time crunch in this half of the class.

In summary: first half was a little more well organized than the second half, find a pset group to work with, go to section and office hours if you can

This course will probably have a bimodal distribution for its ratings. In the first section of this review, I will discuss the first half of the class. In the second section, I discuss the second half. Finally, I conclude and suggest avenues for future exploration.

First half: David L. is such a good professor! He is so nice and really cares about everyone. The material can be a bit dry at time since the first half is really about methods, getting you familiar with dynamic program, Bellman equation, stochastic processes, etc, but he is a very engaging lecturer. He's done this class forever, so lectures are incredibly efficient at getting you to know what you need to know and understand it at a deep level. HWs could be a bit long but nothing too egregious; very clear and relevant questions. He also uploads solutions a day in advance so that if you get stuck, you aren't stuck forever and can get a little hint. Midterm was incredibly fair, maybe a tad on the easier side.

Second half: David L. is pretty funny and seems like a great guy, but not always the best professor. Lectures tended to run over time and we wouldn't finish all the content in models, so he made it so that sections covered new content that wasn't taught in class. Homework assignments were not clearly worded and took a very long time to complete. The midterm was also confusingly worded and on the long side for the amount of algebra required. It was also a bit strange when he pointed at an Asian student on the first day and asked if he was from Japan to make a point about economic growth across countries (the student was of Chinese origin). His research is incredibly interesting though, and his macrodevelopment class next semester sounds exciting.

Takeaways: This is a required class for the PhD sequence so there is not much choice there (unless you are an undergraduate, of which there were several in this class; not sure if this class is the best choice for undergraduates since 2010A is probably a much stronger signal unless you plan on taking 2010D in the spring). David Laibson is excellent. The second half will likely change next year since David Lagakos was a visiting professor; even if he is teaching it next time, I'm sure it'll be more polished the second time around. Mixed feelings overall about how much I gained from this class.
