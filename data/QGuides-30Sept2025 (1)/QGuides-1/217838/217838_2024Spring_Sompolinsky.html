So, you're interested in "computational neuroscience." A classic blunder.

The primary assumption in computational neuroscience is that we can model the human brain with mathematical models. For simplicity, we almost always assume that these models are linear. Oh, also, we don't have any axiomatic basis for the models we choose; mostly, we assume that the brain behaves in a certain way and we just... "poof" a model into existence that captures that behavior.

This actually isn't uncommon in biological sciences. Often, the true system dynamics are too complicated to derive a perfect model from first principles, so you make some assumptions and hope that the model you poofed into existence captures the dynamics you expect and can make reasonable predictions. Then, you go test whether that's true. The orgo rules are a great example of something really complicated being modeled very well in a simple, predictable manner.

The issue is that computational neuroscience skips the validation part after it poofs its models into existence. Maybe this just isn't the part of comp neuro that Sompolinsky focuses on, but I was extremely unimpressed by the models we obtained. They rarely had a testable biological interpretation, and when they did, the tests always always ALWAYS showed that the model sucked. Your brain isn't doing linear transformations to compress sensory data, and to suggest that this is the case is... maybe reasonable until you test the theory and see that it is completely untrue. Any sane person then rejects the model and tries something new. Not us, though! We persevere with our blatantly wrong models.

Eventually, this class just completely gives up pretending like computational neuroscience has anything to do with the brain and gets down to the really interesting stuff–––how neural networks (in the computer science sense) simulate learning. Sadly, this is where the class is just pathetic. You end up studying extremely basic networks (linear binary classifiers), which are being taught by a statistical physicist who freely admits that these networks are not biologically plausible, but who strongly believes that the brain should behave according to simple rules so we restrict ourselves to these absurdly basic models.

So, this begs the question: if this isn't how the brain works, then why are we taking a sub–introductory level machine learning class offered by the physics department? My opinion: you're being scammed. Take CS 181, or, better yet, pop on down to MIT for one of their ML classes. Or, better better yet, go take a normal neuroscience class. Clearly you're interested in neuro, and I'm telling you, this isn't it. This is baby ML at best, and pseudoscience at worst.

This is a great class if you are interested in comp neuro, or more generally physics and the brain. You learn models for long–term and short–term memory, how the brain tracks the spacial features of your environment through attractor networks, and lots of cool stuff + one last lecture that connects everything to the philosophy of mind. Sompolinsky is one of the pioneers of the field, and you get to learn from him the models he developed, as wellng as a few latter lectures dedicated to the stuff he is currently working on. After taking this course you will have the necessary knowledge of the major models of memory, learning, etc that are need to understand comp neuro literature. It is definitely a physics class, not an mcb class. There is a lot of dynamics, ODEs, stats, geometry, etc. Would recommend having a physics / applied math undergraduate background having taken stat 110 and stat mech (I didn't have these and got by, but had to learn quite a bit along the way).
