Take this class if you want to read good books.
If you're on the fence, TAKE THIS CLASS. Regardless of whether or not you're an English concentrator, this class is beneficial to anyone who loves reading and writing, especially creatively. Professors Mukherjee and Serpell are incredible lecturers, and they put together a truly diverse and engaging syllabus. The TFs were extremely approachable and helpful, and the sections were welcoming (they felt more like a conversation than a seminar). This class encourages you to take a few hours a week to read some seriously good books, and also gives you the incentive to write creatively, if you benefit from that structure! TAKE IT.
If you want to take an English class where you really delve into and discuss the texts, this isn't the class for you. It's mostly just a lecture where the professors tell you how they interpreted the book, and then you move on. That's not to say that their insight isn't interesting, but it can be a bit boring to sit there week after week.
i liked this course! it can be a bit disorganized at times, but the stuff we read is really fun, the assignments are pretty cool (most of them are creative), the course staff is really kind, and lectures are still really wonderful to listen to. it does get pretty theory–heavy, which, in the beginning, was kinda cool, but it eventually didn't feel super applicable. i think it would have been nice to change things up and do close readings every once in awhile rather than strictly focusing on theory, but oh well. still a good course! and required for english concentrators. lol.
Future students, take this class if you want a general, broad overview of contemporary literature through really high–level, engaging lectures. Be willing to do the reading, as that is the single most important component of the class. If you want to explore literature in any more specific capacity, probably opt for one of the English electives that focuses on a particular writer, era, or kind of text, though. Still, I enjoyed this class very much.
This is a fantastic course and I recommend it to anyone, English concentrator or not. That said, the lecture and section will not teach you the skills required to complete the assignments; so, do not take this as your first writing heavy course at Harvard unless you have a strong background from high school or are prepared to attend office hours.
This course is an incredible introduction to some of the great recent works in the English language! Not too hard, and most of the pieces we read in class were very enjoyable. I think the class is very accessible to non–concentrators, but as an upperclassman concentrating in English, I found some of the lecture material a little basic.
This class sucks but just take it. Honestly look at the syllabus and professors beforehand and see if it's worth taking that specific year (they change professors every year). The TF was new this year and the other one quit after like two weeks, which meant that I ended up being graded on creativity for a standard book report (literally lost 10 points for it "not challenging the genre of the book report") when the professor agreed that it wasn't supposed to be graded on creativity, yet my grade never changed because they didn't have the organization or skill to regrade or organize the class or grading consistently (took with Mukherjee and Serpell). Know exactly what they're grading on and ask for the rubric before handing in your first assignment. We also had quizzes at least once a week on the reading but they were for participation, but they weren't? And we never got feedback or the answers on them? And the questions were dumb like "what color was this random character who appears in a single moment of a 300–page novel wearing?" Not on actually understanding the methods or work itself. Check that the TF has experience teaching Harvard English classes as well.
This semester, there were three assignments, each of which took me about 4 hours. Creative writing is faster than academic writing, and 5 pages double–spaced is very short. I never did any of the readings and the assignments aren't based on the readings. I'm not a good writer and I put in very little effort, so I'm probably not quite going to get an A, but it's very doable. The few times I showed up to lecture, I felt bad for the professor because there was never more than one or two people paying attention. Take it if you want a basically zero work class; otherwise I found it pretty uninteresting.
I'd really recommend this class! I know it always gets mixed reviews because it's a difficult balance between what's "mandatory" to cover and what students are interested in, but in general it's a great reading list, not a super heavy workload, and I really got a lot out of the course as a whole.
I highly recommend this course as an intro to English!

Take English 10! I'm a first year and debated between this course and Hum10, and I have to say that English 10 is the right choice for any modern lit enjoyer and potential English concentrator. Serpell and Mukherjee are so knowledgeable (go to their office hours!) and the class was a delight.
Features a fantastic reading list of contemporary literature
I am genuinely grateful to this course because of the professors and readings. The lectures are eye–opening, and the professors care about their work and their students. Going into office hours to speak to them made this course way more enjoyable, as they have so much knowledge to share and are enthusiastic about sharing with students. We also had students share their work on the last day of class, which was a great note to end on :)

The issues with this course I had are mainly related to the TF aspect, which can obviously vary from person to person. We only had one TF leading sections and grading papers for much of the course, which I think posed an issue.

I wasn't totally satisfied with the feedback we got, since I did not personally find it very logical based on the outline or prompt we received, but I think this probably varies! Most people I talked to generally reflected my assessment of grading, as we all generally got positive feedback but were never given A's, which felt really unfair since the essays make up almost your entire grade. It was really frustrating to receive a paper with enthusiastic feedback with one small thing to improve on over and over, with almost none of these supposedly wonderfully argued papers receiving an A. The grading also wasn't super transparent––for some simple assignments, a lot of students, including myself, were docked for not including something that was not in the prompt or rubric. This was a really discouraging class for this reason alone, but at least I now know concentrating in English isn't for me.
I would say that it's really important to pay attention to instructor feedback!
