I really enjoyed this class! it got me thinking about history and histiography in ways that I had never considered before (history as a contingency, etc.) and was really impactful listening to students from the Kennedy school. this isn't your typical lecture class (much more discussion based) but weekly briefs and the short essay help towards collecting your thoughts on the readings! I think I'm a stronger historian because of this class.
– high workload but rewarding the same time. Weekly briefs are also graded generously.
– Professor is not a cynic but rather skeptical with a tint of optimism
– If you want to have a deep dive into modern history from 1900 – now, take this course (no prerequisite)
I love it so much. This is one of the best classes I've ever attended. You actually learn here in every possible way – from reading, from your own writing, from your classmates, from the professor and from the guest speakers. And you learn really useful material. It is not just history, in the way people think about it often – boring, based on memorizing dates and names. It is more than that. You learn about the world TODAY, you start seeing why it is the way it is, and you learn to see the echo of the past in today. This is an extremely useful class for not just policymakers but even those who want to work in the private sector. Because you CAN avoid mistakes of the past if you actually start applying the past correctly.

Of course, this class is also great in terms of giving you a general view and allowing you to form a perspective on the current world order and the reasons for it. Very interesting, very diverse topics, with outstanding discussions that make you think even more.
This is the best course I have taken at Harvard. Applied history goes beyond merely determining what happened in the past. It involves examining why leaders made certain decisions, considering counterfactual scenarios, constructing historical analogies, and using these insights to shape future foreign policy.
The course, to some degree, is what you make it. If you are interested in policy, but find the classes lacking in this regard, use the assignments to engage in what you want. The briefs and the essays offer a great opportunity to go in whatever direction you want so make the most of it.
This was one of the best and most memorable courses I have taken at Harvard (I am in the college not HKS). You engage with the most incredible arrays of concepts, historical incidents, readings, and thoughts from other members of the course . The course description sums up what it's all about so let me tell you about what it cant convey: the depth of knowledge, expertise, varied perspective in the room was truly transformational. You'll hear about the consequences of Afghanistan withdrawal from the perspective of a hostage negotiator there, an Afghani, and a policymaker who contemplated the initial invasion –– and they're all your fellow students. Prof Logevall and his guest speakers are also incredible, kind, and interesting. TLDR if you can get into this class as an undergrad –– even from a non humanities major (I am an AM concentrator), TAKE IT!
TAKE THIS CLASS! This course is a great opportunity to: 1) get to know a world–class faculty member, 2) understand how knowledge of history can be used in a variety of contexts, 3) interact with HKS students and hear about their diverse professional backgrounds, 4) meet great guests. The work is nothing crazy if you enjoy reading and writing, and the content is very practical, providing helpful frameworks that you can apply in other classes/professional contexts.
