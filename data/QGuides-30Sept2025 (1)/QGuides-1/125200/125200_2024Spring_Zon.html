This course was a really fantastic introduction to stem cells, the diseases they treat, and the patients these diseases impact. The most valuable portion of each lecture is the patient presentation, in which we see how a disease and its treatment uniquely impact the individual and their quality of life. Most presenters were compelling, as well, with some variation from week to week. The section portion of the course involves student presentation of assigned papers and discussion. The papers selected each week tended to be very strong, though I do wish some were more recent.

Assignments are not at all painful and entail: a short weekly write–up, one presentation in section, a final paper (divided into an introduction, midterm, and final over the semester). Feedback on the midterm paper, which includes the instructors, was mixed. Be prepared for feedback that doesn't address the salience of your argument, but the beliefs of the instructors on whether the argument is sensible. This is a bit frustrating, as the feedback doesn't require elaboration or evidence; thus, you might hear "X won't work" with very little explanation. The feedback from the section leaders, conversely, was all very helpful! They tended to focus on weaknesses of the argument and its organization with clear suggestions for improvement. Be sure to meet with your TF about the papers before submission. They are very willing to help.

Overall, I would recommend this class to anyone looking for an introduction to stem cells! It isn't perfect, but the unique focus on the patient experience is incredibly valuable and not something that many students get the chance to experience during undergrad.

This class is interesting. If you are coming into it just looking for a survey course on stem cell therapeutics with the chance to hear from actual patients, the class does its job and is great. If you need an upper–level SCRB class that is a relatively low workload, it's great. I perhaps mistakingly came into it expecting a transformative foray into the world of bench–to–bedside stem cell science and the lives of patients, so to me, it ended up feeling like mildly or highly engaging weekly seminars with patient interviews.

That being said, the patients are undoubtedly the highlight of this course as it opens your eyes to the struggle of living with a disease and the good, bad, and ugly of navigating/using medicine. However, the gain you get from hearing the patients is somewhat passive; you have to be actively motivated and interested since the head professors just facilitate Q&A. In other words, the takeaways you form are based on your reception of the information and how you think about it during and after class. While the head professor (either Dr. Zon or Dr. Breault) briefly rehashes the patient in the next class, this is done in 15 minutes and is also somewhat passive. In other words, you get what you put in; there is no formal instruction/presentation on the issues and ethics of the patient experience.

The final paper is strange; you don't get any true instruction on scientific writing beyond just a PowerPoint outline of the scientific method and a logical example paragraph structure. The class very much takes a learn–by–doing approach: for example, you will learn to be more critical by reading more papers, and you'll learn to write a grant by attempting to write one. While there is virtue in this approach, you don't have much guidance during the section (you listen to a student presenting on the paper and just ask the student questions), so you have to ask your TFs for their thoughts and meet with them outside of class to answer further questions and/or strengthen your paper. It doesn't help that there are no set office hours for either the TFs or the professors. On that point, the professors Dr. Zon and Dr. Breault are not particularly accessible as they leave right after the lecture, and often time one or the other missed lectures.

Grading on the final paper and presentation can feel harsh at times; you truly have to put in a lot of effort crafting a wholesome product; the aforementioned lack of comprehensive guidance during the section makes this process a little harder than necessary.

Lastly, the final paper was due on the second day of the reading period, which made finishing the paper a little stressful as it conflicted with my study plans. It would have been much more enjoyable if there were a due date for the paper during the actual final examination period. Therefore, if you're reading this, please be aware of when the deadline for the final paper is well in advance.
