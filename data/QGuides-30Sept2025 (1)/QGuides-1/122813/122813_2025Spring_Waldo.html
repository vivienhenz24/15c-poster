Please note that the following is my opinion, and in no way supposed to be an ad hominem critique of any of the course staff. The anecdotes I include are important to understanding why I have the opinion of the class I do.

My background: CS61, 120, 124, AC209ab, other 2000–level CS, stat classes

I will touch on a couple points:

0 – the readings
Waldo usually puts up 2–3 links for a lecture. One is usually an old paper, sometimes written in a very specific research group, assuming familiarity with terms no one has familiarity with. One is usually a link to a recent whitepaper/documentation (e.g. https://protobuf.dev/). No guidance on how much one should read into one or the other. Some are 60 or more pages, some are just a webpage. 90%+ of the class does not read the papers. When he asked in lecture, "how many of you think you understand how Paxos works," no one raised their hand. When he asked "how many think they do not understand," again no one is bothered enough to raise their hand. His reaction: "That's because none of you have read the paper." He is right. This kind of atmosphere sums up the attitude of students towards the class, and the atmosphere that lectures have.


1 – lectures, the form:
I attended nearly every lecture, kept notes, and still feel like I learned next to nothing. As you probably have heard, the slides are one word each. Ostensibly, this is to remind the professor what the flow is of what he wanted to cover. However, it seemed as though he has forgotten why he put some of the words on the slides, and thus, though he tries to find a way to weave from one word to the next in the context of what is being discussed, the lecture feels like a ramble, rather than us learning anything systematic about a distributed systems topic. This is the case throughout the semester. The most interesting class was when the professor got the CEO of a small Boston area tech company and alum of the class to give a lecture about distributed systems in practice. This was a nice addition. The take–away from this class, ironically though, was "don't build distributed systems unless you really have to."

2– lectures, the content
I think none of the material was particularly relevant to modern distributed systems– though I am still not proficient in them. You come to learn that most of the terms ("RPC, wire protocols, message passing, testing, byzantine failure") are just describing common sense design aspects you will learn anyway when building any larger–scale project. Learning about some obscure aspects of Java was not useful. You do not learn anything about networking, though this layer seems important for really understanding the design of wire protocols. The lectures that actually cover an interesting algorithm/content, like Paxos/Raft/Blockchain, are not well delivered. I learned more from a YouTube video on Raft than from the attempt at explaining the algorithm, which I felt did not cover the basic notions of how a 'quorum' works, for example. These classes felt as though the content was assumed to be known, and only the professor's (usually strong) prior opinion of Blockchain/Raft was being discussed. No real theory of distributed systems is built through the class, it is hard to see how the topics connect to each other. Most people stopped showing up to lecture pretty quickly.

3– the assignments
Nearly everyone in the class used AI to complete the three assignments, the 'design exercises,' as the syllabus allowed. After I coded it myself, I found that ChatGPT O1 could pretty close to one–shot the design exercise based just on the design instructions given. The rest of your task was then to iron out tedious bugs. Many peers reported that they felt like there was "no point in trying to do the coding yourself anyway", because the implementation was mostly just nitpicky design details that didn't teach much about the distributed aspect (e.g. for the distributed messaging app, "If the recipient is logged in, deliver immediately; if not the message should be stored until the recipient logs in and requests to see the message.") This left the in–class demo–days devoid of much interesting discussion. Everyone had pretty much done the same thing, and many groups were just trying to show one another up, or pass something that AI had coded for them off as worthy of extra credit for the peer grading component of the course. Even these opportunities to show off one's work felt demoralizing, unfortunately.
Miraculously, around 3 times more students showed up on demo day than on lecture days. This goes to show that lectures were not necessary to complete the assignment.
The first of the assignments basically was most of the work of the class (though this was not clear from the start) one whole pset was just to redo how messages were communicated for the first assignment. One assignment was to implement Lamport Logical clocks that talk to each other, and most students agreed that they did not see the use in this assignment, that this felt largely like busy–work.
If any feedback was given on assignment, it was usually a couple sentences based on a skim of your work.
The extent of guidance given on the final project was not much more than that we should "demonstrate what we learned in the course" and a couple sentences of feedback on a proposal document. On group size, the professor discouraged any groups larger than two by saying "My general rule is that the difficulty of the final project should be proportional to 2**n, where n is the number of people on the team." This announcement came somewhat late. We were asked to create both the code with testing and documentation, a poster, and a 10–page write–up on our final project. It was not clear why all the different formats were necessary, as only the write–up was looked at it seems like.
Despite all this, I still probably spent around 10h a week on the class.

4 – the grading
Positive: Waldo and the course staff were supportive and gracious of late days for medical emergencies and other valid excuses.

Negative: You are supposed to get enough information from your peers' 15–minute code reviews to give them a grade out of 5. The actual criteria by which you are to grade them are only released a day before the class. In one case, the criteria included that the lamport clock servers that communicate each be their own process, and that this be verified in the code review. This was a minor detail/unclear in the original spec, and many people rushed to redo their implementation, though it was not clear why this was at all important. You were also graded on how well you graded, with the stark warning that your grade would be impacted if your assigned grade was further than 1 point away from the grade the teaching staff give the project

A friend of mine had a Canvas grade of over 96% in 4 graded aspects of the class, coming down basically to two points missed out of 25 based on a misreading of their final project write–up. When the friend wrote an email to the course staff to inquire about some feedback on the final project that could only have been due to a misreading, the professor wrote back to them and said that due to his participation, he would raise their grade from an A– to an A in the class. It seems like grading is essentially up–curved at the very high mean grade that students have in the class (because everyone has done the same on the assignments) and your class grade mostly comes down to the impression the course staff get of your final project. It is still mind–boggling to me that a 96% could equate to an A–. Waldo was unwilling to release information on grade cut–offs:
doing that means I get to deal with an large number of "you gave me three points less than I think I deserve" which is neither productive for learning nor a good useexe of time."
