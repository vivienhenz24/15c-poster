You will dislike this class if you don't show up to lectures, don't do the readings, and wait until the last few days to do the weekly PSET. The PSETS are difficult and take a considerable amount of time, so waiting until Monday to start them is a terrible idea, no matter how efficient you are. It is a waste to not try your best to get a perfect score on every PSET (checking with classmates and going to office hours), as that just forces you to do better on the midterms and final project if you are looking for a good grade in the class. If you give a decent effort and manage your time well, it is absolutely possible to get a near perfect or perfect score on every PSET.

I also read previous Q–Reports for this class saying that the midterms don't actually cover what is taught in class. I heavily disagree. The midterms were completely reasonable. This is not to say they weren't challenging, so don't read this and think you don't have to study for them.

The class was taught phenomenally well. There was never a moment where I asked a question and Wes didn't know how to answer it. His office hours are super helpful as well. Overall, I personally don't think the class is nearly as bad as some of the Q–Report responses say.

This is a class for bio/chem/physics concentrators. This class is very biology–heavy, and at times it felt like the math was secondary to it. That being said, if you are interested in doing research or just seeing topics you have seen before be applied to real–world situations, I highly recommend this class. Wes is an amazing professor and is always available for any help you need. He is clearly passionate about what he teaches, which manifests in his great lectures. The smaller class environment also fosters a more tightly–knit community. I felt like all of his midterms/psets were extremely fair. As for the final project, he is good about advising you along the way, just get started early. Any premed/stem concentrator interested in modeling in research should not hesitate to take this class!
For the first four weeks of class, the scientific articles you will be reading, as well as their accompanying graphs and figures are extremely intimidating, but over time I did gradually develop understanding of the later scholarly articles as I read them, which has profited me not only in terms of the class but in reading scientific papers, in general. The highlight of the class is definitely Wes's teaching. Lecture notes are a must–read and solidify a lot of things. They are well–written and orderly, are as lectures. Very interconnected and brought everything together.

Find a PSET group. Ours ended up being a significant portion of the class. We all sat down together and did problem sets, made our index cards, and worked on our final projects in the same space. These folks saved my life during stressful academic weeks (I was also taking Stat 110.) Make lots of friends!

Do take the first midterm very seriously and study a lot in case the second exam does not go nearly as well (as was the case for myself and some other folks). The exams are not overly hard and the partial grading is nice. There are questions on the exams about the articles you read in class, so actually read them AND go to the class the next day to get his summary of the article as sometimes his (well–thought–out) analogies and background of the presentation appear on the exam. For example, we had action potential curves explained to us as operating like how a toilet flushes (take the class for details, LOL) and that did appear on the exam. Wes is usually good about letting you know which analogies are more important than others. You also can take a front and back index card into the exam.

Beware that the percentage of problem sets in the grading is not that high despite the amount of time you'll be spending on them (hopefully this changes) and that a significant amount of your work is to BUCKLE DOWN ON YOUR FINAL PROJECT. Do not make the mistake of thinking that because there's technically no final exam that the project will be hunky–dory or you will suffer. The class stress level rose significantly during the week that the paper was due.

This is an excellent class that I highly recommend you take if you are at all interested in the life sciences/biology/chemistry. Having a solid background in calculus (AP Calc AB is enough imo, but do the first diagnostic pset to figure out your own comfort level) is helpful, along with a high–school level background with biology/chemistry. You'll likely want a basic working knowledge of concepts like rate law, cell cycles, action potentials, etc. You can definitely take this course as a substitute to Math 21A, and possibly 1B.

Upsides: 1 problem set a week (5–8 questions, reasonable difficulty and TFs are very helpful), Wes is an amazing lecturer and I highly recommend going to his office hours. Final project instead of a final exam, and the two midterms are honestly fine and very similar in difficulty/content to the practice midterms. Being a small class, it's really easy to get close with and make friends with your coursemates make a group to check psets with/study with, and you'll do great. Grading on the psets and exams are fair and Wes really does try to give as many partial points as possible when grading the midterms.

Downsides: the course material does speed up in pace somewhat significantly after the first midterm. There are weekly readings (papers) that you should really try to keep on top of, because otherwise studying for midterms will be a pain (reading–based questions on the midterms make up ~15% of the exam grade). Wes does offer a good amount of meetings/consultations/advice for the final project, but you really need to pick a good initial paper to have a good project and get started on modelling/simulating in Mathematica well before reading period so you can debug and fix your modifications without getting overwhelmed.

This course allows for you to have rough patches, but it definitely will require a mass commitment.
