This is a great class for those interested in learning about the major thinkers and ideas that inform our modern understanding of politics.
TAKE THIS!! Especially if you're interested in political theory (and obviously the history of political theory). Prof March is an excellent lecturer, and engages questions & discussion throughout the class period. take time to engage with the readings (which are fair & manageable) , and ask questions in class. i found every lecture intellectually stimulating and i actually wanted to go to class each week. awesome course
Very good readings and certainly an interesting class but could use a little better structure.
The grading felt very non–transparent: you get minimal comments on your writing (maybe 3–4 sentences with grammatical edits). Otherwise Prof March and Miranda are both kind and answer questions openly. I found it more difficult to do well in this course than other government courses.
March is unfortunately quite outspoken in his views –– I don't think he would be biased in terms of grading if it came to an appeal, but it is unnerving to consistently hear it only from one side.
Depending on the TF, this course is not worth taking. While the lectures were enjoyable and fun, they were sometimes quite loud under the most recent lecturer, and the sections left a lot to be desired. As a sophomore taking the course in the spring, this was my least enjoyable section in college. I would almost go so far as to say I actively disliked going. The TF had too much of an agenda in discussing her views of each author, especially in comparison to what the professor had spoken about that week. Anytime anyone asked a question in section, rather than encouraging discussion and debate in response, the TF took it upon herself to respond. Her responses took up a significant chunk of section and sometimes her "explanations" only made me more confused, but there was never much time to gain clarification before she took questions (as though it were a Q&A) from other students.

Also note, section is an hour and fifteen minutes rather than the (in my experience) more traditional hour–long period.
