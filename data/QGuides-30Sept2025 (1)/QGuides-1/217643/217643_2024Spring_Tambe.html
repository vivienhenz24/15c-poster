I really enjoyed the lectures in class. Professor Tambe brings in a whole bunch of different professors who do AI research across a spectrum of fascinating fields. However, this class was logistically a mess. While the people running this class are very kind, its just very unstructured. We had to do a giant survey paper in 5–7 person teams, and the teams were formed in a very unorganized way on the 2nd class meeting (which I believe caused some of the messiness as nobody knew each other yet). This resulted in my team having to break up and reform a month into the class, and start our work over again, and then we were super behind, which chased us through to delayed our progress with our final project. I would've appreciated way more time on the final project, and less of what felt like stressful busywork (the super long survey paper).
I don't regret taking this class, mostly because it gave me the opportunity to do a really interesting final project and learn about some cool research being done in the field of AI for social impact. Guest lectures can get a bit dry, though, and the addition of the survey paper as an assignment was a bit of a drag.
The topics covered in this class are interesting, so would recommend taking if you're interesting in any application of AI for social impact. The guest lecturers were great too! However, I do think this iteration of the class had a lot more work than I had heard previously, so keep that in mind (they added a ~30 page survey paper this year). Some of the assignments also felt pretty arbitrary, like the paper presentation towards the end of the semester, and I also think the larger assignment deadlines could have been adjusted so they didn't all overlap with each other. When there were no major deadlines coming up though, the class was pretty chill.
So much busywork. A survey paper, weekly reading posts, but didn't really feel like we were learning anything super deeply. Was more so a collection of shallow information than a deeply entrenched learning experience. Not a lot of specialized support on final project, you and your group are kind of on your own for that.
The class idea itself I generally love. You work on three items throughout the semester:
– a survey paper/literature review of the state–of–the–art AI methods in a particular field
– a smaller presentation on an AI paper
– a project where you work with a team to develop an AI model or algorithm concerning a social field (health, conservation, energy, humanities)

Each week you will read articles of how AI has been used in the world for different social goals. Some of the authors of those papers come in then for further discussion, so it's a nice text–to–class kind of feel.

The class itself was somewhat disorganized in that the project felt "pushed off" to favor the survey paper, so most of the work is done outside of the class and less of it in the class. However, I would very much recommend this course to individuals who are interested in a collaborative environment where you can learn about AI's current status and where its trajectory is for the future in terms of societal outcomes.
Im someone whos generally interested in AI for Social Impact and the project/QReports for this class made it seem like a great option. I was wrong.

Pros:
– If you pick a good final project and group, it can honestly be really cool and fun; thankfully I got lucky with this part.
– The survey paper (new for this year) is long and tedious, but can still be a good learning experience.
– Some of the guest speakers do awesome work and are great to meet before/after/outside of class.

Cons:
– The atmosphere during class is awful genuinely one of the most uninspiring environments Ive experienced at Harvard. 90% of the class is on their laptops (including TFs), guest lecturers sometimes talk for the full 75 minutes with barely any breaks or discussions, and multiple times the teaching staff would come in with no content for the day but expect everyone to stay for a work period.
– The teaching staff are nice but can be pretty ridiculous. They randomly decided to add an assignment that wasnt on the syllabus halfway through the semester, constantly loosened/tightened requirements and deadlines, and were too vague about assignments. An example: at 3pm on the day of the final project deadline, they sent an email clarifying that an 8–page paper meant maximum 8 pages and insisting that we cut our papers to that length if they were longer. Seriously?
– The assignments can be poorly thought through insensible page counts, back–to–back deadlines, paper responses that prompt zero originality or discussion (these ended up being 300–word summaries where everyone says the same thing and nobody cares to read what anyone else wrote).
If you're looking for an intellectually stimulating experience, avoid this class –– consider CS 276 instead

Highly recommend this class, it is excellent and you will learn a lot!
Overall, I enjoyed the class–– Professor Tambe is very qualified to teach it, and it was both a reasonable workload with meaningful paper–writing assignments. I learned some helpful things towards AI4SG and feel more empowered to do more in this field.

However, the attendance policy was quite stringent without much transparency. The TFs also seemed arbitrarily strict on certain policies. I would have wished for the professor to be more accessible and involved with student projects. I also would have hoped for more technical rigor in this class, like learning about how to detect certain types of algorithmic biases.

I would still recommend this class, but with just a handful of small reservations.
