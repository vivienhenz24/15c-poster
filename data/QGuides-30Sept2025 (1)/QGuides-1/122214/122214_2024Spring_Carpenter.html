Don't take this course. I find bureaucracy to be quite fascinating and I came in with high hopes for this course, and while this course definitely did teach me more about major theories of bureaucracy, the course was needlessly harsh and unforgiving at every step of the way. Many aspects of the course were opaque or arbitrarily changed (grade breakdown was unclear until the very end of the semester, paper topics weren't announced until a week before the due date, numerical grading scales for papers weren't explained until late in the semester, etc.). Professor Carpenter refused to grant extensions and was just needlessly harsh in running the course (e.g. it felt like we were constantly being reminded about the mandatory attendance policy, mandatory participation, and other grading–related policies), which added a lot of wholly unnecessary stress. Lectures were fine and informative, but "class participation" felt high–pressure and stressful, like we were being quizzed, and the papers were also hyped up in a way that added to the stress. Even though I have a good grade in this course and "ended up fine," I regret taking this course because I truly don't think this course was run in a way that supports students, and I cannot in good conscience recommend this course.
You get out of this class what you put into it. Attend all the lectures and sections and you will walk away with some fascinating ways to view the organizations all around us. At minimum, skim all the readings so you can debate things in class (because Prof. Carpenter *does* like calling on people in lecture!). It is a lot more fun to engage with the class than to float through it.

If you are interested in the premise of this course (understanding bureaucracies), you will probably like it. The structure is that you learn 4 distinct theories of how bureaucracies function, and then see whether they accurately describe real–world bureaucracies in different case studies (e.g. the U.S. military or the FDA). Do note that the course is almost entirely positive rather than normative. You are not asked to consider the ideal way for a bureaucracy to function or which type of bureaucracy is the best at achieving its goals. Analysis is simply: bureaucracy A has characteristic B so it can be described by theory C. As a result, the course does not offer much if you want to learn how to run a bureaucratic organization.

Nevertheless, it is well–taught and the case studies are interesting. The papers are manageable, although instructions are not always perfectly clear. The midterm is not difficult. Readings are dense but can be skimmed. Would recommend.
Great opportunity to delve deeper into the theory of organizations and bureaucracies.
This course is an interesting one. The first weeks are spent learning theory about how and why organizations function. Specifically, students examine Weberian theories of bureaucracy, theories of organizations which stress bounded rationality (i.e. behavioral), among others.

The next weeks of the course are spent on the military. This is an administrative history of the army since the early 1900s, so if you're interested in learning about the laws which have constructed the modern military, this will be fruitful for you. The class is heavily weighted toward this module, and is full of ROTC kids and students interested in the military. Prof. Carpenter also invites several guest lecturers who are informative and engaging.

The next weeks of the course focus on the FDA, using Carpenter's 700 page book as its reading and starting point for discussion. Like the army, this is an administrative historyso, while dryit's interesting to see how the actual sausage gets made in terms of regulations and rulemaking.

The course concludes with a survey of the Federal Reserve and financial regulation.

My one qualm was with the section component of the class. Angelo, the TF, is clearly really smart, but does not manage section time well, and often rushes to cram half the content into the last three minutes of section. Moreover, the prompts were terribly designed (unclear if this is an Angelo or a Prof. Carpenter problem), and the expectations for essays felt terribly communicated. To quote one senior: "the essays in this class were more complicated than my thesis."

Despite my qualms about the papers, this course is interesting not only for the administrative history and organizational theory, but also for the tidbits of history you learn along the way; lectures sometimes digress into discussions of the finer points of the 2008 financial crisis or Operation Desert Storm. Prof. Carpenter is a wonderful lecturer and I feel like I left this course with a great amount of information not only about bureaucracy but also about how the world in general works. I would recommend for those who care about figuring out how the government actually works.
Readings and classroom discussions are interesting, but DO NOT put off any of the papers. They are dense, and the prompts are likely to be a little convoluted. There is not much work outside of the papers, but the papers themselves are a lot of work.
Definitely take this class if you're interested in learning about how government, and organizations more generally, operate. The lectures and readings are great, and the assignments are a bit time–consuming but reasonable and worthwhile.
