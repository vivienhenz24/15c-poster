What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

Comments
I highly recommend taking this class if taught with Professor Patil. Prof. Patil is knowledgeable, kind, warm, engaging, passionate, and curious. He will get you excited about the content being discussed, and will not stop until you truly understand it. He is also extremely accessible outside of class, and more than happy to set up weekly or biweekly 1–on–1 meetings to talk about whatever it may be. Prof. Patil also does a great job of asking the right questions and getting students to ask good questions themselves. I cannot say enough good things about him.

All of this being said, this was the first time Prof. Patil taught this course, so the structure was a bit unorganized at times. Instead of spending the first half the semester on Mark Johnston (and his book Surviving Death) and the second half on Dharmakīrti and his writings, we spent the first two thirds on Johnston and only the last third on Dharmakīrti. This was a result of just how difficult it was to get through Johnston, since Prof. Parimal would have us go over material as many times as necessary until he was satisfied with our level of understanding. Thus, I felt that the last section, on Dharmakīrti, was a bit rushed. Further, the Dharmakīrti text was extremely difficult to read and the content it self was hard to understand. I also thought that some of the lectures/concepts in the Dharmakīrti section were too analytic and abstract from my taste. While Johnston was certainly analytic, all the concepts in this book were much more motivated (for me, at least), and so it was easier and more exciting to do the nitty–gritty work needed to understand his arguments.

Finally, the content is extremely interesting and rewarding. The main concepts discussed were that of the person/self, in various shapes and forms. Mark Johnston's book completely changes the way you think about who you are (as a person), and his understanding of what a person is is revolutionary and has made me see myself in a whole different light. Dharmakīrti, as well, is an interesting philosopher and his argumentative strategies are gripping. I had no prior background in Buddhism or Buddhist philosophy, and felt that learning about Dharmakīrti also served as a good introduction to Buddhist philosophy broadly but especially Buddhist metaphysics of the self.
I can recommend this course for anybody interested in personal identity theory and its relationship to metaphysics, meriology, the questions of identity and constitution.
You will think deeply about what it means to be human.
This is a challenging course but is very well worth it. You learn how powerful logic and philosophy can be, and you also get to doubt huge philosophical questions. It really makes you push yourself to rethink beliefs about mortality and what life and death actually means.
If you've ever been curious about religion and, specifically, different conceptions of self–identity, take this course. Be warned: this course is not a super rigid course, rather loose in structure. If you are someone who needs clear structure in a course, Phil 155 is not for you. But if you are okay being along for the ride, this course is absolute dynamite. Seldom have I taken a course and been so involved with the material, that I found myself questioning the validity of my (fictional) narrative that I've carried all my life. This class pokes holes in the promises you told yourself as a child, the ideas you have for self–gain, and the ways in which you want to live life. I found myself questioning the very nature of my personhood and, as a result, desiring to round out my morals and values through the "greater onward rush of humanity." Besides, Parimal is a S–tier professor. He's the perfect blend of crazy, caring, and genius, and he really cares about meeting with students and walking them along the path of philosophy. I highly recommend this course for those brave souls (if souls even exist) who want to question all they know.
