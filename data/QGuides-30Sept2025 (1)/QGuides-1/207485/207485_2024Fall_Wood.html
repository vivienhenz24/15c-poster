Here are my thoughts on different areas of this course please note that much of this is subjective to whether the lecturer remains the same next fall.

There are several areas where this course needs improvement:
1. Class pacing: The structure of every class isn't strictly a lecture; instead you're given class worksheets and you complete the problems in tandem with the lecturer. I have generally liked this type of class structure before, but I think it has meant that a lot of proof strategies weren't explicitly stated in class and instead required students to develop their own strategies outside of class. This course is marketed as a beginner linear algebra course with an introduction to proofs, so proofs are incorporated throughout all the course concepts. However, the integration of the two halves of this course needs some significant reworking. Most of the proofs that were done in class, especially in the second half of the course, were significantly more difficult than any problem that would come up on a problem set, let alone an exam –– and with a tangential connection to the actual application of the concept itself. I understand that these proofs were intended to show us how mathematicians originally derived the theorems and equations that we were using, but without any explanation of what the actual theorem does, it is impossible for us to understand what equation we're even attempting to derive or what the goal of it is. Additionally, after the 10th class or so, we almost completely left the Hammack book of proofs since we had presumably finished learning the most important proof tools and methods. However, if one of the problems in a later class requires us to use a lemma, then I would presume it is necessary to explain what a lemma is before using it. Overall, I felt that most of the proofs that we did in class were just Wood showing us a bunch of cool moves he could do without explaining the actual strategy of why he chose those specific moves to do –– while I was personally able to build a strong understanding of how to do proofs through my own practice and the problem sets, I think this has been a common critique among me and my friends in the class.
Additionally, we never got through all of the course pack problems in each class. This meant that we didn't get the lecturer's explanation about these concepts, many of which were proof–based problems that it would have been nice to have him walk us through.
2. Problem sets: The problem sets for this class weren't obscenely difficult, and sometimes dare I say fun, but there would usually be around one question on each problem set that had a solution that required such a weird concept or reworking using equation rearrangement tricks and methods that we had never even been exposed to in the class or textbook. The course assistants ended up being hugely crucial in fixing these areas, but I talked to several who even admitted that some of the problems had some particularly nasty algebra and didn't really do anything to build our understanding of the linear algebra concepts from class. I think it's worth re–examining certain problem set questions that were more reliant on these types of weird algebra tricks and replacing them with something more substantive. For example, I found the true/false questions on exams to be particularly difficult because they required a flexible and comprehensive understanding of the connection between matrices, linear independence, rank, and similarity that we truly had not built up anywhere else in the course.
3. Exams: As a relatively fast exam taker, these exams favor those who understand the exam structure and structure their study schedule around it –– simply understanding the course concepts is not enough. I wish more practice exams were available before the exams and that the available practice exams were more analogous to the difficulty of the actual exams. I felt that most of the exam content was typically fair, in the sense that you could predict what was on it, but I do think that there was usually one problem per exam that was about a concept that we had only spent a maximum of 10 minutes (or even less) of class on.
4. Overall conceptual understanding: I generally think that the explanation of the linear algebra concepts needed to be better connected to the geometric representation of vectors (especially for the explanations about change of basis). My biggest critique is that we learned about diagonalization before eigenvalues and eigenvectors when it should really be the reverse. When I took linear algebra in high school, eigenvalues and eigenvectors were some of the first concepts that we learned about, and we definitely learned about them before getting into any applications and matrices constructed using them. It's difficult to understand where the diagonal matrix even comes from without an explanation of these two concepts.
5. The textbook: I didn't end up finding this useful and only referenced it around exam time. Their explanations of some of the class concepts made more sense than how it was presented in the course pack, but generally, you were good to go for the exams as long as you understood the course pack content. This textbook also has a really weak answer key (many of the solutions are not provided), so it doesn't provide very good exam practice.
6. Sections: The section worksheets weren't that strong, especially in the beginning, but they did improve over time. I think that sections were the best place where I built the most exam–relevant knowledge since the problems that I encountered in them were the closest in line with the exam content. I appreciated that he explained a lot of the WHY behind certain concepts, such as why we would introduce the concepts of vector spaces when they seemed to be the exact same as the traditional vector work that we had been doing before these were things that shouldve been explained in class.

Overall, this class has recently seen high yearly turnover with its lecturer, and while I can see the merits of what this course was trying to get at, I don't think the implementation and course support truly did much justice for it. I will say that this course was pretty stressful for many of my peers, especially since its hard to predict your grade since the grade distribution and cutoffs change pretty drastically with each exam. However, this was also the course that confirmed to me that I want to concentrate in Applied Math, so I do think it has its merits and is a class worth your time if you have never done proofs before.
