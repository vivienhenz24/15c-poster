This was a fun course in logic. Prof Goldfarb is leaving after this semester, so this might not be relevant, but he was a fun teacher who was able to tell a lot of stories about the philosophers and logicians that were the ones to make the important advancements in the field that are taught in this class. The psets were generally pretty chill, though it was hard to gauge how you were doing in the class because none of the materials were graded until basically the last few days before the final, since the one TF in the class was sick for a lot of the semester. I remember students coming from a pretty diverse array of backgrounds in terms of logic experience, with the majority not having taken any class in logic, including myself. I took CS 121 before taking this class and found that it was pretty tangential in terms of materialthe idea of uncomputability is touched upon near the end but isn't particularly relevant, so if you've taken 121, then this class won't be going over the same stuff.
I AM NOT DERIVABLE!!!
Stay on top of the readings, and go to office hours whenever possible.
This course is great, and I especially recommend it if you're studying mathematics. You answer questions of the sort "Is every statement either provable or refutable?" or "can you prove that a statement is not provable?" Very eye–opening questions to think about, and made me reconsider the world of math that has been built upon the axioms of set theory. Sadly professor Goldfarb is retiring, but do shop!
Given that Prof. Goldfarb just retired, most of what I say is futile for future students interested in Phil 144. That being said, this was a great course which teaches you everything you need to know about logic. Prof. Goldfarb can be snooty at times, but generates a lot of interest for the subject. Very intellectually stimulating course that I would recommend to others in the future.

This is a fantastic course. It will change the way you understand mathematics.
This course is the culmination of seminal works in logic, taught by a maestro who contributed significantly to the field. Prof. Goldfarb is retiring at the end of this term and there does not seem to be plans for others to
teach 144 in his stead. But, on the off–chance this course is ever offered again (using Goldfarb's "Notes on Metamathematics") or you have an opportunity to listen to a lecture from Prof. Goldfarb, all I can say is: Don't
miss out! With that being said, I'll proceed as per usual. Consider this a glowing review of the soon–to–be–published Notes.:) The material is highly interesting to someone who has spent a lot of time studying and
writing proofs in math. In order to grasp and discuss the philosophical questions raised in mathematics, I really think the content of this course is prerequisite. Goldfarb's lectures were very effective, as his Notes are
too. The content can get technical, but everything is well motivated and the payoffs are worth it! Gdel's work on logic were something that I've always wanted to understand on a deeper level, and 144 helped me do
that and go even further. It's been a very rewarding experience to take this class under Prof. Goldfarb.
