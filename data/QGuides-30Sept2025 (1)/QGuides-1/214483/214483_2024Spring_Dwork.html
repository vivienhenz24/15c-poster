A beauty of a class, rare in the CS department. Really interesting content on "fairness" in general: what is it (can we even define it?), and if so, is it even desirable? How can a machine even be fair? What is it fair with regards to? Expect rigorous mathematical treatment of all of these topics (do not go into this class thinking it's social–science–adjacent due to the topic, it is not) and much more, from the philosophy of probability to equations for kidney disease.

Honestly, even if you are not interested in the above, this class is worth taking for Prof. Dwork alone. She invented the field, and it is clear –– she is an infinite well of knowledge on algorithmic fairness, and getting to learn from one of the titans of theoretical CS was an absolute honor. She is also very human, and incredibly open to meeting outside of class to discuss anything (a very rare thing for a professor of her caliber).

No exams, the final project is open–ended, and the problem sets were a bit grindy and quite long, but you have 2 weeks for all of them and ample late days to cover for emergencies.

This class teaches you all about the theory behind algorithmic fairness. Before this class, I always hesitated to get into the weeds with the various fairness definitions, but now after this class I feel comfortable with the workings behind many of the popular fairness frameworks and how they are motivated/work. You get exposed to lots of material, covering everything from individual and group fairness, learning complexity, regularity, multicalibration, outcome indistinguishability, omniprediction, better worlds, and simulations for real–world ideas, etc. In both the lectures and psets, you get very hands–on to the theory. The course gave me more confidence in understanding the roots of algorithmic fairness, and made me appreciate the overall hardness of the field. The course certainly builds your mathematical skills to understand various complex definitions and theorems. While the content is intimidating, the instructor does her best to make you feel welcome in discussion, and is intent on helping you understand.

CS 226r is an interesting course. I suspect the comments for this course, if theyre released, may be somewhat negative, so Im here to add my 2c.

First, yes, its true: this class was quite disorganized. Lecture slides and homeworks often had typos, and the homeworks were lifted straight from papers so they were hard (and sometimes math–bashy with little technical insight).

Second, however, this course teaches you how to _think_ about research questions. Prof Dwork has made a career about placing societal problems on a firm mathematical foundation. That task is about as nontrivial as it comes; IF YOU SEEK HER OUT, she will shine a light on the ways in which one can take a problem, think about it deeply, and come up with a solution that is not a band–aid but rather a fundamental one.

Also, a note on the material. It is quite hard, but this is the right type of TCS and learning theory in my opinion. A lot of courses, especially in the ML space, teach you techniques to do things. This course teaches you fundamental ways of viewing learning, algorithmic identification, and other core primitives in algorithms theory. Its bodacious stuff.
Algorithmic fairness is an increasingly important field. Cynthia is super down–to–earth even though she pioneered the field. Highly recommend this course to anyone even slightly interested in fairness!
