This was an enjoyable course!

The course covered an introduction to causal inference, including the main question of causal inference, Fisher/Neyman inference methods, regression methods, and some more advanced blocking/stratification methods. Overall, the lecturing was fairly clear, and mathematical details were provided. Prof. Murphy does well to explain the overall intuition associated with the ideas, and presents some examples based on actual work in the field.

The pace of the course is on the slower end; in my view, it could probably opt to cover slightly more material (either in Imbens–Rubin, or perhaps more case–studies which detail issues with standard methods in application). Nonetheless, it is engaging and ensures understanding of material covered.

There are only 5 problem sets, which is generous; they are generally a combination of math and coding, and are fairly instructive and clearly related to the course material. Exams were relatively short in length and often omitted major mathematical derivations, sometimes focusing more on intuition; practice exams / guidelines are provided.

Overall, a well–organized introductory course.
This is a super well–taught, fun course. Causal inference is super important, but I feel like the first time I learned it (in stat 111), I came away super confused about the concepts. Susan is such a wonderful professor. A lot of the derivations we do in class are honestly super cool. While you won't have to remember most of them for the exams, they really provide motivation for what we do in the class. And the exams are way more conceptual than the lectures would suggest. Definite recommend.

But the teaching staff is kind and although most homework assignments are fairly difficult, they are very helpful in learning the material.
This course is very good! Prof Murphy is extremely kind and the class teaches some very interesting stuff. That being said the material can be deceptively easy as alot of it is very intuitive but learning the intricacies of everything is difficult to master. Further the class is heavily proof based so sometimes you can feel very disconnected to whats actually going on. Just study for the exams and do the quizzes which are annoying but fine and you should be good.
I felt that this class was all over the place. The psets, while manageable, are pretty different from the exams; there was little overlap. In class, we spend a lot of time deriving estimators, proving unbiasedness, and doing more proofs. These don't show up that much on the exams and sometimes it's hard to anticipate what will show up.

I found lectures hard to follow. This semester it was 4:30–5:45 which was a very low–energy time of the day. The daily class quizzes were also a pain.

However, over all this course is one of the better ones in the stats department. Psets are every 2 weeks. The class is great for an upper level stat class without being super time consuming.

Professor Murphy is, hands down, the most enthusiastic and kindest professor on campus. Never did I think walking through an algebra heavy proof could be fun, but whenever Im losing focus, Professor Murphy would quickly chime up Oh wait! This is actually super cool! I took this course to learn about casual inference. I now know that I am not a big fan of the subject. Even so, I would take a sequel if Susan Murphy taught it. Please take this if you can. It is such a pleasure.
STAT 186 is a solid introduction to causal inference, mostly in an experimental setting. Prof. Murphy is super nice and approachable, and draws on her research a lot to teach concepts in causal inference. If you're interested in her research and/or experimental causal inference, the class is definitely worth taking. I was more interested in causal inference in observational or otherwise less idealized settings, and the class only started to tackle some of those questions in the second half. Nevertheless, I think the theoretical setup was useful and I got a solid intro to the field. Workload was manageable with biweekly psets, although you need to make sure to be on top of the lecture quizzes (they're released 24 hours before each lecture). Exams are definitely challenging because the notation and small details in general can get confusing–– midterm average this year was around a 60.
