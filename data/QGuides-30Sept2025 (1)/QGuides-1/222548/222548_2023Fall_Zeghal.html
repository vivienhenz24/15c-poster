This is a good course, and if you are a NELC concentrator you're probably going to have to take it at some point anyway. Professor Zeghal is one of the sweetest and most intelligent people I have met, and she was always able to break down complicated things during lectures. Section was very important to understanding the course as well so I would not take that for granted. The biggest thing I would say is to take time to do the readings. I skimmed most of these and was able to get pretty good grades for the course, but I would have had a much easier time if I had actually taken time out of my schedule to read and understand the readings. In addition, go to office hours when you can. I was in a pickle for my final essay, and though talking to the TF and Professor Zeghal was immensely helpful, I would have saved myself a lot of headache if I had just gone to talk to them earlier.
This is a pretty good class for those wanting to learn more about the Middle East and North Africa world. It can be boring at times, but investing in the bigger ideas helps to keep things focused. If you're into learning about government (i.e., comparative governments, religious governance), this is a great one.
Try to keep up with readings as much as possible. There can be a bit of work at times. Also, definitely submit papers for feedback/go to OH. Super helpful!
This is an amazing course. I am a biology concentrator and had no background in the topic, but learned more than I have in most of my classes here. Professor Zeghal and Conor are amazing at what they do, and the material is very interesting and relevant. There is a decent amount of reading each week but it is not unmanageable by any means, and there is a quiz most weeks, but they are short and straightforward. One of the best parts of this class was how much feedback I got on each quiz and assignment from Conor, who was probably the most helpful TF that I have had at Harvard so far. So, if you need a divisional distribution or want to learn about the subject, I super highly recommend this class.
This class is much less history–oriented than you may think: instead of covering the Middle East and North Africa as a typical historical class with events, economic, societal, foreign, and military impact, it focuses much more on the intellectual/philosophical history of the region. We focus more on thinkers/schools of thought than more historically rooted developments. Take that as you will– if you're interested in NELC or intellectual history, this is the class for you. But for someone like me, who is more interested in history/politics, this class wasn't everything I was looking for. I learned a lot about the MENA region and this class taught me to look at multiple perspectives when studying history. However, the density of the readings were at times overwhelming, and I sometimes found myself wanting to learn about the MENA region through a different scope.

*PS: If you're considering taking this class and the TF is a man named Conor Dube, take the class. He is an incredible TF and he really helped me understand the concepts we were learning. He also provides some of the best feedback I've ever had. He is amazing!
This was an enjoyable and worthwhile class to take if you're looking to expand your horizons and learn more about parts of the world that are often in the public attention but are accompanied by a lack of understanding or genuine awareness.
If you are considering studying NELC or want a primer on the Modern Middle East, this class will provide foundational background. It is a survey course, so there is a limit to the depth any of the topics could be covered, but, by exposing students to a variety of sources, perspectives, and approaches, the class can lay the groundwork for future higher level engagement.
I took this course because I had to, but I'm not sure I would recommend it if someone is thinking to take it for an elective. The weekly quizzes are an unnecessary stress sometimes and they aren't graded that easily. The professor and tf are very knowledgeable and accessible, but sometimes the way the information is presented feels not that engaged.
This is a difficult class, but I learned a lot of very important and interesting information throughout the course. Assignments are not graded super harshly, although perfect scores are rare on almost anything. Section is really interesting and engaging and I learned a lot from discussing the readings (section was definitely way above average compared to my other classes). The syllabus is by no means exhaustive, but dives deep into some cool issues such as colonization, women & gender, and Islamist politics. Would recommend to anyone who wants a really in–depth understanding of the Middle East, especially the Arab World.
