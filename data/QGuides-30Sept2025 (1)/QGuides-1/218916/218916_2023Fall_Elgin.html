This is probably the best course at Harvard. You get the opportunity think deeply about what education is and should be, and to reflect on your educational experiences as both a learner and (if applicable) a teacher. In section, you get the opportunity to put the educational philosophy we learn into practice by teaching the course yourself and working through the process of developing a lesson plan, trying things out, and reflecting on your experience. If you're interested in going into education, or if you just want to get more out of your educational experiences, take this class!!!!!!
I took this course to satisfy the divisional requirement. The workload is pretty easy. You have a bit of reading, maybe 2 hours maximum per week if you are really reading everything (most weeks <1hr of reading) and a 200–300 word response essentially graded on completion. You also have to teach one of the sections, but this doesn't really count for any part of your grade from what I see. As for the midterm paper, only 1500 words, but beware that they typically give out bad grades on it. Seems like they should provide more guidance rather than just giving people a bad grade.

The final paper was pretty grueling to write as the topics didn't seem to me to be super interesting.

Overall, the course was pretty interesting. You get a pretty diverse set of perspectives from the readings and the lectures are pretty enjoyable. By the end of the semester though, it seems like we are talking about the same things over and over, which gets boring. As for the assignments, just beware that your grade is essentially made up of only 2 papers, so if you do poorly there is not a lot of opportunity to improve.
The grade is almost entirely determined by two philosophy papers, so be wary of that if you don't have experience with that type of writing. Otherwise, the discussions every week were super interesting and I enjoyed the different perspectives on education I was able to learn about. It would be a great class to take pass/fail.
This class is a great discussion–based class for those interested in how education has evolved in philosophical thinking. The readings are more than manageable, and the professor is an excellent and knowledgeable person in the field.
This class is interesting and rewarding. If you're going to dedicate the time to do the readings, you'll get a lot out of the course content. Lectures are long and it was at 8:30am, so sometimes it was a battle, but asking questions keeps you engaged and they're really the backbone of the course. Would highly recommend to anyone thinking about why we educate the way we do.
I don't recommend taking this course unless you are a philosophy concentrator, have extensive experience with philosophy writing, or you're prepared to do the work to learn how to write excellent philosophy papers. I went in thinking it would be ** and it wasn't. The lectures are nearly 2 hours long and there are no slides to follow along with. The discussion component is during the 1 hr section on Wednesdays, which is also mostly lecture (but from your peers who were assigned to teach the content from that week). Also, because there are only 2 papers the entire semester (the main graded assignments) they're a HUGE portion of your grade and happen to align smack dab in the middle of midterm week and then finals. Read the syllabus and grading policy carefully. I did enjoy moments where we were encouraged to be critical of information being taught (even information from Elgin herself) and the ability to reflect critically on the education I've received thus far but the course definitely is not the most engaging it could be given the interesting nature of the material.
I recommend this class, the Professor is very passionate about education philosophy and very very knowledgable. There are only two assignments each worth 45% of our grades which can be a bit scary but there is plenty of time and content to develop a great essay. There are also weekly discussion posts and everything is great.
Take it!!! The material is so good and so important even for those who don't plan to go into education.
