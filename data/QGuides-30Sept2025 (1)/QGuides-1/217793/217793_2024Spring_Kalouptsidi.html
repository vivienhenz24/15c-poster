If you have any interest in how firms behave and market power, you should consider taking this course. The course balances real–world applications with economic theory, so it is nice to analyze real firms and how they compete with each other, such as Amazon and Walmart. The workload is manageable and consistent throughout the semester. It's nice to engage with the material in different assignment formats: short responses to the cases, one empirical problem set, and standard problem sets.
The content of this course is very interesting. Their are many real–world examples used that will allow you have make more well–informed economic decisions. Moreover, the assignments are very manageable to complete. However, sometimes the deadlines are not explicitly laid out and the work can come in waves. Additionally, you need to study for the final and make sure you fully grasp the different market structures discussed in the course.
Loved it. Super interesting and the HBS cases are great. Gets very math heavy in the second half of the semester.
Really good ec class to help balance your schedule. The HBS cases are super interesting and discussions are intuitive. Assignments and final were extremely fair, and you can do well by paying attention to lecture and going over psets. Section was extremely helpful and I highly recommend attending for pset help and content review in the later half of the course.

Pretty easy, even if you have not taken microeconomics before. CS concentration senior here. The case studies, one of the main reasons why I took this course, were kind of old (from c. 2017 or so) and we probably only go through 2~3 a semester.
I would definitely recommend this class or ECON 1640 for those interested in market power and industrial organization. This class is very nicely built around both of those ideas. You'll learn from case studies what real–life companies are doing and competing, how they price their products, how they interact with each other, and how they run their businesses, which I find to be really cool. The class stresses more realistic–like economic models of business, including n–number firm oligopolies and decentralized market economies.

– Some micro–economics knowledge is required (ECON 10A definitely). I took 1010A concurrently and am doing totally fine. Calculus knowledge is definitely required: basic integration, derivatives, nothing crazy.
– Section helps a TON! I loved my section leader this year and find checking work with others to be a very productive time then.
This is a wonderful course that I highly recommend! You learn so much about real–life businesses ranging from Amazon to Uber which is widely fasinating to anyone looking to study the business world from an economic point of view. That being said, it is not an easy class. The math and methodology tested in the problem sets / exams are not taught in lecture, so reliance on office hours and optional section is a must to be successful in the course. Make sure you have a strong economics and math background before taking this. Professor Kalouptsidi is incredibly smart + Steph is a wonderful resource. I truly learned a lot from this course, and it serves as my favorite Economics elective to date.
