What Im about to say is ONLY applicable to Prof. Sius Math 113: Take this course if and only if you are very serious about math, and want to spend all of your time on this course. Professor Siu was an unreasonable lecturer, leaving every single student confused and lost in every lecture, while showing little to no empathy. The lights in our classroom (which are motion sensored), would often turn off multiple times in class, because no one was paying attention. Prof. Siu didnt care that his students were lost – he only cared about getting through the material. Our TFs were also equally lost, and openly admitted that they did not know what was going on in 113. Our TFs also shared that we learned everything taught in Math 213, the graduate level version of this course. It felt unfair at many times throughout the course when our TFs were unable to explain concepts learned in class, or answer questions in office hours. That being said, the material learned in 113 with Prof. Siu was absolutely beautiful. Complex analysis is such an elegant area of mathematics, and you really feel proud of yourself after completing each problem set, which were all extremely difficult. While it bothers me that this course was not taught at an appropriate pace, this is still probably my favorite course taken at Harvard. The material really is so beautiful, and I am actually thankful that Prof. Siu truly pushed everyone to their limits. Because of this, my mathematics skills flourished exponentially. For that, I will be forever grateful.

DO NOT TAKE THIS CLASS WITH SIU. I REPEAT, DO NOT TAKE THE CLASS WITH SIU. I cannot stress enough how horrible this experience was. I will try my best to write something coherent, but the TLDR is to run and never take a class under Yum–Tong Siu. He has lost all touch with reality.

I took Math 113 to satisfy my analysis requirement for the math joint. I was advised to skip 112 and go into 113 because that is what cool people do I guess. Regardless of whether this is true or not (I can maybe see why it doesn't matter too much to know 112 content, but it would definitely help), that means I was already at a disadvantage. Little did I know that Yum–Tong Siu was completely out of his mind. It's all still too raw to write anything coherent, but I can say a few things:

First, except for the first homework which was so easy it was hard to believe it was a higher–level math class, not a single homework took me less than 13 hours. Again, keep in mind I am a dumb math joint so this is probably not the same for others. But what I can say is, at some point during the semester, the CAs who were really really knowledgeable and smart and had taken 213 (grad level complex analysis), could still not solve all the problems. What's more, every time during office hours I would learn that the problems in our 113 homework were problems from previous years' 213 homework. How is this reasonable? I have no idea...

The pace is incredibly fast. I have friends who took this class last year, and even they complained about it being hard. But we were all surprised when we realized we had already covered EVERYTHING they covered in previous years by week 5. How? I could not tell you. I have no idea at what point it got this bad this fast, but anything past week 6 was already grad–level complex analysis. And I want to stress how real this is, I promise it is not just me being dumb and complaining about it. The description of the class on the class website reads: "Analytic functions of one complex variable: power series expansions, contour integrals, Cauchy's theorem, Laurent series and the residue theorem. Some applications to real analysis, including the evaluation of indefinite integrals. An introduction to some special functions." If you look at the class website, you'll see how we were done with all this by the end of February, and still had two months to go... was it useful? No. Did I learn anything? No.

Now, for the true problem: THE EXAMS. We had a closed–book take–home midterm and final. I want to emphasize the CLOSED–BOOK part of them. You were not allowed to use any resources while taking them. Even before the midterm, we tried to convince him to let us have a cheat sheet or something, but he said that we shouldn't worry about it, that it shouldn't be too hard and it was just meant to test a general understanding of the content. LIESSSSS. The midterm was basically another pset: 6 pset–level questions to be solved in 4 hours (which is a problem if on average they take more than 10...). And the final were 12 pset level questions in 8 hours.

I am honestly too traumatized to keep writing about this, so I'll stop. If this is completely incoherent, I'm sorry. It is all just too raw. I hated this class. Do not take anything Siu teaches (although I feel obligated to say he is a really nice guy to talk to, just completely detached from reality.)

Math 113 this year was a rollercoaster ride. The course went really fast until mid semester, covering what is usually covered in 113 in a semester in 5 weeks. However, this meant that we touched upon some of the more advanced topics such as biholomorphisms, Riemann surfaces, schwarz Christoffel transformations, Riemann mapping theorem, elliptic functions, and so on. Complex analysis is a really beautiful subject and I am glad to be exposed to these advanced topics without needing to take 213a.

Note that this also means the course pace was insanely fast. Professor Siu is really insightful and has extremely deep intuition, but he also lectures quite fast and is very ambitious about what he wants to cover. This means that the course was a lot harder than the average 100–level math course you'll take here. I enjoyed this because I feel like I would not have learned as much if this course was taught by a different professor. Proceed with caution if you are not extremely interested in pure math, and complex analysis in particular.
