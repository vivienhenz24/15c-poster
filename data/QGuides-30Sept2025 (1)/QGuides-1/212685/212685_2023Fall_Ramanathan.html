Physics 286 has been one of my favorite classes here at Harvard. I deeply enjoyed it. and , as someone with absolutely no background in inference/machine learning, it provided an amazing introduction to the fields. Prof, Ramanathan is an extraordinary lecture (most of the times), and his way of presenting the materials is so clear and easy to follow that it makes you truly understand. He guides you through how you would think about it, and I think it was an absolutely pleasure to have been lectured by him. This course quite literally changed my life and my goals/priorities as an aspiring scientist, and I think it was an overall amazing experience.

But don't get me wrong, the class had many many many things wrong with it, and I am not sure I would recommend it to everyone. Even my comments on prof. BUT THE REAL PROBLEM IS THE WORDING. WHOEVER WROTE THE PSETS CANNOT FOR THE LIFE OF THEM CONVEY INFORMATION THROUGH WRITTEN TEXT. This was a problem even for the first psets, but it just kept getting worse and worse. VERY VERY OFTEN THE QUESTIONS WERE JUST UNREADABLE, AND YOU COULD STARE AT THEM FOR HOURS AND STILL NOT UNDERSTAND WHAT IT WAS TRYING TO ASK. Now, they are not harsh graders, but the fact that more than once an announcement was posted ON THE DAY THE PSETS WERE DUE to clarify the question AFTER THEY HAD BEEN POSTED FOR 2 WEEKS ALREADY is just inexcusable. And, while it is true that they had a grace day for those of us who might have needed some extra time to finish, I just think something needs to REALLY CHANGE for next iterations of the course if it is offered again. The class material is not easy, but the psets were almost always much harder than they should have been not because of the material itself, but rather because you just had no idea what they questions were even asking. And that leads me to the second area of improvement (to put it lightly): the TFs

I would never comment on their inteligence/abilities/preparation. I am sure they are extremely knowledgeable and prepared. The material in this class was so interesting. I have seen a lot of bits and pieces of it before spread across different classes, but I think the way that it was presented together made me understand it all so much more and made it all so much more interesting and intuitive. I think Sharad's lecture style is great. His philosophy seems to be that if he can't derive the things he's writing on the board in his head live in front of the class (and instead needed to look at his notes), then he shouldn't be writing it. I think this made me learn a lot. There are way too many physics classes where you just watch a professor copy equations onto a board, but this style of teaching where he really derived stuff with us made the lectures so engaging and made me feel like I learned so much more.

This material taught in this class seems important to many different areas of research, and I would highly recommend the class. BEWARE: the homeworks can be very challenging and often hard to understand. This class may take a significant number of hours each week if you want to do the homeworks well and correctly. If you have a light workload in your other classes, you should definitely take this. But if not, you will stress yourself out. It covers all fundamentals to get a solid foundation for understanding machine learning concepts. It covers wide range of topics, starting from Information theory, transitioning into Bayesian Inference and ending with Machine learning. This huge range of topics covered is both its strength and weakness.
On one hand one gets to learn everything all in one package, and hopefully understand how one field influenced/based on the other. But on the other hand it feels like there is not enough time to understand anything and all you are doing is just solve pset questions, without caring much what we are trying to learn.

As someone who didn't have strong foundation in stats/probabilities I found first part of this course very challenging. As someone who didn't have much coding experience, I found second part of this course challenging.
So, this is just my opinion, but I believe this course would benefit a lot from splitting it into 2 parts, one taught in Fall and second in Spring. This would allow more time to get a solid grasp of the Information theory and most importantly Inference, as these are very important fundamental blocks to get proper understanding of Machine learning. Then in the next semester, more time could be dedicated to properly show how Inference is fundamental to ML, and how it is integrated in various ML concepts/techniques.

While I learned a lot from the course, I would lightly caution others against taking it.

To start, I did learn quite a bit from this class, and the skills gained are useful. I took this with a physics background and found the machine learning to be the most useful. The assignments had us implement algorithms using the concepts from class, so you will naturally learn both how to code these algorithms and how they work. Professor Ramanathan is an energetic lecturer who for the most part invites student engagement.

However, there were drawbacks. The biggest issue was the incoherence of assignments, notes, office hour explanations, and most communication from course staff to students. The questions on assignments were worded in such a way that students were left guessing as to what was being asked. The problems contained many typos, and it was often impossible to interpret what the original words and phrases were. Sometimes variables were left undefined and sentences were incomplete. At one point I thought it was purposeful, since we talked about noisy communication channels in the course. Even on our exam there were many typos and vague questions, where it was unclear what was being asked.

Students often expressed their confusion to the TFs, who usually told students to interpret the problem however they chose and write an explanation of their interpretation at the top of the problem. That is to say, even the TFs were confused about the problem statements, and we never got satisfactory answers. If you want to learn about how machine learning algorithms work from an information–theoretic perspective and then implement them for homework, this is a way to do it. However, if there's a less disorganized means to that end, I would take that instead.
