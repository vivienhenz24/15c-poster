What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

Table for [QTitle].
Comments
You likely will have to take this class. You get out of it what you put into it. If you want to gain real skills and experience by building something cool, you can. If you want to not do much, then depending on your teaching staff, you probably can. If your group decides to put effort into your project, it can be a good bonding experience bc it can be a lot of work, and you all have to work together and rely on each other to carry their weight.
Here is what I wish the teaching staff said on day 1: "Sometimes as an engineer, you are presented with a specific problem and must design a solution. That is the exercise of ES 100. Other times, however, the question is just 'how do we make more money as a company?' That latter question is more difficult in that you need to first find a problem that exists in the world or a hole in the market that you think you can fill. That is essence of ES 96: can you develop a product by first finding a question and then developing solutions. There are three phases to this process: research, making a problem statement, and prototyping a solution. But these phases are not one after the other. Instead, you will spend the first month of the class just researching problems in this specific industry. Then for the two weeks after that you will start writing problem statements AND doing research as you are developing your research. Then the last 6 weeks of the class will involve prototyping AND modifying your problem statement AND doing research. You develop a problem statement but realize it is not supported by research in which you have to modify the problem statement. You may develop a solution that works, doesn't address your problem statement, but could address another problem. So by the end you are bouncing between all three of these phases: research, problem statement, prototyping."
Overall, this course was deeply unsatisfactory. The teaching staff were highly disorganizedassignment deadlines were often announced the night before, and we werent informed about a major 100–page report until less than two weeks before it was due. It often felt like the instructors forgot we had other classes and commitments, expecting ES96 to take up our entire week.

That said, I did appreciate that our section was at least able to accomplish something meaningful. Ive heard from peers in other groups that they would sit in the SEC for hours with no direction or progress. Our industry clients frequent visits and helpful feedback were a definite highlight and made the experience somewhat worthwhile.

Still, ES96 has a well–known reputation in the department for being chaotic, and this iteration only reinforced that. The lack of structure and clear communication pushed a disproportionate amount of responsibility onto a few highly involved students, which was unsustainable and exhausting.

This course desperately needs a clearer curriculum, better planning, and more respect for students' time and bandwidth.
