This course was very well balanced and the workload was quite manageable. It gives a great breadth and some depth into materials chemistry, which I think is useful for any chemist to have! Highly recommend.
This is a difficult course to recommend. It is very broad and surface level, and it did not make me want to learn more about materials chemistry. However, if you feel strongly that materials chemistry (solar cells, magnetic devices, porous nano materials) is an interest of yours, you will be exposed to a broad array of these topics. Exams are mostly memorization.
jarad is a great teacher, joy was a great TF. if you can get past the monotone lecturing, it's actually very very very interesting.
TAKE THIS COURSE!! The material (haha) is really interesting, relevant, and it's taught in a way that focuses on application to the field and your work. Jarad is a great lecturer and his office hours are super helpful for clearing up any confusing concepts!
Take detailed notes in lecture!
Loved this class! It was not that challenging as long as you are interested in the subject. I thought it was a good survey course. Some people thought the topics were a bit scattered, but the point was to expose us to a lot of different things. The proposal at the end was a fun exercise and forced me to do a deep dive into current literature.
must take this class if you're interested in materials chemistry
Professor Mason is an excellent lecturer with a deep knowledge of the field, but doesn't hold students to a rigorous standard, for better or for worse.
Take this course (after you take Chem 40 or have taken some beginning inorganic equivalent though)! A lot of great topics in materials chemistry are covered, ranging from solid–state synthesis, electronic structure of solid–state materials, magnetism (lot of this, which was great!!), nanomaterials, optical treatment of solid–state materials (i.e. photovoltaics, etc.), and extended materials (like MOF's and Zeolites). None of the other inorganic classes at Harvard really cover any of these topics, so it was really nice to learn new stuff while still applying background knowledge from previous inorganic classes. (But seriously @ the person considering taking this without taking Chem 40 or a beginning inorganic class DON'T DO IT! If you do, well, can't say nobody warned you! :) )

There were 7 PSET's total, 2 exams, and a final presentation / written proposal. While the last two weeks were a little bit annoying b/c PSET + exam + presentation + written proposal, it wasn't so much work the whole way through, and the exam content was 100% fair! As a grad student who is finally done taking classes and has taken too many classes by this point (ranging from awful to great btw), I can confirm this is definitely one of the good ones! Joy was a fantastic TF who knows the material incredibly well, graded super quickly, and was overall very on top of it! Jarad was an awesome lecturer as well, and I learned a lot in the class that will be super helpful going forward I'm sure!

As a side note, I didn't crack open a single textbook or recommended reading, only studied based off the lecture notes and did perfectly fine in the class. So uhh go to lecture if you take this class; it's def worth it!
This is a great upper level chemistry course to help fulfill the chemistry requirement. The psets are quite straightforward and graded generously. The exams are also quite straightforward and the final presentation is amazing over a more standard final exam. Material is interesting and not too hard. Great for people who want to explore more material science and don't want too much work.
Make sure to prioritize attending this class, as you won't get much out of it without being there and wanting to be there
This class is nice and interesting! Psets and exams are fair, take some studying but not overly difficult. Don't forget about the research proposal, it can sneak up on you. As long as you do good reading and make an effort to find a topic that you are genuinely interested in, it should not be that bad.
Make sure to take really good notes and attend lecture because they are not posted anywhere. As long as you go to lecture and understand the psets, you should be fine for exams.
Jarad teaches this class well and it is very well organized. Like other grad–level chem courses, the work assigned is very fair, and exams are representative of material presented in lecture and on problem sets. Final project and presentation are also typical of most grad chem courses.
