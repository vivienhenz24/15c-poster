191 is a great course! The experiments are interesting, Joe and Jieping are extremely patient and knowledgeable, and there are many opportunities for feedback. This class was a highlight of the physics concentration!
This course is an absolute treasure that the Physics Department must protect at all costs! The vast array of experiments, instrumentation, written wikis, and knowledge available from Joe, Jieping, and the faculty is unparalleled. And the amount of history that is stored in the setups makes the experiences fantastic. When you start with an experiment, go through the bench notes: you will find notes from students that are decades old. My partner and I googled a few names from the induced radioactivity bench notes, and found that one of the students had become a Physics professor at Cornell. While taking this course you will feel a part of the history of physics at Harvard, and just that makes it so worth it. Practically – ask if you are stuck, be prepared to do a lot of independent reading and work like a researcher!
I would not take this class unless you're doing it for honors. In any case, I'd still recommend waiting until senior spring to take it (more experience with physics, nice community of seniors in the class, and grading ambiguity is less stressful because post–grad plans are figured out).

Overall, this class does what it is supposed to: teach some essential lab concepts, gain experience with best–practices in an experimental lab, and get practice writing papers. That does not mean that it is enjoyable. The 4 hour classes are LONG, and this type of work is often difficult and frustrating. Additionally, you will often have to wait quite a long time for people to come help you, and it takes weeks to get feedback on the things you submit.

With that being said, Joe and Jieping are incredible. They are both patient and helpful, and it was cool to see how excited Joe got when talking about science.

Experiments that I recommend (either from personal experience or talking to others in the class): Quantum Hall, Compton Effect
Experiments that I don't recommend: NV centers, Optical and Magnetic Tweezers, Slow Light, Semiconductor Hall Effect
You will get from this course a lot lot more than what you ever expected initially going in! You need to choose your experiments wisely based on what you are interested in and choose your partner well to complement your skills. Use the resources provided in staff and professors and you will succeed, ask them questions!
This course takes a lot of time (8 hours of classes a week ends up feeling much longer than you expect) but is worth it. You'll become comfortable with basic lab techniques and get a feel for doing data analysis and writing papers. Joe and Jieping will be your best friends at the end of the semester. 2 very important recommendations: make sure to come into the class with a lab partner that you know you work well with, and always start the papers early – they take a LOT of time.
If you like experimental physics, this class is super fun. They can be a bit annoying about missing class for grad school visits, but seem to be understanding about other things. The papers are definitely a lot of work to write, but other than that nothing in the course really requires effort outside of class.
Take it. This class is a must take for any physics student who might consider doing research. It is really satisfying to work on the experiments and be forced to produce 2 papers (or 3 papers if you don't do a presentation). Joe and Jieping are also AMAZING. I couldn't have asked for a better teaching staff. Joe and Jieping are the most patient and knowledgeable preceptors ive ever had. They know the ins and outs of every experiment, and they are incredibly patient and helpful. I canot express how thankful I am to them.
Physics 191 is a great course which teaches you a lot about experimental physics. The lab staff are fantastic and the lab wiki is very detailed and a great resource. Feedback throughout the course was lacking and would have been useful, however.
The class is definitely a good concept. With that being said, I think that there is just a lot of class time (8 hours a week) and that you need about 60% of that time to actually do the experiments.
