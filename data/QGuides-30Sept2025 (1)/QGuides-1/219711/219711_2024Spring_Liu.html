Do not take this!! So disorganized, you won't learn anything, topics are completely irrelevant
This course is not too difficult, reviews the basics of ML, goes a bit into newer methods, and then has a research component. Its a good way to satisfy a requirement and learn some ML, especially if you're interested in the research component.
This class is a good option to get that last semester of the methods course out (definitely seems a lot more manageable than Bayesian). However, the way it was taught this time around made it a lightly frustrating experience because there was so little clarity about the assignments and deadlines, the assignments were only kind of useful, the lectures were often very confusing, and I definitely felt like I learned less than I could have in a class that was more thoughtfully administrated which a bit more focus on what would actually foster student learning. I see how the project could have been really useful, but I think everyone just ended up throwing together something at the last minute because we weren't really given the structure and advance notice throughout the semester to get something more substantial together. We also never received feedback on anything during the class, which was too bad.
Very confusing class. Some parts were very good, others did not feel useful at all. On top of that, a lot of work.
I think this is a good option for students who need to taken some advanced GOV methods class to fulfill a requirement (whether A.B., PhD, or similar). It's pretty realistically paced with well–spaced assignments, and probably less of a pain than several other courses in the GOV 2000 series. If you budget your time well, you should be okay. I didn't find anything that conceptually difficult, but working in R is a huge pain if you're not that fluent. Almost all of my issues with homework, exams etc. were R issues, not ML issues. So I recommend brushing up on R in advance if you're definitely going to take the course. Both of the professors (Liu and Jerzak) were lovely.
Naijia is the absolute best and what you get out of this class is really how much you put into it.
Very disorganized class. Naijia's part was pretty good, but Connor's part was a bit all over the place. Part of the problem is that they did not know to what level to calibrate the course. e.g., at one point Connor asked if we knew what for loops were, but then at other points assumed we had advanced knowledge of using various ML methods in research. I'm sure you'd be better suited taking a class in the STAT or CS departments rather than this class.
This course was a LOT of work, but the work is done in four bursts across the semester (2 PSETs, 1 week–long midterm, and the final project).

It was pretty over–whelming at the start (esp because the lectures are long), but it gets more manageable once you realise you don't need to learn the proofs. Also, Naijia and Connor are SUPER responsive to feedback, so they reduced the length of the later assignments and us use python if we preferred.

On the plus side: there's a lot of support. The TFs and sections were great and Connor runs coding workshops, too.

Overall, I would definitely take it if you want to do an interesting social science–y final project!
This course is one of the worst–organized courses I have ever taken. First, there is very little consistency between the two professors. Second, it took almost 6 weeks for us to receive grades for the first pset (and there are 2 profs, 2 TFs, and multiple TAs), and the feedback was not constructive. Third, there is a huge gap between what is taught in the lecture/section and the coding activities in the psets. Finally, part of the communication between the teaching staff and students is via email and the other half is through slack, it is very hard to keep track of important information. Take it only when you have a specific project in mind and want to learn a particular method, otherwise it would very likely to be a waste of time.
