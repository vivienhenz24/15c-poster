When asked on my Q–Report what my criticisms for this class were, my response was the following:

"The whole course felt sloppily prepared. Material taught lecture–by–lecture felt directionless. Influenced by the environment I felt was being fostered in the course by these aforementioned issues, I decided I'd rather take an attendance drop rather than put myself in that uncomfortable situation again: I turned around and left, with over an hour still left in the lecture. I do not entirely blame the course heads for my personal experience with this course, as it is a new course on an emerging field so everything is still coming together to make this class as great as I honestly believe it could be. Lectures need to contain material that is interesting to students, important to the subject, AND actually assessed to some degree. In a perfect world, people would engage with this course in a meaningful way without any incentive. This is not a perfect world, this is a gened course, which many students are relying on to be the easiest course they take in a given semester. I wanted so badly to care, but given the bare–minimum completion–based grading for most submissions, I felt punished for trying to devote time and energy to the course, until I gave it less. Given some direction, and some instruction on GAI–related skills which can actually be assessed, this course could solve its issues regarding both attendance and engagement. "

The content itself is broad and very introductory–level; this is great for developing a more thorough perspective on the potential, promises, and perils of AIless so for developing an intensive technical background (probably should take a CS class for that, lol). That being said, the course still presents a useful survey of the underlying technology for those with less familiarity. The course assignments are minimal, with a few readings each week. The majority of class is participation–based and encourages showing up to lectures. There is a midterm paper, a final project, and a final exam BUT the midterm paper can be written with the assistance of ChatGPT, and the final project is open–ended, encouraging creativity. Lastly, the section is so much funour TF Elie was a legend (and French). The sections involve further hands–on experimentation with generative AI, highlighting both use cases and limitations. Ultimately, this class encourages some interesting discussions surrounding the nature of this (potentially) transformative technology and presents a wide range of viewpoints through different course materials and a diverse array of guest speakers from many different areas of Harvard.
