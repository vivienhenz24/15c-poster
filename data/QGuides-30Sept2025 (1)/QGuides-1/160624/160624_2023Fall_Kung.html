This class is great! Kung isn't the most animated lecturer (you should probably sit in the first few rows) but his passion for the material is clear. This class offers the opportunity to learn about many exciting cutting edge ML models. On top of that, you have the freedom to explore if something piques your interest. The weekly workload is fairly low, especially relative to most CS classes. However, the final project can be quite time consuming so be cognizant of that when constructing your schedule.
This class is the best synthesis of the current state of artificial intelligence you will get at Harvard. HT pushes you to think deeply about how you can make a meaningful contribution to the field through research. 100% would recommend.

This course is a nice intro to different topics related to computing for ML. We discussed both hardware and software related optimizations. The grading in this class is a bit harsh, and since most of the work is done/graded based on groups formed early in the semester, I would suggest taking this class with a few friends. Prof. Kung really pushed us to come up with something novel at times for our research project, which was frustrating at times when we thought we did, but ultimately very rewarding. Start the project early and really think where you want your impact to be!

I think the topics and papers in the course are extremely well–selected, and does build a strong foundation for what is going on in the state of the art of ML right now. However, you could probably do just as well reading the slides and the recommended papers and learning the course content on your own in a week or so.

One thing to note is that despite the course description, there is not much about hardware in this course –– it mostly covers practical software techniques for ML.

HTK is an extremely slow lecturer. He has a short temper and is often unnecessarily rude and abrasive to students. His comments on project updates are very vague, and I believe he generally takes a simplistic view of research (asking questions like "what is your secret weapon?"). He responds antagonistically whenever he perceives students are "talking back to him" by expressing disagreement or even just asking for clarification, and will just shut down and repeat what he previously said. "Don't argue with me," he says in the first lecture. Sometimes he makes comments about student projects like "what you have is nothing". If he were my PhD advisor, I would probably drop out.

The TFs are great and are clearly doing their best. Also of note, HTK's advice about getting a PhD published online is a good read.
Very interesting material, but I felt like we (understandably) only covered topics at a surface level.
