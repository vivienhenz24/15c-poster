Do not take this course. When I see a Q–guide review say this, I usually ignore it. Don't ignore this one if you want to take a course in which your grade reflects your engagement and understanding of the course content. I do not recommend this class even if you are curious about conservation biology. You are better off learning about the field on your own, outside of this class that grades harshly and haphazardly with little to no justification. The grading of this course is reminiscent of the grading of the first–year pre–med course LS1a, which I took at the same time as this course. This was a surprise to me, especially because LS1a may grade harshly, but they justify their deductions fairly. With a total of 7 written assignments across the whole semester (the eighth and final assignment being a participation grade), I did not expect grading to be so specific and tied to things outside of the course content, like failing to cite, or cite correctly, an outside source on a problem set. There are two exams –– a midterm and final exam –– both of which take place during the 1–hour–and–15 minute class time. They consist of around 3–4 multiple choice questions and the rest (between 8–10 other questions) are short or long answer, which they expect to be as detailed as if we have the textbook and class slides in front of us. This course's grading is not positively correlated to your engagement with or understanding of course content like it should be, and this was an issue not just for me, but seemingly the entire class. Even when concerns are raised, it was like expressing concerns to a brick wall. The TFs were really kind throughout the course in section and lecture and over email, but grading seemed to be an area where they all agreed to not compromise. I do not care about making an A; I care that my grade will not reflect the hard work I put into each of the assignments in this course over one of the longest semesters of my entire life. Even though the content is incredible (and it even inspired me to pursue conservation), I still would not recommend this course unless some serious changes are made. If you want to learn about conservation, just meet with the professor on your own time, as he is wonderful, very kind, and at the top of this field.
