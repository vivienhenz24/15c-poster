This class is a lot of work, but it is definitely worth taking if you are interested in law. Professor Felter is a great resource, but his assignments are quite lengthy.
If not for the workload and grading, I would rate this course extremely highly. The course content is excellent. In addition to learning about the economic analysis of the law, you also get a decent overview of the legal system in general. The readings are generally good (though often very dense) and the sections are informative. I learned quite a lot in this course and Felter clearly knows a lot about the subject.

That said, this section of Ec 970 is much more work than other sections. Readings are twice as long and twice as dense as other sections (you can find the exact page counts in the syllabus). This is much more reading than a typical gov course (by way of comparison, more than Gov 1510 and less than Gov 1061). Writing assignments are long, frequent (the pace was between once per week and once per two weeks), and could be harshly graded. The Stata psets (not unique to this section) were also a major time–sink and are not to be underestimated (even if you have already taken Ec1123). The final paper will take longer than you expect, especially if you are doing any regressions.

Overall, I would give this section top ratings on content and instruction alone. However, it is important to also consider the time and GPA cost of choosing this section over others.
This is a great class, and you learn a lot. Professor Fetter is amazing and probably is one of the most knowledgable people I've ever met. He's able to answer any question, knows an incredible amount about the material, and covers so much ground. He's also just always willing to speak outside of class and does his best to give as much feedback on papers as possible. At the same time, there's also a lot expected of you, with readings taking up much more time than for other sections and way, way more writing assignments. It's also difficult to know exactly what you're being graded on or what you can do to improve your grades on assignments, which makes doing well a little difficult. If you're worried at all about grades, I would probably say to enroll in another section. But if you really just want to learn about the topic or are okay sacrificing a great grade to learn more, this is the class for you.
This is such a good class if you have any interest in law school or just thinking how economics influence the law. Very very hard and intense readings, but if you're willing to put in the work it's fairly worth it.
This course is as good as the courses get at Harvard. Professor Ken Felter is the star and crown jewel of the tutorial program, by my definition, for a number of reasons. First of all, he's a retired partner at a law firm, so he has a bunch of industry experience. He is extremely dedicated to the course and helping his students. He is extremely accessible for office hours and answering a bunch of questions. But beyond that, what is really super outstanding about the course is the content, which is so interesting for anyone interested at all in how the law is made or what the law is at all (like literally everyone has had to have wondered about jaywalking at some point), and also the way in which he engages everyone in the classroom. Each class is like a session where we ask a bunch of questions and collectively learn about the law with Prof Felter. The big elephant in the room is the time commitment for the course. Admittedly, the course has a good deal of reading and assignments, and it's hard to spend less than 6 hours a week on average, even at a minimum, on the course. I think most people will find it worthwhile (evidenced by the other reviews). It is just such an enriching course that even though one might have doubts about joining at the beginning, this is the course to take. Prof Felter also has so many stories up his sleeve from his decades of experience, it is a delight to be in the class. Just be someone who's ready to commit some time and participate in the class. Prof Felter is nice, caring, and an upright person, and he will inspire you in ways, and perhaps convince you that the law (or law school) is something interesting to think about.
