This course is not a breeze but sections are so important and make the course material very manageable because you will learn everything there. The readings and discussions are very thought–provoking.
Do all of the readings, everything in the class is reading based. If you can get into section with Zehra, do it.
This class will challenge you, and yes, there is a lot of reading. However, you will come out having learned and analyzed a lot more theory than the average sociological class. Few classes cover as diverse of theory as this one; you also get to reflect on sociology as a field, acknowledging its roots in colonialism, erasure and more. Be ready to write and make sure you read and ask questions. Zehra was my TF and was so helpful and engaging in section and office hours. Shai is organized and student–centered as well. It is not an easy class, but more important is that it is organized and employs different kinds of supports to help you succeed.
This is a great introduction to sociological theory – the readings are interesting and the course staff are wonderful. However, discussion posts are graded quite harshly.
This class is super interesting and not as scary as it seems. However, your section leader is what makes or breaks the course as far as grading for discussion posts and papers.
This class is structured in a weird way that I was not expecting at first. There is NO LECTURE like ever. Instead, during the lecture time most week you'll be going on field trips to the art museum, libraries, or the ARC. When there isn't a field trip that week, there just is nothing so there are not always things during the lecture time which was very fun to learn and felt good when my Thursdays were entirely off. Because there is no lecture the class is entirely the section. That is where you'll review all the readings, have discussions, and learn more about each theorist. As such, what TF you have is absolutely so important in this course. I had Emma and I can tell you I absolutely ADORE Emma she was the best you'll really want to be in her section she's absolutely amazing and so funny and also really good at teaching you so much material but never making it feel daunting. The essays are fair and they give you plenty of time to write them.
I would only take this course if you required to. However, if you are interested in sociology or exploring the field, SOCIOL1000 will definitely satisfy your needs and be more engaging. For students who have to take this, make sure you have Emma as your TF even if her time slots seem strange, she is the best.
This course takes a lot of time and emotional energy, in and out of class. It can be rewarding if you do the readings and participate in class. If you're not a concentrator/secondary, though, I would honestly just recommend you do SOC1000 and/or some soc electives instead.
The majority of the class is just section, which happens weekly. I think lecture only happened 6 or 7 times the whole semester? But because of this, my section TF and the other students in my section really made the class for me. And I had a really fun time with them. The material is very dense and difficult as expected, but I really appreciated reading works by non–canonical sociological thinkers, which is covered in the latter half of the course.
This course was pretty fun, for a tutorial it was very low stakes and enjoyable. Brace yourself however, you will be reading, A LOT (~100+ per week). Some of the readings are painfully boring and confusing, especially in the beginning half of the course. The TFs are super knowledgable and advocate for your success, they make the 2–hour long section fun (especially Zehra). The assignments are only weekly discussion posts, one midterm paper (5 pages), and one final paper (10 pages), no tests so it is great.

When enrolling in this course, please be aware that the time listed in my.harvard is only for the lecture "field–trips" that occur, and they do not make up the bulk of the class. You will attend your weekly section that occurs at another time more often than the 12:45–2:45 "lecture" that occurs on Thursday.
To get out of this course everything that it can give you requires a lot of work. There are a lot of readings each week, and one discussion post a week can add up to a lot. However, if you have any interest in examining the origins of sociology, how we came to think about the world in the way that we do today,and the theories that led us to where we are today, this is a great course. All of the learning happens in the section, and Emma and Zehra are fantastic people who genuinely care about teaching and making sure that their students understand the content. If anyone requires any kind of accommodations in the course, it is super important in this course to be proactive about those, but once communicating those needs to course staff, they are fantastic about following up and ensuring that students have what they need. The theories can sometimes be difficult to understand, but if you are willing to put in the time to genuinely understand, the reading guides can be amazing resources. Although the sections are two hours, if you have taken the time to do the reading,and with the right TF, , can feel like half an hour, with the time flying by as you break down theories and realize that the readings made more sense than you initially thought they did.
This class is difficult but rewarding. There are a lot of readings about the canonical social theorists such as Marx, Weber, and Durkheim, but there is also a critical evaluation of the sociological canon and how its social context contributed to the erasure of marginalized voices from the canon. The readings are long but have a lot of very insightful content. You will learn to evaluate the strengths and weaknesses of social theories, which is very important for sociological research. I would highly recommend this class if you are interested in understanding the role of theory in social science research.
