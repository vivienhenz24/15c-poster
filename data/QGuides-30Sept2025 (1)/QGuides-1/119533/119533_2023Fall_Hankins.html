What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

Comments
Make sure you do the readings so you can relate the reading material to the lectures. Make sure to take good perceptive notes in lectures. Professor Hankins is extremely knowledgeable so can talk at lengths about all topics which is great so make sure you note down the most important themes he mentions. You can take a lot out of this course if you put the effort in. As you are only graded on participation, a midterm and a final, make sure you are understanding the content as the course progresses and reach out if you have any questions as the TF and Professor Hankins will be readily available to talk.
This course is worth taking! With great depth, it covers intellectual history from the ancient period to late antiquity. That being said, the course felt less like an intellectual history course and more like a political philosophy course. Not a big deal, but perhaps something to keep in mind. If you have any interest in philosophies including Epicureanism or Stoicism, here is an opportunity to learn about them in their original context(s). A huge bonus: no random, busy assignments that take time away from engaging completely with all the readings. So long as you do the readings, pay attention in class, and review your notes now and then, you will learn a lot!
This is a class that will get you a great start in each of the 4 early western schools and their development towards Christian philosophy. Professor Hankins does well in drawing the connections through time and emphasizing this as revealing the intellectual history of this region and period. It becomes clear how the next set of philosophers takes these ideas and works with them. Also revealed is the scope of the original definitions of philosophy; I believe this helps think about what encounter today. Hankins is open to anything. Great Professor.
