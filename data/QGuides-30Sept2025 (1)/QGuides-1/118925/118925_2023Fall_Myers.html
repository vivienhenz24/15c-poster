This class is a pretty straight–forward continuation of chem20. If you took chem20 it should be pretty similar in dynamic but with a lot more reactions and memorization. I recommend sitting every week after both lectures and include all new reactions in a reaction sheet, I promise this will make this class a lot better and not overwhelming at all. Take advantage of all the office hours, the teaching staff is pretty kind once you get to know them. Exams are pretty fair as well if you keep up with the material and mae the practice exams. I feel like this course Stockholm syndrome–d me. It is by the hardest class I've taken at Harvard, but probably my favorite. Yes, the nearly bi–weekly exams, weekly psets, and post–lecture problems are a lot, but I learned so much interesting chemistry. But still be warned, this class is not for the faint of heart. The weekly problem sets are do–able in one office hours session, but only if you begin them before you go. The post–lecture questions are completion–based, but take them seriously because exam–time you will be thanking you. As for exams, they were fair and the grading for them was also very fair, but the preparations for them were grueling. Allow yourself at least a week before exams to study, because seven CHEM 30 lectures is a lot of content. I typically spent about 6–8 hours a week on this class, but for the week before exams, it would go up to 14–16.

Professor Myers is fantastic and clearly very passionate about organic chemistry, and that translates to the enthusiasm of the rest of the class. He applies all of the chemistry he teaches to real–world examples, which made the class feel more meaningful than your average science class. All of the TFs and PSLs were kind and very knowledgeable. Their office schedules were also incredibly consistent, which made scheduling time for the course much easier.
Probably took this course because you have to and already took Chem 20, so not much need to convince you to take this course. I really liked Dr. Myers, and I think he is actually quite a good lecturer and great at teaching. But I think there are a few key problems with this course, which won't necessarily distract from your learning, but are rather annoyances. The entire course is very print–based, which means handing in psets in person and hard printed handouts. But I think you will walk away with a beautiful understanding and appreciation of organic chemistry – it's hard to not want to take additional chemistry courses after this!

I think the amount of content and speed at which we learn it is unlike most classes I've taken, and it is really gratifying at the end. Chem 30 is a small class so it is easy to get one–on–one help if you go to office hours. The TFs and CAs are highly organized, run consistent and helpful office hours, and grade exams / psets incredibly quickly. Professor Myers is clearly very knowledgeable and knows so much about organic chemistry. One of the best parts of the class was the Friday review sessions that Professor Myers would run. Chem 30 is a wonderful class! I personally enjoyed it even more than chem 20, mostly because of the greater emphasis on synthesis. You definitely learn a lot of material very quickly in 30, but it feels easier than chem 20 because you've gotten over the activation energy of learning orgo, so the speak. If you felt comfortable with chem 20, you have nothing to worry about going into this class, especially if you enjoyed the synthesis aspects. Some of the mechanisms can feel a little complicated at first, but Meyers does a great job explaining the underlying chemistry beneath every reaction, and I found that it was really difficult to get confused. Teaching staff is also great, though significantly less available than the chem 20 teaching staff. I didn't find that a problem, though, since we definitely got enough support throughout the class. All in all, a great continuation of chem 20, and it certainly made me love orgo even more.
This class is HARD. Absolutely it is. It's harder (in my opinion) than chem 20, mostly because there's somehow even more to memorize in this class. Exam questions can pull from any little concepts covered, so you really have to be thorough when preparing. I also struggled with the teaching style more in this class, I'm not as good at talking about research or discussing more arbitrary concepts and then applying them to PSETs and exams. Myers is great, but he definitely teaches the class with more of an emphasis on applications of what we learn in a lab (example, if it is explosive, if materials are cheap, who discovered a reaction, etc)... which while great and interesting, it's tricky to apply those discussions (or at least separate them from the actual material) and understand reactions in a problem solving context. However, I was able to find more success a couple of ways. Reviewing the concepts, rewriting every reaction with pen and paper before even starting the PSETs just helped me see the mechanisms and reagents again. I didn't go to section, which may be a hot take, but it's optional and I don't really learn well by working with/discussing with others, so I applied that time to working on my own. When studying for exams, redoing ALL of the hard problems multiple times, from all the practice tests to the hardest PSET synthesis problems, and even hard problems on the lecture integrations, really helped with active memorization and nailing down concepts. General advice is be prepared to give this class a lot, every week. If you slack off even a little one week, while possible to recover, it just makes your life insanely difficult later on because some concepts do build upon each other, or at least one little concept may be crucial to solving synthesis problems. Try really hard to not miss classes, show up to Friday reviews if you can, and stay on top of everything as much as you can and you'll be fine.

Not the type of enthusiastic course community that I was expecting given how challenging the course is and perhaps could be better fostered if sections were run smoothly. Course staff weren't the most approachable, but were very on top of things– like returning assignments, exams or feedback.
Overall, I would take this experience over Chem 17/27, but just be willing to put in more hours than you expect. Don't underestimate how much commitment this class is and probably don't take it with 2–3 other pset courses because it WILL get to be too much.
Also, put your focus on memorizing reagents and less mechanistically since there is a bigger focus on synthesis and box problems than expected. Start studying at least a week in advance and you'll be fine. Hold yourself accountable to this since it's so easy to get behind. Once you're behind, you have to "relearn" everything and it just becomes too much.

Prof. Myers is a great lecturer. You come out of the class with a greater appreciation of chemistry and you'll be able to understand relevant literature.
Chem 30 will solidly teach you all the fundamentals of organic chemistry that you will need. You will learn THE key reactions and mechanisms that form the basis for orgo. If you work in a lab doing orgo work or plan to do so, this is an incredibly useful course. This course has easily been the most translatable to my lab work, and every day in lab I use what I learned in this course. Note that this is a very very difficult class. By the end of the class, I had over 500 flashcards for the various different reactions, mechanisms, etc. that we covered. Every lecture is structured the same – Professor Myers fills all 6 blackboards with reactions and mechanisms. Myers is super brilliant and knowledgeable in the field, especially with the history and context of reactions which keeps lectures engaging. It can be hard to keep up at times, but reviewing the lecture handout after class always helps a lot. PSETS will require you to go to office hours and plan ahead. There are lecture integration problems (1–2 pages) due before every single lecture, and 3 in person midterm exams as well as an in–person final. You will have to spend a lot of time studying for these, but you will be sure to learn a lot. There is also weekly section and problem solving (1.25 hours each) on top of the weekly lectures. Expect this class to be your biggest time commitment if you want to understand everything and do well.

And although this sounds easier, it was actually pretty difficult because the information was presented in a far less organized way. Lectures move super fast, but more importantly, it can be really hard to tell what information is really important to memorize, what reactants need to be written down in a synthesis or box problem, and for a lot of them, no mechanism is shown (and not on handouts or any notes or anything). Even if they are mechanisms that we should be able to figure out, it would be really nice to be able to check to make sure they are right. Section was invaluable for helping to rectify this, and at least for the most important reactions, we could ask our section leader to give us the need–to–know in an organized way, but sections were right before the pset was due, so not the most helpful for those. Also, psets were due on Thursdays but included content that we didn't learn until Tuesday. Overall, TFs and PSLs were great and super helpful and the psets were not too bad, so it was ok workload–wise, but it was just not the best way to really learn a lot of orgo. I loved Chem 20, but this class was just ok. I would still take it, and I know a lot of students who really liked it so maybe it's just me, but just know that it's possible that you won't love this class as much as 20.
