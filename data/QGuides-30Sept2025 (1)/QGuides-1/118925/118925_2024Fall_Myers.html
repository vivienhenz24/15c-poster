While there are more reactions, the exam questions are significantly easier than in Chem20. So there is a limited number of ways they can test each reaction. This is why I would argue that Chem30 is overall easier than 20.

Material–wise, Chem30 finishes the carbonyl chemistry you started in Chem20 (and some of which is taught more superficially in 17).

There is no beautifully bound lecture notes or practice problem booklet like in 20 – but don't let that stop you. SCORE: 9/10 class. One of the best I've taken at Harvard or will ever take despite not wanting to pursue chemistry. Just fun puzzles and trauma bonding. I made great friends in this class :)
If you want to learn a whole lot of reactions (six pages of them per lecture) then this is the class for you. If you don't want to spend a semester memorizing reagents and mechanisms for a whole lot of reactions, maybe don't take this class. I know a whole lot of reactions now, after I've taken this class, and I have a greater understanding of what reacts with what, but it's in the same way that I speak English grammatically correctly because I grew up hearing people speak English correctly, and not because I learned English grammar rules. That is to say, purely from doing it so much, you get a better feel for which mechanisms will happen and which won't, but you aren't necessarily taught that or why. Prof. Meyers does make remarks to that effect in class but at least for me this felt like more of an afterthought compared to the rote memorization of mechanisms and reagents that was required just due to the volume of the material. Also, if you don't due well memorizing off of reaction sheets, it's entirely worth it to try a different method of memorization. For me, what worked best were diagrams with different functional groups and arrows between them with the reagents necessary to transform one functional group into another.
Going to lectures and completing problem sets (though both necessary) are not sufficient to do well in this course. So practice, practice, practice! And then, study some more. I had neither the time nor the will to apply myself to learning the course content outside of class, and that was evident in my grades. That said, Myers is a pretty engaging lecturer, and this course has been one of the most unique classes I've taken in my time at Harvard. Course staff can be pretty unsympathetic to struggles in the course, but they are always willing to provide practical advice about paths to success. The lab component of the course is also super interesting!
TL;DR: Chem 30 is the hardest class I've ever taken, but is super rewarding. Professor Myers is a great lecturer and really knows his stuff, but be prepared to spend A LOT of time on this class memorizing the material.

Here's how the course worked for me: in class, Professor Myers presented a flurry of mechanisms and reactions, and I quickly copied those down (be prepared to write fast). I didn't really understand them the first time around (this is okay) because it's a challenge to keep up with Myers' pace, but it's nothing to worry about Next, I would attempt the Lecture Integration problems (a short worksheet based on the new content from the last lecture that is due every lecture) to test my knowledge. Then, I would look over the pset and see how much I remembered, and do the first few problems. Then, I would look back at the lecture boards / lecture recordings as a guide, and attempt all the other problems, and go to office hours and check everything one more time before submitting my pset. For tests, there are ample practice materials, but be sure to do ALL of them: I know I definitely needed the practice. If you do all the practice materials, the exams will feel fair and simple.

Chem 30 is a HARD class, but it was definitely an enjoyable experience. Everyone is pretty much in the same boat: you will need to memorize a LOT of reactions, but it all feels fun and new. Your synthetic capabilities will grow exponentially compared to just having taken Chem 20. Finally, the community in the course is excellent. The teaching staff is absolutely stellar: they are super qualified and really know their stuff. The other students are also struggling through the same content you are, so it's not hard to make new friends. All in all, Chem 30 demands a large time commitment, but is an awesome class with great people.
