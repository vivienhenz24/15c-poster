Learned a lot in the class! Although most times it seemed chill, but really it's not
The course material is incredibly interestingit opens by framing the subject around philosophical questions like what does it mean to learn?, which I found very engaging. From there, it builds a solid theoretical framework to explore those questions. Overall, the higher–level ideas were intellectually stimulating and well worth the effort.

The problem sets, however, are a different story. They often require digging into the nitty–gritty details, some of which are left for students to figure out on their own, which can make them quite challenging. That said, there are only four problem sets (including the take–home final), so the workload is manageable, especially with the extra time allotted. Id strongly recommend starting early and using office hours to bounce ideas off TFs and peers.

I listed 8 hours per week, but that varies a lot. On non–pset weeks, I spent around 2 hours reviewing class content. On pset weeks, I spent up to 12 hoursroughly every three weeks. Its not a super time–consuming class, but it demands deep focus to fully grasp and apply the theory–heavy concepts.

The final project offers flexibilityyou can choose between a theory or an experimental track, which I really appreciated.
Interesting material, you get to learn a lot
It's obviously a privilege to learn from Prof Valiant, who pioneered so much of the content covered in this course. However, the lectures and content can get a bit dry and repetitive at times. Some of the concepts are interesting but do not feel particularly motivated or applicable.

The problem sets are relatively light for a grad class, though some of the problems can be more complicated than they seem. It was also a bit frustrating that we got very little guidance/instruction on what was expected of us for the project (e.g., scope, length, content, etc.) until pretty close to the project deadlines.

Overall, I think this course is worth taking to get a feel for some of the foundations of computational learning theory, but don't expect a class that feels very exciting or very motivated.
Youre probably debating between this course and other theory courses if youre a concurrent masters student, and I recommend taking this one. It might not seem like the typical graduate–level class, with three psets and a take–home final, but although the psets are challenging, theres usually a three–week gap between them, which makes the workload manageable. Overall, Id say the difficulty level is moderate in terms of time commitment. The TFs are great and provide strong support, and the final project is a good opportunity to apply what youve learned. All in all, Id say take it, you wont regret it.
The course is cool, if you're a theorist who's vaguely interested in ML this course is for you. Leslie is incomprehensible at times but clearly very smart and very cool.
This course was a big disappointment. To be fair though, the Q reviews from previous years were not great, but I expected better and discarded the reviews. I was wrong. The course was pretty bad. Lectures are extremely dry, psets can literally be solved without any knowledge from the course (I.e. you can look up things in the textbook). Hence, the psets were a waste of time since I don't really think I am finishing this course with a better understanding of learning theory.

I think many harvard CS courses suffer from this problem, but sometimes the teaching is so bad that you are better off learning the material on your own (or even better, take a similar course at MIT). This course was a very good example of this.
The course materials are interesting, but this year, the course was hardly organized. Deadlines were unclear, assignments weren't posted until very late, and I received very little feedback on my work for the final project although I reached out for it. I really think with the right teaching staff the course can be a lot better.
Professor Valiant may be trying his best, but unfortunately, the lectures were just not good at all :(. He has difficulty explaining even the simple proofs, and the first few lectures especially are very slow. That said, lectures are not mandatory (if you're someone who likes this), and I believe that at least 80% of the class never attended lecture after the first one or two. I do think some of the content in this course is pretty interesting (from the perspective of a math person), but I thought the course just moved very slowly, and we didn't get to anything really really cool. If you're interested in ML from the CS side, there are probably many better courses to take. In summary, I just didn't get a lot out of this course, and didn't feel like there were many chances to learn a lot.
Assignments were the main component of this course. Assignments were challenging but interesting. I often found myself spending hours on a single question without making any progress. This would be a great course if the lectures were more engaging.
