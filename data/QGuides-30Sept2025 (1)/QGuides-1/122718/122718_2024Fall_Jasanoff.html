SPP is a fantastic but complicated class. The syllabus is unbeatable if you're looking for an introduction to science and technology studies as a discipline/methodology. Prof. Jasanoff, it should go without saying, is knowledgeable about such a great breadth and depth of things that the class is honestly worth it just to have two hours of time each week set aside to witness a great orator orate. However, there is the massive caveat that this class is offered via HKS, so most of the class population comes from their various mid–career masters programs, which are such mixed bags. I'm a student at the college, so the experience was definitely different from any FAS course, especially because the weekly meetings took the form of a pseudo–seminar discussion that could never really get off the ground because the class was so large and was almost always distracted away from the assigned readings into talking about specific examples that few people could meaningfully comment on.

All that said, SPP and Prof. Jasanoff are iconic for a reason, and its great strength is that there is something for everyone. Mixing HKS/GSAS/undergrad students together is always a challenge and I think SPP meets it to the extent that it can, although I would still say the size of the class was the main inhibitor to productive discussion. Take it, do the readings, go to Prof. Jasanoff's OH, learn from your classmates of all intellectual/professional stripes, write the paper––but just understand that the bulk of your learning will occur outside of the classroom.
This class is amazing. Professor Jasanoff has an incomparable depth of experience and her resulting insights will help shape your thinking in many ways.

I like the content, but had a really hard time eeling like I was learning from my peers during the course.

This course is ideal for individuals who aspire to create a positive impact in society and work toward building a more inclusive and equitable world through thoughtful governance. Science, Power, and Politics (SPP) challenges students to critically analyze the complex interplay between these domains, offering invaluable insights into how they shape societal structures and influence change. It is a transformative experience for anyone committed to driving meaningful progress in policy, science, and social equity.
SPP is a really, really interesting course, and you can't find a similar one at Harvard. Sheila is a true leader in the field and it shows. It's a privilege to learn from her. However, the course could benefit from some restructuring. The class time is primarily spent engaging in class–wide discussion, which can become very disjointed and deviate heavily from the readings themselves. It would be beneficial if Professor Jasanoff dedicated more time lecturing on the content of the readings before moving into their real–world implications. However, the readings are very well–chosen and extraordinarily thought provoking! I would recommend this class to anyone interested in critical theory and the intersection of science and politics if you don't fall into that category, I would urge you to choose another course.
This is a great class that challenges conventional connections between science, knowledge and democratic politics. The reading list is very long but the best thing out of this class is getting a sensibility about how some of the recurrent authors in the list have approached the intersection
My background: CS + social studies double concentration, taking this class for my social studies focus field as a junior.

Pros:
– Amazing material and readings: It's the critical theory of science, which allows for a dimension of analyzing and parsing "big" issues: vaccines, trans–affirming medication, public health officials, knowledge dissemination and control, etc.
– Sheila Jasanoff: Outright brilliant. She challenges students and truly pushes them to be better thinkers and reasoners.
– Timeliness: All STEM concentrators need to take this class to understand the sociality behind what they think is hard science.

Cons:
– Sheila Jasanoff: While brilliant, she is also quite intense, and sometimes, in a bad way. She can be very condescending toward students who don't accept her definition of STS, and the classes are essentially not a seminar but, rather, a call–and–response, where someone says something, and she responds. The two hours really dragged on at times. People warned me that she was like this, so I was not surprised. I was able to get a lot out of the course, still. I think teachers can be intense and should shape up thinkers. But I think she goes overboard with her intensity, which makes the material harder to approach.
– Format: 2–hour "seminar" with too many people dragged on.
– Readings: Also way too much reading to be feasible.

As you can tell, the pros and cons overlap. I think the material of this class makes it a must–take, but the prof and the style might not be your speed. Take what you can. But learning how to view the world through constructivism, co–production, black–boxing, symmetry, etc., is super helpful, even if you ultimately agree with the framework or not.
