This is an incredible class. Applying to this seminar is more than definitely worth it. Prof. Mankiw is a great discussion moderator and you'll enjoy every second you are in it. Its also a tight knit group of students, were I met many I'm sure I'll still be friends with afterwards. If you get in, take it!! Mankiw is world famous and literally writes the textbooks students at many other colleges use. The students he selects form a wonderful group (it’s very competitive to get in) and he’s just such a wonderful combination of brilliant and extremely approachable and humble.
This class was one of the most rewarding academic experiences I've ever had.

For some background on myself, I'd had no formal economics background and was simply a student interested in politics and current events anticipating a concentration in economics before law school.

To offer some context about the class, Professor Mankiw (crazy successful, crazy cracked economist — even though you wouldn't be able to tell by how down–to–earth and approachable he is,) selects 12 students — 6 that lean left economically and 6 that lean right. Each week, students either read, discuss and debate a book on economics (with topics as diverse as two–parent households to immigration to healthcare) or a classmate's oped (each student is tasked with writing one throughout the semester.)

The structure of the class itself is such that, at least in my experience, the group of students selected for the seminar are some of the most talented I've encountered on campus — including upperclassmen I've met who've taken this class in years prior! Second, for someone with no formal economics background, I never felt like I was drowning in this class. Rather, if you keep up with the readings you'll feel more than comfortable when it comes to participating in the debates. (Side note — while it can be easy to skim the readings because it's a low stakes class, I promise if you do stay on top of them it's so much more fun.) Personally speaking, this class deepened my interest in the field of economics substantially, and genuinely made me excited to come to class each week.

Perhaps most importantly, while Professor Mankiw's views are generally common knowledge, it never feels like he is spotlighting his opinion in class — something many other professors on this campus could quite frankly learn from. He is incredibly good at removing himself from the conversation and serving as more of a hands–off moderator when it comes to the debates, and as far as I was aware, all students felt comfortable sharing their views from all across the political spectrum. Probably some of the best friends that came out of this class were from "opposite" sides of the aisle. Especially for those students that may lean right economically, this is a space on campus you'd feel most safe to express your views without fear of judgement.

If you are seriously interested in this class (it's awesome do it!!) be sure to rank it first — because of the number of students that apply, Mankiw only looks at those applications.

To sum it all up, the only broad suggestion I had for the class was to have it meet twice a week — really, it was that good.

Tl;dr: Easily the best first–year seminar available, a must–take for anyone interested in econ/policy/gov, wonderful community, lively discussions, better than Furman’s.

Course Format:
6 books and 12 student essays discussed throughout the semester.

Course Readings for 2023:
1. The Worldly Philosophers, by Robert Heilbroner
2. Capitalism and Freedom, by Milton Friedman
3. Equality and Efficiency: The Big Tradeoff, by Arthur Okun
4. We’ve Got You Covered: Rebooting American Health Care, by Liran Einav
and Amy Finkelstein
5. Open Borders: The Science and Ethics of Immigration, by Bryan Caplan and
Zach Weinersmith
6. The Two–Parent Privilege: How Americans Stopped Getting Married and
Started Falling Behind, by Melissa Kearney

As you can see, there is a diverse array of readings: #1 is a history of prominent economists and their contributions, #2 is a libertarian/conservative take, #3 is the liberal take, #4 is on healthcare, #5 is on immigration, and #6 on a social issue. I think the reading list changes each year so don’t take this as scripture.

General Thoughts:
What really makes the course special is Mankiw (who does a great job moderating the discussions) and the other students (who, by nature of the competitive application process, are really smart/accomplished). More specifically, I think this seminar does a great job teaching you how to aggressively defend and debate your positions without getting personal/emotional. There aren’t many ‘safe spaces’ at Harvard to freely discuss controversial topics, but this seminar is certainly one of them. You also get to meet some of the most interesting people in freshman class right off the bat.

Compared to the Furman seminar, I would definitely rank this #1. I get the sense that the community was much stronger in this seminar, and the conversations/debates/readings were more interesting (from what I’ve heard).

He also wrote a New York Times article on this seminar (https://www.nytimes.com/2017/09/08/business/getting–along–when–you–disagree.html) which is pretty cool.
