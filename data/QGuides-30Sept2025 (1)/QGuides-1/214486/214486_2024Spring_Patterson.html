To be honest, I took this class only because it fulfilled both my GENED and my secondary GHHP requirement. Overall, the TF teaching staff and content in the class exceeded my expectations, but I am still disappointed by Professor Patterson's approach to the class. Aside from the days where we have guest speakers, the lectures often feel disengaging and I get the sense that this course is somewhat of a historical artifact at Harvard, where you are "talked at" in lectures and hit with outdated ideas. The terms and language used by Professor Patterson (eg. "backwards countries" to describe North Korea and India, along with his claim that Japanese internment did not constitute the definition of "forced labor") is, at best, a dismissive claim that makes me feel less confident about his teachings, and at worst, an offensive microaggression that made me feel unsafe and angry with the course. I actually stopped going to lectures after the third class when that happened, and did not return until Professor Patterson mandated attendance, because I just did not feel welcome in such a lecture environment. Perhaps I had an inflated expectation of Professor Patterson too –– for someone who has coined influential terms about slavery as "social death", I did not expect him to be careless in his language and application of terminology in what feels like a racialized way (primarily dismissing Asian examples of forced labor, such as the Chinese coolie trade and Japanese internment, as "less bad" in definitional terms). I think this course has (or had) a lot of potential, but it is due for an overhaul and updating to be accountable to more critical views of racism, colonialism, and structural violence.
