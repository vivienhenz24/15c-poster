Review:


Although the time commitment for this class averages out to be about 13 for me, the week to week of the class was extremely variable. Notice that the first couple weeks of the class were unbareably hard because of its long psets. I had an amazing schedule. I was waking up at 7, sleeping at 12, etc. I was on top of my game! I was doing all the readings for my classes. I was writing my reading responses with so much attention to detail. Then, the third and forth psets came in. They hit me like a truck! I pulled an all nighter for each of the psets even though I put in 50 hours a week for my classes overall. The ripples from those all–nighters made my whole semester miserable. Because I pulled an all–nigher, my other work had to be delayed. Then, the work after that got delayed. Stat 111 caused a domino effect that ruined my semester. I unequivically blame Stat 111. I have no extracurricular activities. The ones I was in, I gave up on ALL of them, just to be able to keep up with myy classes. How can a pset for a class take ~20 hours to finish? None of the CS124 psets took that long. I got an A in Stat 110 and in 111. The concepts in both I love and I have covered somewhat before because of math olympiad. I got the psets done. Every week. I pulled back–to–back all–nighters because I am not a quitter. But the psets were simply too tedious for the first 4 weeks. Legend has it that one of those weeks, MORE THAN HALF of the class had not submitted the pset by the deadline and took the extension. I was determined to not take them since I knew that my second half of semester would be even worse (it was worse indeed). Because of this, apparently, Joe decided to give everyone an extra extension. IT DOES NOT HELP to make the course lighter after the fact. I couldnt care less about the easier weeks afterwards because they are not the issue here.

Make the time commitment an even 13, heck an even 16, but do not make it 27 hours one week and 7 some other week. I pulled a total of 7 all–nighters for this class. Having done all those all–nighters, I think my life was shortened measurably. I think I might have killed millions of neurons in pursuit of perfection in this class. I blame myself for creating my schedule first, but then I blame Stat 111 next. In total, I spent 169 hours on this class. With that, this class takes third place among all the classes Ive taken with CS61 and CS124 coming in first and second with 196 hours and 180 hours total respectively.

If the course continues like this next year, please use your extensions on the earlier, tedious psets. The later psets, as seen in the data, are relatively fine.

Anyways, how to do well in the class? Know stat 110 well. Know your conjugacies, know your pdfs, know the properties of the distributions youll be working with. Memoryless property, chicken–egg etc. Read the stat 111 textbook (a new addition to the course this year). The textbook is amazing. It has too much info that is not all relevant to the class. But the textbook is a superset of the class. Read the book and the lectures will become review. If you read the textbook, youll also see special derivation etc. that will give you new methods of problem solving for the psets.

You better go into the class with a pset group. My god was it necessary. You should to be able to talk through problems and ideate. Going to OH for every question is hard. Collaboration within the confines of the collaboration policy is key.

The TFs are nitpicky. The grading RUBRIC is nitpicky. The psets were graded out of 48 for most psets and each question has an excrutiating amoung of subparts. Each subpart is worth, say 1 point. YOU WILL LOSE 0.5 of that for the smallest of reasons. Note that your psets are worth 45% of your grade and so each pset is almost 5% of the grade. Be as perfectionist as possible. Double proofread just to make sure. The smallest things! Because of the denominator, each small loss adds up quickly and suddenly you might find yourself with whole percentage points lost on your final grade because of silly mistakes. A friend of mine experienced this. I repeat, BE PERFECTIONIST. There are exponentially diminishing marginal benefits to another round of checking your pset. But you do want to get an A, right? So buckle up and do it.


I did not go to a single lecture for the class. Totally unnecessary. Watch them all online. I have a panopto extension that allows me to watch the lectures at arbitrary speeds. Watch it at a speed comfortable to you. I watched at 2.5–3.5x speed. Frequently stop the lecture and take notes. Tips for R, LaTeX and submissions:
For R:
Learn how to vectorize everything! It significantly increases your productivity and reduces execution time. Functions such as replicate, sapply, lapply together with anonymous functions are VERY helpful for coding up stuff. Watch some tutorials and it will pay dividends as the semester goes along.

There will be a lot of times where you will have to do some calculation/data generation and then repeat it for some B times. I recommend writing a function that does the procedure for one iteration of it and then using the replication/application functions mentioned above to process it for the total B times. Ends up being very clean and fast code.

Learn to use the documentation.

Use ChatGPT for your graphing needs. You have the data, let GPT handle the specific code needed to display it well.

R Studio:
Its a piece of S**t. Compared to VSCode, it is garbage. But you are sort of stuck with it. I explored running R in VSCode, in NVim, etc. None are as good imo. LEARN THE KEYBOARD SHORTCUTS. Please! I cringe everytime someone manually goes to the beginning of a line to change that line to a comment. Use Ctrl+Shift+C instead. Learn how to copy and past whole lines and reorder them with just alt lol. If you have the time, change the shortcuts to those you are familiar with. This will noticably improve your quality of life. When you're close to the deadline, the last thing you want is for your progress to be halted by R Studio.

How to put code in your pdfs? Here's my process. I hated R Studio, so I only used it to **write** my R code and test it. Once done, I used RTEX to compile my final doc. I use a VSCode extension called LaTeX Workshop to compile latex on my local computer (eliminates reliance on janky harvard wifi and Overleaf). This extension can be used to write rtex files as well.

The workflow goes as follows: Download that week's pset. Optionally generate a pdf for my ipad with more spacing between questions to handwrite some answers. Copy the .tex file into a separate folder and name it with the .rtex extension instead. copy over csv files and resources that the .tex file needs to the same folder. Whenever I want to insert R code, I write the following incantation:

<<cache=TRUE>>=
[MANY LINES OF R CODE HERE]
@.

The extension neatly runs the code and plugs in both the result and the code into the pdf file. Super convenient. The cache=TRUE allows for computation to not be repeated for each LaTeX compilation round since simulations can take some time to finish. It only reruns if you change the code withing the incantation. Writing latex itself is slow and so I try to write in the fastest medium. Questions about explaining intuition are done in RTeX. Questions about code get done in RStudio and then the code gets copy–pasted into Rtex as well. Having done the pset, the pdf generated in computer is sent to ipad for final assembly with handwritten answers. Never had an issue with using handwritten submissions for some questions/parts.

General tips:
– You can choose between reading the textbook or going to lecture. You could also just watch the lecture recordings, especially since this class takes place in the cold of the (nominal) spring semester. You'll get that reference if you take the class.
– Start psets early, go to office hours early (but not too early, because the TFs don't have the solutions before Sunday night and will only be able to answer half your questions)
– If you're struggling with concepts, go to section or Conceptual OH (if they still offer it)
– Be prepared for a lot of Stat 110 review throughout the course (Adam's law was used pretty much every week)
– Try to get some R experience before starting the class. Take advantage of vectors in R, don't try to write ugly Python scripts (avoid loops at all costs!). This will make the R questions on the psets the easiest and most fun.
– Do ALL of the practice midterms/finals before the respective tests. They teach you the format/what to study for/sometimes there are repeated concepts.
– Half the pset problems will require a "By Theorem X from the textbook..." to solve; don't try to start from scratch.
– Don't expect much application from this class. Stat 100/102/104 are much more application–based, while this class is just spamming 110 concepts and some occasional calculus.
– You honestly won't take away too much from this class alone (unless you're planning to study theory), but it will prepare you for most 100+ level stats classes in which you will learn important applications.
– Don't take this class for funsies. Decide whether it is worth the time and effort in the long run.

Prerequisites: STAT 110 is the only course you need to do well in this course. You will want to have a strong understanding of STAT 110 concepts. For many students, they take 111 after 110 so most of the 110 material is still fresh, but if you have been a year or more removed from 110, you'll want to do a little review of important 110 concepts. That said, you'll always have the 110 book as a resource, and if you use that religiously, completing assignments shouldn't be too difficult. Some students will say taking CS181 prior to this course (or concurrently) will help, though the concepts aren't really all that similar past the first few weeks of the course. In short, 110 in terms of preparation should be fully sufficient.

HWs: The homeworks are similar in style to 110 with a few differences. Each assignment will have 3–4 questions that will include theoretical components or simulation tasks that you will do in R. The R parts of the problem set aren't that intensive, so if you've never worked with it before, you can definitely get the hang of it pretty quickly. My recommendation for the problem sets would be to always tackle all the problems yourself before working with a friend or going to OH. The problem sets are written in a way that's very instructive, so they can help you reinforce the material week–by–week.

Lectures: The lectures are fine. Like 110, the lectures don't really add much to the course. Everything you'll ever need to know will be covered in the textbook and the homeworks. The most important thing is to make sure you read the textbook. You can honestly get through this course just by carefully reading the textbook and never even going to lecture.

Exams: Like 110, 111 gives you a lot of practice material for the midterm and final. This year, we had 5 practice midterms and 5 practice finals. At the bare minimum, you should do all the practice exams. Then, I would probably do as many of the section problems as possible. For the midterm, the exam is a bit more of a time crunch, so I would recommend doing all the practice midterms in a timed environment (1 hour and 15 minutes). For the final, you have much more time, so if you can't block out 3–hour blocks to do the exams, just make sure you're doing the problems at the very least.

I'm not going to be unfair and say the course has actually taught me nothing, as I have enough trust in Prof. Blitzstein to know if this were truly the case, I would not have been able to complete any of the assignments or exams without too much trouble. However, in terms of an understanding of how the material fits as a coherent whole and of any actual intuition of what anything I'm doing actually means, I would say that I, after an entire term of studying statistics, came out of the course knowing far less than I do about anything else that I have ever tried learning in my life.

The factors that contributed to this in my opinion are as follows:

1. The structuring of the course. The order of presentation of the material was an absolute mess. We jumped around from topic to topic after what felt like 5 seconds spent on each thing we learnt for reasons still unbeknownst to me, which I absolutely hated as it made it feel impossible to actually internalise the information and think about what anything actually meant. We started the course on an absolutely disastrous opening lecture – I vividly remember sitting there hearing about a thousand different words belonging to the subject's technical jargon just thrown onto the board that all proceeded, without fail, to go straight over my head. (In fact, to this day I still don't really understand the seemingly ubiquitous use of asymptotics – surely we need to determine some sort of bound for when this will fail for a small enough number of trials or something no? Surely we can't just be like "oh right yh this is just a nominal CI" and walk away happy. Was all fun and games when we were still working probabilistically and come up with whatever scenario we wanted but we're supposed to be using techniques from this course to model actual data for crying out loud. Also, how can we just shove an MLE into a noninjective function and use invariance regardless – just doesn't make sense in my head). The examples given in lecture at times felt like just an excuse to go back to Stat 110 and play around with our beloved named distributions instead of to build any intuition whatsoever for what's going on generally, and other times were just poorly chosen and failed to illustrate anything substantial. Sad.

2. The problem sets. I'm not going to complain too much about the exams – lowkey I think they're weighted too high percentage–wise on the grading breakdown anyway (like in most of Prof. Blitzstein's courses) and I can imagine most people are grateful that they feel like things don't feel too different from 110 here because they've done this for a full year minimum now at this point and so have gotten used to expect something of this style. Also there's only so much you can test on in a 75–minute closed–book exam. But otherwise, I felt as though the problem sets contributed very significantly to the issue I was talking about earlier. First off, they were so incredibly long and tedious – I once remember counting 25 or something subparts to a single question – which just makes things feel bashy and annoying. I understand that the objective, laundry–list style of the problem sets makes things way easier for the TFs to grade, but pedagogically I think this is a terrible idea as it gives students no room to gain intuition for what they're actually doing (I was so fixated on just bashing what the problems were asking me to bash and get them out of the way that I didn't even have time to think about, for example, how different estimators used to estimate the same estimand were performing relative to each other and whether there were common themes between scenarios in which a type of estimator generally performed 'better' in. In all fairness this was dealt with somewhat when we discussed NEFs and Cramer–Rao lower bound but it was definitely frustrating being so annoyed by bashing to even care about any of this anymore). Speaking of the TFs by the way, they seem to be chosen via quite an arbitrary metric – some of them are extremely good and seem to understand things at a really deep intuitive level (shoutout to Jarell Cheong, Jonah Brenner and Russel Li), and on the other end of the spectrum, I have witnessed some be completely incompetent at explaining concepts (even in week 2) and have also been completely helpless to students until there were a solutions manual right in front of them. Hopeless. Secondly, to build off what I said about the exams earlier, I felt as though one was generally able to just do the p–sets purely off 110 intuition a lot of the time, which was very frustrating because I just didn't feel like I was learning very many new approaches or thinking about anything other than 'right ok another application of Adam's Law/ Eve's Law and out comes thing that they wanted me to calculate'. To be fair, Prof Blitzstein does often mention that he tries to make Stat 110 a good way to prepare for Stat 111 and 171 though so I'm presuming it might just be that, but it does still (at least on the surface) make it seem like we haven't actually learn anything that deep this term. All of this just made the p sets for the course feel extremely tedious and dull (the whole likelihood to score to MLE, then to Fisher info then to MLE asymptotics character arc just gets really tiring after a while and makes you feel like a trained robot doing it out for the n–millionth time. Cringe). I have to admit, although I sucked at R, it was nice to see it be used in the course as I felt like being in touch with the implementation side of it a bit more at least gave a different perspective, but the length of the problem sets just made it feel like it was just another chore to be dealt with. At times the tediousness, length and one–dimensionality of the assignments just made the course feel like you're being constantly hunted down by a wasp with no stinger who tries to attack you and doesn't really succeed in doing any actual damage. Pain.

3. Neil Shephard's lecturing. I'm sure the man is a really nice person in real life and he genuinely gives the energy of someone who'd be interesting to have a long deep chat with especially if you're doing Ec and Stat together as your field of study, but I simply cannot pretend to have learnt very much at all from his teaching. His complete disregard for motivating whatever concept he's covering in lecture as well as his impossibly slow lecturing speed just makes it extremely difficult to understand both what he's doing and why he's doing it. In terms of time and blackboard management, Neil really does not succeed on either to be perfectly honest, and you will more likely than not find him rambling on for around 20 minutes about impertinent points and making confusing mistakes on the blackboard when attempting to state and prove results. Furthermore, you will often find that his lectures sometimes just fail to cover some fairly significant information (i. e. things we were expected to use on our p sets) – this was never a problem in any of Prof Blitzstein's courses before which makes the identity of the elephant in the room extremely obvious. To be fair, he did say in the opening lecture that he was just going to present the "highlights" and that he was making no guarantees on being able to make it through everything like that just seems like an excuse I'd use to not have to prepare for lecture if you ask me. Definitely the worst lecturer I have had this year (but definitely not at this school though – will hesitate on using too many superlatives here when they're not warranted), and having Professor Blitzstein co–teach the same course makes you really aware of the difference in effectiveness. Shame.

P. S. The 111 book is incredibly waffly and thus (at least for me) impossible to actually learn from – I definitely much preferred the style of the textbook for Stat 210.

Overall, I was really not happy about Stat 111 this year due to the utter failure of the course with regards to the construction of a solid intuitive framework of the subject within my brain, and also how unjustifiably time–consuming and dull the course was for a very significant portion of the time I spent on it. Nevertheless, I did feel like the course definitely had its good points, especially when ever so often an intriguing elegant result comes your way during lecture, and even more especially when Prof Blitzstein resurrects Neil Sheppard's awful lecturing. Also, as I said earlier, no two people are interested about exactly the same things in this course, so depending on your own personal goals what I feel about the course might not apply to you, so despite my scathing review I could definitely still see 111 working well for a lot of people as a first course in inference. Finally, and perhaps most importantly, this course has not waivered my interest in the subject one bit (currently hoping that Stat 211 will help me gain a level of intuitive understanding that I would be happy with), and has encouraged me to continue checking out more courses in the statistics department here at Harvard.
