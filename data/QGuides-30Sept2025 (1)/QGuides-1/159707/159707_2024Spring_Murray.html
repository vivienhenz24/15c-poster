***If you have decided you are taking this class***
LS50 is A LOT. It is so easy to go through the course feeling like you understand the content without fully mastering it. I definitely felt like this during LS50A. During winter break, I developed a "strategy" so that I wouldn't feel the same way in LS50B. I stuck to it, and it definitely worked. Here's what I did:
– Don't go into lectures feeling like you have to understand everything. Realistically, you won't. Instead, focus on identifying key concepts introduced during lecture. For example, when learning about a new enzymatic pathway, don't waste your energy trying to memorize specific facts (e.g. what amino acid residues do kinase X phosphorylate?) Instead, take a step back, and think about what this specific example reveals about biology (e.g. not all phosphorylation events entail catalytic activation).
– Set a time of the day to rewatch lectures. While this is definitely NOT necessary to be "successful" in the course, making a habit of rewatching lectures will most certainly make the difference between understanding the concepts and knowing how to use them to solve problems. I didn't do this during LS50A, and my grades in the midterms were good. But I started doing this during LS50B, and my grades in the midterms were great.
– Don't be afraid to ask questions. Sometimes it feels like everyone in LS50 understands the topics better than you do. Don't let this feeling stop you from asking questions.
– Start the PSETs by yourself. Do not go to OHs until you have tried every single problem by yourself. This is something that I didn't do during LS50A, and it definitely affected my learning process. This does not mean that you shouldn't go to OHs: they are so much fun, and a lot of the learning happens during them. Just don't start working on the PSET at OHs. It will stop you from having to figure out how to approach problems by yourself.
– Be proactive about meeting with the course staff. They are so helpful and interestinggetting to know them has been one of the highlights of LS50.

Looking back at a full year of LS50, I feel proud of the work I have accomplished and the friends I have made, but I also have some serious regrets. I am thankful for the support of the teaching staff and the CAs, who pour their heart into the class. I learned to think about science in a way that is applicable to problem–solving and research. That being said, here are my regrets.

1) The community: LS50 is known for its community, but looking back I don't think there was ever a cohesive class community. Rather, there was a series of cliques, and while people were close with the people in their group, they were distant or even hostile with other members of the class. Also, a few students were allowed to dominate the class environment by shaming people for asking questions in lecture or section with zero repercussion. Additionally, people in the class are VERY competitive, especially when it comes to research opportunities. This is especially prevalent in the spring semester, when students are competing for the elusive PRISE fellowship. The drama surrounding this entire process was very hurtful and catty and made me seriously question the community in this class.

2) The exams: The exams in LS50 are ridiculously hard, and some students will never achieve a passing grade on a single midterm. I understand challenging students with difficult exams, but to me the exams in LS50 transitioned from being constructive to just downright defeating. The philosophy of LS50 is very much "break you down and build you up," but I ended up just feeling broken down.

3) Course preparation: I feel that the Q guide is not the most honest about needed prerequisites for this class. I came in with a strong background in biology, chemistry, and calculus and still felt monumentally behind my peers. This is because many people in the class come from prep schools or science academies and have education that goes far beyond AP or IB. If you are coming from a public school background, know that you will most likely be in the minority and consider this before taking LS50. Personally, I found this environment to be extremely draining on my mental health.

4) Pre–med requirements: If you are pre–med, think carefully before taking this course. Taking LS50 means that you will not be able to comfortably take math, stats, or PS11 like other freshmen. I found that LS50 messed up my course sequence and did not fulfill as many requirements as I thought it would.

In its coverage of many relevant intermediate biology topics, I think students who take LS50a are able to succeed in many upper–division MCB/SCRB/Neuro classes if they choose to take them in the spring.

The faculty are amazing. Andrew Murray is the mastermind behind LS50 and is truly an engaging lecturer who is also an excellent teacher who really values the learning experience of his students. Emma Nagy is the workhorse of LS50, and she is the kindest, most wonderful lecturer who genuinely cares about how her students are doing. Ben is ok. The topic–specific faculty are similarly awesome: Kara is such an amazing teacher and has inspired much interest in developmental biology. Doeke is iconic. Cara is super genuine and teaches with incredibly clarity.

As for pre–requisite knowledge, I only took non–AP bio/chem in high school, but I did not have many difficulties learning basic chem/bio because the class does an excellent job establishing fundamentals in both areas. I would say, however, it is very important that you have a decent grasp of BC calculusthis is what I would consider to be the only true pre–requisite. Still I know some people in the class who only did AB and it still turned out fine in the end, so its really about how much you're willing to put in!

Moreover, the social aspect of this class must be addressed. I made so many awesome friends in this class. While these friendships were established in LS50a, they really had time to grow and develop in LS50b. It really is an awesome social environment: you get to see your friends every day, you get to go to lunch with them after class, you get to go to dinner with them after section/lab, and you get to (trauma) bond over psets. Spending so much time together, you will be sure to make friends in LS50 if you want to do so!

For any LS50a–ers wondering about LS50b, I would recommend 97% of you take it. It covers a lot of important mathematical/computer science topics. However, if you are already advanced with math and are planning on taking stat 110/additional classes in statistics/math, then I lowkey would not recommend you continue with LS50b due to its high coverage of topics that you already know or will learn soon. Instead, you should probably just take other science or math courses.
