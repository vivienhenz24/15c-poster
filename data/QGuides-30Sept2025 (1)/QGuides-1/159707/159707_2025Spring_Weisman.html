Overall, I liked the content of LS50b more but its a lie to say that its traditional bio/chem in the second half of the semester. I included a few notes below:

– You WILL learn linear algebra in two weeks, but some of the hardcore material is honestly too much if you dont have a *strong* mathematical foundation. It sometimes took a toll on both the physical and mental health of people I was close with in the class, including myself. No pset should cause people to pull multiple all–nighters or feel any negative thoughts; however, the tight–knit community allowed us to support each other during rough patches. This may be controversial among course staff but after talking with peers, lots of stat mech and dynamical systems could be replaced with more niche bio topics that set us up better for orgo (like Chem 20 – which you shouldnt take with LS50b!), SCRB 10, etc. Yes, were at Harvard; yes, this course is voluntary; yes, were supposed to be the brightest future for science; nonetheless, we are 18/19–year–olds figuring out our identities and the course expectations were too intense at times.

– The rumor that every individual who takes this course gets an A and will get into PRISE/research village program is false. Out of the ~40 students, 15–17 will get an A; 15–17 will get an A–; and about 7–9 will end with a B+. I would assume its possible to get lower if you slack off significantly or cheat on exams. While participation in class is important (DONT BE SCARED TO ASK QUESTIONS DURING LECTURE IF YOU TAKE LS50), your grade is basically dependent on the 3 midterms. You should get above the median on 2 out of 3 if you want an A, which is not always doable without serious dedication or being an inherently gifted mathematician/budding biologist with an innate understanding of chemistry. Practice problems are critical versus passive studying, though the quantity of practice problems were sometimes lacking.

– I dont necessarily regret taking LS50 and I know the course staff gets frustrated when students only think about grades, but its possible to be intellectually curious AND care about grades. LS50 may have prevented certain fellowships later on in college (lots of students get As broadly due to easier intro courses/grade inflation), and its unlikely that one can make the Phi Beta Kappa GPA cutoff with A–s or B+s (it counts for 2 classes each semester), even with straight As for subsequent semesters. There is no shame in taking LS1b. Since LS50 is mostly quantitative, its hard to say that I am less prepared for graduate school in biology/chemistry or even medicine in the future just because I struggled with topics like stat mech and Markov chains.

– Students from amazing STEM magnet, international, or private schools have a clear advantage and it at times disadvantages those from under–funded or STEM–lacking high schools. I feel like there is a general correlation between getting an A in LS50 and doing Olympiads in HS, attending magnet schools that teach linalg/orgo, not being first–gen, etc. Though I want to avoid making generalizations, and if you devote sufficient time, anyone can succeed!

– Some weeks feel quite disjointed and comparable to academic whiplash with topics alternating on different days. I dont think its an issue per se, but it sometimes makes reviewing material challenging. I think this was expected going into the course. Also, I wish the course staff did more direct Python instruction or a spring workshop, since many of the PSET Colabs would take hours on end.

Take everything with a grain of salt. By the end, if you accept the challenge, you will truly embrace the class motto By all means necessary. Otherwise, please be sure to enjoy your freshman year at Harvardits a pretty special time!

LS50 is definitely a special class, and it most definitely is not for the faint hearted. After taking this course, I have thought very hard about whether I regretted my decision, and the answer is no, I don't. However, I think prospective students should know a few things before enrolling.

LS50 is an intensive introduction to the life sciences with lectures every day for the full year. Although you aren't required to take it both semesters, it is highly encouraged to continue throughout the year as concepts build on another. There are several brilliant rotating professors who take turns teaching you different parts of chemistry, biology and statistics and all have their strengths and are both brilliant scientists and good people.

The all nighter's in the library 2 days before the midterm when you're on your 4th celsius and you still have 8 lectures to go through, that 8 page essay you're hit with right after the exam because you've put everything else in your life off to study for this exam, or spending 6 hours on part b of a pset question because stat mech is hard, this will be your reality if you don't have an excellent educational background. I don't regret taking this course because of the people I met, but I honestly cannot recommend it to someone who came from a really terrible high school or doesn't have a thorough background in Calculus, Chemistry, and Bio because it really does make a difference.

If you plan on taking this course, it's excellent for a pre–graduate school path because of the sheer amount of content it covers. You'll be able to explore so many different interesting fields and learn how to think independently. Some general advice on the course:
– The TFs will cycle throughout the year, some of them will be extremely helpful with PSETs and exam content, you just need to figure out which
– The CAs are absolutely wonderful, and a great resource for help on PSETs
– Not all of the lecture content will be relevant to your exams
– If you have no background in coding, I highly recommend watching some videos or teaching yourself some basic python. Coding is apart of almost every single PSET and it can be quite tedious if you've never done it before
– On exams, displaying an understanding of the topic even when you have no idea how to answer the question can typically get you some credit
– Take some time later each day to go over the lecture and ask yourself "What do they want me to take away from this", I started doing this 2nd semester and found it quite helpful
– If an outside resource presents itself, use it. It can't hurt and it will usually help

And by far the best thing you can do if you take this course, is make friends. Your classmates will make this course so worth it (and bearable at times). When I was pulling all nighters in lamont, the friends I was with and our 3am CVS runs made me keep going. When it was 4am and I was crashing out about stat mech, it was a friend who stayed up with me and helped me find the algebra mistake in my derivations. The community you can find in this randomly selected group of 40 people is truly something special :)
