Great class; takes effort but easy to excel in. The midterms were a bit rough, but for the most part the exams were fair. Cora is a great prof!
This course will teach you how to do computations. Embrace that fact and have the mindset of "How do I calculate this?" and you will do great. Of course, computations can be kind of boring. If you want, you can then think about the interesting physical ideas that the course introduces but that is marginal for your success in the course.
OMG I LOVED THIS CLASS. Quantum mechanics is such a cool topic and I'm so glad I decided to skip 15c and take it. I was worried because Cora tells everyone in the beginning "I'm gonna assume you all took 15b and 15c" but to be honest, you're totally fine without 15c. Besides, quantum teaches you all the stuff that's actually relevant moving on from 15c without you having to go through the 15c growing pains. It's awesome because taking 143a will open a lot of gates for you: you have more options for physics courses or research labs to join. It's a great pill to swallow.

I personally am not a fan of the psets. They felt very computational to the point that I was questioning if I'm taking QM or Math 1a. It's literally pages and pages of stupid Gaussian integrals where you have to integrate by parts like 3 times for one integral. And there's like at least 12 integrals to evaluate on most of the early psets. I think I would've done better if the psets were conceptually challenging and tested my understanding of the material instead of my ability to compute integrals. But that's more of a problem with the book that the problems are sourced from, I suppose. Speaking of the book, Griffith's book is great (a pretty standard textbook) and it was pretty clear and easy to follow. I highly recommend reading the book because it's very easy to follow along the derivations, get lost in the math, and forget about what you're doing at a conceptual level.

In terms of pre–reqs, DEFINITELY TAKE LINEAR ALGEBRA BEFORE. You absolutely MUST understand how to diagonalize matrices, the concept behind eigenvalues, vector spaces, etc. Knowing Fourier Analysis helps too. I found my knowledge from 21b transferred beautifully. Thinking of constructing eigenbases, understanding the spectral theorem, etc can help you establish a stronger understanding of the mathematical concepts involved. Of course if you want an even more rigorous introduction to QM you could take 251. But the point of this course is to provide you the basic tools to start doing real physics.

There are likely freshman who want to skip 15b, 15c and take this course. I think you could totally do that as long as you have taken 21b/22a. If you've taken Physics 19, you'll find that some things are familiar. If you have zero experience with linear algebra, definitely do not take it. It's impossible to take this course without linear algebra–– you would either have to crash course it or you would be memorizing formulas without really understanding.

I loved this course so much and it has definitely made me hungry to learn more physics!!
A very interesting class! Worth taking if you enjoy math and linear algebra
I dont particularly have strong feelings for this class, which is not a negative thing for me to say. I was mostly just doing work and it almost felt mechanical. The problems were always from the textbook which added to the mechanical feel of the class. But I would still say that we learned interesting stuff and this definitely opened a new world of physics for me. Im really excited to learn further quantum physics so I guess that is a good takeaway of mine! This class just leaves you wanting more and wanting to learn more deeply.
If you are a physics student, you have to take the class. If you do not have to take it, I still think this can still be a valuable class if you want to know what quantum mechanics is. The approach is largely computational, so don't expect extensive philosophical discussion, but at least you should be equipped to have those conversations and know some of the things that make quantum mechanics what it is.
If you are able to take 15C as a prereq instead of coreq I think the material would've been a lot more digestible, as I was definitely having a hard time keeping up with the material in both courses though if you need to take them at the same time (like maybe if you are a junior wanting to take higher level courses like me), taking both is still doable! Cora was incredibly lovely and passionate, I would definitely recommend taking the course if she's teaching again!
READ THE TEXTBOOK! And if its been a while since you've taken Linear Algebra, then refer to the textbook's appendix, which makes for a very helpful refresher on the necessary concepts. I simply did not find the lectures sufficient to learn the material, and, in all honesty, I learned most of the material from reading.

For the engineers out there thinking that it would be cool and quirky to study quantum mechanics, be very wary of the work that it takes. It is just as difficult as its reputation purports and in the end, you will end up with very niche knowledge about a topic that will have almost no practical application. That said, if quantum subfields of electronics or particle accelerators are your thing, then go for it, but as a mechanical engineer, I unfortunately never see myself using this information except for the intuition it gave me for real–world applications of linear algebra.
This is a hard class, but it's not impossible. I definitely needed to put time into office hours and section, and even then I still didn't grasp 100% of the course material. Like many physics classes, there are casual references to things beyond the scope of the class that can be very confusing and overwhelming, and some people come in with a very strong prior knowledge base. As someone who came in knowing pretty much nothing about quantum, I recommend blocking these things out as much as you can and focusing on the fundamental things the course is trying to teach you. If you're awake and paying attention you'll be okay.
I would definitely take this course if you are interested in physics! It is a fun course and you learn a ton from it.
Please read the textbook. Griffiths saved my life in this course.
This class is pretty tough conceptually. Workload it's not too bad. I'm an engineer and took this class as a secondary requirement, and its not really like any other physics class I've taken. Lots and lots of theory and not many physical applications until you get to the hydrogen atom at the end of the class. If you like physics for its physical applications to the world (15a, 125 enjoyers), this probably isn't the class for you. That said, the content of the class is cool and interesting, but it is very confusing. I found it most helpful to read through the chapters in the textbook a few times over throughout the semester (only four chapters are covered 143a so this doesn't take very long). Overall, its a good class and one that I am happy to have taken, but more to make conversation rather than to apply it to my future studies/career.
