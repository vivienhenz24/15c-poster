Easily one of the best courses I've taken at Harvard. Professor Miles and Hannah, the TF, are so knowledgeable and passionate. The classroom environment felt welcoming. We truly built a community of care while exploring some of the most interesting things I have read.
Take this class! It gives you so much hands on experience with being critical of the immediate world around you, and the realities of American Slavery in New England. We went on multiple field trips, primarily to the Royale Slave House and Quarters in Medford (across the street fromTufts), and also visited the Arnold Arboretum to document the site of the Giles family, who were an Afro–Indigenous family who lived on the land that is now the Arboretum's Bussey Hill. We also read many engaging sources from journalists, academics, and creative writers. Highly recommend, I also made genuine friendships in the class in a way that I haven't in other courses I've taken here.
HIST 1945 is for people who truly want to do something beyond read and talk. Dont get me wrong, theres a lot of that, But the reason I took this course was the action component. Its easy to feel like youre in an ivory tower at Harvard. This class makes that feeling a little less prevalent.
One of the best history courses I have taken as a senior concentrator. Prof. Miles and Hannah are an incredible pair. Taking a course with Prof. Miles is a must, but especially a seminar, as she is truly fantastic at leading one – beginning with some time to know each other (not ice–breakers, but really understanding your "why" for the course and for caring about history in general...it built trust between us quickly, which showed in our discussions), setting high expectations for the rigor of our conversations off the bat – asking follow up questions, synthesizing our conversations, and pushing our ideas. She is truly the best at cultivating conversations that not only bring the readings to life while also pushing your critical thinking skills.

Discussions were genuinely rigorous, readings were always relevant and illuminating, and the final project was a fulfilling challenge to actually put our seminar discussions into practice our discussions by having to think critically in an applied way (since the final projects were shared with the Arboretum to be implemented). If you want to be challenged in a new way as a history concentrator by being pushed to think creatively and practically (I would not recommend this to freshman/sophomores or anyone not already familiar with history), then absolutely take this course.

Prof. Miles is one of the few professors on this campus who really cares for you as a person. Yes, she brings snacks to class (which is genuinely a gesture to be grateful for), but she also wants you to come to know yourself more through this course – the course challenged me to think critically about my own morals and future as a historian, because of Prof. Miles's genuine care for how history does, can, and should exist for the public. There were a few times when she asked us at the start of class to reflect about what we want to contribute to the world, and it honestly makes me emotional to remember. It's easy to lose sight of why you're studying at Harvard, but Prof. Miles will not let you forget it. This class shifted the way I think as a historian, but it was also was philosophical in a way that was fulfilling for me beyond being a historian. I will continue to think about the questions raised in this class as I go throughout my life, which is not something I can say for many courses at this school.
This is a course worth taking! You can only expand your definition of history and how you can apply it to the rest of your life. This course reinforces how history impacts people and society and is necessary for anyone even a little interested inhibitory and public history.
This class is a wonderful opportunity! Tiya Miles is a genius and also very kind and caring, you will be lucky to take a class with her. If you are at all interested in a carer in history, I would highly recommend it––it is a great deep dive into public history focusing on one of the most important and most misrepresented topics in American history: slavery.
I wouldn't recommend this course for graduate students even though it is cross–listed. It felt more like auditing an undergraduate class. That being said, some of the other graduate students were great thought partners, and the conversations we had because of this class were interesting.
