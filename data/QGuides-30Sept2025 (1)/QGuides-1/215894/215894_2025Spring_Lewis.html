There's a qguide review from Fall 2022 that was super long and listed a lot of concerns with the course. After taking the class, I agree with that review in its entirety – I would recommend going back to read that review, as that student explains the issues better than I do.

To reiterate some points and to contribute my own experience:
1) lack of diversity of opinion. All of the texts and the lectures effectively drilled in one viewpoint: borders are bad, and all the solutions we've tried so far to make it better are also bad. We especially bash on nationalism and vaguely suggest that imperialism was better. It seems like the takeaway is that humanity should all embrace each other as brothers in arms and magic away all the borders, with little to nothing about the real logistics of this. Classmates have expressed to me that they don't feel comfortable raising their hands in class to ask about realistic/logistical issues with reducing borders, because it runs contrary to the messaging of the class.
2) the assignments don't teach you to think. There's a midterm where the entire point is to word vomit everything you can remember the professor saying in one specific lecture. There's a memo you have to write using only the sources they give you, with little to no context and without being able to search up anything yourself, "because it's realistic", so you end up sort of summarizing and rephrasing what the sources say with no new synthesis or insight of the information. The point of this assignment is apparently to realize that if we were the colonizer, we would also have made the same terrible biased decisions. The final project is a podcast and it has to be 5 minutes long. This is at best 750 words. This project feels like it's encouraging us to do a cursory investigation into a random border, and then to create a short, quick, easily digestible product spouting a clear, un–nuanced argument for popular consumption. Sure, maybe "real change is done by podcast episodes" (?????), but I personally feel that as a course, it would be much more of an engaging and intellectual exercise for us to just write a paper, where we can fully engage with all the gray areas. It's lowkey harder to learn how to edit sound, than to do the research and form an argument for this.
3) no historical cohesion. The syllabus jumps all over the place (U.S. to Europe to Asia, etc.), with vanishingly little historical context. We go to section and we're expected to have an intelligent conversation about these borders while knowing nothing about the history of the peoples who lived there or primary sources from them. I'm a STEM major and the last time I thought about the Samoan experience under the League of Nations was never. Understandably, everyone just lands on the same, ground–breaking argument: although Samoans aren't white, they're people too, and should be treated as such. This is about how deep the discussion gets across the course.
4) my enjoyment of this class was genuinely reduced by the professor's management of the lectures and her passion for speaking. Every time we play a video or open the presentation or something, the entire lecture needs to be put on pause while we hear about the step–by–step procedure of opening a browser window. Meanwhile, although the guest lectures are excellent and delivered by professors knowledgeable in the field, the Q&A section is a bit odd. We're asked to submit questions the night before, and then the professor reads them out loud herself. One time, she said "I'm just going to read ALL of these out loud and then you can pick one to answer", and then the guest professor stood there and we all sat there while she rattled off a whole list of questions. Another time, she distributed index cards and suggested that anyone too shy to raise their hands could write the questions down there instead. At the beginning of the Q&A section, she walked around the class verbally asking each table if there were any index cards for her to collect (there were none), requiring us all to sit and wait for her to walk around the entire classroom narrating her journey rather than allowing us to start asking questions while she checked.

The silver lining: You don't really need to do the reading. The readings assigned are always super long and complex, but we don't actually unpack any of it. In section you might be expected to discuss it with a partner and the TF might walk around, but it's nothing a general skim and maybe a closer reading of the intro/conclusion won't solve. It's not the worst class to take if you just need to knock out a gened requirement and have a great deal of patience.
