Hi all! This comment is meant to serve as a very wholistic rundown of the course. Do not fear this course, you can do it.

Teachers and Material Learned
– DS covered: arrays, graphs, heaps, hash functions
– Algorithms
(1) search and sort algorithms
(2) graph traversal/connected components/best path algorithms
(3) greedy algorithms
(4) dynamic programming
(5) hashing algorithms
(6) random algorithms
(7) approximation algorithms


Background Knowledge
– CS 20: good for the graph algorithms and also knowing how to use proofs and Latex
– STAT 110: the second half of the course after the midterm stresses a lot of knowledge about expectation (LOTE), some distributions (binomial, first success, geometric), and probability/combinatorics, all of which are taught in STAT 110
– CS 120/121: generally recommended for NP/NP complete lectures. Also, the format of these classes with CS 124 is nearly identical, so it feels really nice and smooth for a transition


PSETS
These are generally long. Some PSETS can be upwards of 20 pages of writing. So, how do you make this manageable? Simple! Read below:
– Read lecture notes or at least attend lecture. Topics carry straight from the lecture.
– Go to section or at least read the section notes. Many times, some ideas in the section carry straight into the PSET
– You have about 1–2 weeks per PSET to complete it. I recommend the minute it comes out to go to OH and work on it with the group there. Working on it with a group makes it seem way more manageable than struggling alone.
– You've probably heard this before, but having a group of ~3 people you can trust to help and check over work saves you for this class. So just find some buddies and play around with it for the first few weeks to see what is best.
– On average, I attended 4 OH per week to get the PSETS done. If you just plan ahead and know what office hours you want to go to, you should be totally fine.
– I always say start earlier so that you don't feel stressed for time. Time isn't really a big factor for most cases, and they have late days you can use, which is very nice.
– The 2 week PSETS are generally longer than the 1 week PSETS but the length is still appropriate
– On average, doing the PSET from scratch to typing it to double checking it takes about 12–15 hours per week


ProgSETS
– You'll have around 3 programming sets, in my year done with partner
– Know Python and/or C++ for these
– Typically not that taxing, you usually code up an algorithm you learned in class or try some experiments in understanding the best approaches to algorithms.

Because course policy says that if you submit > 2 days late with illness, youll have the pset excused but we will increase the weight of your exams to make up for the pset which might sound good but the average exam mark is below 50% – it is NEVER WORTH IT unless you are too ill to even finish half the pset. One of my friends was not so lucky. They had a more severe documented illness that prevented them from handing in a pset on time even with two late days. Course staffs response? You get a zero.

It may have helped if the psets were reasonable in this course, but sadly, that is not the case. They will generally be at least twice as hard as anything discussed in lecture and will take well over 10 hours. Well CS124 has one of the worst curves in Harvard CS, with only 20% getting an A and 20% getting an A–.

The lectures are okay (sometimes too handwavey), but sections are good. The pacing is really fast though, so you dont explore algorithms in depth – for the DP unit, we really only talked about 0–1 knapsack and string problems before being thrown into the next unit.

This course could have been good if they made the pset cadence longer (every ~2 weeks), added a major coding component to each one, and split the course into two semesters (with more depth and breadth). Or alternatively, it would have been nice if a CS 124 alternative that focused more on coding existed, but unfortunately, no such class exists at Harvard. I do not recommend this course and I do not recommend getting sick during this course, but sometimes we dont have a choice in life.

My advice for future students taking this class:
KEEP UP WITH SECTION EVERY WEEK! This is super super important. Sections help you understand the algorithms conceptually, understand what the process of running them looks like, and learn relevant information for the week's pset. There have been countless times when I was completely lost after a lecture and then everything clicked when I went to section. The 124 TFs are really good teachers. Take advantage of that. Find a section that works well with your learning style and stick with it. If you absolutely have to miss a week, make sure you watch the section recording.
GO TO OFFICE HOURS. Office hours are invaluable in this course. Make sure you go to them to ask questions about the pset and inevitably to beg for the answers.
MAKE SURE YOU THOROUGHLY UNDERSTAND YOUR PSET ANSWERS. In my opinion, it's okay to work with a super cracked pset group and leech off of them/office hours for some pset answers. But it is SUPER important that you understand the solution and why it works. The psets are designed the way they are for a reason. They teach important conceptual topics/problem–solving strategies. The intuition behind them is very helpful for a general understanding of topics, and variations of pset problems may come up in exams. (One of the finals questions this year was pretty much just a reduction to an algorithm we had to give in a previous pset).
MAKE SURE YOU DON'T FALL BEHIND. It is very easy to fall behind in this course, and it's very hard to catch back up when you do. If you are confused about a topic after that week's section, make sure to ask questions about it or figure it out as you work through the corresponding pset problem. I think understanding an algorithm conceptually is the most important thing, because once that makes sense, everything else will (more or less) fall into place after a lot more thinking. This will also help with problem solving on exam problems.
CHECK AND USE ED. Ed is the platform we use to ask questions about lectures and psets. If you have a question, don't be afraid to use Ed.
