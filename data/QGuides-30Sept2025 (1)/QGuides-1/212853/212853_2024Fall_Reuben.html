This fall was the first semester this class had as much interest as it did (because of the events of the last year or so at Harvard), and it was clear that the teaching staff was re–engineering the class in order to suit this demand. On many counts they did a good job: the final group project was generally well–organized, Julie Reuben's lectures were fantastic, and the selection of readings always felt relevant. Other parts of the class were extremely annoying: the Perusall annotations were so much more ineffective than simply doing the readings in PDF format, the in–class debates/open comment periods were mostly useless, the teaching staff solicited but never really did anything with our feedback every week, and in general there was a hand–holdy feeling to much of the way the class was organized.

Obviously you can't expect a gen ed to change your life, and even though no discussion ever really got deep enough to tackle any questions head–on, I think this class did a good job of introducing us to frameworks/ways of organizing debates over higher ed that allow you to parse the minefields of public discourse about it. Julie in particular did her best to prefigure her own pedagogical values in the classroom, and I was very grateful for her care and attention on this point. If/when this class is offered in the future, I think the teaching staff will have learned a lot about running a class like this at this scale. Grading was pretty generous as far as I can tell, and the readings were not excessive at all.
