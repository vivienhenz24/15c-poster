I was confused about the workload for this class going into it, so I'll explain below for anyone also confused or trying to balance out an already difficult courseload.

Assignments:
This class is almost entirely based on effort rather than correctness (but they know if you completely BS assignments or don't put any time into them, so don't do that). If you just put in 1–3 hours for each big weekly assignment (readings/learning catalytics = 1 chapter per week and ~12 multiple choice questions, desmos tutorial = 30 shorter questions, challenge = 3 long multi–step questions), and spread it across several days, then it's super manageable and flexible to work with (cramming is also doable but just more exhausting lol). It's just like psetting but without the crying. I used to spend way too much time on assignments in the beginning of the semester, but once you get used to the flow of it and get a better feeling for student expectations, then it's much easier to work more efficiently. then, during one half of class (2x a week) you're expected to meet with your team and review your responses, discuss them with a TF, and then receive the answer key and submit corrections/a short reflection for each tutorial and challenge. If you're efficient with your time, these corrections can usually be entirely done during class. You also need to re–do your learning catalytics questions as a team after you do it individually, and have to make sure that you get at least 50% correct.

Projects:
the other half of class is spent on either project prep in the lab or "skill session" activities in which you learn skills specific to your project, e.g. arduino coding. your projects and skill sessions can usually be done entirely within the allotted class time, it's great. The projects are pretty relaxed since you have time to work on them during class, as long as everyone in your group pulls their weight, then it's a breeze. I got lucky with great groups (they're randomly assigned) but you can always communicate to the course staff if you're struggling. The final project was much shorter than the 2 semester projects, which meant that the class was done before reading period.

Attendance:
you have the option to complete your team homework reviews on zoom if some members are sick, but usually always have to be in person for the project/skill session stuff anyways so you basically need to attend every class, but the course staff are pretty understanding if you can't make it for some reason, so just keep in touch with them if you miss anything.

Grading:
You're required to submit pretty much all of the assignments in order to get an A in the class, but because you're not graded on correctness for the work you complete outside of class, this automatically saves you a lot of stress/time compared to freaking out about psets at office hours. Absolutely prioritize filling out all of the surveys for the class throughout the semester. If you complete 2 specific surveys in the beginning of the semester and then 2 again at the end, you get 6 extra credit points that can be used to boost individual assignment grades. You can use this boost once in each category (so you can use two points to boost a challenge and a tutorial, but not two challenges). You also need to use one of these points if you missed a deadline and want to submit an assignment for credit. grading is based on a 3–point system; 0=no credit, 1=needs improvement, 2=A+, 3=super A+ (you can use 3's to boost 1's, also just once per category). I would try to save these for the end of the semester for when the content gets harder. You also have the option to just submit a redo request for an assignment that you already submitted that got a 0 or 1, which does not use up any of your extra credit points or 3's. IMO this is the best option because if you retain all of your 6 extra credit points, you can boost your letter grade. This class gives you so many opportunities to improve your grade.

overall, i could not be more thankful that this class exists, and it is a certified premed senior spring course in my book after years of exam courses. I took PS2, and then AP50A. I actually didn't mind PS2 at all, but avoided PS3 because of the scary Q guide. If you're deciding between PS3 and AP50B, here's what I'll say: If you do every assignment for AP50B, you will get an A, but at the cost of a lot of your sanity. The workload is never ending, with at minimum 5 weekly assignments due Sunday, Tuesday, Thursday, plus weekly team meetings, lab reports, etc. The textbook is on Perusall, which is one of the most frustrating ways to have to read and annotate a textbook while it feels like Big Brother monitors your engagement. Everything is team–work based, which can be fun sometimes depending on your team. Assignments are effort–based, which means that your effort will likely go to getting them completed and submitted, not actually learning the material. I will definitely need to go back and teach myself E&M before I take the MCAT. Eric is LOVELY and so passionate about teaching, but unfortunately, I don't think the design of the course is very conducive to learningat least it wasn't for me. Ultimately, if you are okay to suck it up, do constant busy work for the semester, and not learn much, knowing that you have a guaranteed A, take AP50B. But if I could go back, I would seriously consider taking PS3 now that there is a new professor. PS3 is the traditional pset–midterm–final class that you're probably used to by now, whereas AP50B was a whole 'nother beast. Choose wisely!!!

This class was genuinely the worst class I have ever taken in my time at Harvard. It was disorganized and a complete waste of time. This course is claims to be a flipped classroom but it fails to give students adequate materials, activities, and general structure to successfully teach themselves the concepts at home. The main form of "teaching" is the textbook written by the prof that students read at home. We are required to add comments and questions in the book but TFs don't even look at the comments so often questions go unanswered unless another student is able to supply an answer. The textbook can be really confusing and since it is the only source of teaching, if you don't understand the textbook you are basically screwed for that entire week. The in–class portion often feels like a complete waste of time. We sit on Zoom and go over homework we already completed. Then we wait, sometimes up to an hour to go over what we talked about with a TF who often gives a poor explanation or rushes through the discussion because tons of other teams are also waiting to be checked in. The assignments themselves take so much time to complete and one of them, the Readiness Assurance, requires you to schedule a time outside of class to meet with your team and go over all your answers together and re–do the assignment. So you do almost all the work outside of class plus you need to teach yourself the material on your own as well. Assignment grades are updated extremely late on canvas and make–ups take even longer. Often you will complete an assignment and by the time you can do a make up it has been 2 months since you initially submitted the assignment. To make everything worse, there are too many point people in the teaching team so communication is so frustrating because you are shuffled through like 4 people before you can talk to someone who can even help you. Plus this means you might be in contact with like 5 people at once for different issues which is super overwhelming. Overall I'm really disappointed with this class. It requires so much work and time outside of the 6 hours of class a week and I barely feel like I learned anything.
I would very much like to caution future students about taking this class. It is true that you can get a good grade in this class by putting in the work, but it comes with several costs. Despite trying very hard, I struggled to learn the physics in this class and am walking away with an understanding of the concepts of physics, but little ability to do real physics problems (as would be prompted on the MCAT or in future classes). Because there is so much work outside of class, it is hard to spend time trying to actually understand the physics and the only true exposure you are given to the topics is your own reading in the textbook (the tutorial usually only covers one or two of the week's topics in depth). The group aspect of this class is a double edged sword– it can be helpful and very fun when you have a good group and absolutely terrible when you get a bad one. I had great groups some of the time and, other times, was left to do entire group projects on my own without support. The teaching staff was incredibly friendly, but they were understaffed and our team was often required to stay after the 3 hour period to do the work. Eric, on the other hand, is a very nice professor and clearly believes in the structure of the course. I am not recommending that the class should be avoided– I'm just encouraging future students to weigh their options before taking the class.
