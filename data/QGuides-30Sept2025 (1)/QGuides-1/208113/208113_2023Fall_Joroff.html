What i learned as someone who has never taken an ESPP course before and doesn't do anything climate related:
– i learned how legal regulations and structures work, how policy initiatives are shaped by existing laws, how laws enforce corporations or government bodies to do climate stuff, and how congress makes regulating agencies to enforce climate stuff. This is not only applicable to the EPA and climate space, but to all aspects of law and regulation, which is cool since the knowledge is transferable!
– I gained experience in writing different tones of voice, and catering my writing to be concise and specific to the audience depneding on the context or type of document i was writing (ie. comment letter, legislation, corporation climate statement, case study summary)
– i learned how legal precedent sets boundaries for what can be done in the future with the law and how this aids or inhibits current climate advocacy

This class is a lot of work, but it is a rewarding experience. Being exposed to concepts of law and litigation as it pertains to climate change was incredibly intellectually stimulating. Even if that is not your intent, it may be helpful to know about the legal side of climate change.
Take this junior seminar! You learn about interesting topics like tort and takings law in a way that applies them to climate change adaptation and mitigation, looking at related issues from legal, legislative, and private perspectives. Professor Joroff has a real–world job in Boston city government and has a history of working in environmental law, so she is very well–versed in course topics and can tell several fascinating and informative anecdotes about cases she's worked on.
