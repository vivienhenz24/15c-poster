This is a fantastic course! Professor Freed is amazing! Highly recommend it! One of the best course I’ve ever taken! Many materials can be involved, but don’t worry, because you can always pick them up along the way and the professor and the teaching fellows are very nice and helpful!
as an undergrad who (maybe mistakenly) took this class out of curiosity, this class was baffling in many many ways. at face level, this class is extremely challenging even to the most prepared. the level of math that dan just casually mentions as elementary facts that one would have learnt in kindergarten is kinda insane. i am by no means a smart person, but as an undergrad who has taken many math classes and one grad class on differential geometry, it was still really really difficult to follow dan's lectures at many points as he truly goes way and above what one learns in a usual math class. since dan is a geometer at heart, he will go on and on about advanced concepts in geometry, and even though he does not require background in these topics as a hard prereq for the class, be warned that you will become lost approximately 2 lectures in if you have not studied symplectic geometry before. furthermore, as the name of the class might suggest, being a physics student amongst a sea of mathematicians might be the biggest advantage that one might have in this class. i think it is no surprise that even though the class was packed at the start with many graduate students from both math and physics departments, by the end a majority of the physics grad students stayed on, since a strong background in physics (or at least having seen this stuff before in physics) gives you a strong starting point in terms of motivating the material that dan talks about, something that i feel is lacking in those that have no seen quantum field theory before. as a physics student with just a semester of qft under my belt, i was able to appreciate maybe half of the physics that dan talked about.

homework was okay at the beginning but as the semester progressed, it got much harder to even know what to write down for problem sets. dan was really not kidding when he said that the problems were open ended and meant to challenge the grad students. it was perhaps a bit too open–ended, to the extent that at many points even the graduate tfs were not sure what the solution to the problem should be, or even how much you were supposed to write down, which was pretty funny and at least gave me some comfort that i wasnt a fool struggling in the class because of my severe lack of mathematical preparation. also, it was a bit pointless when most of the graduate students dont even look at the psets anyways :( i kinda feel bad for this, but towards the last few psets i just wrote down stuff that i thought was sort of correct and handwaved a whole bunch of stuff since i wasnt even sure how much i needed to answer anymore

despite all this, dan is an extremely good lecturer and probably one of the kindest and at the same time most cracked professors i have seen at harvard. i guess the math administration finally did something right for once in their lives by pulling dan over from austin, because his exposition is extremely clear and he is one of the rare few professors that actually knows everything off the top of his head. at the very least, he gives off the impression that he prepares for his classes and does not stumble over his proofs, which is hard to come by in the math department. he is also a kind soul and even reduced the number of problems on homeworks cuz he knew that many of the undergrads were struggling to keep up in the class (this almost moved me to tears). plus he has a sick sense of humour which is all that matters. overall a 10/10 mathematician and a 10000/10 human being. despite how well he taught, i still managed to fall asleep in almost every class, but i feel like thats more of my fault than his. i do have to say though that his voice is extremely calming which definitely contributed a lot to that
