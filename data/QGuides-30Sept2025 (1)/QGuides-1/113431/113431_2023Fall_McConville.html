I can say with the utmost certainty that this was far–and–away my least favorite class that I have ever taken at Harvard. First and foremost, I believe that calling this a statistics class is misleading. There is very little in the way of statistics that is accomplished in this class. It felt that every statistical concept beyond median and mean was very briefly touched on, and then immediately replaced with a string of code (my most memorable moment in lecture would certainly be when integration was explained by way of showing a sum, and then transitioning to an integral, telling us that essentially, one was magically "transformed" into the other).

This class rapidly transitions from what should be a statistics and data science class to (what felt like) a pure data science class, generally forgoing any semi–rigorous explanation of a concept; replacing it with a transient explanation that leaves much to be desired. I believe that there is a balance to be struck between rigor and what is actually within the scope of the class (this isn't supposed to be Stat 110, after all), but I can say that this class definitely does not exemplify the ideal balance of these two core values. Many key concepts are glossed over in favor of their R counterpart. Conceptually, these topics aren't even terribly difficult. So, if it is an explanation you are looking for, I would urge you to take another class.

The homework assignments, by–and–large, were rather fair, albeit rather redundant in several aspects. I believe that there lies yet another imbalance here: You must assign enough work to keep the class engaged, but not so much that it would become tedious. While completing the homework, I felt rather disengaged by the third or fourth time that I had to perform the same problem with minor changes in a single assignment. It becomes a matter of "how can I get this done as quickly as possible," rather than "how can I engage with this best to maximize retention?"

The midterm (only one, thank goodness) was also less than satisfactory. The problems weren't inherently difficult, but the time that was allotted for their completion was rather optimistic to say the least. So, be prepared to be rushed.
A quite odd portion of the class is the "participation" grade. This is comprised of one (1) visit to an office hour, and submitting two questions in the class Slack channel. I didn't have any questions, so it was a little strange going to an office hour – only to leave after we made contact with the CA.

To give credit where credit is due, Professor McConville is quite pleasant and rather fair. There were multiple occasions where she would move a deadline back or otherwise encourage attendance/participation. She frequently made herself available during office hours, and it does seem as if she cares about her students. My section, while ultimately rather pointless (yet required...), was also tolerable. There were a few insights, and the CA made it a low pressure environment, but I cannot overstate how little I wanted to attend the section. Additionally, if you pay attention to the lecture slides, they will contain the majority of the answers for your homework assignments; making the homework a little more approachable, but still less than ideal. In accordance with what was actually taught, I know that I must divulge my biases. I enjoy the rigorous side of things, and I like knowing the whole topic before working on problems related to it. Furthermore, I am not a big computer science student. As such, it may make sense as to why I didn't enjoy this class. So, as you make your judgements, keep this in mind.
