My favorite course in the philosophy department! Must take.
Great class, definitely take if you are interested in analytic philosophy or economics. Stanczyk is a great lecturer, and brings a certain seriousness to class that I really appreciated. The readings are all pretty great, and I felt inclined to try to read them all because they were genuinely interesting. The class material got slightly less interesting with respect to time, though. In my opinion, the purely theoretical discourse on Rawls' principles of justice, equal opportunity, and the allocation of scarce goods were all top tier. I gained a strong foundational understanding of the theory. However, I felt like much of the rest of the class was application of this theory to various social problems. This was certainly important––don't get me wrong. But it felt less rigorous and theoretical, and more empirical. I certainly did appreciate all the material criticizing Rawls, especially the readings from G.A Cohen. Definitely an higher–tier philosophy class overall.
Take this course to gain a very important perspective on the structure of our current society and how it allows for a wide variety of inequalities. Professor Stanczyk is awesome!
I would tell future students that this class is an amazing opportunity to learn about injustice and inequality.
If there's one class you need to take at Harvard, I think this is it. It's the best–structured class I've ever taken with such engaging lectures and a very doable workload of weekly readings and a couple papers. This is a class that really makes you think, and I'll be thinking about the things we talked about for a long while.
i think the course name is not the clearest, so i'd say this class is half rawls; half using political philosophy for public policy. you will probably like the class slightly more if you like the second half better, but ymmv. prof stancyzk is great at breaking down & reconstructing arguments in class. if isaijah is still tfing he is the best!
This class is pretty openly a view of inequality from a very, VERY left wing lens. I'm pretty moderate politically, so it was definitely an interesting sight to see. Some takeaways:

This is less of a philosophy class and more of a sociology/anthropology of why america–bad–capitalism–bad class. You start with the works of Rawls, but pretty much all the rest of the readings are either politically charged ramblings or direct appeals to emotion. This is not to say the readings were bad; Waldron's article on the lack of freedoms for the homeless, in particular, was extremely striking. In general, I'd say the readings are extremely one sided, but at least interesting. Philosophical rigor is rather low.

Prof. Stanczyk is every stereotype of a male humanities professor rolled into one. He's pretty nice, and quite funny, which is always good. However, his lectures are just an endless onslought of pure appeals to emotion with very little in the way of actual philosophy. He also very clearly leans extremely far to the left on pretty much every issue, and invited speakers who openly described themselves as socialist. This is neither good nor bad in a vacuum, but definitely something to be aware of when stepping into this class.

Both Isaijah and Rachael are absolutely phenomenal, wonderful people and teachers. Spend as much time with them as you can. Grading was extremely impressive and left lots of hints, and they would make sure to make help and office hours available when you needed it. Extensions were available no questions asked on all essays. They're both also super smart, which doesn't hurt.

Sections are good. If you have any opinions to the right of Bernie Sanders, you'll often feel a compulsion to bite your tongue. I'm relatively moderate by American political standards, so I had times I was happy to speak up and times I figured it best to shut up and let everyone else cook.

Grading is not harsh.

Overall, solid class. Very great CAs, cool (albeit very slanted) readings, good vibes.
Amazing class! The readings are super interesting and will give you incredible new insight on a topic you may feel like you already know well. Professor Stanczyk is so so knowledgeable and great at breaking down the complex readings we do.
It's a really great class, and a way to further explore objections to injustices we see in contemporary US society.

This course was absolutely integral to informing my conception of inequality, and the pervasive way in which it influences our society.

My favorite class of all time at Harvard. I loved the lectures, the readings, the content, the discussions, etc. More importantly, I really felt like I grew in a big way in this class. It may be worth noting that I am a CS major who has not taken many other courses focused on social justice, so I can't speak to the overlap this course will have if that's already your bread and butter. But as someone without much experience, coming into this class with some intro philosophy and an open mind, I felt that I developed:

1) Much better general critical thinking skills (interpretation, evaluation, argument, etc)
2) A rigorous theoretical foundation for social justice
3) Solid opinions on specific issues, now built from first principles
4) A new tolerance and ability to engage with opposing views

Moreover, the workload is very reasonable and manageable, in a way that I felt encouraged genuine engagement rather than busy work.

I cannot recommend this class highly enough!

A great philosophy class to take for those interested in a philosophy class centered in the everyday experiences and issues we face. Stancyk is an excellent lecturer, who is very good at outlining the concepts. Not too theoretical, with a helpful basis in Rawls and then throughout the course, a larger focus on main themes (such as workplace rights, immigration, etc) through contemporary philosophy, and occasionally legal lenses. Lots of support writing the essays, with great TFs. I also really appreciated that the final essay was due right before reading period, so it did not interfere at such a stressful time.
Be cautious about taking a course as difficult as this, especially if you have little to no experience in political philosophy. The readings are tedious, and the lectures can be dry. Most lectures include handouts (uploaded to Canvas afterwards) that detail the lecture word–for–word. The lectures from guest speakers were the most engaging of the course. However, the lecture topics are fascinating, and section discussions are often lively and engaging. Once you get past the first few weeks, the course begins to dive into topics that are more familiar to a general audience, but the readings and assignments still require much thought and effort. Start the writing assignments early. Go to TF office hours often. I did not find the department writing fellow to be very helpful or have much availability. If you are willing to put in the time to read, write, and re–write, then this course may be for you. It does a great job covering a wide range of pressing topics and helping you understand common political debates through a philosophical lens.

The teaching team, likewise, is incredibly invested in your wellbeing and success within the course. The assignments are brief, insightful, and challenging, yet the grading wasn't as harsh as I thought it would be. The class also attracted a diverse group of students from Econ/CS concentrators to graduate/extension students and beyond. Overall, an amazing experience that I'll be remembering for years.
This course is easily the best I've taken at Harvard so far. It allowed for so much flexibility and leniency in making time for the class while still allowing me to learn so much. Lucas reconstructs each main argument in the readings in every class, and he teaches so so well and articulately. Rachael, the TF, is the incredible kind and sharp, and every class and section, I felt like I walked away with a new understanding of the issue we were discussing that week. The first few weeks can be a little difficult with Rawls (which was still incredibly important and relevant even now), but the readings get a little easier in the second half and really focus on some of the biggest news and most pertinent issues of today. I can't recommend this class enough! Even as an elective, I think it's manageable and worthwhile.
Lucas taught this course phenomenally, and Rachael was a great TF.

This course really centers around a philosophical approach to defining justice and societal structure, and then applying those theoretical principles to real and pressing issues – race, gender, poverty, homelessness, immigration, and more. Lectures recap readings very well, and Lucas sometimes draws very interesting questions from them, but readings are still necessary when tackling them for papers. Sections were very engaging thanks to Rachael, but seemed to mostly be TF–dependent. The workload was relatively light, but papers were also mostly written at your own pace – extensions were generous. As someone who took this in their first–year fall, there are definitely no prerequisite skills required and I got a lot out of the class, but having any context around philosophy or social science helps.

Overall, a great course to take if you're interested in a philosophical understanding for why society feels so intuitively unfair.
