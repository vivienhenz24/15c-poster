Everyone at Harvard should take this class. Literally every time I went to lecture it felt like the most important learning to be done in my college career. You will discuss difficult and pressing questions of inequality in all sectors the workplace, law enforcement, gender, deep poverty, homelessness, to name a few. The readings are quite short and you should absolutely do them in order to get the most out of lecture that you can.

Lucas is the best lecturer I have ever witnessed. He makes handouts for class outlining the points he will make in lecture the handouts are fairly long, and every class I doubted if he would make it through all of the material. But every single time, he did each word served a purpose. He is a thoughtful and brilliant speaker.

The papers are very fair you do a short writing assignment reconstructing one of the readings, and then the paper is basically just adding a critical evaluation to the writing assignment.

You will leave this class a better person, a more informed citizen, and a smarter Harvard student than who you were before.

Professor Stanzcyk is among the smartest and kindest people I have met. His lectures are extremely well–structured, and his wit and clarity fill everyone with engagement and willingness to participate in the discussion. Outside of class, he always makes himself available to the students. The other teaching assistants were also invaluable and very considerate. I would enthusiastically recommend this course to anyone who wants to learn about theories of social justice (with an emphasis on Rawls) and immerse in a semester of thinking about pressing political issues. The course does not require a considerable time commitment as the readings are brief and engaging. Moreover, there are only four writing assignments, no midterms, and no final exams. It is an excellent opportunity to expand your knowledge, meet one of the most incredible Harvard professors, and have enough time to read about topics that interest you.

BEST CLASS IVE EVER TAKEN
AMAZING class. I came in as a CS concentrator who thought philosophy was mostly abstract BS, but I left understanding how rigorous and useful philosophy can be. The class is basically a deep dive on Rawls' theory of justice for the first few weeks, then applications of that theory (and related arguments) to contemporary social problems.

Each class Professor Stanczyk (super cool in a slightly aloof phil prof kinda way) gives a detailed reconstruction of the argument from the reading before introducing problems or critiques. That structure and the episodic format of the class means you can do as much or as little of the reading as you want, depending on how much time you have and how interested you are in a given day's topic –– while still learning a ton from the lectures. Even if you've taken something like justice, you'll get much deeper, more nuanced takes from this class.

There are only 4 papers, and you can base the longer ones on your shorter papers, so it's really only like 2 papers. The papers aren't easy As, but I found them very fun ways to dive deeply on a particular issue.
This course can be challenging if you haven't learned much about Rawls or have little experience engaging with philosophical topics in an academic setting. Despite the possible learning curve, this course is really interesting! More than anything, Professor Stanczyk is an amazing lecturer and professor! It's clear that he really cares about the subject and his students. Overall, I found this course to be very rewarding even though I'm not concentrating in philosophy!
