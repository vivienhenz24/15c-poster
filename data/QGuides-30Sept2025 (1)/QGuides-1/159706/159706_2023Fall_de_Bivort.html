In theory and in many aspects, this class is great – a diverse cast of passionate lecturers, a strong support staff (daily office hours with CAs and highly available course instructors and recorded lecturers), and a small class atmosphere with the ability for questions and discussion that prioritizes learning over an emphasis on grades.
I really enjoyed the class, but there's also some reservations I had.
A jam–packed curriculum means that you don't go super in–depth into topics but also disadvantages those without background knowledge – while not strictly necessary, it is VERY helpful if you've taken at least one of ap bio or ap chem (or equivalent), have knowledge of calculus, and have a basic understanding of combinatorics and probability because the first few weeks go by very fast.
The class meets every day (in addition to weekly section and lab) so it's quite the time commitment, and you also cover a lot of ground every week. Additionally, the staggered lecture structure (MWF is one topic while TTH is another, for example) can induce a sort of whiplash. The most important thing is to not fall behind and to do the psets legitimately. Doing the psets legitimately and truly understanding the content will benefit you so much in the long run in terms of retention and examination – cumulative exams on three different topics spanning three branches of science are already hard enough.
The best part of the class is the friends you make. The small class size and the daily meetings means you will have a lot of opportunities to find friends and strengthen those friendships over shared struggles like stat mech (trauma bonds >>>). And that's a really powerful thing for your first–year fall.

There are two ways to view LS50. There is a rose–tinted view where you can emphasize the amazing instructors and CAs and how much your mind will absorb in just a few short months. Then there is perhaps the more honest view, where all of the things I just mentioned might be true, but there are serious challenges over the course that will make you doubt your abilities and resent the overbearing workload. I will attempt to provide both.

Rose–tinted view: LS50a is genuinely impressive in how it surveys an immense amount of content in a short period of time. You will learn everything in the life science toolbox: biology, chemistry, coding, statistical mechanics, and math. No other course at Harvard is this broad while giving you a solid foundational preview of these domains. Your friends in LS1A will learn things that seem more conventional for a first–year chemistry and biology class, whereas LS50 sort of assumes that you already know enough of the basics to take your knowledge of biology to the next level. The average person in LS50 struggles but learns a lot from it. You will get the PSET on Friday night and have no idea what you're looking at, but by next week, you will have persevered and learned from the hours you poured into it. The faculty is absolutely amazing, and each is engaged in their own fascinating research in some way. The CAs are GODSENDS (special shoutouts to Chase, Robin, Sandhya, and Camilo) who are incredibly intelligent, patient, and kind. Also shoutout to Piyush and the lab part of this class! This is truly such an underrated opportunity to get your hands dirty with research and develop an original idea of your own.

A more critical view: There will be MANY lectures where you have no idea what on earth is going on. You'll be sleep–deprived from psetting late the night before, zone out for two minutes, and then realize you missed five stat mech derivations that seem to have come from nowhere. You'll listen to that cocky kid in class answer every one of the lecture questions in a heartbeat before you've even had a moment to digest it. You will get bad grades that would probably give your high school overachieving self a heart attack. Often, you will question if you're actually learning stuff or just going through all the motions to understand a single lecture just to walk into class the next day and be greeted with another unbelievably impossible lecture. For all that they do, some of the course staff can be intimidating at times, and you'll worry that they're judging you for not asking enough questions or not excelling on every test (bear in mind they probably aren't :) ). That said, if you decide to commit to LS50 and are re–reading the course evals to refresh on what exactly you've gotten yourself into, know that if you're struggling, you are NOT alone, you ARE capable, and if you're struggling, you are doing it right!! (Conversely, if you're breezing by, just don't be that guy you can be helpful without being condescending; that's what science is about!)

Note that you absolutely CAN do other things alongside LS50 (have a lively social life, be very involved in clubs, take other challenging classes, play a sport, etc). Just know that it is then unrealistic to compare yourself to the LS50 student who DOES make their first year all about LS50. You will likely not feel as satisfied with yourself or your mastery of the content I (and many I know) really struggled with this. Many of your peers will have some sort of natural inclination or heavy prior experience in the material to whom this advice will not necessarily apply. Just know that if you decide to do LS50 with lots of other commitments, you will probably get a little bit less out of the course, and it is up to you to weigh whether potentially being more well–rounded is worth some of the depth you will inevitably miss out on.

TLDR: This is a *really* hard class, and be honest with yourself before making a quick decision. You can be happy and successful in LS50, and you can also be equally happy and successful without it. To each their own.

Taking LS50 entails a significant time and energy investment, and your approach to the course will ultimately determine whether that investment is worth it. Here are some things to consider:
– If you are passionate about STEM and want to pursue a career in research but you dont really know what flavor of science interests you, take LS50. You will benefit tremendously from it. Alternatively, if you are certain that you want to pursue a research career in chemistry/biology, I would consider taking the exam to place out of LS1A, and then enroll in both LS50 and either one of CHEM 10 or MCB 60. I would try both classes during the add/drop period and then make a decision.
– If you prefer problem sets over readings/essays, dont be scared to take another PSET class alongside LS50. If you want to focus on LS50, take it alongside a Gen Ed and a freshman seminar or Expos; I would particularly recommend following this approach if you are worried about your background for the class (it will give you time to rewatch the lectures, go over the concepts, go to office hours, etcetera). I would not recommend taking this class alongside reading–intensive humanities/social sciences classes; you will suffer.
– Feeling worried about your background for this class is very valid, especially if you are an international student or if you went to a high school with limited advanced course offerings. Many people taking LS50 took college–level math/physics/chemistry courses during high school, and this difference in backgrounds sometimes feels very evident. If you are worried about your background, know that you can do perfectly fine, but you will definitely need to work harder (which is totally fine!)
– Be aware that LS50 attracts the most motivated Harvard students, and that this can be a double–edged sword. You will meet the most brilliant and inspiring individuals, but you will also encounter a few people who are more driven than they are kind (although the former far exceed the latter, so dont worry about this too much). If you are looking for a tight–knit community, you will definitely find it in LS50!
– Take advantage of all the resources facilitated by this course. Go to office hours. Schedule one–on–one meetings with Andrew to talk about research. Go to the MCB Thursday seminars right after lecture. Do the optional readings. If you are a naturally curious person, doing these things will nourish your curiosity and bring you a lot of intellectual satisfaction. They will make LS50 a very enjoyable and unique experience that will define your first semester of college.
