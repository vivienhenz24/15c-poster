If you are like me, you might believe that game theory is easy (from your experience in Ec 10a) and believe that this course will be similarly easy as it does not require a lot of math. Although it is true that there is no higher–level math involved, it is not as easy as you would expect. You actually need to do a lot of practice problems as solutions are not intuitive. Exams can be challenging and require a fair bit of memorizing the course material. On top of all that, the course is graded on a reasonably harsh curve (the median grade is a B).

Course material is fairly interesting at times (especially the evolution unit) but very abstract. It is hard to think of actual real–world applications for much of the course.
This course is a relatively chill introduction to game theory the class moves fairly slowly before the midterm, but picks up a little after that. Exams and assignments are reasonable, though the final was a decent amount harder than the midterm.
Great class to take, would recommend.
Take this course if you are looking for an econ elective with a reasonable workload. Most weeks you won't have much, but the content can be challenging if you don't stay on top of it while you learn. The content isn't too applicable to the real world but is an interesting look at economic theory.
This course is quite easy and takes little time. Take it if 1) you are interested in game theory, 2) you need an easy course to balance other courses
Make sure to do the readings and go to section! Section was crucial to understanding the subject matter of the course and knowing how to do the problem sets.
The good things about this class: there are only four problem sets which are not too bad and other than that there is just a midterm and final exam, so not too assignment–heavy. They give you only problem set drop, so if you play your cards right, you only have to do three of them. Section is optional. Also, game theory is pretty interesting and fun. It's accessible in that it requires little prior knowledge besides being able to solve systems of equations.

The bad things about this class: there are readings that are kind of optional but it's still annoying that they're there. They don't give you a lot of study materials for the midterm and final. The final exam also had short responses and an essay in addition to traditional problems, which kind of hurt my hand. Also, the professor is committed to curving the class such that the arithmetic mean is the cutoff between a B and a B+, which is kind of weird in my opinion. I think if you have an "A" numerically speaking (like a 93–100 average) you should be able to count on getting an A in the class. However, don't expect a curve to help you. With that being said, the problem sets and exams weren't hard in my opinion, so it's not like a curve was really necessary.

My advice: I think there are better classes to take to fulfill your economics concentration/secondary. If you're just super interested in the material, go for it. And if you basically have to take this course because others don't work for your schedule, then it's also not the worst class you can take. An A is totally doable, just go to class and stay on top of the problem sets :)
The tests reflect PSet content– make sure you know them well. Classes can be a bit confusing at times, but slides are very clear when reviewing for assignments. I think game theory is a interesting way of thinking and definitely provides a good background.
Professor is a nice guy but lectures can be pretty dry and the class materials are not great. Content is pretty interesting, you do game simulations in class (sometimes with small amounts of real money) which is a plus.

Be warned! I heard the grade distribution is really tough, something like half of students get a B or lower. So, watch out if you care about GPA!
Great course! Pretty chill introduction to game theory that doesn't go too deep into mathematics/simulation. Take this if you want to study the background and important concepts behind game theory and cooperation more specifically.
Take another class on this topic if you are interested. This one is not engaging or expansive in any way. The materials are dry, look like they are from the 1990s and unrelated and you have no idea what the midterms and exams will look like based on the lectures. The readings are incomprehensible and are pretty much non expected at all. Professor Neugeboren seems like he has no interest in teaching and it very cold and unencouraging.
This class is really interesting if you like to learn about a few classic game theory games that demonstrate the many key properties of game theory. Lectures were typically broken up into review of last weeks material, and new material, which was helpful. There are four psets throughout the entire semester, but they were usually only 4 questions long. Psets 1, 2, and 4 were all very manageable. If you follow along in class & go to section(please to go section, its where youll learn how to apply the concepts learned in class and also go over problems that are analogous to the ones on the pset. Also, our TF Mehar was amazing, but she is graduating so not sure who the TF will be next year. Your TF will absolutely make or break your success in this class!!), youll be okay on the psets, and they dont take very long. Pset 3 was definitely by far the most tedious pset, so please budget extra time for it.

There is a long reading list for this class, but honestly, you can get by without having read any of the readings just pay attention in class, especially during Unit 4 because the essay prompts on the final exam will be largely based off of Unit 4.

Lecture can be hard to follow and confusing at times, and I know many people who skipped lecture often and still did well on the psets. I would encourage going to lecture if you can though, we play a lot of fun in class games (if you win you get money too!) that demonstrate the theory we are learning. The class overall can feel an unstructured sometimes, but I took away many lessons from game theory overall, and would recommend with slight reservations. if you want a more in–depth understanding of game theory, I would probably take another class. However, if youre looking for a chill introduction, this is it! Learn a decent amount, light workload, and great vibes overall!
This course is a GREAT economics course that gives students a fun way to learn about core economic concepts of rationality and cooperation. If students commit to the course, it will give a lot back in return. The professor and TF were both invested in student success and were excited about the course content. It is a fun course, either for new economics explorers or seniors looking to round out their economics requirements.
The class can feel a bit dry at times as lectures are pretty traditional that sometimes moves a bit slowly, and sections have an emphasis on problem–solving. However, it provides a pretty interesting introduction to game theory, and I really loved the in–class games we got to play (with real rewards). The concepts aren't hard at all to grasp, and I took this class with no econ background (I am a CS/Physics major). Some notational stuff takes a bit of getting used to, so just go to OH to clear any confusions up. Overall, the psets were extremely manageable and the exams are written in a way that doesn't throw any curveballs and you should be completely prepared for as long as you do the psets.
