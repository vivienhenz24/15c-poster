This is an excellent course, and I wholeheartedly recommend it to everyone. As Prof. Coleman says, everyone will deal with loss at some point, and this course was an incredibly poignant way to confront that reality. The final project is among the most rewarding projects I've ever completed.
Take this course! Professor Coleman is so passionate!
This course is very easy, but sometimes the work can seem trivial and the grades given may seem nit–picky. I often found the course more annoying than difficult. The professor can sometimes have a very pretentious air, so avoid this course if that might really bother you.
If you are looking for comfort after a loss of your own take this class. I was a bit hesitant at first but I found the readings and conversation around all sorts of losses so rewarding. It does require reading books, writing short responses and a research paper but it was quite manageable after the first few weeks and it will definitely be a memorable class for me!
Each class was taught with care for the material and for the student. I left the class with a deeper understanding of the many different types of loss we experience in our lives. Loss is a fact of life as Dr. Coleman always stressed. We will all be confronted with it. Thank you Dr. Coleman for your considered approach to this subject matter.
INCREDIBLE CLASS. The selected readings and books are excellent; Joan Didion's "Year of Magical Thinking" is a must–read. Professor Coleman is a great lecturer and it is so clear that she is passionate about her students and the powerful topic of grief (be prepared to discuss some very sad but meaningful poems and art!). Section was probably my favorite section from any class I've taken at Harvard, highly focused and great conversations. This class does have multiple weekly assignments and readings that you must stay on top of, but the quizzes are more than fair (ridiculously easy). This class had the most helpful feedback on improving my written work that I've ever received at Harvard. The final paper is highly personal (prompt: write about a loss in your community) and has the potential to be a lifelong memory; it was for me.

10/10 class, though not a gem due to multiple weekly assignments. Definitely my favorite GENED I have taken, by far.
This course is a lot of work for a gened. You get one response paper every week, a quiz every two weeks, two discussion posts every week, a in class midterm, a scalar contribution, and a 10 page paper. This is definitely too much work for a gened. The sections are not helpful at all. Section always run over time, which causes me to almost always be late for the class after unless I sprint.
This class is great! This course was, is, and will be comforting and enlightening for people who might have suffered a loss or to anyone because loss is inevitable. This class may have several assignments due each week but they are totally manageable.
Truly one of the best classes you could possibly take at Harvard. If you're considering taking it, this is your sign.
I would take this course if you're very interested in poetry and music that people wrote in response to loss. The class is ambiguous at certain points, but embrace the abstract nature of loss. Each person experiences things differently. I think this principle applies to this class.
You should open your heart and be prepared to be changed! Professor Coleman is one of the most involved professors I've had the privilege of learning from at Harvard. She pours her all into each lecture, and she makes it a point to meet in person with each of her students in the first couple of weeks of the semester. If you want to get a lot out of the course, you should be prepared to work hard on weekly writing assignments and make sure that you are engaging with the readers. It will allow you to participate in section discussions and feel proud of the work you've done. Professor Coleman said that if you give your all to the course, she will give her all to you, and I think that this was a fair promise. If you commit to the class, you will walk away with a richer, deep, and even bright understanding of what it means to be a person who experiences loss. I think that it rewards introspective and contemplative thinkers! The section leaders (Will and Nadav) were out of this world, too. Many, many kudos to their hard work and warm support throughout the semester. I felt so privileged to have that team of three.
This course really helps you grow as a person. It makes you think about a tragic concept to be prepared for when you have to confront loss or comfort others who are grieving. You will come out of this course more empathetic.
* I would definitely take this course and it teaches valuable lessons for the future. Professor Coleman can have negative outbursts at times, which can be uncomfortable and unwarranted, so if you are willing to look past that, it is a great course.
Make sure you're prepared to handle the emotional weight of the subject matter. The topic will get darker than most things you'll learn at Harvard, but it is still oh so informative. I genuinely think there is no other course that can foster such a sense of empathy and help me understand such an important and inevitable part of daily existence.
Great class. Workload isn't too much at all and the content is pretty interesting. I wasn't expecting a lot going into the course but I often found myself having many engaging discussions about the material both inside and outside of class. It is a rewarding and enlightening experience!
I believe that every student should take this course. The reading material alone is magnificent. Professor Coleman puts an incredible amount of effort into crafting every aspect of this course. This course covers a subject which every student will face in their life after Harvard. I could not recommend it with more enthusiasm.
There are a lot of readings for this class–– some can be dry, and others will change your life and fundamentally alter the way you view grief. This is not a class catered towards STEM majors. Be aware that the majority of lectures are composed of poetry readings, with a tendency towards classical works.

Take this class if you enjoy analyzing art and literature, not if you are interested in the science behind grief and mourning. You do read some psychological studies, but I found them to be a weaker aspect of the course–– the main emphasis is on the "aesthetics and culture" category.

Professor Coleman is an excellent lecturer and a very engaged teacher, which is why I decided to take the course.
