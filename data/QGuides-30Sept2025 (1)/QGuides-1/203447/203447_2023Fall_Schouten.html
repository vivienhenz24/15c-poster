TAKE THIS COURSE!!! Educational justice is a topic with which nearly every Harvard student has a vested interest, so the content of the course is consistently engaging. The course is also structured in a way that makes it accessible to everyone, even those who have no philosophy background. What sets Phil 177 apart, however, is not just the subject matter or the course structureâ€”it's the exceptional professor, Gina! Gina brings a rare combination of thoughtfulness and approachability to the classroom, creating an environment where every student feels seen and heard. Her genuine passion for the subject matter is surpassed only by her commitment to each student's personal and intellectual growth.
This class is a MUST if you are interested in education/education policy. The conversations Professor Schouten encourages are incredibly enriching and so are the readings. Definitely time consuming readings and final case study project (lots of group work), so that is something to be noted that can be stressful.
This course is incredible if you're interested in exploring the philosophical problems that come with the way we do and distribute education more deeply. It truly feels like an educational experience that you'll take with you long after you're done with the class.

The class culture Professor Schouten creates with the seminar is an amazing and inviting one. Individually, you'll grow as a writer and philosopher as you think about these issues methodically in your papers.
If you are looking for a course with an excellent discussion and an interesting group of people, I would highly recommend this course. In terms of workload, this will probably be a 4th course for most given that a lot of the course is participation, and the quantity of assessable work is really limited to the two critical papers. On the other hand, if you do want to make it a big commitment, Gina gives excellently Indepth feedback on the papers, and you could push yourself to write excellent papers ––– this is however up to you. If you are not interested in educational justice (i.e. hadn't thought about it much until seeing this course in the directory, I would recommend you look elsewhere ––– the readings may be interesting still, but you may not be able to contribute as well to the atmosphere of the discussion. Overall great course, and the Professor has clearly put in a lot of time and deep thinking as to how to structure the course to maximize the quality of discussions.
