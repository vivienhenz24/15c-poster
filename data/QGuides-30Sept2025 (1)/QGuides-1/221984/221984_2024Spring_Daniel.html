take this course! prof daniel is really nice and you even get snacks in class sometimes :D i really learned a lot here and enjoyed things so much. the class itself is super manageable, you have readings and a memo each week, then a few assignments building up to a final project. there is a no electronics policy which is kind of ironic given the focus on inequality in this class. it's not too terrible, but if you feel really strong it could be an issue. otherwise though if you have the time and are in any way interested in food, this class is an absolute must!!
I loved this course! The readings are all very interesting and Dr. Daniel makes the course approachable to people who don't have a sociology background. If you want a more nuanced view of food inequality and really understand how deep this issue goes, definitely take this class.
To future students considering this class, I wholeheartedly encourage you to take the leap. This course on the sociology of food inequality is not just another academic endeavor; it's a transformative journey that will broaden your perspective and ignite your passion for social justice.
TAKE THIS COURSE! As a second–semester senior without an ounce of sociology experience, I found the course a perfect introduction to sociology, food systems, and qualitative research. Caitlin is one of the best course heads I've ever head – her enthusiasm was infectious and she was always so responsive and willing to give feedback. Though the syllabus may appear to have a lot of readings, there is a good mix of pop–culture, news articles, and theory, which ends up grounding the lay readings in the field. It can sometimes be a lot of work, but it was honestly really helpful to have a paper proposal, outline, and literature review completed before the final paper so that you a) get feedback on your ideas before writing the paper and 2) aren't completely drowning during reading period. All in all, take the course – you won't regret it!
I really learnt so much from this class beyond food deserts –– class discussions with Caitlin were engaging and incisive, and it never felt too theoretical.
This is a wonderful class! I learned a lot about the food scene in the U.S. and challenged my pre–existing "access" and "cost" arguments about food insecurity. I learned how to think about the deeper socio–economic issues that perpetuate food insecurity and look to the underlying issues rooted in poverty. If you are taking it as an elective, be ready to dedicate time to doing the readings and writing the memos every week. This is not a class where you can glance or bs the readings and just speak up in class and be done. You need to engage with the course deeply, but it will pay off in class, as you'll leave feeling fulfilled!
This was truly a magnificent course! The arc of learning and the layout of the course was so well done, from the very beginning when we debunked common misconceptions about food inequality to the end when we revisited some of those misconceptions and reflected on what we had learned. I thought that all of the readings/videos were super interesting and drew from diverse perspectives. I also appreciated that many of them were first hand accounts from people experiencing the effects of food inequality (not just sociologists and experimentalists purveying their own opinions). Never once in class did I feel like I wasn't engaged. Professor Daniel did a great job encouraging participation, facilitating thoughtful discussion questions, and giving everyone a chance to speak without judgement. The atmosphere was very welcoming and we all felt comfortable with each other by the end. Overall, I feel like I learned so much and it is all stuff that I can apply to my own life and the ways I view the world around me. TAKE THIS CLASS!!!
