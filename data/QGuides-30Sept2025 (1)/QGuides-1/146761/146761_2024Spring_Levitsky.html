This was not an easy class for me. It is difficult to understand expectations for papers unless you meet constantly with the TFs (which I highly recommend you do). Take notes on the lecture hand outs you receive in the exam since they will be outlining his major talking points and allow you to get organized. Write the definitions for the words to know at the end of each lecture since 9 of these will be on the exam. Also, the class works by exploring the concepts outlined in the syllabus by using cases/examples from Venezuela, Chile, Brazil, Mexico, and Argentina. It's useful to create a timeline of these nations as you go BUT only as they relate to the concept of the week (Ex. different dictatorships across these countries). You only need to know a 1–2 sentence summary of the readings so you can bring them up in section or on the exam and relate them to the cases. I also recommend that you find a group to study with early in the semester. This can help make the readings more bearable.

Overall, it's an incredibly interesting and rewarding course but there is no "ease–in" phase you will jump right in from day 1. While Levitsky is an incredible lecturer you will not need to remember everything he says since many very specific facts will be included. I recommend this class if you're willing to put in the work for it and learn something new. Good luck!
