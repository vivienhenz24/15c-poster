Really interesting structure with all group work on the board and with teaching staff coming around to assist. Almost zero lectures, so can sometimes be difficult to fully grasp the material. The teaching staff is awesome!!!!! Wasn't a big fan of notion.
This is an awesome class that was taught very well this semester. Would absolutely recommend even for non–astro–concentrators!
Some people might give this class a lot of criticism, but genuinely it is one of the best classes you will take at Harvard. Professor Johnson's concept of the course is extremely untraditional (short PSETs with in–depth writeup requirements, and group chalkboarding in class), which makes the class a good distraction from the rest of your college courseload. You probably will not learn "as much" about basic astronomy as you would in a cut–and–dry astro class, but I think that Professor Johnson's focus is not on the concepts of the class, but rather on the practice of science. A lot of us never really learn what it means to think like a scientist and collaborate like a scientist, and this class is a great introduction to all of that. You probably will take this as a concentration requirement, but you should consider taking it even if you're not a concentrator––it's a glorified pset class with a light workload, and you will get a lot more out of it than other Q–guide comments might suggest.
This class will really teach you a lot and it is super fun and gives a wonderful overview of stellar and planetary astronomy!
A good intro to astro. The course is not the most organized, but the teaching staff are very helpful and enthusiastic. If you are used to learning from organized slides and notes, this will be a big switch. There is A LOT of room for misunderstanding about what you are learning or what you are supposed to take away. That being said, if you put in the work and engage with the teaching staff, you can solidify what you are confused on. It is also very low pressure, which makes learning confusing and difficult topics a lot more enjoyable. Take with an open mind and don't too intimidated!
This class has a very unique structure, where almost all of the formulas and material is learned through worksheets that students do in small groups. It's an interesting concept, but it necessarily limited the scope of the course quite significantly. If you have some background in math or physics, you may find the material to be overly simplistic, and the (well–intentioned) interference of the course assistants can often come off as more grating or condescending (and sometimes wrong) than helpful. I tried too hard to take this class seriously at the start of the semester, and had a bad time during the first month or so; eventually I just tried to have fun with it, and I enjoyed the latter half of the semester significantly more. If you're taking this for your concentration or looking for a rigorous course in astronomy, temper your expectations the structure of the course unfortunately makes it impossible to really go in–depth with some of the concepts. But if you just go in looking for a fun time, I do think it is possible for everyone to enjoy this class!
I enjoyed this course! I've always really enjoyed astrophysics material, and I've got to say that this course has only helped with that. If you like working in small groups on worksheets instead of just sitting in a lecture, this is the course for you! I felt a lot more comfortable working in a smaller group to solve problems rather than just going into homeworks blind about what solving astrophysics problems entail. The midterm and final were barely like a test at all, the structure of them was essentially people come in to visit the course who have a wide range of backgrounds and in small groups you work to talk about the problems with them. As someone who always struggles on tests due to anxiety, I really enjoyed this method because it never felt like there was any negative outcomes. If you have trouble explaining a problem, another group member or even a CA/TF/Prof would help you out, which I really enjoyed since it felt more welcoming. The problems sets were a bit all over the place in terms of difficulty, but the CAs and TFs were super helpful in answering questions and helping out. I would recommend this course if you can commit to going to essentially every class and if you like learning, because this course is tailored to the learning and solving experience rather than tests.
Interesting class with a great professor and course staff! The active learning is definitely unconventional, but is pretty helpful, and the course emphasizes student learning and understanding, so it's an encouraging atmosphere. If you have a solid physics/math background some of the content might seem pretty repetitive. The lab weeks can be quite time–consuming though, so keep that in mind, and towards the end of the semester there's a couple of pretty difficult homework assignments. The active learning is so unstructured that it can be confusing to figure out what to do/where to start, which is frustrating and makes the homework difficult if your group hasn't finished a worksheet, but you can ask the staff for help.
Astro 16 is a low–work class that isn't difficult, at least grade–wise. You'll learn a decent amount about the basics of astronomy/astrophysics (although you probably won't realize it until the end of the semester). The midterm and final, at least for this semester, was to teach a guest student about what you'd learned in the class – pretty much every student in the class enjoyed that format. Professor Johnson is someone who definitely cares about the learning of every student and is invested in your education, always willing to take and answer questions and seems passionate about what he teaches and how he teaches.
The whole mantra of the class is "Process over Product." This means that there are few lectures (they are great when Prof Johnson gives them), and pretty much all the learning is from worksheet problems done in groups on the board in class. Unfortunately, this means that the content is often unclear, especially when trying to do the homework.
The main problem I had with the course was getting mixed feedback/help from the TFs and Prof. There were many times when we were working on a problem on the board and one TF said we weren't doing it right, then 45 minutes later another would come up and say we were doing it right the first time. I think this is a result of not having solutions to most of the worksheets created until the end of the semester. They also had the habit of focusing on the neatness of our work – we'd be trying to solve a problem, but they would ask us to rewrite everything on the board in a neater way. In my opinion, this was counterproductive as working out difficult problems is meant to be messy. It led to us often having to focus more on the visual appeal of our work rather than the actual concepts and process overall.
Don't let what I've said dissuade you from taking the class. You'll learn a quite a bit and probably enjoy it if you like this format, and the labs are pretty fun.

You will gain the most from this class if you come to sections each day ready to learn and collaborate with others as you work through problems. The homework sets are pretty challenging but they aren't too frequent at all and as long as you take advantage of office hours, you should be able to get them done. The exams are also very relaxed, as they are meant for you to just review your understanding and ensure that you conceptually can grasp the content that's being taught, as opposed to simply memorizing derivations and having to write them all out by hand.
This class was engaging and really helped me think about problems and problem solving in a different way. It had a completely different style of learning, with most of the learning done with groups at a chalkboard. We had very interesting discussions about scientific research in general too. Professor Johnson is extremely enthusiastic about the topics we learn and is always willing to adapt. When he or the TFs don't know the answer to someone's question, they will research their question and then explain a thought–out answer to the question in the next class.

Sometimes it was hard to gauge how effective the style of the class was (with minimal lectures and most of it being work on the chalkboard). Sometimes, I questions how much information I was actually retaining or learning. However, by the time the midterm and end of the semester rolled around, I realized I had learned and retained a lot more than I had thought, and my knowledge came with more understanding because most of what we learned we had derived on the chalkboards.
This is a phenomenal course! The teaching staff truly cares about all of the students, and you build really strong bonds throughout the year while practicing active learning of astrophysics. We literally spent a whole class one day talking about post–grad research and how the government controls everything (including the future of science). There is very little physics knowledge that you need prior (just like forces), the majority of the prerequisites come from math. You'll be fine with 1B, but some familiarity with 21A is helpful for the more complicated stuff. The staff focus on making this class have a different approach to learning than most other intro STEM classes, and it really pays off because it turns into a noncompetitive, low stakes environment where you can just ask questions and talk about science. PSETs can be a LOT of work, because many questions are very open ended and indirectly related to what we learned in class. However, office hours are usually helpful. Also, you will need to use Python and LaTeX quite frequently, so familiarity with these is helpful but not required (I learned both this year and did fine)
