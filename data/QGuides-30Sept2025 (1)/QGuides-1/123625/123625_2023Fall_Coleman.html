I have a very love hate relationship with this class. It is an amazing course. Prof. Coleman is an amazing teacher who really cared about her students and is really good at making you learn all the material. But it is also a very time consuming class and probably the most difficult Classics Department course I have taken so far (ps. I have not taken 112s). Epigraphy is very nitty–gritty, and there are a lot of memorization in this class: abbreviations, epigraphic conventions, dates, historic context, so many things. And Prof. Coleman is not particularly a nice grader. Your quiz grade can easily go from A in the previous one to C or D in the next one, because so much of it depends on whether you are attentive enough to figure out everything on the stone. We have a lot of reading in English that are pretty overwhelming to get through but are never discussed in class. Not a lot of Latin reading for homework, but what we read in class is HARD. Most inscriptions or graffiti we translate are legalese (with tons of clauses), or have archaic spelling, or misspelling, or just poor grammar that hardly makes sense. You really have to think outside of the box and know your original forms and charts. There could be a million way to translate habere and facere in a legalese context. And you also have to learn so much weird vocab, anything ranges from "tunnel" to a kind of gladiator. The final project is pretty cool, but since so much scholarship on epigraphy in general is written in Italian or German or French, it was a pain to google translate every single article that is mildly relevant or to channel all the Latin you know to try to read Italian... I enjoyed writing my final paper and felt really accomplished afterward. But I do not miss the process of researching.

I do not regret taking this class at all. It is really cool and refreshing to learn and read the regular Roman Latin, to laugh at their jokes, to emphasize with parents who lost their child. It also made my Latin grammar improve a lot, since I have to think in reverse on what ending should this abbreviated word has. Again, Prof. Coleman is a great and concise teacher on the basics. And I would definitely love to take more epigraphy classes in the future if there are any chances. But it was a challenging and time consuming class, and you should consider it at your own discretion.
This is a terrific course! Latin epigraphy is a difficult subject, and deducing, transcribing, and interpreting the often very concise and minimalistic, or in other scenarios highly formalized inscriptions, though requiring considerable effort, is an extremely rewarding experience! The course provides a glimpse into both the many ways in which Latin was used in the ancient world, as well as the lives of people, societies, and the empire as a whole. Estimates of literacy in the Roman Empire are, broadly, rather low, and much of the extant poetry and literature is contained within the higher echelon of society, but inscriptions and graffiti yield a view of the other side: epitaphs, emotional expressions, occupations, advertisements(!), financial and legal documents, and so on. Professor Coleman is an amazing professor and class is always very enjoyable; moreover, the final project (though long) was a terrific experience at trying out epigraphy oneself would highly recommend!
The class is fairly difficult if you aren't used to reading inscriptions, but the challenge feels like it's worth it. The professor is a delight to learn from, and she is very thorough in the resources and knowledge she provides. One of my favorite classes I've taken.
I learned so much through this class and it was an incredibly rewarding experience in that regard, but the class was definitely more rigorous than I expected and required a lot more time put into it than I initially anticipated. Epigraphy is a very precise discipline and as a result requires strong Latin skills and critical thinking, along with being meticulous about the smallest of details. Overall, I built a lot of skills in this class and would definitely recommend to anyone seeking to take a class where they'll learn lessons that'll probably stick with them for life (and will become very useful whenever you go to a museum), but you really must make this your top–priority class or somewhere close to it, since this class requires many hours of hard work and determination.
It might be hard, and a lot of work at times, but the experience of taking the class and the skills you acquire are worth more than any grade you could possibly get, and so I highly recommend taking this course.
