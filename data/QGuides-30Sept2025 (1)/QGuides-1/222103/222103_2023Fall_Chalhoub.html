Comments
This was one of the best classes I took at Harvard! The texts are so interesting and we looked at them in a unique way. Professor Chalhoub was so knowledgeable and had incredible points about the books, but he also really let the discussion go wherever the students took it.
This is an excellent course taught by an extraordinary professor who facilitates very interesting discussion in class, maintaining a great balance of sharing his knowledge and facilitating contributions from students. The subject matter was extremely interesting, and would appeal to students coming from a variety of disciplines, including Literature, History, Sociology, Philosophy, etc. I could not reccomend Prof. Chalhoub more highly, who genial, gets along so well with his students, and generates so much enthusiasm for what he has chosen to teach.
If you would like to develop a more deliberate relationship with fiction and appreciate the various ways that truths can be interpreted and discovered in language, you should take this course. Professor Chalhoub is a uniquely gifted professor. He allows the discussion to go where it needs to go and will provide his insights when needed. I have taken two courses with Professor Chalhoub, and each created such an enriching and free–spirited learning environment that is rather difficult to come by at Harvard. I was glad to be a humanities student in his presence and in the presence of my thoughtful peers. I will also add that Professor Chalhoub is one of the most deliberate professors I have had when it comes to what history is, what it means to study history, what it means to write and read in historical ways, etc. These metahistorical questions are deeply engaging and essential, but they are not often interrogated to the extent they deserve to be.
This is an amazing course! Sidney is a fantastic professor. In addition to being a great human being, he has an incredible reading list and knows how to teach relevant aspects of history and archives while allowing for a lot of genuine enjoyment of literature. The workload is usually one book a week (occasionally it's a few journal articles) but it's really doable because the readings are genuinely enjoyable. If you are at all interested in ideas related to history, archive, fiction vs. reality, and/or literature, I would 100% recommend this course!
This is a very unique History course, and while it heavily revolves around reading novels, it is quite different from what you might find in the English or Hist/Lit department. You will learn important skills like close reading and textual analysis, but all with a historical lean to it. You will not come away from this class with specific names and dates from Brazilian history, but you will engage with some of my favorite novels in recent memory, with the works by Machado de Assis being a particular highlight for me. Course assignments are manageable, but a good sense of time management and critical reflection are necessary to keep up with the roughly one novel a week cadence. I also have the highest praise for Professor Chalhoub, who consistently delivers some of the most exciting seminar topics in the department in a low–stress, high–energy, extremely positive environment. As a small seminar, you will have plenty of opportunities to make a meaningful contribution and learn from one another, but also get to know Professor Chalhoub, whose availability and flexibility are unmatched. I highly recommend any course he teaches, even if you aren't coming from the strongest background in that subject matter.
This class will change how you read fiction. You come out of it with a deep respect for the way in which fiction serves a a reliable source of historical knowledge. Professor Chalhoub is one of the kindest, most caring professors I've had, and so he's willing to conform the class discussion around what we find most interesting.
This is one of the best classes I've taken at Harvard. You'll learn how to read in a new way, especially if your background is in literature. You'll likely be introduced to Machado de Assis for the first time, and I think our entire class agreed that everybody should read him. Professor Chalhoub is brilliant, and yet you can tell he really does care about what students have to sayhe often opens class with "I assigned this because I wanted to see what you'd think about it." You really do get out of this class what you put in, so DO THE READING. It's mostly fun and it's literally all you talk about for two hours.
