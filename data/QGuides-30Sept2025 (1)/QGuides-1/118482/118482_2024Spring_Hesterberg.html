I only took it for my CS requirement, and it was fine. The exam average is like a 30% which I thought was insane.
This class is really fun, in no small part due to I think Adam having fun teaching it. He puts a lot of work into his lectures, making sure that big proofs are broken down into incremental steps, and that the material is grouped into relating concepts. Adam also frequently relates graph theory concepts that we learn to the cutting edge (Hadwiger's conjecture), making our work feel relevant despite it being an intro class. He cracks a few jokes that I honestly really liked, and his brain works so fast that he has an almost immediate answer to edge case questions brought up by students during lecture. Can't overstate how clear I found his lectures.

As a CS student taking this class instead of CS124 in my senior year, I found that I enjoyed graph theory way more than I thought and I didn't feel utterly lost on problem sets the way I did occasionally in CS61 or CS120.
This class is a good alternative to CS124. The psets are challenging and require quite time–consuming, but they are manageable if you make use of the office hours. The teaching fellows were great guides throughout the course content.
It's lowkey hard but not crazy. Take it if it fulfills one of your requirements. It's kind of interesting at times and the problem sets are manageable if you go to OH. With Adam, exams have been crazily difficult, but if it's a different professor it's probably fine. Lectures were not the most useful if I'm being honest, so I ended up self–learning a lot of it (but also I usually can't really pay attention in lecture in general), and it was fine. I liked that it just has two midterms (non–cumulative) and no final, no final project. It's just psets and two midterms which was nice for me.

I took this class because I skipped CS20 and this was the most reasonable "formal reasoning" requirement for CS that I could find. My takeaway is don't skip CS20, but if you need to take this class it's probably the least painful formal reasoning after CS20 in my opinion. If you have taken CS120 and CS124, you might be wondering what the overlap looks like and/or if you should still take this class. In my opinion, the overlap is not that much; you'll see concepts from those classes come up again like coloring but, to me, the main difference is that in 120/124 you're talking about algorithms for graphs whereas here we're talking about graph theory and properties of graphs. This turned out to be more boring than I expected (I think I liked the algorithms part better) because you sometimes prove things that honestly seem pretty useless (prove that if graph X has this Y property then it can be colored with 3 colors or has this many cycles idk).
I would highly recommend to take this class even if you don't need it as a requirement, take it as an elective. You learn a lot about graphs which can be applied to many CS problems but I think the way this class makes you learn about things differently and makes you think about things like induction in a new light is really cool.

The average on the first midterm was (I believe) a 36% and was one of the hardest exams I've ever taken. Going to office hours and section certainly helps with the problem sets. The course moves at a good pace and is a good option for an applied math requirement. Other than the exams, the problem sets are definitely doable and the course overall isn't extremely difficult.
This course is difficult but it also forces you to put the work in to deeply understand major problems in graph theory and combinatorics. I would recommend it to more advanced students interested in pursuing mathematics.

Each lecture is carefully crafted, and you can tell that Adam really enjoys teaching these areas. The psets are difficult but still completely doable, but the midterm I thought was unnecessarily difficult. It featured problems that were at least as difficult as pset questions, but I figure that the class is curved so it doesn't matter too much.
This class is really cool! I think it's definitely difficult at times, particularly for the problem sets and the midterms especially with Adam, but I think the work was definitely worth it. Find a TF that will help you since problem sets do get pretty hard towards the latter half of the course, but it was really interesting overall!

If you are willing to put in the time, this is a great course.

These are exciting topics! I'd encourage you to take this course if you are excited to learn about graph theory and combinatorics. Not much is needed as prerequisites, though if you've never done math proofs you'll have a bit of a learning curve. For the combinatorics portion, your ability to do algebra will become strong if it is not already. For the Graph Theory portion, I'd encourage you to not wait long to ask for help – a lot of time can be spent unprofitably by drawing your graph constraints incorrectly, it is a skill that took me many repetitions to gain. Good luck!
This is a fun course to take if you are interested in graph theory. If you are taking this to get out of CS 124, I will say that this is significantly less work than CS 124, but the content will probably not be applicable outside of a theoretical CS framework (if at all)––you'll probably feel more motivated just taking 124. [Just take 124.] The math/theorems/problem solving in this class is fairly fun & a lot more grounded than most "proofs" I've done in other CS classes, but I think the exams this year were significantly tougher than previous iterations of the course. Additionally, I struggle to see where what I've learned in this class will translate to anything I do in the future––you learn some cool problem solving techniques & theorems though! I would recommend this class only to people who actively think they are interested in graph theory (and are willing to learn some cool tricks with combinatorics, though this is kind of a side quest you do in the class).
